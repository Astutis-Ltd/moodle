The roles and functions of occupational health specialists

In complex organisations with a spectrum of occupational health hazards a multi-faceted team of professionals may be required to help manage the risks.

The key professionals are likely to be an occupational physician and occupational health nurse / adviser. Other professionals who may have a key role to play include:

  • toxicologists
  • physiotherapists
  • ergonomists
  • microbiologists
  • psychologists
  • health physicists.

Occupational hygienists may also work within the team, although as discussed in Element B4 occupational hygiene is a distinct profession with a more proactive role in the measurement and evaluation of occupational health risks.

Occupational health services are often contracted out to specialist service providers.

Occupational physicians / occupational health nurse

Occupational physicians and nurses are usually experienced medical professionals who have obtained additional occupational health qualifications (see later).

Activities undertaken include:

  • Medicals to establish baseline health characteristics. Note: pre-employment medicals are no longer allowed. The Equality Act 2010 makes it unlawful, except in certain circumstances, for employers to ask about a candidate’s health before offering them work
  • Health surveillance. The on-going assessment and / or medical examination of certain employees at regular intervals
  • Managing sickness absence through return to work interviews etc.
  • Providing a treatment service. Typically a lesser role, however treatment services can provide business benefits by reducing the need for time away from the workplace
  • Assistance with health-related risk assessments
  • Counselling. Confidential listening services and referrals to specialist counselling services
  • Drugs and alcohol at work testing and support (discussed later)
  • Health education and health promotion. Promoting healthy lifestyles, good diet and exercise is likely to impact positively on staff availability for work
  • Rehabilitation. Facilitating return to work following illness or injury by a managed process of lighter duties etc.
  • Ill-health retirement
  • Health advice for overseas workers
  • General advice to the employer on policy etc.

Related professionals

  • Occupational hygienist’s role in the assessment of the working environment for occupational hazards opens the door for liaison with occupational health professionals and the assessment of health effects on individual workers
  • Ergonomists may be involved in the assessment of musculoskeletal risks and the design of the layout of displays and controls
  • Physiotherapists can assist with the rehabilitation of staff following injury
  • Health physicists are responsible for assessing radiation doses in workplaces using ionising radiations
  • Laboratory staff have a support role with biological monitoring
  • Depending on the size and nature of the organisation, other services such as dentistry, dieticians and alternative therapies may be provided.
