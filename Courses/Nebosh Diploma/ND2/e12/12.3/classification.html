Classification of HAVS symptoms

A clinical examination is carried out to, where possible, diagnose HAVS or CTS and exclude differential diagnoses.

There are a range of tests that can be used to look for symptoms such as sensitivity, dexterity, grip, neuropathy (nerve damage), carpal tunnel syndrome and blood flow / pressure.

The classification scheme known as the modified Stockholm Workshop scales should be used to classify neurological and vascular symptoms (see the following table).

The Griffin method is a system for allocating a weighted numerical value to each phalange affected and calculating an overall score for finger blanching in each hand as illustrated in the image below.

This is a useful, practical means of monitoring progression of symptoms in individual fingers. It does not take account of the frequency of attacks, which may be more relevant in assessing functional disability. Attacks can lead to a variable degree of blanching. In this case, the worst distribution should be recorded.

A score is given for blanching each phalange (part of each digit) as shaded in the image below. A total value for each hand can also be given. The score for the left hand is 16 and that for the right hand is 4.

Stage Criteria Assessment
Left hand Right hand
Sensorineural (SN)
0SN Vibration exposure but no symptoms
1SN Intermittent numbness and / or tingling (with a sensorineural, SN, score of >3 and <6)
2SN (early) Intermittent numbness, and / or tingling, reduced sensory perception (usually an SN score of >6, <9)
2SN (late) Persistent numbness, and / or tingling, reduced sensory perception (usually an SN score of >9, <16)
3SN Constant numbness and / or tingling, reduced sensory perception and manipulative dexterity in warmth (and an SN score of >19)
Vascular (V)
0V No attacks
1V Attacks affecting only the tips of the distal phalanges of one or more fingers (usually a blanching score of 1 - 4)
2V (early) Occasional attacks of whiteness affecting the distal and middle (rarely also the proximal) phalanges of one or more fingers (usually a blanching score of 5 - 9)
2V (late) Frequent attacks of whiteness affecting the distal and middle (rarely also proximal) phalanges of one or more fingers (usually a blanching score of 10 - 16)
3V Frequent attacks of whiteness affecting all of the phalanges of most of the fingers all year (usually a blanching score of 18 or more)
4V As V and trophic changes

    Definitions

  • Intermittent - not persistent
  • Persistent - lasting more than 2 hours
  • Constant - present all of the time
  • Occasional - 3 or fewer attacks per week
  • Frequent - more than 3 attacks per week.

Note: The staging is made separately for each hand. The grade of disorder is indicated by the stage and number of affected fingers on both hands, eg stage / hand / number of digits - “2L(2) / 1R(l)” indicates:- stage 2 left hand, two fingers affected / stage 1 right hand, one finger affected.

To separate early and late stage 2 vascular, the terms occasional and frequent are used in conjunction with the Griffin blanching scores. If there is a mismatch between frequency of attacks and extent of finger blanching then the extent of blanching should be given more weight.

The implications of future hand-arm vibration exposure following diagnosis of HAVS should be discussed with the employee to address balancing the risk of disease progression whilst remaining in a role necessitating vibrating tool use.

The methods for assessment and prediction of progression of HAVS are not precise and do require clinical judgment.

The ability to handle a tool safely whilst experiencing vascular and / or neurological symptoms should be considered as part of the assessment process, as a recommendation of ‘not fit for work’ may need to be made on safety grounds.
