Introduction

As was explained in Element 3, health surveillance is a term used to describe a system of ongoing health checks, typically required by law, whereas health monitoring is used where the causal relationship between exposure and harm is less clear and consequently there is no specific legal duty.

As will be shown, health surveillance (medical surveillance) is required to check for the early signs of vibration white finger, whereas more general health monitoring arrangements are use to track the health state of drivers exposed to whole body vibration.

Medical assessments are more appropriate for HAVS as the early signs of Vibration White Finger (VWF) can be tested for.

Health surveillance

Health surveillance is a programme of systematic health checks to identify early signs and symptoms of disease and to allow action to be taken to prevent its progression. It also checks the long-term effectiveness of control measures to safeguard the health of employees (including identifying and protecting people at increased risk).

In the case of HAV, one of the aims is to prevent employees developing hand-arm vibration syndrome associated with loss of hand function. It is possible that your employees who are exposed to vibration have mild symptoms of HAVS, the relevance of which they are not aware. Health surveillance can help them to recognise the significance of their symptoms.

Appropriate health surveillance must be provided for hand-arm vibration-exposed employees who:

  1. are likely to be regularly exposed at or above the EAV of 2.5 m/s2 A(8)
  2. are likely to be exposed occasionally at or above the EAV and where the risk assessment identifies that the frequency and severity of exposure may pose a risk to health
  3. are at particular risk even if exposure in the current job is below the EAV.

Health surveillance involves working through a number of tiers as follows:

Tier 1:

Initial assessment 		A short questionnaire used as a first check for people moving into jobs involving exposure to vibration. Completed questionnaires should be sent in confidence to an occupational health professional because they contain information about the individualâ€™s medical history.

Tier 2:

Annual screening 		A short questionnaire issued once a year to employees exposed to risks from vibration to check whether they need to be referred to Tier 3 for a clinical assessment.

Tier 3:

Clinical assessment 		A questionnaire that asks about relevant symptoms and a targeted clinical Examination by an occupational health professional are recommended.

Tier 4:

Diagnosis

A formal diagnosis carried out by a doctor with appropriate expertise.

An employee diagnosed with HAVS should be reassessed at regular intervals and may need additional assessments in order to monitor any progression of the disease.

Tier 5:

Optional testing 		A referral of the employee to a specialist centre for further tests for HAVS. The final diagnosis of HAVS still depends on the judgement of the doctor and cannot be based on these tests alone.
