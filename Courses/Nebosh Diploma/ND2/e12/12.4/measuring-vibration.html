Measuring vibration magnitude

An accelerometer is used to measure the root mean square acceleration. An accelerometer is sensitive to acceleration along a single axis. To assess the total vibration entering the hand, measurements should be taken along the orthogonal axes, x, y and z. A tri-axial accelerometer can be used to measure the three axes simultaneously.

Root mean square a statistical measure of the magnitude of a varying quantity.

As there is a clear dose response relationship for hand-arm vibration (not so for whole body vibration), the accumulated dose over the working day is the measure of interest. This is known as the A(8) and is comparable to the LEPd for noise.

The A(8) is, like the vibration magnitude, expressed in acceleration units of m/s2.

If the total exposure time happens to be exactly eight hours (unlikely in practice), the daily vibration exposure has the same value as the average vibration magnitude.

If the exposure time is less than eight hours (typically the case), their exposure is less than the average vibration magnitude.

If an employee’s daily exposure time is more than eight hours (rare in practice, but possible if long shifts are worked), then their exposure is greater than the average vibration magnitude.

Exposures that lie in the lowest area (for example: a magnitude of 3 m/s2 and a duration of 2 hours) are below the Exposure Action Value (EAV), those in the middle area are above the EAV and those in the top area are above the Exposure Limit Value (ELV).

Manufacturers’ information on vibration emission, as declared in the equipment’s handbook, is often significantly lower than levels measured in ‘real life’ use of equipment. The HSE advises doubling handbook estimates before using them for estimating daily exposures.

If the employee is exposed to vibration from more than one tool or work process during a typical day, information will need to be collected on likely vibration levels, together with estimated ‘trigger time’ for each type of tool used and / or activity undertaken. This information can then be input into an exposure calculator, such as the HSE’s Hand-arm vibration exposure calculator, to assess each employee’s daily exposure.

Exposure points are generally easier to work with than the A(8) values and their use is based around these simple rules:

  • exposure points change simply with time: twice the exposure time, twice the number of points
  • exposure points can be added together, for example where a worker is exposed to two or more different sources of vibration in a day
  • the exposure action value (2.5 m/s2 A(8)) is equal to 100 points
  • the exposure limit value (5 m/s2 A(8)) is equal to 400 points.
