PPE and wearable technology

Anti-vibration gloves

Several different types of anti-vibration gloves are available. The amount of vibration reduction they provide depends on the thickness and softness of the lining, which is usually a resilient gel, foam or rubberlike material or an array of air bladders.

Gloves are generally:

  • most effective at higher frequencies, above the main vibration frequency of most power tools and at frequencies whose contribution to the frequency-weighted vibration magnitude is likely to be minimal
  • ineffective at reducing the most problematic lower frequencies of vibration that have the greatest effect on exposure.

To protect against lower vibration frequencies the contact areas of the glove need to contain thicker resilient material, which can seriously limit dexterity, making them unsafe to use. Comfort may also be affected, making them unacceptable to employees.

If operators need to tighten their grip on the tool to compensate for the effect of glove wearing this may actually increase the risk of vibration injury.

Gloves marketed in Europe as anti-vibration must carry the CE mark and have been tested and found to meet the requirements of BS EN ISO 10819.

Wearable technology

A range of wearable technologies are available for measuring exposure to hand arm vibration. Sensors can be integrated into gloves or wristwear to monitor exposure dose and provide early warnings that the daily exposure action value is being approached.

Although the technologies continue to evolve there is research to confirm the accuracy of many of the available devices. Concerns include accuracy over time, impact of wear and tear, need for recalibration and the human factors regarding wearing and looking after the equipment and responding to warnings in a timely manner.
