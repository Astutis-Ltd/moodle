Risk assessment

An employer who carries out work which is liable to expose employees to risk from vibration is required to make a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risks to health and safety created by that work.

The risk assessment must include an assessment of the daily exposure to vibration by means of: observation of working practices and reference to relevant information on the probable magnitude of the vibration. The employees are liable to be exposed.

The risk assessment must also consider:

  • the magnitude, type and duration of exposure, including any exposure to intermittent vibration or repeated shocks
  • the effects of exposure to vibration on employees whose health is at particular risk from such exposure
  • any effects of vibration on the workplace and work equipment
  • the proper handling of controls, the reading of indicators, the stability of structures and the security of joints
  • any information provided by the manufacturers of work equipment
  • the availability of replacement equipment designed to reduce exposure to vibration
  • any extension of exposure at the workplace to whole-body vibration beyond normal working hours, including exposure in rest facilities supervised by the employer
  • specific working conditions such as low temperatures
  • appropriate information obtained from health surveillance including, where possible, published information.

At its simplest the process involves:

  • making an inventory of equipment that may cause vibration and the sort of work it is used for
  • collecting information from the manufacturers’ handbook regarding: make, model, power, vibration magnitude, other risk factors etc.
  • listing all employees who use the vibrating equipment and for what
  • accurately recording the exposure time (‘trigger time’ that workers hands are in contact with the vibrating equipment for HAV)
  • consulting with employees about their experiences and any particular concerns
  • recording the relevant information including the assessment of who is likely to be at risk.

Work activities can then be grouped as high, medium or low risk where high risk means exposure is above the ELV and medium risk above the EAV and action prioritised accordingly.

A more detailed assessment can be made by using manufacturers’ vibration magnitude information and measures of exposure time in conjunction with the HSE’s exposure calculator or ready reckoner.

If information is not available and vibration measurements are required they need to be made by a competent person with the appropriate specialised equipment. Measurement results can be highly variable and a competent experienced assessor is essential to produce accurate representative vibration data.
