Example 2 - Leptospirosis

Leptospirosis is a type of bacterial infection that is spread by animals. It is caused by a spirochaetal bacteria called leptospira.

Two types of leptospirosis infection can affect workers in the UK:

  • Weil’s disease (Leptospira Icterohaemorrhagiae)

    • This is a serious and sometimes fatal infection that is transmitted to humans by contact with urine from infected rats

  • The Hardjo form of leptospirosis (Leptospira hardjo)

    • This is transmitted from cattle to humans, also by infected urine.

Once a young animal is infected, they shed the bacteria in their urine for the rest of their life. Most animals have no symptoms, but a small percentage die from the disease.

The leptospirosis bacteria can remain viable for longer periods in water (and rats tend to live in close proximity to water), however, it is inactivated once it dries out.

Disease Leptospirosis
Occupational context
  • Anyone who is exposed to rat or cattle urine or to foetal fluids from cattle
  • Farmers are the main group at risk
  • Vets, meat inspectors, butchers, slaughter men, sewer workers, and workers in contact with canal and river water
  • Recreational water users, for example: canoeists, wind surfers, open water swimmers, jet skiers, pot-holers, etc.
Occurrence
  • Through skin cuts and scratches
  • Occasional exposure through the mucous membranes of the nose and eyes after contact with infected urine or contaminated water
  • When cattle urine splashes into eyes
  • When cattle urine droplets are inhaled.
Symptoms
  • First phase - mild leptospirosis: no symptoms or sudden onset of flu-like illness, with a severe headache, chills, muscle aches, and vomiting
  • Second phase – severe leptospirosis: a return of fever, jaundice, red eyes, abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and a rash
  • In more severe cases, there may be kidney failure or meningitis.
Treatment
  • Antibiotics, given early in the course of the disease
  • Intravenous antibiotics may be required for more severe symptoms.

Control of leptospirosis

General precautions

  • Pest control to kill the rat population (this will help to limit the rat population, but it will not eradicate them totally)
  • Control leptospirosis in the animal through, for example, vaccination
  • Providing rest areas away from animals
  • Providing separate hand washing facilities, including hot running water, soap, paper towels or a hand dryer
  • Avoid drinking water being contaminated by animal excretion
  • At-risk workers should be provided with, and be instructed to carry at all times, a workers card (yellow card) to help early diagnosis and treatment
  • Information, instruction and training on the risks and precautions to be taken.

Safe working practices

  • Not using mouth to mouth resuscitation on new born animals
  • Avoid tools or equipment likely to cause abrasion or puncture wounds
  • Maintaining good standards of hygiene in livestock areas
  • Regularly clean, disinfect and remove faeces from animal areas
  • Covering all cuts and broken skin with waterproof plasters before and during work
  • Not touching rats or cattle with unprotected hands.

PPE

  • Wearing protective clothing, for example:
    • gloves and overalls
    • eye and face protection to avoid urine splashes
    • RPE to avoid aerosol inhalation.
  • Cleaning of PPE
  • Leaving work wear at the workplace for cleaning.

Personal hygiene

  • Washing hands / arms:
    • before eating, smoking or drinking
    • after handling any animal or possibly contaminated materials.
  • Washing cuts and grazes immediately, with soap and running water.

Recreational water users should:

  • Maximise dry training to limit the potential contact time
  • Avoid stagnant, slow moving water
  • Wear protective footwear and minimising skin contact by wearing a full-body dry suit
  • Shower after being in water and washing hands thoroughly before eating
  • Take care not to swallow water
  • Protect all existing cuts and abrasions with waterproof dressings
  • Wash down equipment and dry suits and reporting any sighting of rats to ensure effective rodent control in and around water
  • Seek medical advice and treatment for any cuts or bites received whilst in the water.
