Zoonotic diseases

The Centre for Disease Control (CDC) in the USA defines zoonoses as

infections or infectious diseases transmissible, under natural conditions, between vertebrate animals and humans.

Zoonotic diseases may be endemic (enzootic) or epidemic (epizootic). See the glossary of terms at the end of this section for definitions.

At least 61% of all human pathogens are zoonotic.There are approximately 40 potential zoonoses in the UK and approximately 300 000 people in a variety of occupations are potentially exposed.

This suggests about 4 300 and 12 500 cases annually. Although most of these infections are mild and self-limiting, there is increasing evidence some acute infections may cause long term health effects.

The HSE has produced guidance aimed at agricultural workers, which outlines the duties under CoSHH and the relatively simple control measures which may be required e.g. hand washing.

Note - The 2020 syllabus does not designate specific zoonotic diseases for study.

This section utilizes three examples for illustrative purposes only:

  • Animal influenza
  • Leptospirosis
  • Psittacosis.

The principles of control will be applicable to other similar diseases.

In an open book examination you may be expected to research other diseases.
