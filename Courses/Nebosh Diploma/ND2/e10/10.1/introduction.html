Introduction

A ‘biological agent’ means a micro-organism, cell culture, or human endoparasite (an internal parasite, such as roundworm), whether or not genetically modified, which may cause:

  • infection
  • allergy
  • toxicity
  • … or otherwise create a hazard to human health.

The definition of a ‘biological agent’ includes:

  • Micro-organisms such as bacteria, viruses, fungi, and prions
  • Parasites, for example: malarial parasites, amoebae and trypanosomes
  • The microscopic infectious forms of larger parasites, for example: the microscopic ova (eggs) and infectious larval forms of worms (helminthes).

A prion is a type of protein that can trigger normal proteins in the brain to fold abnormally.

Most biological agents are infectious but some agents can be harmful in other ways, for example: via the production of toxins, or by inducing allergic responses.
