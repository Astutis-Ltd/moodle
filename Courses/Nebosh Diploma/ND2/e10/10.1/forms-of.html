Forms of biological agent

The main classes of harmful biological agent (micro-organisms) are:

  • Bacteria
  • Viruses
  • Fungi
  • Protozoa
  • Macrobial parasites.

Bacteria

Bacteria are living microscopic organisms (typically < 1μm), which lack cells with internal membranes. Bacteria contain DNA, but this differs from cellular DNA in that it has a circular arrangement, rather than linear. Bacteria are single celled and can reproduce by duplicating themselves - they do not need a host to survive. Bacteria (for example: tetanus and most forms of pneumonia) are responsive to antibiotics.

Bacteria may be classified in a number of ways:

  • Their need for oxygen for growth. Aerobic bacteria (aerobes) can survive and grow in an oxygenated environment, whereas anaerobic bacteria (anaerobes) do not require oxygen for growth. Anaerobic, bacteria can survive in places where there is less oxygen, such as human intestines and cause gastroenteric illness
  • Their reaction to a gram stain test - gram positive or gram negative

Viruses

A virus is smaller than one cell. It lives within a cell (intracellular) to survive and derives its ability to multiply from its host cell.

Viruses are 20 to 100 times smaller than bacteria and cannot be seen by light microscopy. The largest viruses (poxviruses) are about 450 nanometres in length and the smallest viruses (polioviruses) are about 30nm.

nm = one billionth of a metre = 0.000000001 metre

Viruses are not really ‘alive’ as they cannot reproduce outside of a living cell. They reproduce by transmitting their genetic information from one cell to another. However, they can damage or kill the cells that they infect, causing disease in infected organisms. Some cause cancers by stimulating cells to grow uncontrollably.

The body will fight off most viruses over the course of time, but viruses are not responsive to antibiotics. Antibiotics are intended to kill living things and a virus is not a living thing. Therefore viruses cause many infectious diseases that have no cure.

Antiviral treatments are difficult to create because of the large number of variant viruses that can cause the same disease. A further concern is the difficulty in disabling a virus without disabling healthy cells.

In addition, vaccines do not cure viruses, so the only way to protect a human from viruses is through specific vaccines, which help to build antibodies against the virus. There is not a vaccine for every virus.

Fungi

Fungi are a diverse group of organisms that obtain food by direct absorption of nutrients. The food is dissolved by enzymes that the fungi excrete, and is then absorbed through thin cell walls. This is then distributed by simple circulation, or streaming, of the protoplasm.

Fungi may be single-celled or multi-cellular.

Fungal infections include ringworm and athletes foot. In healthy people fungal infections are usually mild, involving superficial sites, and they clear up spontaneously. However, if the immune system has been compromised in some way by stress or other factors, humans are much more susceptible.

Occupationally the spores of the aspergillus fungus can cause aspergillosis or ‘farmer’s lung’, (allergic inflammation of the alveoli).

Fungi do not respond to antibiotics in the true sense - they do respond to a fungicide.

Fungal agents, such as fungal pneumonia, reproduce in two ways:

  • Asexual (without partner) by producing a new vegetative growth, i.e. before the fungal pneumonia is inhaled, it is an external fungus which reproduces on its own
  • Sexual (inside host) by producing spores, i.e. after it is inhaled and takes hold, it begins to reproduce sexually and relies on its host.

Protozoa

Protozoans are living, motile, single-celled organisms that can live inside or outside host cells or organisms. They can only divide within a host organism.

Most antibiotics, such as penicillin, don’t work on protozoan diseases. However, they are susceptible to some antibiotics such as sulfonamides.

There are four classes of protozoa, all of which have parasitic representatives:

  • Sarcodina - amoeboid protozoa such as Entamoeba hystolica, which causes amoebic dysentery
  • Flagellata - such as Trypanosoma, which causes sleeping sickness
  • Ciliata - such as Balantidium coli, causes dysentery
  • Sporozoa which include the malarial parasite (plasmodium), cryptosporidium and toxoplasma.
