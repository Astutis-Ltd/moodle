Route(s) of entry

Inhalation

Once breathed in, some substances can attack the nose, throat or lungs while others get into the body through the lungs and harm other parts of the body, for example: the liver.

Pulmonary aspiration is also a consideration. This occurs when food, stomach acid, vomit, or saliva is inhaled into the lungs. This can cause infections and pneumonia and can result in asphyxiation and death.

Skin contact and absorption

Some substances damage skin, while others pass through it (absorption / pervasion / permeation). Substances absorbed into the bloodstream can cause systemic problems affecting other target organs and systems, as discussed. Skin gets contaminated by:

  • Direct contact with the substance, for example: if you touch or dip hands in it
  • Splashing
  • Substances landing on the skin, for example: airborne dust
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces (including contact with contamination inside protective gloves).

Injection

Risks from skin puncture such as butchery or needle stick injuries are rare, but can involve infections or very harmful substances, for example: HIV, drugs.

Ingestion

People transfer chemicals from their hands to their mouths by eating, smoking, biting their nails etc. without washing first.

Exposure to the eyes

Some vapours, gases and dusts are irritating to eyes. Caustic fluid splashes can damage eyesight permanently.

Ears

Once hands or earplugs are contaminated, dirt and the chemicals can be deposited in the ears by contact. This may cause dermatitis of the ear etc.
