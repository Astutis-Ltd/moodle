Fibres, fume and smoke

Fibres

These are tubular structures with a length to diameter ratio of at least 1:3 (i.e. 3x longer than wide).

Fibres of 0.5 μm or less can enter the lungs and become trapped in the alveoli. These can cause lung damage, for example: scarring (fibrosis), with a resulting loss of lung function. Larger fibres may be ingested by macrophages.

Fibres may be:

  • natural mineral, for example: asbestos
  • natural vegetable, for example: cotton / jute
  • synthetic mineral, for example: MMF (manmade mineral fibres).

Fume

Fume is formed by the vaporisation or oxidation of molten metal, for example: lead fume/ welding fume. The particles are usually less than 1μm and therefore are respirable.

Smoke

Smoke is formed from combustion products. It contains particles suspended in gases. Like fume, the particles are usually less than 1μm and therefore are respirable.
