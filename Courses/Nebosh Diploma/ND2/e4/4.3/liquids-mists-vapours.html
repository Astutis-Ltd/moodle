Liquids, mists, vapours and gases

Liquids

Many hazardous substances, for example: acids and solvents, are liquids when they are at normal temperature. However, many liquid chemicals give off vapours, which can be inhaled and which may be highly toxic, for example: volatile organic compounds such as toluene, xylene, isocyanates etc., whose boiling point is below room temperature (see ‘vapours’ below).

Liquid chemicals can be absorbed by the skin. Some liquid chemicals (such as acids) may cause immediate skin damage and they may or may not be absorbed into the bloodstream as well.

Other liquids pass directly through the skin into the bloodstream, for example: mercury, where they can travel to different parts of the body and cause damaging effects.

Mists

Mists are tiny droplets of liquid, which are suspended in the air. The liquid droplets are created by spraying, pickling, foaming and electroplating, for example: a mist created by water from a shower which may contain legionella, or an oil mist or an acid mist created when an acid solution is boiled. The droplets are less than 20μm in size.

Vapours

A vapour is the gas phase of a material, which is normally liquid under standard conditions. Many liquid chemicals evaporate at room temperature, which actually means that they form a vapour and stay in the air.

The vapours from some chemicals can irritate the eyes and skin. There can be a variety of serious health effects from inhaling certain toxic chemical vapours, for example: rhinitis, asthma, sensitizing asthma, dermatitis etc. Vapours can also be flammable or explosive. Mists and vapours are often invisible.

Gases

Some chemical substances are in the form of a gas when they are at a normal temperature. However, some chemicals in liquid or solid form become gases when they are heated.

Some gases can easily be detected by their colour or smell, but there are other gases that you cannot see or smell at all, i.e. they can only be detected with special equipment, for example: carbon monoxide.

Gases can be inhaled.

Some gases produce immediate irritant effects, for example: nitrogen dioxide. The health effects of other gases may be noticeable only after a person’s health has already been seriously damaged, for example: cyanide. Other gases are ‘inert’, they are not poisonous to the body but may cause asphyxiation if they replace the oxygen in an enclosed space, for example: nitrogen and carbon dioxide.

Gases may also be flammable or explosive.
