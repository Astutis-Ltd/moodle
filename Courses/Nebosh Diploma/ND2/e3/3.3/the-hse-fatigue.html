The HSE Fatigue and Risk Index (FRI)

The Fatigue and Risk Index (FRI) was designed primarily for comparing different shift schedules but can also be used to identify any particular shift, within a given schedule, which may be of concern. It contains two separate indices, one related to fatigue (the Fatigue Index) and the other to risk (the Risk Index).

Whilst the FRI is a useful tool, which can be used to help assess the risks of fatigue and injury, it should not be relied upon as the sole or primary means of assessing these risks.

FRI outputs should also be considered in conjunction with the guidelines in HSG256 feedback from staff on how tiring they find their work patterns.

When interpreting fatigue index scores it is important that the scores for each shift are considered rather than the average scores over a schedule.

Fatigue Index

Fatigue index scores from 0 to 100 represent the average probability expressed as a percentage of high levels of sleepiness. A score of 50 on the Fatigue Index represents a 50% chance of:

  • employees achieving a Karolinska Sleepiness Score (KSS) of 8 or 9
  • workers experiencing fatigue to the extent that they may struggle to stay awake on that particular shift.

HSE does not currently specify threshold scores for fatigue. Recent research by the Health and Safety Laboratory (HSL) found that the great majority of working patterns surveyed produced fatigue scores less than 30 - 35 for day shifts and 40 - 45 for night shifts. These figures were what was achievable in the rail sector at the time.

Risk Index

A risk index score of 1 is based on the average level of risk of accident / error attained in studies on people working 12 hour shifts on a 2 day, 2 night, 4 rest day schedule in the rail sector. Consequently, a risk score of 2, could be interpreted as a doubling of risk on that particular shift schedule.

NB both indices are based on group data and do not take into account individual differences such as age, specific work-related issues e.g. exposure to chemical hazards, or social factors such as lifestyle and domestic responsibilities.
