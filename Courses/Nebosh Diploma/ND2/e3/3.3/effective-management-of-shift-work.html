Effective management of shift work

As mentioned, effective management of the health and safety concerns associated with shift work should be driven by the findings of a ‘suitable and sufficient’ risk assessment with input from the workforce to ensure the identification of hazards and consideration of groups and individual workers that may be more vulnerable to harm.

Once the shift work risk factors are understood sensible, proportionate improvements must be made to control the risks ‘so far as is reasonably practicable’.

There are many different shift-work schedules and each schedule has different features. The sheer diversity of work and workplaces mean that there is no single best practice shift management system that provides a universal solution.

The following factors (see table below) should be considered when determining suitable arrangements.

Factor Considerations
Workload

Consideration should be given to the mental and physical demands of the work and the impact on fatigue, concentration and productivity.

The workload should be appropriate for the length and timing of the shift.
Work activity If possible, a variety of tasks should be scheduled into the shift plan. Variation through a range of tasks with different mental and physical demands can promote alertness and relieve physical fatigue.
Shift pattern

Permanent regular shifts allow workers to adjust their body clock, but permanent night shifts are generally discouraged due to concerns of chronic sleep debt, fatigue, ill-health and impact on family life.

Rotating shifts limit the amount of night work as the shifts are shared. Constant rotation can prevent the body clock from adapting.

Forward rotating schedules and fast rotating schedules (every 2-3 days) have been shown to be least harmful.
Shift timing

Daytime shifts are preferred as they do not disrupt the internal body clock.

Only a small number of workers can successfully adapt to night work.

Early morning starts (before 0700) are not as harmful as night shifts but can also reduce sleep and increase the risk of fatigue and ill-health.

Afternoon shifts are suitable for most workers with minimal negative impact on the body clock. Can significantly impact on family and social life.
Shift duration

8-hour shifts are considered to be the optimum length for sustained and consistent work.

12-hour shifts are popular as they make the working week shorter.

Shifts longer than 8 hours should generally be avoided where work is demanding, safety critical or monotonous, and / or there is exposure to work-related physical or chemical hazards.
Rest breaks within shifts Frequent short breaks (5-15 minutes, every 1-2 hours) can reduce fatigue, improve productivity and may reduce the risk of errors and accidents, especially when the work is demanding or monotonous.
Physical environment

Provision of suitable lighting and heating.

Provision of adequate welfare facilities including the means for workers to make a drink and heat up food and to take their longer / meal breaks away from their workstation.

First-aid facilities including a trained first-aider, if necessary, should be made available for all shifts.
Management issues

Increased levels of supervision may be beneficial during key periods of low alertness, e.g. during the night and early hours of the morning, following lunch and towards the end of long shifts.

Suitable procedures are required to manage the potential impacts of overtime, shift-swapping and being on stand-by or call-out.

Tailored training and / or information regarding the risks associated with shift work should be available for workers, their families, their supervisors, safety representatives and management.

Effective communication systems are required to ensure the handover of accurate, reliable information across shift changes.
Welfare

Under WTR, employers are required to ensure that workers are fit for night work and must offer a free health assessment to anyone who is about to start working nights and to all night workers on a regular basis.

Lone-working considerations should be addressed as part of the risk management process.

Altering the design of shift schedules and making changes to the shift-work environment may help solve some problems, but create others.

To find out if changes to the shift-working arrangements have reduced risks the risk assessment should be repeated and monitoring systems should be implemented to check that shift working arrangements are effective and do not negatively impact health or safety.

Indicator Examples of possible performance criteria (measures of success)
Fatigue Assessment tools such as the HSE’s Fatigue and Risk Index Tool may be used to identify if changes in the shift-work schedule have reduced fatigue.
Sleepiness at work Tools such as the ‘Epworth sleepiness scale’ may be used to determine sleepiness.
Accidents, near misses and safety-critical events If the shift management arrangements are effective a reduction in incidents might reasonably be expected.
Absenteeism Effective shift management arrangements should result in a decrease in absenteeism.
Staff turnover Effective shift management arrangements should result in a decrease in staff turnover
Employee welfare Focus groups, interviews, questionnaires and observation can be used to determine if workers view the shift-working arrangements positively.
Performance, productivity and profits Effective shift management arrangements should result in an increase in performance, productivity and profitability.
