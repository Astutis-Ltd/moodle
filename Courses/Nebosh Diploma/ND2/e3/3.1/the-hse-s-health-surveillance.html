The HSE’s health surveillance cycle

The HSE has proposed a ‘health surveillance cycle’ (see image below) a framework for determining the health surveillance needs of a particular workplace. The employer has a central role in every aspect with involvement from employees to ensure effective implementation.

Is health surveillance needed?

Health surveillance may be required where a risk cannot be reduced or controlled to such a level that it will not be harmful to health.

The following conditions should be met for health surveillance to be appropriate:

  • The work is known to damage health in some particular way
  • There are valid ways to detect the disease or condition. To be effective, health surveillance has to be able to reliably show that damage to health is starting to happen or becoming likely. The technique must be accurate, safe and practical
  • Damage to health must be likely under the particular conditions at work
  • Surveillance should benefit the employee in some way.

Appropriate health surveillance

The range of techniques available and levels of competence required are shown in the table below.

Procedure Who can do it?
Self-checks Employees exposed to hazards that have been properly trained in how to look for easily recognisable signs and symptoms of disease.
A responsible person making basic checks for signs of disease Anyone trained to identify straightforward signs and symptoms caused by working with certain substances or processes. For example: managers, supervisors or first aiders can be trained to undertake skin inspections where people work with detergents, metalworking fluids or shampoos.
Enquiries about symptoms, inspection and examination by a qualified person An occupational health nurse checking for signs of asthma, or an audiologist carrying out hearing tests.
Clinical examinations

Carried out by a doctor, or be supervised by a doctor.

May be a legal duty (for employees exposed to lead, for example).

The nature of the tests will require the expertise of a doctor to interpret the results and advise on their significance.

Examination by a doctor is also likely to be necessary where health surveillance by a responsible person or an occupational health nurse has identified possible work-related ill-health that requires further investigation, diagnosis and treatment.
Biological monitoring and biological effect monitoring

Carried out by or supervised by a doctor. Examples include: tests for lead, mercury and carbon monoxide in blood or cadmium and fluoride in urine.

In some circumstances, the taking of samples can be straightforward and be carried out by a suitably trained person.
