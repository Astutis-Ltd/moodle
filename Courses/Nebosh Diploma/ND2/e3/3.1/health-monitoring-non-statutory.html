Health monitoring / non-statutory health surveillance

It is implicit under the general duties of the Health and Safety at Work Act that the medical fitness of employees is a component of a ‘safe system of work’, to the extent that the effects of a medical condition are foreseeable. Employers therefore need take into account the individual employee’s fitness in respect of activities where their own health and safety or the health and safety of others may be affected.

The Faculty of Occupational Medicine’s (FOM) guidance on health surveillance for those in safety-critical roles, defined safety critical roles as:

“..those involving activities where, because of risks to the individuals concerned or to others, the employees need to have full, unimpaired control of their physical and / or mental capabilities…”

Those working in safety-critical roles should not be suffering from medical conditions, or be taking medical treatment likely to cause:

  • sudden loss of consciousness
  • impairment of awareness or concentration
  • sudden incapacity
  • impairment of balance or co-ordination, or
  • significant limitation of mobility.

It should be noted that much of the monitoring can be undertaken through surveys and questionnaires with the workers providing a self-assessment of their current condition. Occupational health nurses or physicians may then conduct follow up assessments of groups or individuals based on the findings of the self-assessments.

The following areas are specifically mentioned within the syllabus.

  • Mental ill-health and work-related stress
  • Work at height
  • Driving occupations (e.g. FLT Operators)
  • Alcohol / substance misuse at work.

Mental ill-health and work-related stress

After initiatives to improve the workplace in accordance with the stress management guidelines it is necessary to monitor the impact of any interventions and employee surveys and interviews may well be used to determine whether the workers are experiencing any improvements as a result of the change. Line managers may notice early signs of mental ill-health through changed appearance, performance, behaviour etc.

There are numerous psychological tools and test available to professionally evaluate workers general mental health and wellbeing, as well as signs of stress, anxiety and depression.

Work at height

Given the seriousness of fall from height accidents a regular medical to check the workers general health, hearing, eyesight, lung-function and Musculo-skeletal condition is recommended. Workers should also be questioned regularly regarding health conditions such as vertigo and any medications which could cause drowsiness or affect balance etc.

Driving occupations (e.g. FLT Operators)

Potential forklift truck operators must be:

  • Reasonably fit, both physically and mentally, to safely control and operate lift trucks, with the learning ability and potential to become competent operators
  • Reliable, with a responsible attitude to their work.

Fitness for operating should always be judged on a case-by-case basis, matching the requirements of the driving task with the fitness and abilities of the operator.

For most work with forklift trucks, a standard of fitness equivalent to that for the DVLA Group 1 entitlement (ordinary driving licence holders) would be appropriate.

The DVLA guidance for medical professionals to assess medical standards of fitness to drive on UK roads allow for the exclusion of drivers for a range of medical conditions in the following categories:

  • Neurological disorders
  • Cardiovascular disorders
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Psychiatric disorders
  • Drug or alcohol dependency and misuse
  • Visual disorders
  • Renal disorders
  • Respiratory and sleep disorders
  • Impairment of cognitive function
  • Miscellaneous conditions.

For higher risk activities where the standards for fitness may need to be higher, at level 2, periodic fitness screening is recommended at five-yearly intervals after the age of 40 and annually after age 65.
