What is biological monitoring?

Biological monitoring measures the amount of chemicals which have been absorbed into the body. It is a subset of health surveillance that involves the measurement and assessment of workplace agents or their metabolites (substances formed when the body converts the chemical) in exposed workers. Measurements are made either on samples of breath, urine, blood, or any combination of these.

Biological monitoring measurements reflect the total uptake of a chemical by an individual, by all routes. It is often used to complement personal air monitoring, which measures inhalation of a chemical in the air in a personâ€™s breathing zone.

Biological monitoring may be particularly useful in the following circumstances:

  • When there is significant absorption by non-respiratory routes, for example: significant skin absorption and / or ingestion
  • Where control of exposure depends on Respiratory Protective Equipment (RPE)
  • Where there is a reasonably well-defined relationship between biological monitoring and effect
  • Where it gives information on accumulated dose and target organ body burden that is related to toxicity
  • When there is a specified guidance value against which a comparison might be made (such as in EH40).

Biological effect monitoring is the measurement and assessment of early biological effects in exposed workers, caused by absorption of chemicals.
