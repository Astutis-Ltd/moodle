Biological monitoring programmes

The main elements of a biological monitoring programme are outlined below, they should be thought through carefully before monitoring begins:

Step 1 Define the purpose of the programme.
Step 2 Appoint a competent person to manage the programme.
Step 3 Define the monitoring strategy.
Step 4 Consult on the programme with employees or their representatives.
Step 5 Discuss and agree the programme with the individual employees concerned.
Step 6 Establish procedures for sample collection, storage, transportation, analysis and quality assurance.
Step 7 Establish procedures for feedback, including interpretation or results.
Step 8 Ensure arrangements are in place for acting on the results and evaluating the effectiveness of the programme.

Step 1: Define the purpose of the programme

The programme may be required because, for example, there may be a new process involving hazardous substances, employees have reported symptoms or health effects from hazardous substances, or possibly an improvement notice has been issued.

Step 2: Appoint a competent person to manage the programme

There should be access to appropriate specialist expertise able to design, implement and review the monitoring programme.

Step 3: Define the monitoring strategy

The monitoring strategy depends on what is being measured. It will need to consider who to monitor, how often and when to take the samples.

For chemicals not assigned a BMGV, the strategy needs to be established with knowledge of the absorption and metabolism of the substance and any factors that influence them, and to consider how the substance is used in the workplace.

Step 4: Consult on the programme

It is important to consult with employees before the biological monitoring programme starts.

Where an employer is required to carry out a biological monitoring by law, there is an obligation on employees to co-operate. In particular, employee participation is required under the Control of Lead at Work Regulations 2002 (CLAW).

Step 5: Discuss the programme with individual employees concerned

Biological monitoring involves measurements on an individual’s body fluids (or breath) so it is important to ensure that individuals’ rights are protected and that:

  • employees give informed consent before samples are taken
  • samples are only analysed for the substances for which informed consent was given
  • individual measurements are treated as confidential
  • the result(s) are explained to employees.

Step 6: Collect, store, transport and analyse sample

Collection

Urine samples require use of the correct type of contamination-free container. Employees will need to change out of their work clothes and wash their hands before providing a sample, otherwise there is the possibility of inadvertent contamination of the sample.

Blood sample collection involves the puncturing of a vein and so must be carried out by a qualified person, for example: a physician, suitably qualified nurse or phlebotomist. Because of the additional risks associated with taking blood, it is recommended that urine or breath (i.e. non-invasive techniques) are used for biological monitoring wherever possible.

Storage

When samples cannot be sent to the laboratory immediately, it is important to make sure that the samples are stored properly or the biological monitoring results may be inaccurate and misleading.

Transportation

It is important to ensure the sample container does not break in transit, and does not contain any sample leakage.

Analysis and quality assurance

Analytical laboratory services should operate a quality assurance scheme and be linked to a recognised quality standard. UKAS accreditation provides formal third party verification of a laboratories competence and ability to fulfill the requirements of the relevant international standards.

Step 7: Give feedback

Individuals need to be informed of their results with reference to any BMGV (or any other criteria used for interpretation), what it means and what action or follow-up will take place.

When there is a possibility of non-occupational exposure to a chemical, for example, solvents in DIY materials, it may be necessary to compare a sample obtained after work exposure with a pre-work level.

Step 8: Act on the results and evaluate the effectiveness of the programme

Where the results indicate that you are likely to need to reduce exposure (where a BMGV is exceeded, for example), or where accumulated data shows a trend towards this, it will be necessary to look at how the substance is being handled.

Follow-up monitoring or increasing the frequency of monitoring will need to be considered. This will ensure that any changes made to control measures have been effective.

Where specific guidance is available for a chemical this may contain further information on what action is necessary.
