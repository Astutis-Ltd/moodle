Legal requirements for biological monitoring

CoSHH

Biological monitoring (biological effects monitoring) may be appropriate under Regulation 11 of CoSHH (health surveillance) where it is possible to link results from biological monitoring to an adverse health effect. Under Regulation 11 (2)(b) of CoSHH health surveillance by biological effects monitoring is appropriate where:

...the exposure of the employee to a substance hazardous to health is such that an identifiable disease or adverse health effect may be related to the exposure, there is a reasonable likelihood that the disease or effect may occur under the particular conditions of his work and there are valid techniques for detecting indications of the disease or the effect.

Biological monitoring can also make a valuable contribution to complying with other regulations under CoSHH, notably:

  • Regulation 6 (Risk assessment) where the results of relevant health surveillance must be considered as part of a health risk assessment
  • Regulation 7 (Prevention or control of exposure) by providing evidence as to the effectiveness of preventive and protective measures
  • Regulation 10 (Exposure monitoring) in circumstances where air sampling alone may not give a reliable indication of exposure.

Control of Lead at Work Regulations 2002 (CLAW)

While the general philosophy of exposure control for lead is consistent with CoSHH, lead is subject to separate legislation - CLAW. Regulation 10 of CLAW (medical surveillance) specifically requires biological monitoring and lays down biological monitoring limits.

Regular biological monitoring of the level of lead in blood or urine must be undertaken to detect any absorption of lead.

For biological monitoring of occupational exposure to lead and its compounds, except lead alkyls, blood-lead concentration is the most commonly used indicator.

Blood-lead levels are usually checked every three months, especially for a woman capable of having children and young persons under 18. It may be more often if work involves a process where lead could be rapidly absorbed e.g. work on lead-burning processes where exposure to lead fume could be high unless properly controlled. The doctor may check blood-lead level less often if exposure and blood-lead levels do not usually change very much. This could be every 6 or even 12 months.

Employees should be told if their exposure to lead is ‘significant’ i.e.:

  • Breathing zone: where any employee is or is liable to be exposed to a concentration of lead in the atmosphere exceeding half the Occupational Exposure Limit (OEL) for lead
  • Ingestion: where there is a substantial risk of any employee ingesting lead
  • Skin absorption: if there is a risk of an employee’s skin coming into contact with lead alkyls, or any other substance containing lead in a form which can also be absorbed through the skin.

If it is significant, it will be necessary to:

  • Provide protective clothing
  • Measure the level of lead in the air, and tell employees the results. If exposure to lead cannot be kept below the OEL, Respiratory Protective Equipment (RPE) must be issued
  • Arrange to measure the level of lead in the blood and tell employees the results.

If the amount of lead in an employee’s blood reaches the action level an investigation will be required to ascertain why this has happened and try to reduce it to below that level by:

  • Carrying out an urgent investigation to find out why
  • Reviewing control measures
  • Taking steps to reduce the employees’ blood-lead concentration below the action level, as far as is reasonably practicable.

If, despite all the control measures the blood-lead level reaches the suspension level the doctor will repeat the test.

If this confirms the result of the first test, the doctor will usually decide that the employee should not carry on working with lead. Action must be taken on the doctor’s decision, and the employee will not be able to work with lead again, or be exposed to it, until the doctor considers it safe to do so.

If a woman is pregnant, the lead in her blood can pass into the blood of the baby she is carrying which could affect its development. As soon as pregnancy is confirmed the doctor will automatically certify that you should not do work where your exposure to lead is significant.

The law also gives greater protection to young people under 18 because they generally have less experience working with a substance as hazardous as lead.

It is, therefore, against the law for women capable of having children, and for young people under 18, to work in lead smelting and refining and in most jobs in the manufacture of lead-acid batteries.

The most common method of biological monitoring for occupational exposure to lead alkyls involves measurement of total lead in urine. Total lead in urine reflects exposure to both inorganic lead and lead alkyls.

In general urine samples will be taken every six weeks though the frequency may be increased to weekly if results are over 95μg Pb / g creatinine (20 for women of reproductive capacity) or at the doctor’s discretion if over 110 (25 for women of reproductive capacity).

Practical difficulties with biological monitoring

Practical difficulties that may be encountered during biological monitoring are that biological monitoring is normally conducted on a voluntary basis, unless required by statute e.g. lead. Consequently the informed consent of those involved would have to be obtained and their concerns overcome.

Other difficulties include:

  • The availability of suitable facilities or a location to carry out the monitoring especially if this has to be done at the end of the shift
  • The availability of specialists to carry out the monitoring e.g. If blood samples are to be taken
  • Maintaining the integrity of samples to avoid cross contamination and ensuring there is no possibility of cross infection
  • The fact that there are few guidance values available for comparison
  • Exposure may be non-occupational
  • The cost of carrying out biological monitoring is high.
