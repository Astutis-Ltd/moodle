Dilution ventilation

Dilution ventilation is a term used to define the flow of air into and out of a working area, so that any contaminants are diluted by adding some fresh air. It can be provided by:

  • Natural ventilation which relies on wind pressure and temperature differences to move fresh air through a building and is usually not fully controllable
  • ‘Forced’ or mechanical ventilation which uses mechanical supply and / or extraction to provide fresh air and is controllable.

Dilution ventilation is sometimes called general ventilation. As ‘general ventilation’ is also used to describe a buildings overall heating, ventilating, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems ‘dilution ventilation’ is the better term when referring to contaminant control.

Systems can also be designed to displace rather than dilute contaminated air. At its simplest dispersal ventilation involves injecting air into a space in such a way that new air displaces the air previously there without mixing with it.

When used to control airborne contaminants dilution ventilation is limited to the following situations:

  • small quantities of contaminants are released at fairly uniform rates
  • there is sufficient distance from the source to the worker to allow for dilution
  • contaminants are of relatively low toxicity or fire hazard
  • no air-cleaning device is needed to collect contaminants before the exhaust air is discharged
  • the diluted contaminants do not cause corrosion or other problems.

An example of a work situation where dilution ventilation would be useful is in diluting organic vapours from low toxicity solvents such as in floor tile adhesive.

Conversely dilution ventilation has a number of limitations, i.e.

  • It does not completely remove contaminants
  • It requires large amounts of makeup air to be heated or cooled.

And it is not suitable for / is ineffective in:

  • Control of highly toxic chemicals
  • Control of exposure to dusts, or metal fumes or large volumes of gases or vapours
  • Handling surges of gases or vapours or irregular emissions.

When designing a mechanical dilution ventilation system careful consideration must be given to the locations of the air inlet and exhaust relative to the worker and the source of the contaminant. Looking at this illustration it is easy to picture air moving through the work area in a straight path from the air inlet to the exhaust fan, however the reality is somewhat different. The incoming air diffuses throughout the room. Some of it passes through the zone of contaminant release and dilutes the contaminants to a lower concentration. The dilution continues as the material moves farther from the process until the contaminated air is removed by the exhaust fan.

The position and number of air inputs should be arranged so as to help deliver effective dispersion and dilution of the contaminated air. Consideration will need to be given to the impact of cross drafts and thermal / convection currents and to avoid any ‘dead spots’ where stagnant contaminated air may sit. It is worth noting that complete mixing of air rarely occurs in real world situations.

Equations for calculating dilution or displacement airflow are beyond the scope of the Diploma syllabus. However, you may be expected to calculate a room’s volume in metres cubed (length x width x height) and to determine the number of air changes per hour if given a volume of air throughput per hour (in m3 or litres - where 1m3 = 1000l).
