The identification and assessment of work-related mental ill-health

The HSE’s Management Standards approach to tackling work-related stress establishes a framework to help employers tackle work-related stress and, as a result, also reduce the incidence and negative impact of mental ill health.

There are several different types of information or data that can be used to help identify, in broad terms, whether work-related stress and mental ill-health are potential problems in the workplace including:

  • existing sources of information available within the organisation
  • surveys
  • other ways of obtaining information about groups
  • other initiatives.

Existing sources of information

The following information may be useful in identifying work-related stress ‘hot-spots’ and suggesting what the likely underlying causes may be.

Sickness absence data High levels of sickness absence may indicate a potential problem area, however stress and mental-ill health sickness absence is sometimes not reported as such, because of perceived stigma.
Productivity data Where performance drops below expected standards (when compared with previous years or between different parts of the organisation) the reasons should be examined to determine whether or not working methods or conditions are causing work-related stress which is affecting performance.
Staff turnover High rates of staff turnover may be indicative of a hidden problem with work-related stress.
Performance appraisal Performance appraisals offer an opportunity for one-to-one discussions to explore staff perceptions of levels of work pressure.
Team meetings Provide useful opportunities for staff to identify and share views on issues that may be potential sources of undue pressure.
Informal talks Regular informal chats can help gauge the mood of individuals and teams.
‘Walk-throughs’ and ‘talk-throughs’

A walk-through is where a manager walks through a section and observes work processes to assess whether there are any obvious aspects of the job which may cause excessive pressure.

A talk-through involves someone describing what happens when a task is being carried out. It can offer staff an opportunity to think about tasks in terms of the potential they have to lead to work-related stress.

Surveys

Surveys can be a useful source of information on whether work-related stress is a potential problem for the workforce and, if so, who is likely to be affected and how.

The validity of self-report and questionnaire-based surveys is often called into question because they are dependent on how people ‘feel’ about issues. However, evidence suggests that individual perceptions play an important role in predicting stress-related ill health.

The HSE Management Standards Indicator Tool and associated guidance etc can be found on HSE’s stress at work website (Work related stress - Tools and templates (hse.gov.uk)).

Other ways of obtaining information about groups

Toolbox talks Existing arrangements in place for encouraging participation and consulting with employees such as ‘toolbox talks’ can be an opportunity to inquire about potentially stressful issues with workers.
Focus groups

Provide an opportunity to explore work-related stress issues in more depth, with more time set aside for such discussions than may be available within standard team meetings.

The Management Standards approach suggests that focus groups are used as to evaluate the risk by exploring problems and developing solutions, although smaller organisations may find it more useful to use such group approaches during the earlier stages of the risk assessment process.
