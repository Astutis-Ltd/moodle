Managing workers with mental health conditions in the workplace

An early conversation

As soon as a line manager notices, or is made aware that a colleague is experiencing mental ill-health an early conversation is called for to determine what the issues are and what support may be available. If the colleague is unwilling to discuss concerns with their line manager a referral to occupational health services, an employee assistance programme or HR may be appropriate.

Emotional distress

Where a colleague is clearly emotionally distressed it is particularly important that the manager remains calm and collected. If the distress arises in a work meeting it might be appropriate to ask the colleague if they would like someone else to accompany them. Otherwise a calm conversation in a quiet place to determine whether they wish to go home, or need you to contact a family member, their GP, or someone in their health support team.

If someone is experiencing hallucinations or mania, they may need immediate medical help. If an employee is disturbing others and refuses to accept help, advice should be sought from the occupational health provider, the person’s GP or the NHS via 111 or 999 in an emergency.

Ongoing illness

Colleagues with ongoing mental health problems should be treated in exactly the same way as any other member of staff, unless they ask for help or demonstrate clear signs that they need it. It is discriminatory to make assumptions about people’s capabilities, their promotability or the amount of sick leave they may need because of their illness.

Coping strategies

Most people are encouraged to develop a coping strategy as part of their care. This often involves noting signs of a possible relapse and taking pre-emptive action, such as cutting down on work, being careful about drinking alcohol, taking exercise and finding time to relax. It is important to support the employee at this first warning stage. Small, inexpensive adjustments may well prevent a more costly period of illness.

Advance statements

Some people find it useful to draw up an ‘advance statement’ which explains how they want to be treated if they become unwell. The statement can cover practical arrangements such as details of the people who need to be contacted or provided with information. If an employee draws up an agreement it should be put into practice to maintain trust.

An advance statement which relates to the workplace could include:

  • signs that indicate the person is becoming unwell
  • who to contact (e.g. close relative, care coordinator or GP)
  • what sort of support is helpful and what is not.
