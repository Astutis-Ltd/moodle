Public health interventions / health promotion

Health and wellbeing as experienced and observed in the workplace, is the product of two major forces:

  • What employees bring with them to the workplace in terms of personal resources, health practices, beliefs, attitudes, values, and genetics
  • The physical and psycho-social impacts that work at the workplace has on the employees.

In addition to directly impacting upon an individual’s health, work can also make it difficult for employees to take care for their own health through excessive demands on time and energy through long work hours, trave time, overtime etc.

Until relatively recently most research was around personal health practises with the employer facilitating healthy lifestyle choices through subsidised gym memberships and fruit baskets etc (the ‘bikes and bananas’ approach).

As discussed earlier ‘wellbeing’ is a much broader church than occupational health and requires a lot more organisational input than just facilitating healthy lifestyle choices around smoking cessation, alcohol reduction, healthier diets and increased exercise levels.

The workplace provides an enormous opportunity for public health / health promotion initiatives to help improve the health of the working population because of the relatively unfettered access to a stable working population with established communication systems and social networks and the associated opportunities of positive peer pressure.

To be effective the right interventions need to be focused on the right people at the right time, and worker engagement and participation is key to the success of any workplace initiative.

Some organisations (e.g. the NHS) appoint ‘health and wellbeing champions’ as a means of distributed leadership for health and wellbeing initiatives. These are people from any level of the organisation who are keen to promote, identify, and signpost ways to support the wellbeing of their colleagues. Familiarity with their work areas and colleagues, means they are well placed to recognise what may work best in their particular work environment, and to make suggestions regarding appropriate interventions.

As with any other workplace initiative or intervention it is important that the impact is fully understood through regular review of appropriate performance indicators such as absentee rates and referrals to support services etc. Successes need to be shared and celebrated with the workforce through effective communication and lessons should be learned from interventions that were not effective / less effective than expect so that the challenges can be understood and hopefully overcome next time around.
