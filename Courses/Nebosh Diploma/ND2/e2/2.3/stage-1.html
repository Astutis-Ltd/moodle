Stage 1: Finding out if there’s a problem (Identification and risk assessment)

Finding out whether or not violence in the workplace is a problem is not always easy, as staff can hide their concerns. Effective consultation, taking on board the views of staff who deal with the public and have first-hand knowledge and experience of actual or potential workplace violence, is essential in establishing the true scale of the problem.

Information from near miss incidents, where no-one was actually hurt in spite of the potential for harm, can be useful in preventing future, more serious occurrences.

The initial investigation should cover all violent incidents from minor verbal abuse to serious physical injury where staff have felt threatened or experienced undue stress.

The relationships between the outcomes should present as the accident triangles discussed in Unit 1, Element 5. The numbers and sub-categories will vary, but essentially a percentage of verbal abuse incidents will escalate to physical violence, and a smaller percentage may involve weapons and could ultimately be fatal.

Techniques or tools that may be used to build up a picture of workplace violence incidents include:

  • existing health and safety and security reports
  • safety representatives / representatives of employee safety and health and safety committees
  • interviews with individual staff
  • employee questionnaires
  • small staff discussion groups
  • specific staff meetings.

Formal procedures for reporting incidents and collecting and analysing data will help to establish whether there is a pattern of incidents, and identify particular targets or practices at risk.

Reporting systems and forms should be clear and easy to follow, and staff should be encouraged to report confidentially any incident which causes them harm or concern.

Risk factors

Cases of workplace violence may be categorised in various ways. Table 2.10 identifies three categories of work related violence, and a fourth category which is not directly work-related but can occur in the workplace.

Category Description
Criminal intent Perpetrator has no legitimate relationship to the business and is usually committing a crime such as robbery in conjunction with the violence.
Customer / client The perpetrator has a legitimate relationship with the business and becomes violent while being served by the business. This category includes customers, clients, patients, students, inmates, and any other group for which the business provides services.
Worker-on- worker Bullying and harassment which may escalate to physical assault.
Personal relationship Not directly work related – includes victims of domestic violence assaulted or threatened while at work.

Workers engaged in the following areas of work are most at risk of occupational violence:

  • Giving a service
  • Caring
  • Education
  • Cash transactions
  • Delivery / collection
  • Controlling
  • Representing authority.

Triggers for violence

Beyond personal characteristics that may make an individual more or less likely to be violent or aggressive, research has identified a huge number of triggers and escalators (or accelerators) of violence and aggression which often work in combinations to agitate an individual and trigger an aggressive or violent response (see image below). These triggers and escalators will vary greatly between workplaces.

The estimated number of violent incidents at work fluctuates on an annual basis with no clear trend.

Data gathered for the 2019 / 20 Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) before data collection was suspended in March 2020 due to Covid lockdown measures showed that:

The risk of being a victim of actual or threatened violence at work is not significantly different to the previous few years, with an estimated 1.4 % of working adults being the victim of one or more violent incidents at work.

An estimated 307 000 adults of working age in employment experienced violence at work, including threats and physical assault.

There were an estimated 688 000 incidents of violence at work, comprising of 299 000 assaults and 389 000 threats.

The Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW):

  • Monitors the extent of crime in England and Wales
  • Used by the Government to evaluate and develop crime reduction policies and to obtain vital information about the changing levels of crime over the last 30 years
  • 40 000 households are invited to participate each year with a 75% uptake
  • Home interviews replaced with shorter telephone interviews during lockdown period(s).

The CSEW shows that there is large variation in the risks at work across occupational groups. Overall, respondents in protective service occupations, such as police officers, were at greatest risk of assaults and threats while working at 8.4% (6x the average risk of 1.4% across all occupations).

Health and social care associate professionals and health professionals have a risk of around 2.5x the national average at 3.9% and 3.3% respectively.

Managers and proprietors of public houses, off-licenses etc also had an increased risk at 2.9%.
