Stage 2: Deciding what action to take

Having found out that violence could be a problem (hazard) for employees, employers need to decide what needs to be done by working through the risk assessment process.

Decide who might be harmed, and how

Employees likely to be at greater risk were discussed earlier. Potentially violent customers / service users etc should also be identified in advance so that the risks from them can be minimised.

Evaluate the risk

The key factors that can influence the likelihood and severity of violence at work, which can be controlled or influenced by the employer can be broken down into three key areas:

  • the environment
  • the design of the job
  • the level of training and information provided.

Risk controls

Risk control measures will usually involve a combination of measures to:

  • Improve the working environment (Physical);
  • Design the job to reduce risk (Organisational) ; and
  • Provide staff with appropriate information and training (Behavioural) .

Controls will vary depending on the areas of work. Some examples of effective control measures are given below.

The environment (physical control measures)

Example 1: Retail store

  • The amount of high value goods kept to a minimum;
  • Valuable goods displayed behind or close to the till;
  • Secure stockroom to hold high value stock;
  • The till is next to the shop entrance and visible from the street;
  • Staff have a clear view of the entire shop and the street immediately outside; and
  • CCTV cameras provide a deterrent and / or evidence for prosecution.

Example 2: Hospital A&E waiting rooms

  • Providing continual updates on waiting times;
  • Comfortable seating;
  • Appropriate lighting levels;
  • Improved acoustics – more sound absorbent surfaces;
  • Comfortable temperature / ventilation;
  • Pleasant décor; and
  • Means of passing the time such as television and / or magazines.

Example 3: Licensed premises (pubs and clubs)

Premises design in terms of layout, security provision and the general environment can help reduce the risk of crime and violence to staff.

  • Premises design / layout can improve customer visibility and limit accessibility to target items, e.g. location of cash tills, designing out blind spots and counter height and depth;
  • Suitable lighting at entrances / exits, reception areas and car parks;
  • CCTV as a deterrent and to collect evidence to convict offenders;
  • Security devices such as alarms and locks, window restraints (bars and shutters);
  • Security personnel with a suitable licence to practice from the Security Industry Authority (SIA).

Job design (organisational control measures)

The range of organisational control measures is extensive and varies greatly between work sectors, examples of measures taken in the retail and licensed trades include:

  • Providing adequate staffing levels to reduce the likelihood of frustrating customers;
  • Reducing the levels of cash handled and stored;
  • Arranging secure cash collections;
  • Electronic customer databases;
  • Security personnel.

For organisations with peripatetic workers, such as estate agents:-

  • Avoiding lone working situations; and
  • Systems for tracking and communicating with field based staff.

Training

Training can be given to enable staff to:

  • Reduce the likelihood of triggering aggressive behaviour (improving customer service);
  • Understand the triggers for violent behaviour;
  • Avoid confrontation;
  • Defuse (De-escalate) situations;
  • Physically defend themselves; or
  • Restrain an aggressor.

Verbal de-escalation

Verbal de-escalation is appropriate when a potentially violent situation threatens to erupt on the spot and no weapon is present. Reasoning with an enraged person is not possible. The only objective of de-escalation is to reduce the level of arousal so that discussion becomes possible. Post–incident support

Following a violent incident involving an employee it is important to respond quickly to avoid any long-term distress to the directly affected employee or others.

When planning post incident support the following should be considered:

  • Debriefing: victims may need to talk through their experience as soon as possible after the event. Specialist counselling may be required;
  • Time off work: will depend on the degree of physical or psychological harm and the individuals recovery process. The return to work may well need to be managed;
  • Legal help: in serious cases there may be criminal and civil legal action. Legal help may well be appropriate; and
  • Other employees: may need guidance and / or training to help them respond appropriately.
Self-control Physical presence De-escalating conversation
  • Appear calm, centred and self-assured
  • Use a modulated, low monotonous tone of voice
  • Do not be defensive
  • Be respectful even when being forceful
  • Be aware of back-up options (leaving / telling aggressor to leave / calling for assistance / calling the police).
  • Never turn your back
  • Stand at an angle so you can sidestep away if needed
  • Always be at the same eye level - encourage the client to be seated, but if he / she needs to stand, you stand up also
  • Allow extra physical space between you – about four times your usual distance. (See notes on proxemics – below)
  • Keep hands out of your pockets, up and available to protect yourself.

Do not:

  • Maintain constant eye contact. Allow the client to break his / her gaze and look away
  • Smile
  • Touch
  • Argue or try to convince
  • Be defensive or judgmental
  • Speak calmly at an average volume
  • Respond selectively - do not answer abusive questions
  • Explain limits and rules in an authoritative, firm, but always respectful tone
  • Empathize with feelings but not with the behaviour
  • Do not argue or try to convince
  • Tap into the client’s cognitive mode – move the conversation away from feelings towards thoughts
  • Suggest alternative behaviours
  • Give the consequences of inappropriate behaviour – explaining that these are institutional requirements and are not personal
  • Transfer your sense of genuine interest of calmness, and of respect
  • Trust your instincts – if de-escalation is not working stop and leave.
