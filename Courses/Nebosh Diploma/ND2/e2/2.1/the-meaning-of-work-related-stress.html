The meaning of work-related stress

In engineering terms, when a force (pressure) is applied to a structural component stress (expressed as units of force per unit area) is created within the material, giving rise to a deformation called strain. When the force applied exceeds the materials capacity to take the strain it will break.

People are no different. Each individual has the capacity to cope with a certain amount of pressure, beyond this limit (or ability to take the strain) the individual will ‘break’ and suffer psychological harm.

There are many models illustrating how workplace pressure affects workers. None are specified on the NEBOSH Diploma syllabus. We are going to look briefly at:

  • Bucket theory
  • Yerkes-Dodson law.

Bucket theory

The ‘bucket theory’ models human beings as buckets. Each person’s bucket has a different capacity representing their ability to cope with pressure.

Pressure may pour into the bucket in different ways:

  • a crisis may be thought of as another large bucket load poured directly in
  • job dissatisfaction may be considered as a continuously running tap
  • minor irritations can be thought of as a dripping tap.

Each ‘bucket’ also has pressure relief arrangements to prevent it overflowing, such as coping mechanisms, social activities, holidays etc.

When the bucket is not large enough to cope with the pressures poured into it, it overflows indicating stress related illness.

In this model, if a person’s bucket is largely full from domestic pressure, it can literally be a drop of additional workplace pressure that causes their bucket to overflow.

Yerkes-Dodson law

Psychologists Robert Yerkes and John Dodson claimed there was an empirical relationship between arousal and performance, as long ago as 1908. The ‘Yerkes-Dodson Law’ asserts that performance increases with physiological or mental arousal (stimulation / pressure) up to a point, but when levels of arousal become too high, performance decreases.

More recent research (Lupien et al 2007) into the effects of stress and stress hormones on human cognition showed the relationship between human memory performance and circulating levels of glucocorticoids (stress hormones) as an upside down U shaped curve, which the authors noted resembled the Yerkes–Dodson curve.

HSE guidance on work-related stress

The HSE recognises that a degree of stimulation or pressure in the workplace is beneficial, but that excessive pressure can result in stress related ill-health. The HSE has defined stress as:

… the adverse reaction people have to excessive pressures or other types of demand placed on them.
