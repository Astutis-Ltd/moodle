The CoSHH regulations

In 2005 the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (CoSHH) Regulations were updated and made a major change in the UK approach to Occupational Exposure Limits (OELs):

  • The CoSHH updates introduced a new type of limit, ‘Workplace Exposure Limits’ (WELs), which are not to be exceeded, to replace the previously used ‘Maximum Exposure Limits’ (MELs) and ‘Occupational Exposure Standards’ (OESs)
  • CoSHH required assurance that exposure to substances, that could cause occupational asthma, cancer, or damage to genes passed down from one generation to the next, were reduced ‘as low as reasonably practicable’.

The first requirement of CoSHH is to prevent exposure to substances hazardous to health ‘so far as is reasonably practicable’. Where prevention is not reasonably practicable adequate controls must be implemented.

Control of exposure to substances hazardous to health is adequate if:

  • The principles of good practice set out in schedule 2A of CoSHH (see later and Element 5) have been applied
  • WELs have not been exceeded
  • Exposure is reduced to as low a level as is reasonably practicable for:
    • carcinogens
    • mutagens
    • respiratory sensitisers or any other substance which the risk assessment has shown to be a potential cause of occupational asthma.

To achieve this all possible routes of exposure should be considered and control measures provided, which are proportionate to the risk and regularly reviewed. It is also important that employees are given information, instruction and training on the risks involved and the control measures to be followed. In cases where there is no approved Workplace Exposure Limit (WEL), an in-house standard should be set.

If a WEL is exceeded there may be something obviously wrong which can be corrected. If the reasons for the excessive exposure are not obvious, a more detailed investigation may be needed.

Workplace Exposure Limits (WELs) apply only to:

  • people at work
  • conditions where the atmospheric pressure is normal, i.e. between 900 and 1100 millibars.

WELs are expressed in the following way:

  • parts per million (ppm) for gases and vapours
  • milligrams per cubic metre (mg / m-3) for dust, fume, airborne particles.

A WEL is averaged over a reference period (referred to as a time-weighted average or TWA) to which employees may be exposed by inhalation.

Short-Term Exposure Limits (STELs)

15 minute reference period

  • Protect against acute adverse health effects arising from brief exposures
  • Examples: eye irritation, narcosis from an alcohol based substance, irreversible tissue damage such as asbestos.
Long-Term Exposure Limits (LTELs)

8 hour reference periods

  • Control health effects arising from prolonged or accumulated exposure
  • Restricting the total intake by inhalation over one or more work shifts
  • Examples: ammonia, styrene, trichloroethylene.

If the working exposure is less than 8 hours, the Long-Term Exposure Limit (LTEL) may be exceeded in proportion to the hours worked, as long as the Short-Term Exposure Limit (STEL) is never exceeded.

Some dusts have a specific Long Term Exposure Limit (LTEL), for example: silica dust. However, where there is no Workplace Exposure Limit (WEL) assigned, dust may still be harmful, especially if it is respirable, i.e. enters the alveoli. In this case CoSHH sets the WEL for ‘nuisance dust’ Long Term Exposure Limit (LTEL) as:

  • Respirable dust = 4mg / m3
  • Total inhalable dust = 10mg / m3.
