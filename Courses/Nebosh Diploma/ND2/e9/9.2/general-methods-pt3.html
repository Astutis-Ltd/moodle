General methods for sampling (part 3)

Method of sampling

For personal sampling, the sampler is attached to the wearer within his or her breathing zone, and the pump (if external to the sampler) is connected to it by a length of flexible tubing and worn on a belt, harness, or in a pocket.

The breathing zone is the space around the worker’s face from where the breath is taken, and is generally accepted to extend no more than 30cm from the mouth. Personal sampling instruments are normally mounted on the upper chest, close to the collar-bone.

The process for taking a personal sample is as follows:

  1. A filter is dried out in a clean area and weighed
  2. The sampling head is cleaned before use
  3. The pre-weighed filter is placed in a sampling head in a clean, dust-free environment
  4. Each sampler is labelled so that it can be uniquely identified and sealed with its protective cover, or plug, to prevent contamination
  5. The volumetric flow rate is set, for example: two litres per minute (±0.1 litre / min)
  6. Each loaded sampler is connected to a sampling pump, ensuring that no leaks can occur
  7. The protective cover from the sampler is removed
  8. The sampling pump is then switched on, and attached to a calibrated flow meter so that it measures the flow through the sampler’s inlet orifice(s)
  9. Air is drawn through the filter over a known period of time, for example: 4 hours
  10. The filter is then sent to an approved laboratory to be dried out again for gravimetric analysis
  11. The filter is re-weighed.

The volume of air passing through the sampler is calculated by multiplying the mean volumetric flow rate in cubic metres per minute by the sampling time in minutes.

Time-Weighted Average (TWA)

The term ‘8-hour reference period’ relates to the procedure whereby the occupational exposures in any 24-hour period are treated as equivalent to a single uniform exposure for 8 hours… the 8-hour Time-Weighted Average (TWA) exposure.

Example: 8-hour TWA calculation for lead

Results of air monitoring should normally be reported as an 8-hour TWA. If one whole shift sample is taken, the results should be calculated as follows:

  • If the working shift is exactly 8 hours, the result of a whole shift sample is the 8-hour TWA
  • If the working shift is less than 8 hours, the TWA can be calculated by assuming a zero exposure during the remaining time…

…for example:

Exposure time Occupational exposure
An operator works for 7 hours 20 minutes on a process involving lead. The average exposure to lead (other than lead alkyls) during that period is measured as 0.12mg / m3.

To calculate the 8 hour TWA in this case:

  • First we need to convert the time (7 hours 20 minutes) to hours, as a decimal number:

    7 hours 20 minutes = 7 20/60 hours = 7 1/3 hours = 7.33 hours

  • As the shift is less than 8 hours, we also need to calculate the difference between the shift length and 8 hours:

    8 hours - 7.33 hours = 0.67 hours

  • Finally we need to use the equation (see previous page) for calculating the TWA where, for an 8 hour shift we have an occupational exposure of 0.12mg / m3 for 7.33 hours and an occupational exposure of 0mg / m3 for the remaining 0.67 hours.

8-hour TWA =

(0.12 x 7.33) + (0 x 0.67)

8

Note: This exposure to lead is significant because it exceeds half the occupational exposure limit for lead, but is below the limit itself.

Note: Including times multiplied by zero concentration (which equals zero) adds nothing to the calculation but should be included to ‘show your workings’.

If the working shift is more than 8 hours, a TWA should be calculated as a representative 8-hour period of the working day. If the exposure to lead over the working week is longer than 40 hours, this should be taken into account when interpreting the results, preferably by an experienced occupational hygienist.

Fibre sampling

Fibre sampling uses a similar technique to personal particulate sampling. Here the fibres are collected using a cowl sampling head and counted using microscopy.

A measured volume of air is drawn through a membrane filter of mixed esters of cellulose or cellulose nitrate, with square grids printed on.

The image below shows that the cowl points down, and the air is drawn in from the top.

The filter paper is mounted for microscopic analysis using the acetone-triacetin mounting method. Condensing acetone vapour collapses the filter pores, adhering the filter to the glass slide and turning it into a transparent plastic film with any asbestos fibres contained close to the upper surface.

A known fraction of the filtered deposit is examined using x500 phase contrast microscopy to count all fibres seen in a known number of graticule (grid) areas.

The calculated total number of fibres collected on the filter is divided by the volume of air sampled to determine the fibre concentration in terms of fibres per millilitre of air (f / ml).

Measurement principles for dust and fibres

There are no short term quantitative or semi-quantitative direct reading instruments or grab sampling techniques for dusts and fibres. All samples have to be analysed in a laboratory.

Dust
Gravimetric analysis This is a calculation based on the weight of the dust collected and the volume of air over a specific time.
Physical analysis This determines the particle size and shape using microscopy or photographic methods.
Chemical analysis This is used to determine the substances within a given sample. This may be made up of a variety of different dusts.
Fibres
Microscopy
  • Fibres are collected and treated with a solution of acetone and glycerol triacetate to make the filter transparent. The fibres in a measured area of the gridlines are measured using Phase-Contrast Optical Microscopy (PCOM)
  • A statistically significant number of microscope ‘fields’ is viewed and the number of fibres within a ‘graticule’ (the grid of intersecting lines) are counted
  • Fibres of a specific size and shape are counted.
