Interpreting an occupational hygienist’s report

There are no specific legal requirements for an occupational hygienist’s report, however, as a minimum the report should contain the facts, and may contain some opinion.

Introduction

To assist the reader it is useful to explain the background and why the occupational hygienist’s report was needed (the purpose), the terms of reference under which the report was commissioned and what information was provided, for example: reported symptoms or improvement notice etc. Also the date(s) of visit(s) and the site(s) visited should be clearly identified.

Process description

Where a survey of an area, plant or process is conducted the following should be described in the report:

  • The area / plant / process surveyed. For example: anodising baths
  • The process measured, the work patterns, and the hazards involved
  • References to other documents such as data sheets
  • Photographs and diagrams
  • An indication of how working conditions influence exposure. For example: higher than average production levels or usual operator unavailable
  • The number of employees, duration of shift(s), task frequency and duration. For example: 9 employees work an 8 hour day for a 5 day week, with 2 hours overtime worked infrequently
  • The conditions at the time, i.e. process conditions, controls in place and their performance. For example:
    • ‘worst case situation, with no controls’
    • ‘as normal, believed to be a representative working day’
    • ‘only bath number 2 was operating’
    • ‘PPE worn’
    • the name and identity of any items examined
    • it takes about 30 minutes for 5 bags to be opened and poured into bath 2 every day etc.

Methods and measurements

Where exposure or other measurements are conducted, the following should be included in the report:

  • What measurements were taken (long and short-term, personal / static sampling), and how. For example: for airborne contaminants, full shift personal sample, grab sample, etc.
  • Method of measurement. For example: pump, tube, badge, filter, filter holder, dosimeter numbers etc.
  • How and where samples were analysed. For example: flow rate, calibration, who collected samples, who analysed samples
  • Exposure limits or exposure benchmarks, and whether these are protective of health
  • Any industry standards of good control practice
  • Locations, and duration of personal sampling and the names of the individuals concerned.

Results and discussion

This part of the occupational hygienists report should include:

  • Results, related to a plan of the process including identified exposure sources
  • Results of personal sampling should be compared with the relevant exposure standard, for example: for airborne contaminants, TWA (LTEL) and STEL
  • A discussion of results including:
    • the adequacy of controls and an assessment of risk
    • an explanation of general trends and unusual high or low trends should be included
    • Results should also be compared with any previous surveys at the premises.

Conclusions and recommendations

Conclusions should be drawn about whether or not the relevant exposure standard(s) have been exceeded, and if the work could harm employees’ health. The occupational hygienist may express opinion at this stage, for example:

  • Identification of tasks not measured that are likely to be an exposure source
  • Any work groups that could be exposed but were not measured, etc.

Conclusions should also be drawn about adequacy of control and any further practical actions to eliminate or reduce the assessed risk so far as is practicable.

Recommendations should be selected using the hierarchy of control approach (PPE being the last resort), and guidance on an appropriate time frame to implement actions given, such as urgent, short, medium or long term. For example:

  • ‘Temporarily cease work on bath 2 until corrective actions have been implemented’
  • ‘RPE is a short term interim control. In the longer term engineering controls…’
  • ‘A preventive maintenance program should be implemented as soon as practicable’
  • ‘Periodic reviews to determine if control measures need to be modified should occur at least once a year’.

Recommendations arising from regulatory requirements or similar guidelines should reference the relevant source document(s), for example: CoSHH Regulations, EH40 etc.
