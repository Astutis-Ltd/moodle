General methods for sampling (part 1)

Qualitative sampling methods

Qualitative methods are simple observational techniques that do not involve any quantified measurement. They rely on judgment, knowledge and experience. The threshold of smell and the Tyndall light beam are examples of qualitative sampling methods.

Threshold of smell

American Occupational Exposure Limit (OEL) standards provide the threshold of smell for all applicable substances. This can be a good indication of how close to the Workplace Exposure Limit (WEL) exposure may be, but can also be dangerously unreliable. Where the threshold of smell and the WEL are very close, smell is not a reliable qualitative measure. The table below shows the threshold of smell and WEL for some common chemicals.

Chemical Threshold of smell WEL Implication
Ammonia 5.75ppm 25ppm Smell may be an indicator of WEL
Carbon monoxide 100 000ppm 30ppm Extreme exposure to WEL before smell
Hydrogen sulphide 0.0005ppm 10ppm Smell not linked to WEL
MDI (isocyanate) 0.384mg / m3 0.02mg / m3 Exposure before smell
Styrene 3.44ppm 100ppm Smell in advance of WEL

In cases when the chemical can be smelt, the worker / assessor may already be exposed to hazards above the threshold of harm. In the case of carbon monoxide the threshold of smell is over 3 000 times greater than the threshold of harm, i.e. death occurs before it can be smelt. In other cases such as hydrogen sulphide the smell will be obvious at levels that are not harmful and may cause workers to believe that the substance is harmful at lower levels.

Tyndall light beam

Dust particles are often too small to be seen by the naked eye. Using a Tyndall lamp (a dust lamp) to pass a strong beam of light through a dust cloud makes the dust particles visible to the observer by forward light scattering.

Semi-quantitative sampling methods

Semi-quantitative monitoring methods have some ‘rough’ quantified measurement, however, this is often not precise.

Semi-quantitative or short-term (grab) sampling techniques are like taking a photograph. A grab sample takes an immediate sample of air for identification of the contaminant and a ‘snap shot’ of its concentration in air at that time.

Note: In comparison, quantitative or long-term sampling techniques are like making a film, providing more detail.

Direct reading instruments

A hand bellows is used to draw a known volume of air through a stain detector tube. The contaminant in the air reacts with a reagent in the tube to indicate the level of contamination in the air. This technique:

  • takes a grab sample
  • takes an environmental sample (rather than a personal sample)
  • provides a direct reading
  • uses an active method.

Note: Hand bellows and stain detector tubes are typically referred to by the trade name ‘Draeger’, (although, there are other manufacturers such as Kitigawa).

The strengths and weaknesses of using stain detector tubes are summarised in the table below.

Strengths Weaknesses
Relatively cheap. Substance specific - the correct tube must be specified for the suspected contaminant.
Simple to use - no major costs for training or expertise. Only suitable for chemical contaminants (gases and fume).
Direct reading - immediate result.

Not very accurate - variables include:

  • bellows efficiency
  • wrong number of pumps
  • cross sensitivity and date sensitivity of tubes
  • sampling point - proximity to contaminant.
Provides an indication of the need for more sophisticated measurement. A grab sample may miss the presence of the contaminant.
