Vapour and gas sampling

Personal and static monitoring of gases and vapours can be carried out either:

  • actively, i.e. with a sampling pump

    • OR

  • passively, i.e. diffusively across a membrane, with no pump.

Active sampling

A known volume of air is drawn across the sampling medium and measured over a period of time.

It is then concentrated by passage through a solid or liquid sorbent. The material may absorb (in the material) or adsorb (on the material) the gas or vapour.

It is then analysed in a laboratory.

Note: There are a range of sorbent materials. Charcoal is one of the most common and is used for absorbing a range of organic solvents.

A direct reading instrument may be used, for example: a Draeger tube, or an impinger.

The illustration below shows a midget impinger which contains a liquid through which the contaminated air being sampled is bubbled, for example: acid mist through water.

This method is not used frequently as the contents of the impinger can spill out and contaminate the operator.

Passive sampling

The advantage of passive sampling is that no pump is required, which means it is much easier for operatives, and sampling can take place over a number of shifts if necessary. The ability to carry out long-term sampling is particularly useful for gases such as nitrous oxide sampled over days or weeks.

The illustration below shows the following methods:

  • The badge sampler has a flat permeable membrane supported over a shallow layer of sorbent
  • The tube sampler has a smaller permeable membrane supported over a deep tube filled with sorbent.

Sampling starts when the cover of the badge or the end of the tube is removed. The contaminant diffuses through the sample medium at a calculated rate and then the samples are sent to the laboratory for analysis for a TWA concentration to be calculated.

In general, the Regulatory Authorities have been reluctant to accept diffusive monitoring methods, due to concern about accuracy. This is centred around calculating the precise volume of contaminated air, which will differ with wind speed (drafts) and will affect the amount of material absorbed onto the sampling medium.

Measurement principles for vapours and gases

Chemical analysis requires a packed tube or sampling head with a filter impregnated with an adsorbing material, for example: charcoal or silica, is attached to a pump. After sampling it is desorbed and analysed in a laboratory.

As with particulate sampling it is important to:

  • calibrate the pumps used
  • draw a known volume of air through the filter or tube
  • take a number of samples to minimise errors in measurement
  • ensure the sampling head was placed in the breathing zone and to use the appropriate methods (MDHS) for determining the hazardous substances.

Note: Concentrations are expressed in parts per million (ppm) or milligrams per metre cubed (mgm-3).

Analysis techniques for vapours and gases

There are a range of analytical techniques involving spectrometry, spectroscopy and chromatography that can be used to identify specific substances and their concentration.

Infrared (IR) spectroscopy is one of the most common and widely used spectroscopic techniques due to its usefulness in determining structures of compounds and identifying them.

Gas chromatography mass spectrometry (GC / MS) is an instrumental technique, comprising a gas chromatograph (GC) coupled to a mass spectrometer (MS), by which complex mixtures of chemicals may be separated, identified and quantified.

Direct reading instruments

Direct reading instruments can be used to give a continuous reading of levels of a specific gas or vapour in the atmosphere. Some will also calculate time-weighted averages for comparison to Long-Term Exposure Limits (LTEL) and Short-Term Exposure Limits (STEL).

Portable instruments are available for measuring levels of oxygen, carbon monoxide, hydrogen sulphide, chlorine, and other gases. These are often used for monitoring gas levels in confined spaces. Some instruments will monitor for combinations of three or four gases. These ‘three in one’ or ‘four in one’ multi-gas instruments, continually measure oxygen level, flammable gas and one or two toxic gases such as carbon monoxide and / or hydrogen sulphide.

The equipment will normally give an alarm if dangerous levels of oxygen or toxic gas are encountered.

Similar equipment may be used as monitoring equipment for static sampling in some industries.

Explosimeters are used to indicate the levels of flammable gas or vapour in air, and will be set to raise the alarm well in advance of reaching flammable or explosive levels.
