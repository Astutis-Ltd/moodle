Monitoring

Monitoring of hazardous substances is necessary:

  • when failure of control measures could result in a serious health effect
  • to demonstrate that a WEL is not being exceeded
  • as an additional check on the effectiveness of control measures
  • when changes in work occur that could affect employees’ exposure, for example: increase in quantity of a substance used, new systems of work or new plant.

Monitoring is not necessary if it is immediately obvious that exposure is being adequately controlled.

Monitoring is not appropriate, unless a reliable, effective monitoring technique is available.

There are different types of monitoring and equipment that may be required. These relate to the following terms.

  • Type of sampling: personal or static (environmental)
  • Timescales: short or long term
  • Types of equipment: direct reading or indirect reading, active or passive air sampling.

The following table compares the terminology, which will be used throughout this element.

Personal or static sampling?
Personal sampling Sampler heads are attached to a person to measure the specific exposure of an individual, by taking air from the workers ‘breathing zone’.
Static (environmental) sampling Samplers are placed in fixed positions in the workplace and provide a measure of the general workplace airborne contamination.
Short-term or long-term sampling?
Short-term sampling Used for chemicals which give acute symptoms (irritation, etc.) and for spillages, etc.
Long-term sampling Used for particulates and to ensure the WEL is achieved, control measures are adequate, etc.
Direct reading or indirect reading equipment?
Direct reading

Gives an immediate, in-situ reading.

Example: Stain detector tubes, gas detectors and more sophisticated equipment such infra-red analysers (spectrophotometers).
Indirect reading Requires a sample to be taken to a laboratory for analysis which may take days.
Passive or active air sampling?
Active air sampling Uses a calibrated air pump to draw a known volume of air over the reagent, over a known period of time.
Passive air sampling Allows air to diffuse over the sampling head which may be a badge, impinger / bubbler or dosimeter tube (no pump).
