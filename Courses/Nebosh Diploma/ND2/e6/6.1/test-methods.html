Test methods and legal requirements to test new substances

REACH is based on the principle that industry should manufacture, import, use substances or place them on the market in a way that, under reasonably foreseeable conditions, human health and the environment are not adversely affected.

Manufacturers and importers are required to collect or generate data on the substances and assess how risks to human health and the environment can be controlled by applying suitable risk management measures.

To prove that the obligations are met and that the process is transparent, manufacturers and importers are required to submit a registration dossier to the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) containing:

  • a technical dossier including data on the substance
  • for substances in quantities of 10 tonnes or more per year per registrant, a chemical safety report assessing how risks to human health and the environment can be controlled.

The type and quantity of information that is required as a minimum to meet the obligations under REACH depends on the quantity that is manufactured or imported into the EU. Annexes VII to X of REACH specify the minimum data requirements for a given substance according to its tonnage - as shown in the table below.

As each new tonnage level is attained, the requirements of the next corresponding REACH annex must be addressed.

The standard requirements may be adapted (waived or increased), when appropriately justified. Each of the REACH Annexes VII to X lists specific rules for adaptation (for example: exposure or hazard characteristics), according to which the standard information requirements may be modified.

The required standard information set may also be adapted according to the general rules contained in Annexe XI, such as in cases where testing is not technically possible, or testing does not appear scientifically necessary, or based on exposure considerations.

A range of methods for the determination of toxicity and other health effects are detailed in the Test Methods Regulation.

  • if the substance is a ‘phase-in substance’, i.e. a substance already manufactured or imported, under certain conditions, before the entry into force of REACH
  • if the substance is predicted not likely to meet the criteria for category 1 or 2 classification for carcinogenicity, mutagenicity or reproductive toxicity and the PBT or vPvB criteria (PBT: persistent, bioaccumulative and toxic – vPvB: very persistent and very bioaccumulative) and,
