Toxicology

Toxicology is the science of adverse effects of chemical substances on living organisms.

There are no ‘safe’ substances, all chemicals can be poisonous and cause injury or death. But they can be used safely: the effect depends on the dose and exposure. It is possible by limiting these to handle and benefit from the properties of chemical substances in an ‘acceptably safe’ way.

Toxicological studies aim to assess the adverse effects related to different doses in order to find this ‘acceptably safe’ level.

The work is carried out in two phases:

  1. By collecting data on the properties of chemicals, results of studies and accidental misuse of chemicals
  2. By predicting the effects of chemicals in different situations.

To make relevant predictions there must be information available on the:

  • Substance and its chemical and physical properties
  • Biological system affected
  • Effects or response caused by the substance
  • Exposure (dose, time, situation).

This information is obtained from laboratory tests with cells, bacteria, animals, epidemiological studies and from accidents involving the substance.

Large amounts of toxicological information have been collected into databases and data banks.

Chemical hazard data generation

Article 13 of REACH specifies the general requirements for generation of information on the intrinsic properties of substances.

Information may be generated by means other than tests, provided that the conditions set out in Annex XI (the general rules for adaptation of the standard testing regime) are met.

Human toxicity information should be generated whenever possible by means other than vertebrate animal tests, for example:

  • in vitro methods
  • qualitative or quantitative structure-activity relationship models
  • information from structurally related substances (grouping or read-across).

REACH requires that the methods are regularly reviewed and improved with a view to reducing testing on vertebrate animals and the number of animals involved.

Where tests on substances are required to generate information on intrinsic properties of substances, they shall be conducted in accordance with the test methods laid down in a Commission Regulation or in accordance with other international test methods recognised by the Commission or the Agency as being appropriate.

Eco-toxicological and toxicological tests and analyses are to be carried out in compliance with the principles of good laboratory practice.

The apparent conflict between REACH objectives for generating information on the intrinsic properties of substances whilst reducing the amount of animal testing is partially addressed through the mandatory sharing of animal test data. The data-sharing mechanisms are:

  • Article 26 Inquiry – for new substances not subject to phase in procedures
  • Substance Information Exchange Forums (SIEFs) which allow a new registrant access to study data from previous registrations of the same substance.
