Joints must be in a neutral position When maintaining a posture or making a movement, the joints ought to be kept as far as possible in a neutral position. In the neutral position the muscles and ligaments which span the joints are stretched to the least possible extent, and are thus subject to less stress.
The work should be kept close to the body If the work is too far from the body, the arms will be outstretched and the trunk bent over forwards. The weight of the arms, head and trunk, added to the weight of any load, exerts a greater horizontal leverage, placing the joints of the elbow, shoulder and back under stress.

Twisting the trunk should be avoided Twisted postures of the trunk stretch the elastic discs between the vertebrae, and subjects the joints and muscles on both sides of the spine to asymmetric stress.
Peak stresses are produced by sudden movements Sudden movements and forces can produce large, short-duration stresses. Peak stresses are a consequence of acceleration in the movement.
Postures should be varied, as well as movements Prolonged postures and repetitive movements are tiring, and can lead to injuries to the muscles and joints.
The duration of continuous muscular effort should be limited Continuous stress, as a result of a prolonged posture or repetitive movement, leads to localised muscle fatigue, muscular discomfort and reduced muscle performance.
Short frequent breaks are better than longer infrequent breaks Muscular fatigue can be reduced by distributing the resting time throughout the task duration or working day.
