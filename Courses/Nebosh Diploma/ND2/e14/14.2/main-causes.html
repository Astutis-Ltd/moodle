Acute traumatic injury

Acute trauma occurs when a sudden force is applied to the back and spine, for example: from attempting to lift an excessive load very rapidly, or tripping whilst carrying a load and utilising fast twisting movements with the load to regain balance.

Injury can occur to the vertebrae, inter-vertebral discs, the muscles, tendons and ligaments associated with the back.
Chronic (cumulative) stress

Chronic stress is essentially a collection of minor injuries, each resulting in damage, and not being fully repaired before the next damage occurs. The muscles, tendons, ligaments and inter-vertebral discs may all be affected.

Chronic stress occurs from: poor handling techniques, adopting poor postures, poor muscle condition, and repetitive load bearing movements.
Muscle sprains

Muscle sprains occur when the muscle fibres become damaged. This damage can have a biomechanical and / or chemical cause.

Biomechanical damage can occur through over extension of the muscle, or by operating above the muscle’s safe load operating capacity and prolonged high intensity use.

Chemical damage can cause sprain when there is a decrease of oxygen in the cell, as a result of prolonged exertion by the muscle or impeded blood flow to the muscle.
Degradation

The body becomes weaker and more susceptible to damage as part of the ageing process. Ageing results in:

  • decreased bone density
  • reduced elasticity in tendons and ligaments
  • decreased muscle strength
  • decreased blood flow
  • reduction in the rate at which the body repairs itself.
Prolapsed disc (herniated or ‘slipped’ disc)

A prolapsed disc can be caused by a single event, but is more likely to be caused by an accumulation of factors over time.

A prolapsed disc occurs when the nucleus ruptures through the fibrous material surrounding it. If this gel traverses in the direction of the spinal cord, or an associated base nerve, then it will apply pressure, compressing the nerve, and causing back pain.
