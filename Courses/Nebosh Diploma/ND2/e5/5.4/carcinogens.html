Carcinogens, mutagens and asthmagens

Carcinogens are chemicals that may cause cancer or increase its incidence and that bring about changes in the makeup of cells.

Mutagens are chemicals that induce heritable genetic defects or increase their incidence.

Asthmagens (respiratory sensitisers) are chemicals that are capable of causing or increasing the incidence of occupational asthma.

Exposure to hazardous substances should be reduced in proportion to the health risks. The consequences of exposure to asthmagens, carcinogens and mutagens are potentially so severe and there is limited scientific knowledge on safe levels exposure should always be reduced ‘as low as is reasonably practicable’.

Substances or processes listed in Schedule 1 to the CoSHH Regulations are also included in the definition of carcinogen.

Mutagens

Similarly, under CLP a mutagen is a substance or preparation which is classified in germ cell mutagenicity category 1A or 1B with the hazard statement H340 – may cause genetic defects. Category 2 has the hazard statement H341 – suspected of causing genetic defects.

Information sources

An employer could use the following information sources when investigating whether carcinogens or mutagens are used in the workplace:

  • Chemical labels
  • Safety Data Sheets (SDSs) for substances used in the workplace, particularly relevant hazard statements
  • Annex VI of the CLP Regulation for classification as carcinogen
  • The European Chemicals Agency candidates list or list for substances of very high concern (SVHC)
  • Schedule 1 of CoSHH
  • By the application of the criteria in CLP in a self-classifying approach for new substances
  • EH40 Workplace Exposure Limits
  • Scientific papers
  • Information from trade associations
  • The Chemical Industry Association
  • The International Labour Organization.

Additional control measures for carcinogens and mutagens

The ACoP to Regulation 7 of CoSHH outlines additional controls for carcinogenic and mutagenic substances because of their high-risk nature.

Note: the advice in the 6th edition of the L5 ACoP and Guidance (2013) has been simplified and streamlined from more extensive advice available in the 5th edition (2002).

The following notes explain the application of the key regulations in ensuring the safe use of carcinogens and mutagens in the workplace, and outlines practical advice for additional control measures.

Prevention of exposure (Regulation 7)

The first objective is to prevent exposure to carcinogens or mutagens. Carcinogenic or mutagenic substances should not be used or processed if they can be substituted using a non-hazardous or less hazardous substitute. However, the carcinogenic, mutagenic, toxic and other properties of possible chemical substitutes should be taken into account when considering changes.

It may be possible to choose synthetic routes for the production of chemicals in order to:

  • avoid the use of carcinogenic or mutagenic substances at the start, or as part of any process or activity
  • avoid if possible the formation of by-products, intermediates, wastes or residual contaminants consisting of or containing carcinogenic or mutagenic substances and the conditions under which such substances are used.

Control of exposure (Regulation 8)

If use of a safer alternative substance or process is not reasonably practicable, it will be necessary to achieve and maintain adequate control of exposure.

To reduce exposure ‘as low as is reasonably practicable,’ for substances with an 8-hour long- term WEL reference period, a programme of air monitoring may have to be carried out. This will generally be necessary unless the risk assessment shows that the level of exposure is most unlikely ever to exceed the WEL.

The extent to which the level of exposure should be reduced below the WEL depends on the type of risk presented by the substance, weighed against the cost and the effort involved in taking measures to reduce the risk.

For carcinogens or mutagens, it is particularly important that control of exposure is kept to as low a level as is reasonably practicable. This is because of the high risk of death associated with many forms of cancer, and the fact that the level of exposure affects only the probability of cancers occurring in any exposed population, not the severity of the disease in individuals.
