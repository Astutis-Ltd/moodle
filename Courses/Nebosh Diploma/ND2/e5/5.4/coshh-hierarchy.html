CoSHH hierarchy of control

CoSHH Regulation 7 requires every employer to ensure that the exposure of his employees to substances hazardous to health is either prevented or, where this is not reasonably practicable, adequately controlled.

Substitution is the preferred solution – replacing a hazardous substance with a substance or process which, under the conditions of its use, either eliminates or reduces the risk to the health of his employees so far as is reasonably practicable.

Where prevention of exposure is not reasonably practicable the employer should adequately control exposure levels by applying appropriate protection measures in the following order of priority:

  1. the design and use of appropriate work processes, systems and engineering controls and the provision and use of suitable work equipment and materials
  2. the control of exposure at source, including adequate ventilation systems and appropriate organisational measures
  3. where adequate control of exposure cannot otherwise be achieved the provision of suitable personal protective equipment in addition to the measures required by sub- paragraphs (a) and (b).

The HSE’s Brief Guide to CoSHH ‘Working with substances hazardous to health’ (INDG136) recommends the use of the following seven step hierarchy of control:

  1. Eliminate the use of a harmful product or substance and use a safer one
  2. Use a safer form of the product, e.g. paste rather than powder
  3. Change the process to emit less of the substance
  4. Enclose the process so that the product does not escape
  5. Extract emissions of the substance near the source (LEV)
  6. Have as few workers in harm’s way as possible
  7. Provide Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) such as gloves, coveralls and a respirator. PPE must fit the wearer.

Several controls may need to be used in concert to ensure an adequate level of overall control. Where this is necessary it is essential to insure that the controls work well together, especially for controls at levels 5, 6 and 7.

Eliminate the use of a harmful product or substance

The use or production of hazardous substances may be eliminated by:

  • Changing the method of work to eliminate the task, so that exposure is no longer necessary
  • Modifying a process to eliminate the production of a hazardous by-product or waste product
  • Substituting, wherever reasonably practicable, a hazardous substance for a non- hazardous substance, for example: water based paint instead of oil based paint.

Use a safe form of the substance

The form of the substance may be changed by using a different form of the same substance, for example: exchanging a dust for:

  • pellets
  • a paste
  • a solution.

Modify the process to emit less of the hazardous substance

This can be achieved by:

  • Replacing a spraying process for a painting process which creates less airborne hazards
  • Using an automatic feed for bags of, for example: flour, which rips open the bags, filters the flour through a sieve and then removes the bags via a screw conveyor.

Enclose the process

e.g. a class III microbiological safety cabinet or a shot blasting box.

Extraction at source

Working environment controls such as appropriate levels of general ventilation, and engineering controls such as LEV, will also help to control workers exposure to airborne contaminants (see Section 3).

Minimising the number of workers exposed

Overall exposure can be controlled by reducing:

  • The number of employees subject to the exposure
  • The level and duration of exposure
  • The quantities of hazardous substances present in the workplace.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Where adequate control of exposure to the substance cannot be achieved by other means, the provision of PPE should be considered.

It may be used in combination with other controls.
