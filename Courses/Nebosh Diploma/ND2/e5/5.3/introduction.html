Introduction

The CLP Regulation entered into force in January 2009, and introduced a method of classifying and labelling chemicals based on the United Nations’ Globally Harmonised System (GHS). As of 1 June 2015 the CLP Regulation replaced the CHIP Regulations in the UK.

Chemical classification is used to regulate the supply and use of chemicals. In some cases classification can act as the basis for requiring additional chemical controls, protective measures, or restrictions on sale (under REACH) or use (under CoSHH).

Classification

Self-classification

In most case the supplier needs to decide on the classification of a substance or mixture. Self-classification of a substance or a mixture is based on a four step process:

  1. Collection of available information which can include:
    • Experimental data generated in tests for physical hazards
    • Toxicological and eco-toxicological tests
    • Historical human data such as accident records or epidemiological studies
    • Information generated in in vitro tests
    • Information from QSAR (Quantitative Structure Activity Relationships) or ‘read across’, approaches. Note: These approaches are discussed in Section 2.4.
  2. Evaluation of the adequacy and reliability of the information
  3. Review of the information against the classification criteria
  4. Decision on classification.

If the nature and severity of an identified hazard meets the classification criteria in Annex I to the CLP Regulation, a certain hazard class will be assigned to the substance or mixture. There are hazard classes for physical hazards, health hazards, and environmental hazards.

Note: Hazard classes are discussed in Section 2.4.

Harmonised classification

In some cases (e.g. to protect human health or the environment), the decision on the classification of a chemical is taken at Community level. It is mandatory for the suppliers of the respective substance or mixture to apply this harmonised classification and labelling.

This process often concerns the most hazardous substances. These are usually carcinogenic, mutagenic, toxic for reproduction or respiratory sensitisers.

The list of harmonised classifications (covering around 8 000 commonly used substances) can be found in Tables 3.1 and 3.2 of Annex VI to the CLP Regulation.
