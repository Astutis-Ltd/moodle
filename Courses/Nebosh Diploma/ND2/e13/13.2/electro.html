ElectroMagnetic Fields (EMFs)

ElectroMagnetic fields (EMFs) arise whenever electrical energy is used. Examples include: from electrical appliances at home, from work processes such as radiofrequency heating and drying and from radio, TV and telecoms broadcasting masts and security detection devices.

Exposure to high levels of EMFs can give rise to acute effects, dependent upon the frequency of the radiation. At low frequencies the effects will be on the central nervous system of the body, whilst high frequencies have a heating effect leading to a rise in body temperature.

Radio waves and microwaves

Radio waves have the longest wavelengths in the electromagnetic spectrum, ranging from the length of a football field, to larger than planet earth (static fields).

Microwaves are a portion or ‘band’ found at the higher frequency end of the radio spectrum that are commonly distinguished from radio waves because of the technologies used to access them.

Common occupational exposures include:

Activity Explanation Effects

Induction heating

Low frequency 1Hz - 10MHz
  • Induction heating is used for forging, annealing, brazing and soldering
  • Application of an intense alternating magnetic field to a conducting material heats it by induced eddy currents
  • As the dimensions of the coils producing the magnetic fields are usually small, the risk of high-level whole-body exposure is low, though the exposure to the hands can be high.

Low frequency

  • Indirect - can affect implanted or body worn medical devices
  • Sensory effects - Nausea, vertigo, metallic taste in the mouth, flickering sensations (magnetophosphenes)
  • Health effects - Nerve stimulation, effects on the central and peripheral nervous system of the body: tingling, muscle contraction, heart arrhythmia
  • Also sparks from induced fields may trigger fires / explosions if flammable vapours etc are present.

Dielectric heating
  • Applications include: plastic sealing and embossing, glue drying, fabric and textile processing, and woodworking
  • Leakage fields can be extensive in some occupational situations, resulting in whole-body exposure of operators.

High frequency

Indirect effects: Interference with active or passive implanted or body-worn medical devices, electric shocks, causing electro-explosive devices to initiate, i.e. when used in close proximity to explosives that have an electrical means of initiation.

Sensory effects: Auditory effects such as perception of clicks or buzzing caused by pulsed radar systems.

Health effects: Thermal stress, heating effects leading to a rise in core body temperature or localised limb heating (e.g. knees or ankles).

Contact with charged conducting bodies can lead to RF shock or deep tissue burns.

Intermediate frequency

The health effects of both high and low frequencies can be experienced.

Communication systems
  • Communication and radar workers are only exposed to low-level field strengths in most situations
  • Workers who climb FM / TV towers can be exposed to high levels, and safety precautions are necessary
  • Exposure can also be substantial near transmitter cabinets that have their interlocks defeated and doors open.

Medical exposure

One of the earliest applications of RF energy was short-wave diathermy (use of high frequency electric current to produce heat). It is now more likely that RF fields will be used in conjunction with static magnetic fields in Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), which is lower energy, and the field is almost fully contained within the patient enclosure.
