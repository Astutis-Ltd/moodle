Introduction

Reasonable temperature

The Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations 1992 state that:

Radiation is energy in transit in the form of high speed particles and electromagnetic waves.

The electromagnetic spectrum

Electromagnetic energy travels in waves and spans a broad spectrum from very long radio waves to very short gamma rays. The human eye can detect only a small portion of this spectrum as visible light.

Electromagnetic waves

Radiations are produced by the acceleration or sudden movement of electrons, which results in an interlocked pair of electric and magnetic fields oscillating at the frequency of the electron current. The electric field is created by the charge on the electrons. The magnetic field is produced whenever charges move, and is proportional to the size of the current.

A changing magnetic field will induce a changing electric field and vice-versa. The changing fields form electromagnetic waves.

Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves, but are different to mechanical waves in that they do not require a medium to propagate, i.e. they can travel through a vacuum as well as air and solid materials.

The speed of all electromagnetic radiations in a vacuum is the same, regardless of frequency. It is also the same as the speed of light.

Describing electromagnetic energy

Electromagnetic energy can be described by frequency, wavelength, or energy. The three are related mathematically, so if one is known the other two can be calculated.

  • Frequency is the number of cycles or waves per second, measured in Hertz (Hz)
  • Wavelength is the distance between crests of the wave, measured in metres (m)
  • Energy increases as the wavelength shortens. An electron volt (eV) is the amount of kinetic energy needed to move an electron through one volt potential.

The scientific convention is to use convenient units so that the numbers are neither too large nor too small. Consequently:

  • Radio and microwaves are usually described in terms of frequency (Hz)
  • Infrared and visible light are described in terms of wavelength (m)
  • X-rays and gamma rays are described in terms of energy (eV).
