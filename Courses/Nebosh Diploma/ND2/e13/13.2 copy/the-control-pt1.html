The Control of Artificial Optical Radiation at Work Regulations 2010 part 1

Artificial Optical Radiation (AOR) means any electromagnetic radiation in the wavelength range between 100nm and 1mm which is emitted by non-natural sources.

Regulation 2: Application

Where an employer has a duty in respect of his employees, the employer is under a like duty (so far as is reasonably practicable) in respect of any other person at work who may be affected by the work, except for provision of information and training (Regulation 5) and health surveillance (Regulation 6).

Regulation 3: Assessment of risk to the eyes or skin

Where the employer carries out work which could expose employees to levels of artificial optical radiation that could create a reasonably foreseeable risk of adverse health effects to the eyes or skin, and has not implemented controls in accordance with the general principles of prevention set out in Schedule 1 to the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, the employer must make a suitable and sufficient assessment of that risk for the purpose of identifying the measures necessary to meet the requirements of these Regulations.

As part of the risk assessment process the employer should assess, and if necessary, measure or calculate, the levels of artificial optical radiation to which employees are likely to be exposed.

Assessments, measurements and calculations, must be made in accordance with the following standards or recommendations:

  • for laser radiation, the standards of the IEC (International Electrotechnical Commission)
  • for non-coherent radiation, the standards of the IEC and the recommendations of the CIE (International Commission for Illumination) and the CEN (European Committee for Standardisation).

For exposure situations not covered by those standards or recommendations, the assessment, measurement or calculation should be carried out in accordance with national or international science-based guidelines.

The assessment must consider:

  • the level, wavelength and duration of exposure
  • the exposure limit values
  • the effects on employees or groups of employees whose health is at particular risk from exposure
  • any possible effects resulting from interactions between AOR and photosensitising chemical substances
  • any indirect effects of exposure such as temporary blinding, explosion or fire
  • the availability of alternative equipment designed to reduce levels of exposure
  • appropriate information from health surveillance, including published information
  • multiple sources of exposure
  • any class 3B or 4 laser and any AOR source that is capable of presenting the same level of hazard
  • manufacturers’ information provided in accordance with the relevant EU Directives.

The employer must record the significant findings of the risk assessment, and the measures which have been taken to eliminate or reduce the risks.

The risk assessment must be reviewed regularly if there is reason to suspect that it is no longer valid, or if there has been a significant change in the work to which the assessment relates.

Regulation 4: Obligations to eliminate or reduce risks

The employer must ensure that the risk of adverse health effects to the eyes or skin of employees is eliminated or, where this is not reasonably practicable, reduced to as low a level as is reasonably practicable.

The measures to eliminate or reduce the risk must be based on the general principles of prevention set out in Schedule 1 to the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999.

If the risk assessment indicates that employees are exposed to levels of artificial optical radiation which exceed the Exposure Limit Values (ELV), an action plan comprising technical and organisational measures must be devised and implemented to prevent exposure exceeding the ELV.

The action plan must take into account:

  • other working methods
  • choice of appropriate work equipment emitting less AOR
  • technical measures to reduce the emission of AOR including, where necessary, the use of interlocks, shielding or similar health protection mechanisms
  • appropriate maintenance programmes for work equipment, workplaces and workstation systems
  • the design and layout of workplaces and workstations
  • limitation of the duration and level of the exposure
  • the availability of personal protective equipment
  • manufacturers’ information provided in accordance with the relevant EU Directives
  • the requirements of employees in particularly sensitive risk groups.

If, despite the control measures, employees are still exposed to levels of AOR that exceed the ELVs, the employer must take immediate action to:

  • reduce exposure to below the exposure limit values
  • identify the reasons why employees have been exposed to levels which exceed the exposure limit values
  • modify the control measures to prevent employees being exposed again to levels which exceed the ELVs.

If the risk assessment indicates that in any of the areas of the workplace employees could be exposed to levels of AOR which exceed the ELVs, those areas must be:

  • demarcated, with employee access restricted, so far as is reasonably practicable
  • identified by appropriate signs, as per the Health and Safety (Safety Signs and Signals) Regulations 1996.

Regulation 5: Information and training

Where employees could be exposed to artificial optical radiation which could cause adverse health effects to the eyes or skin of employees, the employer must provide them and their representatives with suitable and sufficient information and training which must include the following:

  • the technical and organisational measures taken in order to eliminate or reduce risks
  • the Exposure Limit Values (ELV)
  • the significant findings of the risk assessment (including measurements and explanations)
  • why and how to detect and report adverse health effects to the eyes or skin
  • the circumstances in which employees are entitled to appropriate health surveillance
  • safe working practices to minimise the risk
  • the proper use of PPE.

Regulation 6: Health surveillance and medical examinations

If there is a risk of adverse health effects to the skin of employees as a result of exposure to artificial optical radiation, the employer must place the employees under suitable health surveillance.

Health surveillance must be carried out by a doctor or occupational health professional, and the risk assessment must be made available to that doctor or occupational health professional.

The employer must ensure that a health record of each of its employees who undergoes health surveillance is made, maintained and kept available in a suitable form.

The health record must contain a summary of the results of the health surveillance carried out.

The employer, once given reasonable notice, must allow an employee access to his or her personal health record, and must also provide the enforcing authority with copies of such health records as it may require.

A medical examination must be made available to an employee if:

  • the risk assessment indicates that the employee has been exposed to levels of AOR which exceed the ELV
  • as a result of health surveillance the employee is found to have an identifiable disease or adverse health effects to the skin as a result of exposure to AOR.

Where an examination is carried out the employer must ensure that a doctor or suitably qualified person:

  • informs the employee of the results of their examination
  • provides advice on whether health surveillance may be appropriate.

The employer must also:

  • keep informed of any significant findings from any further health surveillance of the employee taking into account any medical confidentiality
  • review the risk assessment
  • review risk control measures taking into account any advice given by a doctor or other suitably qualified person or the enforcing authority
  • provide continued health surveillance if appropriate.

Explanatory note (summary)

The Regulations impose a duty to carry out a specific form of risk assessment where an employer carries out work which could expose its employees to levels of artificial optical radiation (i.e. artificial light) that could create a reasonably foreseeable risk of adverse health effects to the eyes or skin, and where those risks have not already been eliminated or controlled (Regulation 3).

Where a risk assessment is necessary the Regulations also impose duties to:

  • Eliminate, or where this is not reasonably practicable, to reduce to as low a level as is reasonably practicable the risk of adverse health effects to the eyes or skin of the employee as a result of exposure to artificial optical radiation, where this risk has been identified in the risk assessment (Regulation 4(1))
  • Devise an action plan, comprising technical and organisational measures, to prevent exposure to artificial optical radiation exceeding the exposure limit values, where the risk assessment indicates that employees are exposed to levels of artificial optical radiation that exceed the exposure limit values (Regulation 4(3))
  • Take action in the event that the exposure limit values are exceeded despite the implementation of the action plan and measures to eliminate or reduce, so far as is reasonably practicable, the risk of exposure (Regulation 4(5))
  • Demarcate, limit access to, and provide for appropriate signs in those areas where levels of artificial optical radiation are indicated in the risk assessment as exceeding the exposure limit values (Regulation 4 (6) and (7))
  • Provide information and training if the risk assessment indicates that employees could be exposed to artificial optical radiation which could cause adverse health effects to the eyes or skin of the employee (Regulation 5)
  • To provide health surveillance and medical examination (Regulation 6).
