Legal requirements

Before 2010 there was no specific legislation for the workplace safety using non-ionising radiations in the UK. Until 2010 the issue was legislated for under the general requirements of these acts and regulations:

  • Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974
  • Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999
  • Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998
  • Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992.

In April 2010 the Control of Artificial Optical Radiation at Work Regulations 2010 were introduced in the UK in compliance with Directive 2006/25/EC the Physical Agents - Artificial Optical Radiation Directive.

On July 1 2016 the requirements of the Physical Agents - Electromagnetic Fields Directive (2013/35/EU) were transposed into UK legislation as the Control of Electromagnetic Field at Work Regulations 2016.

Given the commonality of the parent directives both sets of regulations contain similar duties and require employers to:

  • Conduct a risk assessment and if necessary, measure or calculate the levels of non-ionising radiation to which workers are exposed
  • Ensure that risks are eliminated or reduced to a minimum in accordance with the general principles of prevention
  • Ensure that exposure levels are kept below the Exposure Limit Value (ELV) - see notes below
  • Provide workers who are likely to be exposed to risk of harm with adequate information and training
  • Undertake appropriate health surveillance with the objective of the prevention and the early diagnosis of any adverse health effects.
ELVs for AOR and EMF

Artificial Optical Radiation (AOR)

Annexes I and II of the Physical Agents - Artificial Optical Radiation Directive (2006/25/EC) provide Exposure Limit Values (ELVs) for non-coherent optical radiation and laser radiation, respectively. The ELVs are based on the Guidelines published by the International Commission on Non-Ionising Radiation Protection (ICNIRP).

The UK Artificial Optical Radiation Regulations refer directly to the Directive Annexes.

The ELVs take account of:

  • the biological effectiveness of the optical radiation at causing harm at different wavelengths
  • the duration of exposure to the optical radiation
  • the target tissue.

The use of the ELVs for non-coherent optical radiation is generally more complex than for laser radiation. This is due to worker exposure potentially being to a range of wavelengths instead of a single wavelength. However, it is possible to make a number of simplifying, worst-case, assumptions to determine if a more detailed assessment is required.

The annexe to the electromagnetic fields directive details the exposure limit and action values for electromagnetic fields.

Electromagnetic Fields (EMF)

ELVs are determined on the basis of biophysical and biological considerations, in particular the scientifically well-established short-term and acute direct effects, i.e. thermal effects and electrical stimulation of tissues.

ELVs are classified as ‘health effects ELVs’ or ‘sensory effects ELVs’. Health effects ELVs are those above which workers might be subject to adverse health effects, such as thermal heating or stimulation of nerve and muscle tissue. Sensory effects ELVs are those above which workers might be subject to transient disturbed sensory perceptions and minor changes in brain functions.

ELVs are often impossible or difficult and expensive to measure directly. For this reason a separate set of values, known as Action Levels (ALs), has been produced relating to quantities which can be measured more easily.

The annexes to the directive detail the exposure limit and action levels for exposure to electromagnetic fields in various frequency ranges.

Health effect ELVs for electromagnetic fields are presented as SARs (Specific energy Absorption Rates). SARs as defined by the ICNIRP and WHO are:

a measure of the rate at which energy is absorbed by unit mass of tissue in an electromagnetic field. It is measured in the units of watts per kilogram - W / kg or Wkg-1.

All Specific Absorption Rate (SAR) values are averaged over any 6-min period and localised SAR values are averaged over a 10g contiguous mass of tissue.

As an example the health effects ELVs for exposure to electromagnetic fields from 100kHz to 6GHz, as specified in the EMF Directive are:

Health effects ELVs SAR values averaged over any six-minute period
ELVs related to whole body heat stress expressed as averaged SAR in the body 0.4Wkg-1
ELVs related to localised heat stress in head and trunk expressed as localised SAR in the body 10Wkg-1
ELVs related to localised heat stress in the limbs expressed as localised SAR in the limbs 20Wkg-1
