Lasers

The word laser is an acronym, which stands for Light Amplification by the Stimulated Emission of Radiation.

A laser beam is electromagnetic radiation just like the light from a light bulb, but unlike the light bulb:

  • the laser beam of light is monochromatic, i.e. it emits light of a single wavelength (or small number of wavelengths)
  • the laser beam may be in the infrared, visible or ultraviolet regions of the spectrum depending upon the active medium
  • the individual waves of a laser beam are ‘in phase’ - the laser beam is coherent
  • the laser beam is usually highly collimated (i.e. has low angular divergence and does not ‘spread out’ significantly with distance).

Coherence refers to a property holding for two or more waves or fields when each individual wave or field is in phase with every other one. Lasers, for example, emit almost perfectly coherent light. All the photons emitted by a laser have the same frequency and are in phase.

How lasers work

Energy from a flash lamp, an electrical discharge, or other source is pumped into the active (or lasing) material contained within the laser cavity.

As the energy is absorbed the individual atoms in the active material move into an ‘excited’ state. This energy is re-emitted as optical radiation.

Mirrors at either end of the laser cavity reflect the radiation back into the active medium. This stimulates the active medium into producing laser radiation, which is amplified by the process of stimulation.

One of the mirrors is only half silvered and partially transmits some of the laser radiation as the laser beam.

Types of lasers

Lasers are commonly designated by the active material employed, be it a solid, gas, liquid or semiconductor:

  • In a solid-state laser the active material is distributed in a solid matrix. Example: ruby or ‘Yag’ (Yttrium Aluminium Garnet)
  • Gas lasers have a primary output of visible red light. Helium and helium-neon (HeNe) are the most common gas lasers. CO2 lasers emit energy in the far-infrared, and are used for cutting hard materials
  • Excimer lasers use reactive gases, such as chlorine and fluorine, mixed with inert gases such as argon, krypton or xenon. When electrically stimulated, a pseudo molecule (dimer) is produced creating light in the ultraviolet range
  • Dye lasers use complex organic dyes, such as rhodamine 6G, in liquid solution or suspension as active media. They are tuneable over a broad range of wavelengths
  • Semiconductor (or diode) lasers are not solid-state lasers. They are generally very small, low power, electronic devices that may be built into larger arrays, such as the writing source in some laser printers or CD players.
