Ultraviolet radiation

UltraViolet Radiation (UVR) has shorter wavelengths and more energetic photons (particles of radiation) than visible light.

UVR is sub-divided into three bands, depending on wavelength:

  • UVC is very short-wavelength UVR and is theoretically the most harmful to humans, however UVC radiation from the sun is filtered out in the atmosphere. In practice human exposure is only available from artificial sources, such as germicidal lamps
  • UVB is mid-wavelength and is the most biologically damaging UVR, which causes sunburn and other biological effects
  • UVA has the longest wavelength and is normally found in most lamp sources. Although UVA can penetrate deeply into tissue, it is not as biologically damaging as UVB.

Artificial sources

The most significant artificial sources of UVR are:

Industrial arc welding The levels of UVR around arc-welding equipment are very high, and acute injury to the eye and the skin can occur within three to ten minutes of exposure at close viewing distances of a few metres.
Industrial UVR lamps Many industrial and commercial processes, such as photochemical curing of inks, paints and plastics, involve the use of lamps which strongly emit in the UV range. Shielding should prevent harmful exposure though accidental exposures can occur.
Black lights Specialised lamps that emit predominantly in the UV range, and are generally used for non-destructive testing with fluorescent powders, for the authentication of banknotes and documents, and for special effects in advertising and discos.
Medical treatment UVR lamps are used for a variety of diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. UVA sources are normally used in diagnostic applications.
Germicidal UVR lamps UVC wavelengths in the range 250-265nm is the most effective for sterilization and disinfection since it corresponds to a maximum in the DNA absorption spectrum.
Cosmetic tanning Regular use of a sunbed may contribute significantly to a person’s annual UV skin exposure and the staff working in tanning salons may be exposed to low levels.
General lighting Fluorescent lamps emit small amounts of UVR and contribute only a few percent to a person’s annual UV exposure. Unshielded halogen lamps can emit UVR levels sufficient to cause acute injury at short distances.

UltraViolet (UV) tanning equipment

The use of UltraViolet (UV) tanning equipment, such as sunlamps, sunbeds and tanning booths, intentionally exposes customers to UV radiation. Staff exposure may also be a concern.

Sunbed use causes short term damage to the skin (sunburn) and eyes (conjunctivitis) and in the longer term gives rise to premature skin ageing, skin cancer and cataracts. Exposure before the age of 35 years significantly increases the risk of several types of skin cancer, and younger people seem to be at greater risk.

The Sunbeds (Regulation) Act 2010 makes it illegal for under-18s to use commercial UV tanning equipment. Children accompanying adults, who are using UV tanning equipment, should be protected against stray UV radiation.

The following people should be strongly counselled against the use of UV tanning equipment. Those who:

  • have skin phototypes I or II
  • have large numbers of nevi (moles)
  • tend to freckle
  • have a history of frequent childhood sunburn
  • have pre-malignant or malignant skin lesions
  • have sun-damaged skin
  • are wearing cosmetics (may enhance sensitivity to UV exposure)
  • are taking medications (medical advice should be sought to determine if the medication affects UV-sensitivity).

Skin phototypes

Type I

  • Often burns, rarely tans
  • Tends to have freckles, red or fair hair, blue or green eyes.

Type III

  • Sometimes burns, usually tans
  • Tends to have brown hair and eyes.

Type V

  • Naturally brown skin
  • Often has brown eyes and hair.

Type II

  • Usually burns, sometimes tans
  • Tends to have light hair, blue or brown eyes.

Type IV

  • Rarely burns, often tans
  • Tends to have dark brown eyes and hair.

Type VI

  • Naturally brown / black skin
  • Usually black / brown eyes.

The duration of individual sessions should be limited in accordance with the manufacturers’ advice. Records should be kept of customer exposure to ensure that cumulative exposure is within manufacturer’s guidelines and medical advice.

UV tanning equipment should be operated correctly in accordance with manufacturers’ guidance. The correct lamps should be fitted and the equipment should be set up, calibrated and maintained in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions.

Trained staff should be present while customers are using UV tanning equipment to provide customers with information about the risks and assist as required. Staff should be made aware of the risks to themselves from working with UV tanning equipment and how to reduce or avoid these risks. Staff training records should be kept.

If more than one piece of equipment is operated in the same room screening should be provided between the units.

The equipment should be cleaned and sanitised between customers.

UV eye protection (goggles) should be provided for customers and for staff when maintaining equipment.
