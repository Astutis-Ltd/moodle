Solar radiation

The sun emits all 3 types of ultraviolet radiation discussed earlier. UVC radiation is blocked by the ozone layer and does not reach the earth’s surface, whilst UVA and UVB both reach the earth’s surface and penetrate the skin.

UVA rays have the longest wavelength that can penetrate deeply into tissue. UVA rays can reach and affect the middle layers of the skin called the dermis, causing the skin to ‘age’.

UVB rays are mid wavelength and reach the outer layer of the skin, or epidermis. UVB is the most biologically harmful causing sunburn, damaging the DNA in skin cells which can lead to skin cancer.

Outdoor workers are at an increased risk of skin cancer caused by exposure to the sun (solar ultraviolet radiation). Cancer mortality and incidence data for Great Britain suggest that construction workers account for around 58% of occupational cancer deaths and 55%of occupational cancer cases attributed to sun exposure.

IOSH commissioned research (2015) has identified the need for construction sector employers to develop sun safety strategies and cultures to ensure that the seriousness of the issue is recognised and understood and that an effective framework of controls is implemented and enforced.

The framework of controls should include:

  • Annual training each spring to remind outdoor workers of the risk and necessary precautions
  • Work schedules that minimise sun exposure in the middle of the day, or the provision of sunshades where that is not possible
  • Provision of protective clothing to workers, particularly protective neck flaps for construction safety helmets
  • Provision of sunscreen
  • Provision of drinking water (to prevent dehydration)
  • Requirement to wear suitable clothing such as long-sleeved tops, and trousers, made of a suitable material, to provide protection without increasing the risk of over-heating.
