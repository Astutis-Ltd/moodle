Thermal fatigue and shock

Thermally induced stress is an obvious concern in steam boilers and hot water heating systems. Fatigue failures are caused by thermally induced stress cycling which in a boiler occur during every firing cycle of the burner (burner on / burner off).

Several conditions can contribute to boiler stress and eventual cracking. All involve introducing excessively low temperature water or cool water at high flow rates into a hot boiler.

Although the term ‘shock’ suggests a sudden impact type failure this is rarely the case. Thermal shock occurs when a thermal gradient causes different parts of an object to expand by different amounts. Rapid and uneven expansion and contraction of a boiler’s structure can result in catastrophic failure.

Brittle fracture

Brittle materials tend to fracture suddenly without any signs of plastic deformation. Brittle fractures are more likely:

  • if materials are under high tensile stress
  • at low temperatures
  • as a consequence of an impact.

Thick steel plates and welded joints are vulnerable to brittle failure.

Creep

Creep (a progressive plastic deformation) is observed in all material types. It becomes an issue with metals at temperatures >40% of their melting point in Kelvin (0.4Tm K).

A combination of the temperature and the length of service determines whether or not creep must be considered as a possible mode of failure for a given material component.

Soft metals such as tin and lead creep at room temperature while aluminium and its alloys creep around 250°C and steel creeps at about 450°C.

The stress (force applied) giving rise to the deformation may be due to gravity, centrifugal force or positive pressure in a pressure system.

Creep can be a concern in pressure systems operating at elevated temperatures.

Creep caused the failure of the forged aluminium-copper alloy impellers in the jet engine for the Gloster Meteor aircraft (Britain’s first operational jet aircraft in World War II). The engines were operating at 200°C (a creep inducing temperature), centrifugal forces were applied during the operation of the impellers and consequently the operating life was limited to a few hundred hours before failure. (The problem was soon designed out with the development of creep resistant nimonics / nickel alloys which are creep resistant to 650°C).

Note: There is evidence of a synergistic relationship between creep and fatigue (i.e. their joint effect is greater than the sum of their individual contributions).

Hydrogen attack

Since around 1960, hydrogen attack, or embrittlement, has been encountered with increasing frequency in high-pressure, high-purity boiler systems. It is not encountered in the average industrial plant.

It occurs only when a hard, dense scale is present on the tube surfaces, permitting hydrogen to concentrate under the deposit and permeate the metal.

Hydrogen attack may develop very quickly so constant surveillance of condensate purity is required.

When atomic hydrogen permeates the metal structure, it reacts with the carbon content to form methane. Because the methane molecule is too large to diffuse through the steel, excessive pressure develops within the metal structure, causing the metal to crack along the crystalline boundaries where methane has formed.

Corrosion

Vessel corrosion can result in a range of conditions which may result in catastrophic failure of the system and an explosion. These are:

  • wastage (loss of metal thickness and strength)
  • grooving (Mechanical corrosion, due to expansion and contraction which is accelerated by a build-up of solids)
  • distortion (a situation where excessive scale is allowed to build up on surfaces)
  • sooting which gives rise to sulphuric acid when wet.
