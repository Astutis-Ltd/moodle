What is a work equipment?

Regulation 4 (1) of the Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998 (PUWER) requires every employer (and self-employed person) to ensure that work equipment provided for use at work is so constructed or adapted as to be suitable for the purpose for which it is used or provided.

Work equipment means any machinery, appliance, apparatus, tool or installation for use at work (whether exclusively or not). The scope of ‘work equipment’ is extremely wide. It covers almost any equipment used at work, including:

  • ‘tool box’ tools such as hammers, knives, handsaws, meat cleavers, etc.
  • single machines such as drilling machines, circular saws, photocopiers, combine harvesters, dumper trucks, etc.
  • apparatus such as laboratory apparatus (Bunsen burners, etc.)
  • lifting equipment such as hoists, lift trucks, elevating work platforms, lifting slings, etc.
  • other equipment such as ladders, pressure water cleaners, etc.
  • an installation such as a series of machines connected together, for example, a paper- making line, sound insulation enclosure, or scaffolding or similar access equipment (except where the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015 (CDM) impose more detailed requirements).

Motor vehicles which are not privately owned are also included. However, the more specific road traffic legislation will take precedence when these vehicles are used on public roads or in a public place.

Use in relation to work equipment means any activity involving work equipment and includes starting, stopping, programming, setting, transporting, repairing, modifying, maintaining, servicing and cleaning.

At work – the HSW Act considers an employee to be at work

... throughout the time when he is in the course of his employment, but not otherwise and a self-employed person is at work throughout such time as he devotes to work as a self-employed person.

The suitability and safety of work equipment is considered from three aspects:

  • its initial integrity
  • the place where it will be used
  • the purpose for which it will be used.

Regulation 4(2) of PUWER requires the employer to have regard when selecting work equipment to the working conditions and to the risks to the health and safety of persons which exist in the premises or undertaking in which that work equipment is to be used, and any additional risk posed by the use of that work equipment.

Example: Electrically powered equipment is not suitable for use in wet or flammable atmospheres unless it is designed for this purpose. In such circumstances suitably protected electrical equipment or alternative pneumatically or hydraulically powered equipment should be used.

All work equipment should be installed, located and used in such a way as to reduce risks to users of work equipment and for other workers, such as ensuring that there is sufficient space between the moving parts of work equipment and fixed or moving parts in its environment.

Where appropriate efforts should be made to ensure that:

  • all forms of energy used or produced by the work equipment
  • all substances used or produced by the work equipment can be supplied and / or removed in a safe manner.

Example: A petrol engine generator discharging exhaust fumes into an enclosed space would be of concern. Arrangements would need to be made to ensure that sufficient air of good quality is readily available in the workplace. This may involve the use of:

  • exhaust removal systems
  • natural and/or mechanical ventilation
  • air quality monitoring systems.

Regulation 4(3) also requires that work equipment is used only for operations for which, and under conditions for which, it is suitable. The equipment must be suitable for the process and conditions of use.

Examples:

  • A circular saw is generally not suitable for cutting a rebate, whereas a spindle moulding machine would be suitable because it can be guarded to a high standard
  • Knives with unprotected blades are not suitable for cutting operations where scissors or other cutting tools could be used, reducing both the probability and severity of injury.
