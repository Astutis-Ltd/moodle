Driver fatigue

UK police statistics indicate that fatigue contributes to about 4% of fatal road crashes and 2% of all motor vehicle collisions. It is likely that the true figures are far higher because fatigue is hard to spot and cannot be tested for like alcohol or drugs. Worldwide, it is estimated that between 10% and 20% of all road crashes are fatigue-related.

Most sleep-related crashes happen on motorways and dual-carriageways, probably because of the monotonous road environment and lack of driver stimulation.

At-work drivers are particularly at risk from tiredness, because they tend to spend longer hours at the wheel. Four in ten tiredness-related crashes involving someone driving a commercial vehicle.

The key factors that contribute to driver fatigue are summarised in the table below

Factor Notes
Lack of sleep or disturbed sleep e.g. coping with a new baby, stress or sleep disorders
Time of day the body clock reaches a natural dip, causing drowsiness and reduced concentration in the early morning (0200 to 0600) and early afternoon (1400 to 1600)
Stress typical symptoms include tiredness and difficulty concentrating
Irregular sleep patterns shift workers have been found to be particularly high risk for sleep-related crashes
Driving for long periods driving deteriorates after two hours of continuous driving with concentration levels and reaction times diminishing
Vehicle engineering Modern vehicles are quiet and comfortable with comfort enhancing features such as cruise control allowing a relaxed drive
Medication Some prescription and over-the-counter drugs can cause drowsiness

The early warning signs of driver fatigue are:

  • increased difficulty concentrating
  • yawning
  • heavy eyelids
  • rolling eyes
  • nodding head.
