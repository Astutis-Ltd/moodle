Risk assessment

The responsible person must ensure that a risk assessment is carried out in order to determine the health and safety requirements which apply to the machinery. The machinery must then be designed and constructed taking into account the results of the risk assessment.

The risk assessment and risk reduction process should:

  • Determine the limits of the machinery, which include the intended use and any reasonably foreseeable misuse thereof
  • Identify the hazards that can be generated by the machinery and the associated hazardous situations
  • Estimate the risks, taking into account the severity of the possible injury or damage to health and the probability of its occurrence
  • Evaluate the risks, with a view to determining whether risk reduction is required, in accordance with the objective of the directive
  • Eliminate the hazards or reduce the risks associated with these hazards by application of the hierarchical approach specified in the principles of safety integration.

BS EN ISO 12100:2010 – Safety of machinery. General principles for design. Risk assessment and risk reduction is the current standard for machinery risk assessment.

Determination of machinery limits

Establishing the limits of the machinery should take into account all phases of the machinery life, and consider the characteristics and performances of the machine (or series of machines in an integrated process), and the related people, environment and products.

Use limits include the intended use and the reasonably foreseeable misuse. The following should be taken into account:

Limits:

  • Use
  • Space
  • Time
  • Other
  • Different machine operating modes and the different intervention procedures for the users
  • The use of the machinery (for example, industrial, non-industrial and domestic)
  • General information about the intended user population (for example, appropriate anthropometric data)
  • The anticipated levels of training, experience or ability of users, such as operators, maintenance personnel, etc.
  • Exposure of other persons to the hazards associated with the machinery.

Space limits include concerns regarding:

  • Range of movement
  • Space requirements for persons to interact with the machine, such as during operation and maintenance
  • Human interaction (for example, the ‘operator-machine’ interface)
  • The ‘machine-power supply’ interface.

Time limits include:

  • The ‘life limit’ of the machinery and / or components
  • Recommended service intervals.

Other limits include:

  • Environmental considerations such as recommended minimum and maximum temperatures, suitability for use outdoors, in dry or wet weather, in direct sunlight, tolerance to dust and wet, etc.
  • Housekeeping, such as the levels of cleanliness required
  • Properties of the material(s) to be processed.
