Artificial Intelligence including wearable technologies

The concept of Artificial intelligence (AI) originated in 1956, at a series of academic workshops at Dartmouth College in New Hampshire, United States in which a group of scientists set out to teach machines to use language, form concepts, improve themselves (as machines) and solve problems originally ‘reserved for humans’.

Right now there is great interest in AI research and development (R&D) with billions of dollars of funding being allocated around the world, notably in the USA, China and Israel.

The expectation is that AI will improve productivity. As a consequence, there will be implications for workers and working conditions, but there is little discussion to date on how the introduction of AI into workplaces will impact upon the occupational safety and health (OSH) of workers.

In 2018 the European Commission defined AI as:

“…systems that display intelligent behaviour by analysing their environment and taking actions — with some degree of autonomy — to achieve specific goals.”

.

AI systems are typically categorised as weak or strong.

  • ‘Weak AI’ is where a machine relies on software to guide its investigation and responses such as expert systems and text and image recognition
  • ‘Strong AI’, also called ‘universal AI’ refers to when a machine can demonstrate behaviour that equals or exceeds the competence and skill of humans.

There are a range of health and safety issues arising as AI is integrated into workplaces, notably stress, discrimination, heightened precariousness, musculoskeletal disorders, and psychosocial risks associated with work intensification and job losses. Often, it is not AI technology itself that creates risks for the safety and health of workers, but the way that it is implemented.

Some examples of the current use of AI in HR, manufacturing, call-centres and the gig economy are discussed below.

AI in HR

AI-enhanced HR practices can help managers obtain seemingly objective wisdom about people even before they hire them. People analytics tools can aid employers to “measure, report and understand employee performance, aspects of workforce planning, talent management and operational management”.

Detailed analysis of worker performance can significantly increase pressure of work and result in stress-related illness.

Cobots in factories and warehouses

Robots have directly replaced workers on the assembly line in factories in many cases, and sometimes AI is confused with automation. Automation in its pure sense involves, the replacement of a human part with a robotic alternative (e.g. a robot arm). Robots allow people to be removed from dangerous physical work and environments with chemical and ergonomic hazards, thus reducing risks for workers.

A cobot (Collaborative robot) works alongside employees rather than replacing them (as with robots in established workplace automation)

Lower skilled, manual work has historically been most at risk and is still at a high risk of automation. Now, automation can be augmented with autonomous machine behaviour or ‘thinking’. So, the AI dimension of automation reflects where workers’ brains, as well as their limbs, may no longer be needed.

Cobots are now being integrated into factories and warehouses where they work alongside people in a collaborative way. They assist with an increasing range of tasks, rather than necessarily automating entire jobs. Amazon, Airbus and Nissan are all using cobots to speed up production and increase efficiency.

Three major risks have been identified in human-cobot-environment interactions

  1. Robot-human collision (Machine learning can lead to unpredictable robot behaviour)
  2. Security (Internet links can affect the integrity of software programming, leading to vulnerabilities in security
  3. Environmental (as a consequence of sensor degradation and unexpected human action in unstructured environments).

Human-robot interaction can have both positive and negative impacts on workers physical, cognitive and social health, safety and wellbeing.

Cobots can reduce health and safety risks to workers by undertaking mundane and routine service tasks in factories, which historically create stress, overwork, musculoskeletal difficulties and even boredom as a result of repetitive work.

Cobots can also introduce new physical and psychosocial hazards in the workplace through mismatches in work pace (workers chasing the robots), the potential for robot / human collisions or impacts, a workers responsibility to ‘look after’ a robot’s condition affecting work-life balance and broader considerations regarding the loss of the dignity of work.
