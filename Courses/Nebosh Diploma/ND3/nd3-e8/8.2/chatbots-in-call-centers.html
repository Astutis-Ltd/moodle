Chatbots in call centres

Call centre work is often repetitive and demanding and subject to high rates of micro-surveillance and extreme forms of measurement, and therefore stressful.

Chatbots are an AI-enhanced tool that can deal with a high percentage of basic customer service queries, theoretically freeing up humans to deal with more complex questions.

An increasing number of activities are already recorded and measured in call centres. Words used in emails or stated vocally can be data-mined to determine workers’ moods, a process called ‘sentiment analysis’. Facial expressions likewise can be analysed to spot signs of fatigue and moods that could be used to make judgements and thus lower OSH risks emerging with overwork.

Wearables and AI in (lot size) manufacturing

Wearable self-tracking devices are increasingly seen in workplaces, e.g. the use of GPS, radio-frequency identification and haptic sensing armbands in warehousing.

In lot size manufacturing workers are provided with glasses with screens and virtual reality functionality (e.g. HoloLenses or Google Glasses), or computer tablets on stands within the production line, which are used to carry out on-the-spot tasks on production lines.

The technology allows immediate, ‘on-the-spot’ training in any new tasks required to ensure the effective manufacture of any specific orders received.

Lot size refers to the quantity of an item ordered for delivery on a specific date or manufactured in a single production run.
Haptic refers to the science and technology of transmitting and understanding information through touch.

Platform applications enabling gig work

‘Gig work’ is often obtained by using online applications (apps). Not all online application logarithms utilise AI, but the data produced by client-worker matching services and client assessments / reviews results in scores that influence future client selections.
