CNC machinery

Computer Numeric Controlled (CNC) machinery is increasingly found in engineering workshops.

Most CNC machines are programmed with code generated by a Computer Aided Machining (CAM) programme, as part of a Computer Aided Design (CAD) programme, enabling the machines to repeat operations with increased speed and a high level of precision.

Modern equipment may be purpose built but many older machines (typically lathes and milling machines) are retro-fitted. Retro-fitting CNC devices to machinery that was originally designed to be manually operated, can introduce new hazards and increased risks to the operator.

A previously manual machine will still present the range of mechanical and non-mechanical hazards associated with the particular machinery type. Automation of the machining process can introduce:

  • increased axis speeds
  • the hazard of unexpected movement
  • the possibility of programming error
  • potentially dangerous fault conditions in the control system.

The degree of computer control ranges from complete control of several different machines working together, to partial control of one aspect of a single machine (such as constant surface speed cutting on a ‘microprocessor’ enhanced lathe).

Safeguards should be appropriate to the risks posed. For example, at a fully programmable CNC lathe (where many sequences of high-speed, high-power machine movements may be under computer control) complete enclosure, by fixed and interlocking guards or their equivalents, is appropriate.

At a ‘microprocessor’ enhanced mechanical lathe complete enclosure may not be necessary and safeguards more appropriate to a manually operated lathe may suffice.

Where the speed and power of machine movements under computer control leads to increased risks of injury, the following safeguarding principles should be followed:

  • Fixed and interlocked guards should be provided to prevent access to the danger zone during the automatic cycle
  • Where it is necessary to have powered movement of a machine element, with the interlocked guard open (such as for setting purposes), this should be done under limited speed conditions with hold-to-run controls
  • Enclosing guards should be used to help reduce noise, and control emissions of mists, fume and vapour, either from metalworking fluids or the material being machined
    • Vision panels provided to guarding / enclosures must be suitably resistant to impact and damage that would affect the transparency over time.
