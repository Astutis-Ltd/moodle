3D Printing

3D printers create three-dimensional objects via computers. A computer file “tells” the 3D printer what object to create and how. 3D printers essentially stack layers of a material to get the desired product. Different types of 3D printers do this in different ways, e.g. by using high temperatures, ultraviolet light, or lasers. 3D printers also use different print materials, such as plastics, rubbers, and metals.

Recent published evidence raised concerns about potential risks to respiratory health from fine particles and vapours, for those using affordable polymer filament desktop 3D printers. As a consequence the HSE, working with key stakeholders, commissioned a research report (RR1146, 2019) on “Measuring and controlling emissions from polymer filament desktop 3D printers”.

When heated, some plastic filament materials released chemicals known to be hazardous to health and particulate and VOC emissions vary significantly between filament types.

The main hazards associated with polymer filament 3D printing are:

  • Ultrafine particle emissions
  • Chemical emissions.

Additional hazards include:

  • Moving components
  • Heated components (The nozzle of the 3D printer can reach high temperatures of between 200 - 300oC.)
  • Electricity
  • Heavy equipment
  • Hazardous waste
  • Cleaning chemicals.

The HSE research has recommended control of emissions by the following means:

  • using polymer filaments from reputable suppliers;
  • choosing filament materials with a lower emission rate;
  • setting a lower operating temperature for the nozzle of the printer through which the polymer filament passes;
  • placing the printer inside an enclosing hood fitted with a suitable air filtration system;
  • waiting sufficient time for printer emissions to clear before opening the enclosing hood.
