Control systems

A control system is a system or device which responds to input signals and generates an output signal which causes the equipment under control to operate in a particular manner.

The input signals may be made by an operator via a manual control, or from the equipment itself, e.g. an automatic sensor or protection device.

Signals from the equipment may also include information (feedback) on the condition of the equipment and its response (e.g. position, whether or not it is running or its speed).

Failure of any part of the control system or its power supply should lead to a “fail-safe” (minimised failure to danger) condition.

Control systems must be designed and constructed so that they are safe and reliable, in a way that will prevent a dangerous situation arising.

To be considered safe and reliable:

  • The operation of the control system must not create any increased risk to health or safety
  • Faults in or damage to any part of the control system cannot result in additional or increased risk to health or safety
  • The loss of supply of any source of energy used by the work equipment cannot result in additional or increased risk to health or safety
  • The control system cannot impede the operation of any stop or emergency stop control
  • Faults in the hardware or the software of the control system, or errors in the control system logic must not lead to hazardous situations
  • The control system must be able to withstand the intended operating stresses and external influences (e.g. shocks, vibrations, humidity, extreme temperatures, corrosive atmospheres, dust and electromagnetic radiation)
  • Human error during operation must not give rise to hazardous situations.

Particular attention should be paid to the following:

  • The machinery must not start unexpectedly
  • The parameters of the machinery must not change in an uncontrolled way, where such change may lead to hazardous situations
  • The machinery must not be prevented from stopping if the stop command has already been given
  • No moving part of the machinery or piece held by the machinery must fall or be ejected
  • Automatic or manual stopping of the moving parts, whatever they may be, must be unimpeded
  • The protective devices must remain fully effective or give a stop command
  • The safety-related parts of the control system must apply in a coherent way to the whole of an assembly of machinery and / or partly completed machinery
  • For cable-less control, an automatic stop must be activated when correct control signals are not received, including loss of communication.

The measures which should be taken in the design and application of a control system to mitigate against the effects of its failure should be proportionate to the consequences of any failure. The greater the risk, the more resistant the control system should be to the effects of failure. In many cases bringing a machine to a safe halt may achieve the objective.
