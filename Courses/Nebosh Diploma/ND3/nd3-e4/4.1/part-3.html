Part 3 – Enforcement

Article 25: Enforcing authorities

    The main enforcing authority for the Order will normally be the local Fire and Rescue Authority (FRA). However there are some special areas where others have been appointed as the enforcing authority. These are:

  • The Health and Safety Executive, for the nuclear, shipbuilding and construction sectors (Note: There is also potential overlap in other workplaces where the FRA enforces the RRFSO but the HSE enforces DSEAR – see Element C4 for DSEAR)
  • The fire service maintained by the Secretary of State for Defence
  • The local authority with responsibility for issuing of safety certificates for sports grounds
  • Her Majesty’s Inspectors of Fire Services.

Article 27: Powers of inspectors

    The powers of inspectors set out in Article 27 are those necessary to ensure that the Order can be effectively enforced.

  1. To enter any premises which they have reason to believe it is necessary for them to enter and to inspect the whole or part of the premises and anything in them (note: there is no power of forcible entry)
  2. To make inquiries to ascertain, whether the provisions of this Order have been complied with at a premises, and to identify the responsible person in relation to the premises
  3. To require the production of any records (including computerised records and plans), and to inspect and take copies of, or of any entry in, the records
  4. To require any person having responsibilities in relation to any premises (whether or not the responsible person) to give him such facilities and assistance to enable him to exercise his powers
  5. To take samples of any articles or substances to ascertain their fire resistance or flammability
  6. To cause any article or substance likely to cause danger to the safety of relevant people to be dismantled or tested (but not so as to damage or destroy it unless this is, in the circumstances, necessary).

Note: The powers of inspectors are extended to domestic premises in Houses of Multiple Occupation (HMOs) if there are reasonable grounds to consider conditions exist that may require a prohibition notices (Article 31(7)).

Article 29: Alterations notices

The purpose of an alterations notice is twofold:

  • To assist enforcing authorities in maintaining a risk-based inspection programme by highlighting potentially high life-risk premises where risk levels may change and affect the outcomes of the fire risk assessment
  • It notifies the responsible person (and other duty holders) that the enforcing authority considers the premises to be of high or potentially high risk.

An alterations notice may be served in relation to premises which, due to their nature, constitute a serious risk to relevant persons, or may do so if a change is made to them.

Where a notice is in force, the responsible person must notify the enforcing authority of any proposed changes in relation to the premises, which may result in a significant increase in risk to persons in the event of fire.

An alterations notice may also contain requirements to:

  • notify others that the notice has been served, and the terms of the notice
  • record the results of a risk assessment
  • record fire safety arrangements in accordance with Article 11.

These additional requirements are at the discretion of the enforcing authority and should be applied, as appropriate, in the circumstances of the case.

Examples of premises where use of an alterations notice may be appropriate could include:

  • buildings which rely on critical fire protection elements, such as life safety sprinklers, smoke ventilation, etc.
  • buildings where a fire engineered solution has been incorporated within the building design in order to satisfy the functional requirements of the building regulations
  • poorly managed buildings that are prone to regular fluctuations of risk, or layout, or occupancy.

Article 30: Enforcement notices

An enforcement notice can only be served where the enforcing authority is of the opinion that the responsible person has failed to comply with any provision of the Order (or of regulations made under it).

The measures which could be required to be taken by a notice are limited to those which are necessary to ensure the failure is remedied.

An enforcement notice should set out why the responsible person has not complied with the Order and set out the provisions which have not been complied with. The notice may be framed to give the responsible person a choice between different ways of remedying the contravention – but in some cases, there may only be one solution which would adequately remedy the contravention, and it would be counterproductive, time consuming and costly to suggest there is a valid alternative.

The enforcing authority must give the responsible person at least 28 days to remedy the failure. Any notice issued may be withdrawn or extended, if no appeal is pending.

The right of appeal is by way of ‘complaint for an order’ at the Magistrates’ Courts.

Article 31: Prohibition notices

A notice served under Article 31 is the most stringent measure that can be instigated by an enforcing authority whilst exercising its powers under the Order.

This article enables the enforcing authority to act in cases where it is essential to act quickly, to ensure the safety of relevant persons.

The notice can prohibit or restrict the use of a premises or building to which it is applied.
