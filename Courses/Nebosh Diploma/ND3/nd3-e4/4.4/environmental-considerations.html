Environmental considerations

The Environment Agency for England and Wales, the Northern Ireland Environment Agency and the Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA) jointly produced Pollution Prevention Guidelines (PPG) which were based on relevant legislation and reflected best practice. Although the Environment Agency no longer provides ‘good practice’ guidance, a number of these ‘good practice’ PPGs are maintained in the UK Government National Archives as they continue to be relevant.

  • PPG 21: Incident Response Planning sets out best practice for producing an incident response plan to deal with an environmental incident, and is aimed at sites which do not have a statutory duty to prepare such plans under the Control of Major Accident Hazards Regulations 1999 (COMAH) and / or the Environmental Permitting Regulations 2007
  • PPG18: Managing Fire Water and Major Spillages gives examples of good practice for the protection of the environment in the management of run-off generated in the event of fire (or fire water) and major spillages. The run-off generated in the event of a fire can cause long-term environmental damage that in the case of groundwater contamination may persist for decades or longer. Rivers, sewers, culverts, drains, water distribution systems and other services all present routes for the conveyance of pollutants off-site, and the effects of a discharge may be evident some distance away.

Major pollution incidents can be prevented, if appropriate pollution prevention measures are in place and immediately available. Contingency planning is essential to address both preventative measures and incident response strategies.

Emergency planning should consider fire-fighting strategies and possible methods of reducing the amount of firewater run-off generated, for example by the use of sprays rather than jets, controlled burn and the possible recycling of fire-fighting water, where safe and practicable to do so. This should be agreed with the Fire and Rescue Service.

Containment systems should be designed to prevent the escape of firewater run-off to drainage systems or surface water.

Primary and local containment (bunding) will usually prevent an incident from causing pollution. However additional security may be required to contain firewater run-off, which may amount to thousands of cubic metres and remote containment systems may need to be employed to protect both surface and foul water drainage systems.

    Remote containment systems include:

  • purpose built tanks
  • containment lagoons
  • shut-off (Penstock) valves
  • oil separators.

    Where such permanent systems are not feasible, or a fire occurred outside of a containment area, emergency containment arrangements will be necessary. Examples include:

  • proprietary absorbent (also known as a spill kit)
  • drainage covers and seals
  • absorbent or inflatable booms
  • portable tanks
  • sacrificial areas
  • pits and trenches
  • earth and or sand as an absorbent.
