Fire suppression systems

The Fire Office Committee of Insurers (FOC) published the first British standard for the design and installation of automatic sprinkler systems in 1888.

The Loss Prevention Council, (LPC) picked up responsibility for the updating of the sprinkler rules on its formation one hundred years later in 1988.

The British Standards Institute (BSI) fully adopted the LPC rules as a stand-alone British Standard and published it as BS 5306: Part 2 in 1990.

The standard was superseded by BS EN 12845 in 2003, this has now been updated to BS EN 12845:2015.

The Fire Protection Association (FPA) now publishes the LPC Rules for Automatic Sprinkler Installations, fully incorporating the requirements of BS EN12845+A2 in three parts, supported by a series of Technical Bulletins and Supplementary Information.

  • Part 1 of the FPA / LPC Rules is standard BS EN12845+A2
  • Part 2 consists of a series of Technical Bulletins that clarify, enlarge, amend and, in some circumstances, supersede the contents of BS EN 12845
  • Part 3 consists of supplementary information giving recommendations, a technical briefing note on Legionella and guidelines on various aspects of sprinkler systems.
Water sprinkler systems

A conventional automatic sprinkler system consists of pipes and heat-sensitive sprinkler heads connected to a water supply. Fire is detected by individual sprinkler heads, which open to release water in the form of spray, to the seat of the fire. The alarm is raised at the same time and the fire is kept under control until the arrival of the fire brigade.

When sprinklers are present, the chances of dying in a fire are reduced by 50% to 75% and the average property loss per fire is cut by 50% to 65%.

The basics of a sprinkler system

An automatic sprinkler system comprises of arrangements of pipework held on or near the ceiling.

Sprinkler heads are attached to the pipework, at pre-determined intervals. The pipework is connected via control valves, and pumps to a reliable supply of water.
