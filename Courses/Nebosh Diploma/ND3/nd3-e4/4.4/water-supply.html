Water supply

    Water supplies for a sprinkler system must reliably provide an adequate flow of water at a suitable pressure for long enough to fight the largest expected fire. This will involve one (or more) of the following:

  • town mains
  • storage tank
  • inexhaustible source
  • pressure tank.

Pipework

Risers are vertical pipes which either connect installation valves with distribution pipes or range pipes with distribution pipes.

Distribution pipes are the horizontal pipes which feed range pipes.

Range pipes are the pipes on which sprinklers are attached either directly or via short arms.

Pumps

    Pumps should:

  • start automatically once a sprinkler head opens
  • be either electrically or diesel powered
  • have the facility of manual starting, in case it does not start automatically.

Valves

A valve set controls the supply of water to a sprinkler installation. The design of the valve set varies according to the type of installation (wet, dry or alternate – discussed later).

    The principal valves are:

  • the main stop valve, which controls water entering the installation
  • the alarm valve (situated immediately above the main stop valve), which opens soon after a sprinkler head is activated causing water to flow in the installation.

    Other valves in a wet-pipe installation include:

  • the alarm stop valve, which acts to shut off water to the water motor alarm
  • the drain valve, which allows the water in a system to be drained off
  • the test valve, which allows a small amount of water to drain from above the alarm valve, to simulate the opening of a sprinkler head – testing the action of the water motor alarm.

In a dry-pipe system a differential air valve is used so that a relatively low air pressure can hold back a larger water pressure. The valve also acts as the alarm valve.

Sprinkler heads

A sprinkler head is a heat-sensitive valve (the heat-sensitive element may be a glass bulb, or a fusible metallic link, or a combination of both) which activates at a pre-determined temperature (typically 68˚C – red bulb), opening and discharging water in the form of a spray over an area beneath, usually suppressing and often extinguishing the fire which caused it to activate.

    Sprinkler heads perform the following actions:

  • fire (heat) detection
  • fire alarm trigger – as water moves through the system pipework
  • fire extinguisher – as the discharging water falls onto the fire.
Types of sprinkler systems

In a wet-pipe system all the pipes leading from the water supply, through the various control valves and on to the sprinkler heads, are permanently filled with water under pressure.

Dry-pipe sprinkler installations are appropriate for applications either where there is a danger of damage to the pipework from the water in the pipes freezing, or in situations where temperatures may exceed 70°C.

In a dry-pipe system, the pipes on the ‘active’ side of the installation control valves are charged with compressed air under sufficient pressure to prevent the entry of water into the delivery pipework. Once the first sprinkler head operates, the compressed air escapes through the head, followed by the water.

Alternate wet and dry systems can operate as either type, and the premises occupier has the option of changing from a wet-pipe installation in the summer to a dry-pipe installation in winter.
