Choice of fire detection principle

    No individual detector is suitable for all applications. The choice will depend primarily on:

  • the speed of response required (to satisfy the fire safety objective, for example, safe evacuation)
  • the need to minimize false alarms
  • the nature of the fire hazard.

    The following also need to be taken into account:

  • the nature and quantity of the combustible materials present, including ease of ignition, heat release rate, likely form of combustion (such as smouldering or flaming) and propensity for smoke production
  • probable rate of fire growth and spread
  • the nature of the environment (for example, humidity, temperature, cleanliness, extent of pollutants and nature of work processes)
  • the proposed fire evacuation strategy
  • the height and geometry of the protected area
  • the attendance time of the fire service (particularly in the case of category P systems)
  • other active and passive fire protection measures present
  • the susceptibility of contents to heat, smoke and water
  • the speed of response to fire, and the probable false alarm rates, of different types of fire detector.

Cost, suitability for the environment and maintenance requirements are also considerations. In some circumstances, a mixture of different types of detector may be necessary to ensure early detection, or to minimise false alarms.

The pros and cons of the different detector types are shown in the table below.

Pros and cons of fire detectors

Heat detectors

Pros

  • Can detect clean burning fires, (example, alcohol) before a smoke or combustion gas detector.
  • Greatest resistance to environmental conditions and greatest immunity to false alarms.
  • Require minimum attention during system maintenance.

Cons

  • Less sensitive to most fires than all other types of fire detector.
  • Unlikely to detect smouldering fires.
  • Will not operate until flames reach about one-third of the distance to the ceiling.
  • Not suitable in clean rooms where early warning of the presence of smoke is required.

Smoke detectors (general)

Pros

  • Much faster response to most fires than heat detectors.

Cons

  • Likely to give false alarms from work process fumes and dust, etc.

Ionisation chamber smoke detectors

Pros

  • Particularly sensitive to smoke containing small particles, such as are produced in rapidly burning flaming fires.

Cons

  • Less sensitive to the larger particles found in optically dense smoke from smouldering fires.

Pros

Cons

  • Cannot detect the products from clean-burning fires (such as alcohol), unless incorporating thermal turbulence detection.

Aspirating smoke detection systems

Pros

  • Much higher sensitivity than other smoke detectors.
  • Used in clean rooms where a small fire could cause unacceptable damage.

Cons

  • Relatively insensitive to free burning fires supported by a plentiful supply of oxygen.

Flame detectors

Pros

  • Specialized applications, such as detection of fires in plants handling or storing highly flammable liquids or gases.

Cons

  • Unable to detect smouldering fires.
  • Relatively high cost.

Combustion gas detectors

Pros

  • Most sensitive to smouldering fires.

Cons

  • CO detectors can be immune to false alarms from dust, steam and cigarette smoke.
