Health and safety emergency planning

Regulations 8 and 9 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR) support the risk management requirements of Regulations 3, 4 and 5 by requiring employers to have in place:

  • procedures for serious and imminent danger and for danger areas
  • any necessary contacts with external services for first-aid, emergency medical care and rescue work.

Procedures for serious and imminent danger and for danger areas

    Every employer is required to:

  • have appropriate procedures in place to be followed in the event of serious and imminent danger to persons at work in his undertaking
  • have a sufficient number of competent persons to implement the procedures and effect the safe evacuation from premises of persons at work in his undertaking
  • ensure that employees do not have access to danger areas (access restricted on grounds of health and safety) unless they have received adequate health and safety instruction.

    The ACoP to the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations (MHSWR) (L21 now withdrawn) suggests that procedures are required for situations:

  • presenting serious and imminent danger such as a fire or a bomb
  • outbreaks of public disorder requiring the attention of the police and emergency services.

    The procedures should set out:

  • the nature of the risk and how to respond to it
  • additional procedures needed to cover risks beyond those posed by fire and bombs*
  • the specific responsibilities of individuals or groups of employees, who may have specific tasks to perform in the event of emergencies (such as to shut down a plant that might otherwise compound the danger), or who have had training so that they can seek to bring an emergency event under control
  • the role, responsibilities and authority of the competent people nominated to implement the detailed actions
  • legal requirements for specific emergency situations
  • details of when and how the procedures are to be activated

    * Other foreseeable emergencies in the workplace might include:

  • chemical emergencies
  • plant emergencies
  • medical emergencies, including rescuing personnel from height or confined spaces.

The emergency procedures should be documented clearly, setting out the limits of actions to be taken by employees. Information and training should be provided to employees and others who may need to be familiar with the procedures.

Work should not be resumed after an emergency until all serious danger has passed.

Liaison with emergency services

    The fire and rescue, and ambulance services that may be involved in on-site emergency responses will require sufficient information in advance, to enable effective risk management arrangements to be put in place for foreseeable emergencies. Useful information might include:

  • plans showing the layout of the site
  • the location of site access points
  • bottlenecks or overhead obstructions that might impede vehicle movement on-site
  • details of any hazardous chemicals used and stored on site
  • the main hazards on site such as fire, explosion or toxic release and likely locations
  • the number of personnel on site across shift patterns
  • the location of any emergency control centre
  • the identity and contact details of key personnel
  • details of vulnerable individuals on-site and specific needs for assistance in an evacuation
  • any other useful information that would contribute to the risk assessment for the emergency services own personnel.
