Internal Emergency Plan (IEP)

The internal emergency plan should address the operator’s complete response to a major accident involving dangerous substances on the establishment. It should:

  • concentrate on those events identified as being most likely to occur
  • be proportional to the probability of the accident occurring
  • have the flexibility to allow it to be extended and increased to deal with unlikely consequences which may arise through combinations of accidental circumstances and weather conditions.

    The plan should detail:

  • how people on site (including contractors and visitors) are prepared for an emergency
  • how to control, contain and mitigate the effects of any emergency
  • how to summon assistance from other organisations off site
  • how those who work on the establishment will assist any external organisations, including assisting with external (off-site) mitigatory action.

The IEP should identify nominated key personnel (by name or by job title) with contact numbers. The key personnel are the site incident controller and site main controller.

Site incident controller

The site incident controller requires a thorough knowledge of the overall situation in the vicinity of the incident. The role could be taken on by, for example, the site manager, shift manager or shift supervisor at the time the incident occurred. The role includes:

  • Overall responsibility for taking control at the scene of the incident
  • Determines whether or not activation of the internal emergency plan and external emergency plan is appropriate
  • Assumes the responsibilities of the site main controller until they are in place, i.e:
    • ensuring that the emergency services are alerted
    • ensuring that appropriate establishment alarms are sounded and the public has been informed
    • directing the shutting down and evacuation of other plant areas that are likely to be affected
    • ensuring that appropriate key personnel are summoned.
  • Directs all operations in the event of a major accident at the scene:
    • control of rescue and fire-fighting operations, until the arrival of the emergency services
    • working with the fire service in the search for casualties
    • evacuation of non-essential workers to assembly areas.
  • Also takes responsibility for:
    • setting up a communication point with appropriate communication equipment, for contact with the ‘Emergency Control Centre(s)’ (ECC)
    • giving advice and information, as requested, to the emergency services at the scene (primarily the fire service)
    • briefing the site main controller and keeping the on-site ECC informed of all significant developments.

Site main controller

The role of site main controller could be adopted by, for example, a senior establishment manager, establishment manager or director who has an overall knowledge of the site. The role includes:

  • Overall responsibility for directing operations from the on-site ECC
  • On arriving at the on-site ECC, takes over responsibility for overall control from the site incident controller
  • If a major emergency exists confirms that:
    • the emergency services have been summoned
    • if appropriate, the off-site emergency plan has been initiated.
  • Depending upon circumstances, the site main controller should:
    • ensure that the key personnel are mobilised
    • ensure that direct operational control is available for those parts of the establishment outside the areas directly affected
    • review and assess developments to help predict the most likely development of the incident
    • direct the shutting down of plants and evacuation of buildings, in consultation with the site incident controller and key personnel
    • ensure that casualties are receiving adequate attention and, if appropriate, arrange for additional assistance
    • liaise with the police to ensure that relatives are kept informed of missing and injured people
    • establish contacts with the Met Office for advice on changing weather conditions
    • liaise with external agencies to provide advice on possible effects on areas outside the establishment
    • ensure that all personnel are accounted for
    • control traffic movement within the establishment
    • arrange for an on-going record to be kept of the emergency and the responses undertaken to mitigate its effects, to provide evidence of the decisions made, the mitigatory action taken
    • provide for the welfare needs of establishment personnel
    • establish links with news media and issue information and statements, as appropriate, in liaison with the emergency services
    • ensure that full consideration is given to the preservation of evidence
    • control the rehabilitation of affected areas after the emergency.

On-site Emergency Control Centre (ECC)

The on-site Emergency Control Centre (ECC) is the place from which the internal emergency response operations are directed and co-ordinated. The site main controller, other key personnel, and the senior officers of the emergency services in attendance for tactical and operational command and control will be based at the ECC.

The on-site ECC should have good communication links with the site incident controller and all other installations on the establishment, as well as communication with appropriate points off- site, which may be via the on-site emergency services.

The ECCs should have provision for the following:

  • Equipment for adequate external/off-site communications including telephones and email addresses, etc. dedicated to emergency response personnel, and arrangements to connect the emergency services, to their central control facilities
  • Equipment for adequate internal/on-site communications such as internal telephone systems or radio links
  • Site plans and maps to show clearly the current on-site location of:
    • the areas where hazardous materials are transported, stored and processed
    • any radioactive material in use or storage
    • safety equipment
    • firewater supplies
    • drains and outfalls to watercourses
    • other fire-fighting materials
    • access points to the establishment and the on-site road systems
    • key transport facilities for emergency services, loading and unloading, and evacuation of employees
    • assembly points and casualty treatment centres.
