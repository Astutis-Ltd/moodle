Emergency planning at major accident hazard sites

Major accident is defined in the Control of Major Accident Hazard Regulations 2015 (CoMAH) as:

… an occurrence (including in particular, a major emission, fire or explosion) resulting from uncontrolled developments in the course of the operation of any establishment and leading to serious danger to human health or the environment, immediate or delayed, inside or outside the establishment, and involving one or more dangerous substances

The cyclohexane release at Flixborough in 1974, and the dioxin release from the ICMESA plant at Seveso in Italy in 1976, was the major catalysts for legislating for planning for major accident prevention and control. The EC Seveso I Directive and the work of the Advisory Committee on Major Hazards (ACMH) in the UK led to the introduction of the Control of Major Industrial Accident Hazards 1984 (CiMAH), which were superseded by the Seveso II Directive and the CoMAH Regulations 1999. The Seveso III Directive came into force on 1 June 2015, replacing the Seveso II Directive. To implement this Directive, the CoMAH Regulations 1999 (as amended) have been revoked and replaced by the CoMAH Regulations 2015.

The main aim of the CoMAH Regulations is to prevent and mitigate the effects of those major accidents involving dangerous substances, such as chlorine, LPG or explosives, which can cause serious damage/harm to people and/or the environment. Risks to the environment are treated as seriously as those to people.

A key feature of CoMAH is that the Regulations are enforced by a competent authority, comprising HSE and the Environment Agency (EA), in England and Wales, and HSE and the Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA) in Scotland. The competent authority bodies work jointly and usually provide a single response on all matters to do with CoMAH.

An establishment is subject to CoMAH if it produces, uses, handles or stores on site any dangerous substance specified in Schedule 1 of the Regulations, above a qualifying quantity. Part 1 of Schedule 1 lists named substances and thresholds. Part 2 lists thresholds for other substances that are not named but are classified as dangerous for supply under CLP.

Establishments with quantities equal to or greater than the upper threshold are known as upper-tier establishments.

Establishments with lower quantities, but which are equal to or greater than the lower threshold, are known as lower-tier establishments.

As an example, the thresholds for liquefied extremely flammable gas, including LPG and natural gas (whether liquefied or not) are as follows:

< 50 tonnes

CoMAH does not apply

≥ 50 tonnes

Lower tier

≥ 200 tonnes

Upper tier

Note: The thresholds are ‘anticipated’ rather than actual, and must take account of stock fluctuations and loss of containment or process control.

The general duty under Regulation 5 of CoMAH is to:

... take all measures necessary to prevent major accidents and limit their consequences for human health and the environment.

Part 3 (Safety Reports for Upper Tier Establishments) and Part 4 (Emergency Plans for Upper Tier Establishments) of CoMAH only apply to top tier sites. The emergency planning requirements are laid down in Regulations 12 to 16 and Schedule 4:

  • Regulation 12: Preparation review and testing of internal emergency plan
  • Regulation 13: Preparation of external emergency plan
  • Regulation 14: Review and testing of external emergency plans
  • Regulation 16: Implementing emergency plans
  • Schedule 4 – Information to be included in internal and external emergency plans:
    • Part 1: Internal emergency plans
    • Part 2: External emergency plan.

Changes to terminology from CoMAH 1999 (as amended):

  • Top tier sites are now referred to as ‘upper tier’
  • On-site emergency plans are now internal emergency plans
  • Off-site emergency plans are now external emergency plans.

Lower-tier establishments are still required to establish and implement emergency arrangements as part of their Major Accident Prevention Policy (MAPP) (Regulation 5).

The objectives of both internal and external emergency plans, as defined in Article 12-3 of the Seveso III Directive and in Regulation 11 of CoMAH, are:

  • containing and controlling incidents so as to minimise the effects, and to limit damage to persons, the environment and property
  • implementing the measures necessary to protect persons and the environment from the effects of major accidents
  • communicating the necessary information to the public and to the emergency services and authorities concerned in the area
  • providing for the restoration and clean-up of the environment following a major accident.
