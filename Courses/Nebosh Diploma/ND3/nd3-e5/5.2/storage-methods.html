Storage methods and quantities

Manufacturing and processing industries will require effective arrangements for the storage of raw materials, intermediates, products, and wastes/by-products, which may be in solid, liquid or gaseous form.

Materials may be stored in bulk, such as gases or liquids in tanks, and powders in hoppers, bins or silos. Materials may also be warehoused in smaller containers such as sacks, barrels, drums, carbuoys, etc.

Intermediate Bulk Containers (IBCs) are cubic containers used for transport and storage of fluids and bulk materials. IBCs can be moved by fork lift trucks on pallets or integrated pallet bases and can be safely stacked. They provide a more efficient means of transport than drums and barrels.

Bulk storage of flammable liquids

Bulk storage refers to tanks with a capacity in excess of 1000 litres. Control measures for the storage of flammable liquids can be summarised by the acronym ‘VICES’.

V

 Ventilation Locating storage tanks, transfer facilities, vent pipes, etc. in the open air, in a well-ventilated position will ensure that any flammable vapours given off from a spill, leak or release are rapidly dispersed.

I

 Ignition sources (control of)

If flammable atmospheres occur during normal operation, or due to accidental spills or leakage, the area may be a hazardous area under DSEAR.

Measures to control the introduction of sources of ignition are required in these areas.

C

 Containment

Tanks or containers constructed to a national or international standard to ensure their strength and integrity.

Corrosion resistance and double skinning to reduce the risk of leakage are important considerations.

E

 Exchange (substitution)

The use of higher-flashpoint or water-based products is an example of exchange/substitution.

Reducing the quantity of flammable liquid on site can also be helpful.

S

 Separation

Protects people and property from the effects of a fire at the tank.

Protects the tank from fires which may occur elsewhere on site.
