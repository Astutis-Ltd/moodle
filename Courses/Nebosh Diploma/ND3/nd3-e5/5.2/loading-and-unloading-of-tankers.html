Loading and unloading of tankers and tank containers

Road tankers

Road tankers are designed to carry liquefied loads, dry bulk cargo or gases on roads. They may be:

  • insulated or non-insulated
  • pressurised or non-pressurised
  • designed for single or multiple loads (often by means of internal divisions in their tank).

Tank containers (ISO tanks)

A tank container consists of a stainless steel vessel surrounded by an insulation and protective layer (usually polyurethane and aluminum). The vessel is mounted within an ISO standard steel frame. Tank containers are suitable for combined (intermodal) transport by truck and train.

A standard tank container has a manhole and at least one valve on the top, and a valve at the bottom, and can be loaded and unloaded from the top or the bottom. Loading and unloading is done by connecting hoses of the loading and unloading facility to the valves of the tank.

Loading and unloading of road tankers

The operation of filling any road tanker or tank container with a dangerous substance is potentially hazardous. The loading facilities and transport equipment must all be correctly designed and constructed, and properly used and maintained.

All equipment, including pumps, valves and hoses, should be suitable for the liquids being handled and for the conditions of use, and should be made to an appropriate British (or equivalent) standard.

Location

Loading/unloading bays for road tankers should be located in a safe, well-ventilated position at least 10 m away from occupied buildings, the site boundary and fixed sources of ignition. A remote controlled shut-off valve should be located between the storage tanks and the loading vehicles.

The loading/unloading bay should have easy access and exit for tankers, preferably without reversing. The loading/unloading area and the access road should preferably be dedicated to tanker use only, or otherwise have barrier controlled access to keep other vehicles and pedestrians away during transfer operations.

Road tankers waiting to load or unload should wait in an on-site parking bay, away from the highway and busy internal roads, so that vehicles and documents can be checked with minimum interference to traffic flow.

The bay should be level (or have a gentle gradient to assist drainage) and be surfaced with a material resistant to the liquids being handled. Drainage should be designed to minimise the surface area of any spillage, and lead it away from vehicles to a slop tank or interceptor.

Unloading approaches

There are various approaches to unloading road tankers:

  • Product transfer by gravity – if the storage tank is located at a lower level than the tanker
  • Product transfer using a pump – care must be taken to avoid the implosion of the tanker
  • Product transfer under positive air pressure – pneumatic or ‘blowing’, provided by the truck compressor or at the customer facility.

Bottom unloading (i.e. the product is taken from the bottom of the tanker) is generally applied.

Design of road tanker loading/unloading facility

To minimise the risk of overfilling, tankers should normally be loaded using a flow meter with a trip, to stop the pump and close a shut-off valve automatically when a pre-set quantity has been delivered.

An independent high-level or overflow alarm is recommended, to provide a warning if the meter fails. Meters may be protected by installing flow control valves.

Before liquids are unloaded from tankers into storage tanks, particular attention should be paid to ensuring enough ullage (unfilled space) space is available in the storage tank to receive the load.

High-level alarms should be provided to storage tanks to prevent overfilling, and self-sealing couplings on the hose connections should be considered to prevent spillages.

Precautions should be taken against spillage due to vehicles being moved with the hoses still connected or arms still in place. This can be done by providing barriers across the tanker stance, brake interlocks on the vehicle or breakaway couplings on the hose connections.

An emergency stop button should be installed at the loading/unloading point to activate a quick-action shut-off valve or pump stop control in an emergency. This will enable the operator/ driver to stop immediately loading/unloading if a problem is observed.

Precautions against static electricity should be provided, including:

  • an earthing connection for the vehicle
  • electrically conducting hoses
  • elimination of splash filling, by ensuring that the fill pipe in a top loading operation reaches to the bottom of the tank or tanker, for example.

Additional protection may be provided by an interlock, to prevent operation of the control valve or loading pump until the earth connection is made.

Safe access to equipment and safe means of escape for work above ground level is necessary. For top loading of vehicles, access gantries with stairs or ladders and protective handrails will usually be required. Gantries should be made of fire-resisting materials and, where necessary, should include means of access to the top of the tanker vehicles.

Fire protection measures should be designed to minimise the risk of fire spread. This may be achieved by ensuring that a ready supply of water and/or foam is available from fixed or mobile equipment. Hand extinguishers should also be located at appropriate points within the transfer area. The loading/unloading facilities should also be fitted with easily accessible fire alarm activation points.

Tank connections and fittings

Storage tank filling and emptying connections and openings for dipping and venting should be located at least 4 m from any source of ignition, building opening, trench or depression.

Drains should be either fitted with an interceptor or routed to an appropriate waste collection/treatment facility.

The connecting point for filling or discharge of above-ground tanks should be outside the bund wall, close to the tanker stand, thus keeping the flexible connecting hose short and helping access. The tank connections may need to be protected against mechanical damage by tanker vehicles.

Filling above-ground tanks from road tankers can be done either using a vehicle pump, or a fixed pump on site. Use of a fixed pump has advantages as all the equipment on the vehicle can be switched off during off-loading, and the vehicle flexible hose and coupling is not subject to pump discharge pressures. Filling points should be equipped with non-return valves, close to the shut-off valve, to minimise any spillage if the shut-off valve fails to seal.

Filling lines should be fitted with a suitable flange or coupling to connect with the hose of the delivery vehicle, rail car, etc., and should be capped when not in use. A locking cap may be advisable.

Dry disconnect couplings (NATO standard 3756) are the European standard and both ADR and RID dictate the presence of three closing devices and pressure retaining caps.

Spillage from making and breaking connections should be contained by a drip tray or a low sill, or be drained to a safe place. An interceptor for liquids not miscible with water, or other suitable collection method, may be required.

The end of the tank filling line should extend below the lowest normal operating level of the liquid, to minimise the generation of static electricity from splash filling. To prevent syphoning, the line should be self-draining. Where separate lines are used for filling and emptying, a liquid seal can be maintained by ending the discharge line at least 150 mm above the bottom of the filling line.

To minimise the risk of tank leakage, it is preferable for lines to enter the tank at the top. This may not always be reasonably practicable, particularly for large vertical tanks.

Where several different liquids are loaded/unloaded from a common location, the labelling of pipes and fittings is advisable to prevent loading to or from the wrong tank.

Engineering precautions (incompatible couplings) may be required if there is a possibility of mixing incompatible liquids. Methods of operating isolation and control valves should be indicated by labels or signs where necessary.

Tank connections to underground tanks may be located in the open air above the tank or in a chamber below ground. The chamber may be closed by a watertight manhole cover or it may be raised slightly above ground level.

Valves

Each pipe connected to a tank is a potential source of a major leakage. Each pipeline connected to a tank should be provided with a suitable shut-off valve which is fire-safe and located inside the bund wall and close to the tank.

The tank filling line should also be fitted with a shut-off valve outside the bund wall and close to the filling connection. Any line used only for filling and which enters the tank at the bottom should also be provided with a non-return valve.

Other valves may be necessary depending on process conditions. They should be labelled to indicate their function and their method of operation, where necessary.

Isolating valves must be able to be closed quickly in an emergency. Remotely Operated Shut-Off Valves (ROSOVs) may be necessary.

Tank drainage valves should be blanked off when not in use.

All isolation valves should be tested periodically to ensure they are working correctly.
