Flow through pipelines

All parts of piping systems, including valve seals and flange gaskets, should be made from material compatible with the liquids being handled.

Plastic pipes are more vulnerable to fire and may also cause greater electrostatic problems. They are normally only used if there are particular reasons, such as product purity, and if an appropriate standard of construction can be achieved.

Joints and connections should be kept to a minimum and should not be positioned where they might leak onto electrical equipment, hot surfaces or other sources of ignition, or where they might prevent the use of an escape route.

Welded joints should be used in preference to flanges, to minimise the risk of leakage, although small diameter piping (< 50 mm may have screwed joints).

The likelihood and consequences of thermal expansion and over-pressurisation should be addressed within the system design, to prevent damage to pipework and dangerous releases of flammable liquids. Alternatively hydrostatic relief valves may be fitted.

Pipework should be sized and routed to restrict the contents to a minimum (consistent with pressure-drop requirements). Pipe runs should be in the open air rather than inside buildings, and should be positioned or protected to minimise the risk of impact damage from vehicles.

Pipe runs in buildings should normally be in the open, should be protected from localised hot surfaces or heating systems, and should not pass through hazard areas such as furnace or boiler rooms.

Where ducts or pipes pass through a fire-resisting structure, any gaps between the duct or pipe and the structure should be fire-stopped.

Underground pipework presents difficulties with leak prevention, inspection and repair. If required the pipework should be laid in a shallow concrete or masonry-lined trench which should:

  • be provided with load bearing covers
  • prevent water or moisture accumulating around the pipework
  • allow for inspection of the pipework and, in particular, any joints.

Underground pipes require protection against corrosion (discussed in section on materials failure Element 6). Buried pipes should be polypropylene rather than metallic. Where metallic pipes are used they should:

  • be manufactured from stainless steel or galvanised iron covered with a proprietary anti-corrosion paint or tape
  • have welded joints, unless welding is inappropriate for corrosion protection or other reasons
  • be sleeved (i.e. the inner pipe is protected by an outer pipe)
  • be provided with cathodic protection or a sacrificial anode.

Flexible hoses

Flexible hoses should only be used where rigid piping is unsuitable, such as at filling connections or where vibration is a problem. Hoses should be made to a standard suitable for the application, and should be compatible with the liquids handled. They should be adequately supported by slings or saddles, so that the bend radius is not less than the minimum recommended by the manufacturer.

When not in use hoses should be protected from accidental damage, extremes of temperature and direct sunlight.

Hoses should be inspected each day they are used for signs of leaks, wear and mechanical damage, and examined and pressure-tested at least once a year according to the manufacturerâ€™s recommendations.

Hoses should be electrically continuous or bridged with an earthing cable to avoid electrostatic charging.

Ancillary equipment

Pumps, motors and other equipment forming part of a piping system should be sited:

  • outside of any bunds for storage tanks
  • in a well-ventilated place, preferably in the open air
  • with weather protection where necessary.

Isolation

Suitable valves should be provided within the pipework system to isolate the supply after use, during maintenance and in the event of an emergency.

Shut-off valves should be provided in the following locations:

  • at the external supply point
  • externally at the point of entry into the building
  • at the beginning of each branch from a main supply line
  • near each work position or item of plant supplied by the line, where that position is more than 10 m from a valve provided to comply with the last bullet point
  • where pipework passes through an internal wall, partition or floor.

All valves should be clearly marked and designed to achieve a rapid shut-down of flow. Valves may be manual or automatic, but automatic valves should be installed to be fail-safe.
