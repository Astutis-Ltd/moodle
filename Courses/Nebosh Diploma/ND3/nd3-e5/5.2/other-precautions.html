Other precautions

Written operating instructions should be available at all filling points. They should recognise the specific hazards of the substance being loaded and ensure the correct operation of filling equipment in both normal and emergency situations.

All necessary personal protective clothing and emergency equipment should be available, and personnel should be trained its correct use.

Instructions in Writing (IiW)

IiW in a specified form (see below) must be carried in the vehicle crew’s cab and kept readily available to act as an aid during an accident emergency situation that may occur or arise during carriage.

The IiW are to be provided by the carrier to the vehicle crew in language(s) that each member can read and understand before the commencement of the journey. The carrier is also responsible for ensuring that each member of the vehicle crew concerned understands and is capable of carrying out the instructions properly.

Before the start of the journey, the members of the vehicle crew are required to inform themselves of the dangerous goods loaded and consult the instructions in writing for details on actions to be taken in the event of an accident or emergency.

Note:

IiW are also referred to as Emergency Instruction Cards.

Historically they were referred to by the tradename ‘TREMcard’ and although no longer correct the term is still often used colloquially.

The IiW must conform to a specified four page model as regards its form and contents.

  • Actions in the event of an accident or emergency
  • Additional guidance to members of the vehicle crew on the hazard characteristics of dangerous goods by class and on actions subject to prevailing circumstances
  • Equipment for personal and general protection to carry out general actions and hazard specific emergency actions to be carried on board the vehicle.

A copy of the required Instructions in Writing according to ADR 2013 can be downloaded from the UNECE website.

Labelling of vehicles and packaging of substances

For road tank transport operations and carriage in bulk internal to the UK there are two forms of placarding / plate marking permitted.

Both approaches also include:

  • the four digit UN number as a unique identifier (1282 = Pyridine and 1088 = Acetal)
  • the appropriate hazard label (warning diamond).
Interpreting the Emergency Action Code (EAC)

The first character of the EAC determines the fire-fighting extinguishing medium.

1 = coarse water spray
2 = fine water spray substances
3 = normal foam, i.e. protein based foam that is not alcohol resistant
4 = dry agent (water must not be allowed to come into contact with the substance)

The second character of the EAC indicates appropriate personal protection.

For the letters S, T, Y or Z, normal fire-fighting clothing to the appropriate BS ENs is appropriate.

The letters P, R, W or X indicate the need for chemical protective clothing, with liquid tight connections for whole body.

Violent reaction

P, R, W or Y also indicates that there is a danger that the substance can be violently or explosively reactive.

Contain or dilute?

W, X, Y or Z indicate that spillages, contaminated fire and decontamination run-off should be prevented from entering drains and surface and ground-waters.

P, R, S or T indicates that, where there is an immediate threat to people, spillages and decontamination run-off should be washed to drains with large quantities of water. Care should be exercised to avoid unnecessary pollution of surface and ground-waters.

Public safety hazard

The letter E indicates a public safety hazard, where people should be warned to stay indoors, with doors and windows shut. Evacuation may need to be considered.

Interpreting the Hazard Identification Number (HIN)

The hazard identification number consists of two or three figures. In general the figures indicate the following hazards:

2 Emission of gas due to pressure or to chemical reaction
3 Flammability of liquids
4 Flammability of solid or self- heating liquid
5 Oxidising (fire-intensifying) effect
6 Toxic or risk of infection
7 Radioactivity
8 Corrosivity
9 Risk of spontaneous violent reaction

Notes:

  • Doubling of a figure indicates intensification of that particular hazard
  • An X prefix indicates the substance will react dangerously with water
  • Where the hazard can be adequately indicated by a single figure, the second figure is a zero.

There are many numerical combinations that have special meanings, for example:

22 Refrigerated liquefied gas, asphyxiant
232 Flammable liquid which reacts with water, emitting flammable gases
333 Pyrophoric liquid

Marking and labelling of packages

Marking comprises the UN Number and other information for certain classes (1, 2 and 7). The term is also used to describe the UN package certification details.

Labelling comprises ‘hazard diamond(s)’ with the Class number (for example, 3 for flammable liquids) and subsidiary hazard where specified.

There is an additional requirement to display an orientation label for certain packages.

Approved packaging

Once dangerous goods have been classified correctly the packaging requirements laid out in part 4 of ADR can be applied (Part 6 of ADR details the requirements for the construction, and testing of packaging and tanks).

The international agreements for the carriage of dangerous goods require packaging to be of a design-type certified by a national competent authority. This involves testing the packaging to ensure its suitability for the carriage of certain dangerous goods.

In most cases packaging has to be certified to UN standards. Such packaging is often referred to as ‘type-approved’ or ‘UN certified’. Such packaging is marked in particular ways, prefixed by the UN logo and followed by codes.

As an example, a UN No 1263 paint product (paint, lacquer, enamel, stain, shellac solutions, varnish, polish, liquid filler and liquid lacquer) is to be packed according to the instruction P001, which allows a wide range of packaging types including a ‘steel non-removable head drum’ (a conventional drum with small openings – coded 1A1). In this case the marking would be:

1A1/Y1.6/270/**
GB/abcd

    This is interpreted as:

  • 1A1 steel non-removable head drum
  • Y for PG II, III
  • 1.6 maximum relative density (formerly specific gravity) of contents. Not needed if 1.2 or less
  • 270 Test pressure of drum in kPa
  • ** last two digits of year of manufacture
  • GB country of certification
  • abcd represents the number of the certificate (in GB this is all figures).
