The fire at Albright & Wilson, Avonmouth 3 October 1996

Accident summary

At around 10:30 on 3 October 1996, a tanker containing what was believed to be epichlorohydrin began off- loading at the Albright & Wilson site in Avonmouth, Bristol.

Soon after, a series of explosions destroyed both the storage tank and the road tanker and started a fire, which persisted for an hour. It was later discovered that the road tanker in fact contained sodium chlorite, which reacts explosively with epichlorohydrin. The fire generated a 100 m black plume of smoke containing hydrogen chloride, which drifted across the Severn estuary closing local motorways and rail services.

Three weeks before the delivery was due, the company received a quality certificate confirming the number of the tank container in which the epichlorohydrin would be delivered. However due to a mix-up by the haulier, a different numbered tank containing sodium chlorite was sent by mistake. No crosschecks were made against the original documentation, which would have highlighted that the wrong chemical had been delivered. By the time the haulier notified the company of the mistake, off-loading of the road tanker had commenced.

Epichlorohydrin is a highly reactive chemical intermediate. The presence of both an epoxide ring and a chlorine atom in the molecule allows epichlorohydrin to readily undergo a variety of chemical reactions with many types of compounds.

It is used in the manufacture of epoxy resins and textiles, ion exchange resins, rubbers, and agricultural products.

At high temperatures, epichlorohydrin can react violently and in some cases explosively (spontaneous polymerization) in the presence of certain substances such as acids, alcohols and alkalis.

Sodium chlorite is a precursor solution used to create chlorine dioxide for use as a bleaching agent or disinfectant.

Key failings in technical measures included:

  • No preventative measures were in place to safeguard against the addition of a material reactive with the substance in the storage tank (in this case epichlorohydrin)
  • No sampling of raw materials took place to identify potentially hazardous impurities
  • No checks were made of:
    • the drivers documentation against the original quality documentation, such as for the tank number
    • the hazard markings on the tanker.
  • No operating procedures were in place for the receipt of deliveries.
