Transport of dangerous goods

Dangerous goods are liquid or solid substances and articles containing them that have been tested and classified against internationally-agreed criteria, and found to be potentially dangerous (hazardous) when carried. Dangerous goods are assigned to different classes depending on their predominant hazard, as shown in the table below.

Carrying goods by road or rail involves the risk of traffic accidents. If the goods are dangerous, there is also the risk of incidents, such as spillage, leading to hazards such as fire, explosion, chemical burn or environmental damage.

Carriage of dangerous goods by road or rail is regulated internationally by agreements and European Directives, with biennial updates of the Directives taking account of technological advances.

The international carriage of dangerous goods by road is regulated by the Accord Relatif au Mouvement des Marchandises Dangereuses par Route or ADR , and carriage by rail is covered by the Reglement concernant le transport International ferroviaire des marchandises Dangereuses par chemin de fer or RID (not on syllabus).

Other agreements exist for transport by: sea (IMDG), air (ICAO/IATA), and inland waterways (AND).

The ADR is implemented in the UK by the Carriage of Dangerous Goods and Use of Transportable Pressure Equipment Regulations 2009 (CDG) with certain exceptions and derogations.

Regulation 5 of CDG requires that carriage to be in accordance with ADR or RID and states that:

No person is to carry dangerous goods, or cause or permit dangerous goods to be carried, where that carriage is prohibited by ADR or RID, including where that carriage does not comply with any applicable requirement of ADR or RID.
