Separation and segregation of incompatible materials

The intensity of a fire, or its rate of growth, may be increased if incompatible materials are stored together:

  • Oxidising agents will greatly increase the severity of a flammable liquid fire
  • Packaged free-flowing flammable powder, especially stored at height, can increase the fire spread if the packaging fails.

Non-combustible dangerous substances may become involved in a spreading fire causing toxic materials to be widely dispersed in the smoke plume or carried in the fire-fighting water, leading to harmful consequences off site.

Such situations can be avoided by ensuring that dangerous substances are stored separately in dedicated warehouses or suitable compartments of warehouses.

Goods inward should be assessed as part of a receipt procedure to determine their hazards. Decisions can then be made as to where they should be stored within the chemical warehouse, in accordance with site policies and plans.

Incompatible materials should, where practicable, be segregated in the reception area, and moved to the appropriate storage area as soon as possible.

Consideration should also be given to the risk of substances degrading during storage. Appropriate precautions will include:

  • adhering to recommended storage conditions and times
  • regular inspection for early signs of degradation
  • appropriate remedial and emergency responses in place.

Basic recommendations for the segregation of dangerous substances, according to their hazard classification under CDG, are shown in Table 5.5. Table 5.5 does not take account of chemical incompatibilities. In some cases, different substances that are shown as compatible in the table may react together. This particularly applies to many corrosive substances in Class 8, which may react together to produce heat or toxic gases. Examples are:

  • acids/hypochlorites – generate chlorine gas
  • acids/cyanides – generate hydrogen cyanide gas
  • acids/alkalis – generate heat
  • acids/sulphides – generate hydrogen sulphide.

Generally, the segregation of acids from other substances will go some way to preventing problems arising from incompatibilities.

Energetic substances

Energetic substances are those substances which are capable of releasing energy through rapid exothermic decomposition, or polymerisation. When an energetic substance decomposes it can result in a fire, or an explosion.

  • unstable at or below normal room temperatures
  • stable at normal room temperatures, but react with air or moisture
  • stable at normal room temperatures, but decompose on heating, possibly after a prolonged time.

Energetic substances and their decomposition products may also be toxic, corrosive or irritant.

Spontaneous combustion

Spontaneous combustion occurs when the temperature of a substance increases to its auto- ignition temperature, at which point the substance, or its products of decomposition, can inflame without a recognised ignition source. The temperature rise occurs, as heat is generated in chemical reactions with air or moisture, faster than it can be dissipated to the surroundings.

Pyrophoric substances are a special class of spontaneously combustible substance that ignite readily in air.

Most spontaneously combustible substances that have been classified by the UN system are in Division 4.2 (Flammable solids – spontaneously combustible substances). However, some may be found in other classes. They include:

  • pyrophoric gases which are in Class 2
  • some organic peroxides from Division 5.2
  • some flammable solids from Division 4.1 may also self-heat when stored in large quantities for a long time
  • Division 4.3 contains substances that react with water to liberate flammable gases. Some of those substances react to liberate sufficient heat to ignite the gas.

Pyrophoric substances react with air or moisture. Therefore it follows that measures to prevent fires or explosions are based on adequate containment and exclusion of air or moisture, and that fire-fighting measures should not use water or aqueous foams as the extinguishant.

The following categories provide examples of the most commonly used spontaneously combustible substances:

Metals

Finely divided metals have a large surface area available for reaction with air or moisture. Reactivity is affected by particle size, nature of the surface (such as pore size) and heat of reaction.

Moisture may decrease reactivity because it aids heat conduction.

Some metals may decompose water with the evolution of heat, which then contributes to the temperature rise occurring due to autoxidation. Hydrogen can be produced and may then ignite.
Pyrophoric gases Pyrophoric gases are commonly used in the semiconductor industry. Examples include phosphine, diborane and silane and its derivatives.
Water-reactive substances

Some substances react with water or atmospheric moisture to produce flammable gases such as hydrogen, acetylene, phosphine and ammonia.

Sodium reacts violently with water and produces hydrogen.

Potassium reacts with water to give hydrogen, which usually ignites.
Organometallics

Compounds containing carbon-metal bonds.

Used as intermediates in chemical synthesis or as catalysts in some polymerisations.

They may be gases (trimethyl boron), liquids (trimethyl aluminium) or solids (di-n-butylmagnesium).
Phosphorus and its derivatives

Red phosphorus is oxidised slowly in air and tends to be non- pyrophoric, although large bulk quantities have ignited due to the build-up of heat in the centre of the pile. It may also ignite due to friction.

White phosphorus ignites in air at about 35°C and is normally stored under water. Some phosphorus derivatives ignite spontaneously in air.
Materials liable to self-heat in bulk

Stable natural materials can also ignite spontaneously if allowed to self-heat by oxidation or bacteriological action.

Materials liable to undergo oxidative heating include celluloid, coal, iron pyrites, oils, plastics, sawdust, rags, stearic acid, wood, soybean products and some types of wool. The mechanism of self-heating for many of these materials is oxidation of unsaturated chemical bonds.

Bacteriological action (for example, fermentation) may raise the temperature of some materials stored in bulk. Oxidation may then raise the temperature to the point of ignition. Moisture may increase the rate of reaction, and the incidence of fires in haystacks may be reduced by covering the stack with plastic sheets to keep the rain out. Examples of materials that may self-heat by this mechanism include straw, hay, grains, meal, sugar, and peat.
