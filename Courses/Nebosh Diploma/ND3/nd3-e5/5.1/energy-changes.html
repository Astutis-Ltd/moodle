Energy changes within chemical reactions

All chemical reactions involve a transfer of energy to or from chemicals. This is critical not only in industrial chemistry but also in the reactions of life itself.

In many chemical reactions, energy is given out by reactants as they form products, causing the temperature of the surroundings to rise. These reactions are exothermic.

In the blast furnace, blasts of hot air at 750°C are blown into the base and start a reaction between coke and oxygen:

C + O 2 → CO 2

The reaction is highly exothermic and raises the temperature at the base of furnace to about 2000°C. This is because the stored energy (or enthalpy – symbol H) in carbon and oxygen is greater than the stored energy in carbon dioxide.

Enthalpy changes (ΔH) can easily be found by measuring temperature during reactions at constant pressure.

In the coke and oxygen reaction, 394 kJ (kilojoules) of energy is released for every mole of carbon that reacts.

(C (s) + O2 (g) → CO2 (g)

ΔH = -394 kJ mol-1 )

Endothermic reactions are the opposite of exothermic reactions. In an endothermic reaction energy is taken in by the reactants to form products. The energy comes from the surroundings which lose energy and cool down, resulting in a drop in temperature.

In the blast furnace the carbon dioxide produced at the base reacts with more coke to produce carbon monoxide. This is an endothermic reaction.

(CO2 (g) + C (s) → 2CO (g)

ΔH = +173 kJ mol-1 )

Carbon monoxide taking in energy as it is formed is one of the reasons why the blast furnace becomes cooler towards the top.

Thermal runaway reactions

Thermal runaway begins when the heat produced by an exothermic reaction exceeds the heat removed. The surplus heat raises the temperature of the reaction mass, which causes the rate of reaction to increase, which in turn accelerates the rate of heat production.

As a rule of thumb the reaction rate, and hence the rate of heat generation, may be considered to double with every 10°C rise in temperature.

As the temperature increases, the rate at which heat is produced increases exponentially, whereas the rate at which heat is removed increases linearly.

The heat produced increases with the volume of the reaction mixture, whereas the heat removed depends on the surface area available for heat transfer. As scale and the ratio of volume to surface area increases, cooling can become inadequate. This is a particularly important factor when scaling-up processes from laboratory to production, and when modifying a process to increase the reaction quantities.

Once control of the reaction is lost, the temperature rises rapidly leaving little time for correction. The reaction vessel may be at risk from over-pressurisation, due to violent boiling or rapid gas generation, and the elevated temperatures may initiate secondary, more hazardous runaways or decompositions.

At best, a runaway causes loss and disruption of production, at worst it may result in a major accident. The harmful effects could include:

  • the boiling over of the reaction mass
  • large increases in temperature and pressure resulting in an explosion
  • blast and missile damage
  • secondary fires or explosions (if the materials are flammable)
  • contamination of the workplace by hot liquors and toxic materials
  • toxic clouds that may spread off-site.
