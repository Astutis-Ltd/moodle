Industrial chemistry

Industrial chemistry is the science of developing, optimising and monitoring the chemical processes used in industry for transforming raw materials and precursors into primary bulk chemicals, and ultimately into secondary commercial products for use in society.

Chemical equations are used to understand, describe and balance reactions so that production yields and reaction rates can be optimised.

The principles of iron and steel-making have not changed much since Roman times. The process is simple and uses four major raw materials: iron ore, coke (from coal), limestone and air.

Industrial chemists calculate the exact amounts of raw materials required and advise on the best reaction conditions to ensure efficient, effective, production.

In a blast furnace, haematite (iron III oxide – Fe 2 O 3 ) reacts with carbon monoxide (CO) to produce carbon dioxide (CO 2 ) and iron (Fe).

Balancing the equation shows that one molecule of haematite reacts with three molecules of carbon monoxide to produce two atoms of iron and three molecules of carbon dioxide.

Fe 2 O 3 (s) + 3CO (g) → 2Fe (s) + 3CO 2 (g)

(s) indicates solid (g) indicates gas

Once the amounts of the chemical substances in the equation are known, the masses of the substances involved can be determined using the relative atomic mass (Ar) scale.

Ultimately the yield of iron from a mass of haematite can be determined, as an example ten tonnes of haematite will yield approximately 7 tonnes of iron, under optimal conditions.

The actual yield is likely to be a fraction of the theoretical ideal, as reactions may not be completed in the time available, and product may be lost during the process. The closer the percentage yield is to 100% the more efficient the process.

Economical production is not just dependent upon the yield. The rate of reaction is equally important – the quicker the iron is made the quicker it can be sold or used in steel making.

The rate of a reaction can be controlled by changing the conditions of the reaction.

Increasing the collision rate between particles will increase the rate of reaction. The key variables are:

  • Temperature: Increasing temperature speeds up a reaction (except for those involving enzymes)
  • Pressure: Pressure increases will increase the rate of reactions involving gases
  • Concentration: Increasing concentration normally increases the rate of reaction
  • Surface area of reactant: Increasing the surface area of reactants increases the rate of reaction
  • Catalyst: The presence of a catalyst is intended to increase the rate of a reaction.

A catalyst is a substance that alters the rate of a chemical reaction without becoming permanently involved in the reaction.

It works by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower energy of activation.

A catalyst may be homogeneous (same phase, i.e. solid, liquid, or gas) or heterogeneous (different phase).

Changing temperature, pressure, concentration and surface area may also reduce the yield of the process, so there is often a compromise between rates of reaction and yield, with the aim of maximising the profit from the process.
