Examples of major incidents caused by thermal runaway

Bhopal 1984 – disaster caused by thermal runaway

On the 3 December 1984 a relief valve on a storage tank containing Methyl IsoCyanate (MIC – CH3NCO) lifted releasing a cloud of highly toxic MIC gas which drifted onto nearby housing.

Approximately 2 000 people died within a short period and tens of thousands were injured, overwhelming the emergency services. The exact numbers of dead and injured are uncertain, as people have continued to die of the effects over a period of years. The severity of the incident makes it the worst recorded within the chemical industry.

MIC is a highly reactive intermediate chemical that Union Carbide used to manufacture various pesticides. MIC reacts exothermically with a variety of potential contaminants including rust and particularly water.

The MIC produced at the factory was stored in two stainless steel storage tanks. Stainless steel is inherently safe choice for the tank material. It eliminates the reactivity hazard associated with carbon steel – rust / iron oxide (Fe2O3) is a catalyst for the exothermic MIC trimerisation process that converts gaseous or liquid MIC into the solid MIC trimer (a cyclical molecule made of three identical MIC molecules) and gives rise to a nuisance deposit.

It would seem that exothermic runaway reaction was caused by the unique combination of large amounts of water (120 to 240 gallons) together with chloroform and iron oxide. The MIC itself was stored at a higher than specified temperature as the refrigeration system had been shut down.

The runaway reaction led to an increased temperature and pressure within the tank causing the ceramic seal to the pressure relief valve to shatter and release a cloud of MIC vapour into the atmosphere. The ceramic seal was considered an upgrade to the previous carbon steel one as it eliminated the potential catalytic effect of iron oxide, unfortunately the materials could not withstand the pressures that built up as a result of the runaway reaction).

T2 Laboratories, Jacksonville Florida 19 December 2007

On 19 December 2007, four people were killed and 13 others were transported to the hospital when an explosion occured at T2 Laboratories Inc. during the production of a gasoline additive called MethylCyclopentadienyl Manganese Tricarbonyl (MCMT).

The laboratory facilities were destroyed in the explosion, other buildings were damaged up to a quarter mile radius and reactor debris was found a mile away.

T2 was producing its 175th batch of MCMT. At 13:23, the process operator noticed a cooling problem and called for the owner to be contacted and requested to return to the site.

A few minutes later, at 13:33, the reactor burst and its contents exploded, killing the owner and process operator who were in the control room and two outside operators who were exiting the reactor area.

A runaway exothermic reaction occurred during the first (metalation) step of the MCMT process. The most likely cause of which was deemed to be a loss of sufficient cooling during the process leading to an uncontrollable pressure and temperature rise which burst the reactor. The reactor’s contents then ignited, creating an explosion equivalent to 0.7 tonnes of TNT.

The root cause of the accident was the failure of T2 to recognize the runaway reaction hazard associated with the MCMT it was producing.

The main contributing causes were that:

  • the cooling system was susceptible to single-point failures due to a lack of design redundancy
  • the MCMT reactor relief system was incapable of relieving the pressure from a runaway reaction.
