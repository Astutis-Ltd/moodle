Rough-terrain, crawler and tower cranes

Rough-terrain crane

A rough-terrain crane is a wheeled crane with a telescopic jib that is primarily designed for working on sites, and able to travel on site between lifting operations over dry, firm, un-compacted ground.

Crawler cranes

Crawler cranes are typically used on longer duration operations and for operations with pick and carry duties, and routine movement over relatively short distances.

The high mobilisation and demobilisation costs associated with transport, rigging and de-rigging generally make them uneconomic for short site visits.

Crawler cranes are mounted on a track laying chassis, not wheels. The tracks give them great manoeuvrability, allowing travel over rough ground and access to tight working areas. The lifting capacity of crawler cranes is typically in the range of 15 to 250 tonnes.

Crawler cranes can be used to travel with a suspended load, but the travelling surface must be level and free from any underground hazards.

They have an advantage over lorry-mounted mobiles, as they can generally be moved around site fully rigged.

As with the previous machines there are various additional jib configurations, such as fly jibs or luffing fly jibs.

Tower cranes

Tower cranes provide coverage over large areas but occupy little space at ground level on site. The elevated control cabin gives operators an excellent view of operations.

The relatively high mobilisation and foundation costs restrict the use of tower cranes to longer term operations. There are many different designs of tower crane available.

Trolley jib (saddle jib)

Trolley jibs are the most common type of tower crane. They are available in a range of capacities from 45 metre tonne to over 2 000 metre tonne. Versions are available without an A frame, for use when there is restricted headroom.

Luffing jib

Luffing jibs can be used in place of the saddle jib tower crane where there is an obstruction to the free slew of the jib. Luffing jibs are available in a range of capacities, from 80 metre tonne to 700 metre tonne.

Other designs include:

  • goose neck (bending) jib
  • jack-knife jib – jib completely folds up when not in use
  • flat top jib – no infrastructure above the jib top
  • mobile self-erecting cranes – typically used on smaller projects.

Tower crane stability is largely dependent upon the base, which may be:

  • Cast in: anchored to a large concrete block, which may be incorporated into the foundations of the building or be supported on piles
  • Cruciform: four corner plates, usually founded on four concrete pads, four piles or a large concrete slab
  • Steel grillage: where the limiting factor is the load bearing capacity of the permanent works to which the grillage is attached
  • Rails: generally standard railway type rails, but with more substantial foundations than the typical sleepers-on-ballast arrangement that a railway uses.
