Passenger and goods lifts

The Lift and Escalator Industry Association (LEIA) has estimated that there are 250 000 passenger and goods lifts in service in the UK, each making numerous trips each day.

HSE figures show 266 people have been injured and 4 killed in ‘elevator accidents’ (those taking place in lifts, hoisting devices and elevating work platforms) between 2002 and 2010. Another 182 injuries took place in lift shafts, service ducts, and cellar hatches.

The most common lift-related injuries are minor trips, bumps and pinched fingers that are unlikely to be reported. More serious injuries can occur should something go seriously wrong with the electrical system or safety mechanism of the lift, but these are rare. The counterweighted design of passenger lifts should ensure that they cannot crash to the ground.

Passenger lifts and combined goods / passenger lifts provided for use by workers in workplaces are subject to periodic thorough examination and inspection, as required by LOLER and PUWER (6 monthly if persons are lifted).

LOLER does not apply to escalators or any travelators (moving walkways which transport people). This equipment is covered by Regulation 19 of the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations.

Where a passenger lift is not used by people at work (such as in public areas of a shopping centre) LOLER and PUWER will not apply. However, if the lift is operated by (or to some extent under the control of) an employer or self-employed person in connection with their business, they still have general duties and responsibilities under the Health and Safety at Work Act.

The most common design uses traction steel ropes to raise and lower the car.

The ropes are attached to the elevator car, and looped around a sheave (grooved pulley), which is connected to an electric motor. When the sheave is rotated by the motor, the ropes move with it and the lift goes up or down.

The ropes are also connected to a counterweight (approximately 40% capacity of a full car), which hangs on the other side of the sheave. When the car is 40 percent full (average load), the counterweight and the car are balanced.

Both the elevator car and the counterweight ride on guide rails along the sides of the elevator shaft, to prevent them from swaying back and forth, and they also work with the safety system (built-in braking system) to stop the car in an emergency.

Elevators are built with multiple ropes (usually between four and eight). Each rope is made of multiple strands of steel wire and is strong enough to support the weight of the car and the counterweight. In the unlikely event that one of the ropes snaps, the rest will hold the car up.

If all of the ropes were to break, the braking systems would prevent the car from crashing to the floor.

Passenger lifts have the following safety features:

  • A panel indicating safe working load (weight and numbers of people)
  • Automatic double doors – one for the lift shaft and one for the lift car. The outer doors only open when a lift car is present
  • Human presence detectors on the automatic doors (the safety edge that reopens the door when operated and closes the door after the removal of any obstruction)
  • A control system to ensure an adequate degree of levelling at landings
  • Safety gears to provide controlled deceleration
  • A two-way 24 hour communication system in the event of breakdown
  • Emergency lighting.
