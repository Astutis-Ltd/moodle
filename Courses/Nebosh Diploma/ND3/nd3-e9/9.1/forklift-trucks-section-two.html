Fork lift trucks - section two

Telescopic materials handler (tele-handler)

The tele-handler has a boom that pivots at the rear of the machine, and is raised and lowered by hydraulic rams. The boom can also be extended or retracted (telescoped) to give extra reach or height.

Tele-handlers may be two or four wheel drive, and have two-wheel, four-wheel or crab steering.

They are used mainly in agriculture and construction and can be used with a range of attachments.

Side-loader

The driving position of a side-loader is at the front and to one side of the lift truck.

The load is carried on the deck, the mast being traversed out sideways to pick up or set down the load.

Side-loaders are used for stacking and moving long loads, such as bales of timber and pipes, and may be fitted with stabilisers for use when picking up or setting down loads.

Pedestrian-operated lift truck

A pedestrian-operated lift truck has a limited lift height, usually not greater than two metres.

It may be electrically or manually powered for lifting and for traction.

The operator walks with the machine, and controls it with a handle.

The machine is pushed with the operator facing forwards.

Fork lift truck selection

As with all work equipment a fork lift truck must be suitable for its intended use and the environment in which it is to be used. A range of features should be taken into account when determining the suitability of a truck, including:

  • Power source: choice of battery, diesel or LPG might be influenced by environmental factors such as working indoors or outdoors, or working in flammable atmospheres
  • Size and manoeuvrability: a key issue in environments where space is limited, for example, warehouses with very narrow aisles
  • Operator view and vision aids: depending on the truck type and activity the driver may require a forward and / or rear view, and the ability to see upwards for picking and dropping off loads – vision aids such as mirrors (with Fresnel lenses on high vehicles) and CCTV may be required
  • Rated capacity: safe working loads – weights to be lifted must be within the capacity of the truck
  • Reach: able to retrieve goods from high racking
  • Suspension and tyre choice: a key determinant of suitability for the environment, for example, a warehouse truck would not be suitable for a construction site
  • FOPS: if falling objects are a concern a Falling Object Protective Structure (FOPS) is required
  • ROPS: if rolling through more than 90o is a concern a Roll-Over Protective Structure (ROPS) is required
  • Weather protection: if the truck is used outdoors an enclosed cab will be required
  • Lighting: required if the truck is used outdoors after dark, or on a highway
  • Warning systems: horn, flashing beacons, etc.
