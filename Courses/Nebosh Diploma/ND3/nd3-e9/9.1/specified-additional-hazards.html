Specified additional hazards

Additional hazards associated with fork lift trucks (and other mobile work equipment) that are specified on the syllabus include:

  • Suitability for carrying passengers
  • Unauthorised start-up
  • Overrun of speed
  • Overheating
  • Contact with wheels and tracks
  • Contact with other moving parts, drive shafts, Power Take-Off (PTO) shafts, etc.

Suitability for carrying passengers

People should not be carried in or on mobile work equipment other than in a suitable seat, or on a suitable working platform, which incorporates features for reducing risks to their safety (as low as is reasonably practicable), such as:

  • Restraining systems (full-body seat belts, lap belts, work restraint anchors and harnesses, etc.) to safeguard against falling out of the equipment
  • Falling Object Protection Systems (FOPS) to protect against objects falling on them
  • Guards and / or barriers designed to prevent contact with wheels and tracks.

Unauthorised start-up

All vehicles should be fitted with a device to prevent unauthorised use, such as a switch with a removable key, or a keypad with a PIN number. Starting keys, cards or codes should only be issued to authorised personnel upon satisfactory completion of a suitable training programme.

Keys (where used) should be stored in a controlled manner in a secure place when the vehicle is not in use. A responsible person should issue the key to an authorised operator who retains it (and is responsible for it) until the end of the work period.

Vehicles should never be left unattended with the keys in the ignition or the keypad energised.

At the end of the shift, a truck should be parked safely and the keys returned to the responsible person, or the keypad de-energised.

Safe storage and parking of FLTs

  • When not needed for use fork lift trucks should be returned to a designated parking area
  • The parking area should be on firm level ground and in a safe position (i.e. not impeding vehicle or pedestrian movements and away from emergency exits)
  • When parked the truck should be left with the mast tilted forward and the forks resting on the floor. The controls should be in neutral and the parking brakes applied.

Overrun of speed

Overrunning typically occurs when vehicles are driving down steep slopes, and gravity and momentum exert a greater influence than the vehicle’s accelerator. Ground conditions and the condition of the vehicle may render braking ineffective, and a loss of control of the vehicle could result.

Overheating

Overheating can occur due to friction on overworked bearings or brakes, or overworked electric motors, or failures of thermostats and other parts of cooling systems. The hot surfaces can present an ignition risk in flammable atmospheres and can burn people on contact.

Contact with wheels and tracks

Where there is a foreseeable risk of contact with wheels or tracks when mobile equipment is travelling, people must be kept away from the moving parts.

This can be achieved by the positioning of cabs, operator stations and work platforms, and the fitting of suitable barriers, such as robust guard rails or fenders, in positions which prevent the wheels and tracks being reached.

Contact with other moving parts, drive shafts, Power Take-Off (PTO) shafts, etc.

Appropriate safeguards, in accordance with the requirements of Regulation 11 of PUWER, should be provided to prevent people from coming into contact with the dangerous moving parts of mobile work equipment.

Drive shafts are the means of transferring power from the vehicle’s engine to any powered attachments. On an agricultural tractor this is typically referred to as a Power Take-Off (PTO) shaft. Tractor Power Take-Off (PTO) and the power take-off drive shaft of a machine are very dangerous if not correctly guarded. Every year people are killed or seriously injured and most of the accidents were preventable if the PTO and PTO drive shaft had been fitted with guards of good design, which were properly used and maintained.

The tractor PTO should be protected with a shield covering the top and both sides of the PTO, so that it stops anyone making contact with it. The shield must be well constructed and capable of supporting a downward load of at least 120kg. When not in use the PTO may be covered by a fixed cap.

PTO drive shafts should be guarded by enclosing them along their full length, from the tractor to the first bearing on the machine.

Advice on guard dimensions and design specifications for agricultural PTO drive shaft guards are contained in BS EN 12965:2003+A2:2009 Tractors and machinery for agriculture and forestry. Power Take-Off (PTO) drive shafts and their guards. Safety.

Criteria for wear and strength tests for guards are in BS EN ISO 5674:2009 Tractors and machinery for agriculture and forestry. Guards for Power Take-Off (PTO) drive-shafts. Strength and wear tests and acceptance criteria.
