PUWER Part III: Mobile work equipment (overview)

Regulation 25: Employees carried on mobile work equipment

No employee may be carried by mobile work equipment unless:

  • it is suitable for carrying persons
  • it incorporates features for reducing risks to their safety (as far as is reasonably practicable).

The following risks to the operator, and other workers, due to the mobile work equipment travelling, must be controlled:

  • falling out of the equipment
  • unexpected movement
  • objects falling on them.

Risk assessments should determine the necessity for the following controls:

  • restraining systems (full-body seat belts, lap belts, etc.)
  • Falling Object Protection Systems (FOPS)
  • guards and / or barriers designed to prevent contact with wheels and tracks.

Mobile work equipment should be driven within safe speed limits to ensure stability when cornering, and on all surfaces and gradients.

Regulation 26: Rolling over of mobile work equipment

Where there is a risk to an employee riding on mobile work equipment from its rolling over, it must be minimised by:

  1. stabilising the work equipment
  2. a structure which ensures that the work equipment does no more than fall on its side (a Roll-Over Protective System – ROPS)
  3. a structure giving sufficient clearance to anyone being carried if it overturns further than that
  4. a device giving comparable protection.

Suitable restraining systems must also be provided.

Compliance with this regulation is not required where it would increase the overall risk to safety, or it would not be reasonably practicable to operate the mobile work equipment in consequence.

Regulation 27: Overturning of fork lift trucks

Fork Lift Trucks (FLT) which carry employees must be adapted or equipped to reduce, to as low as is reasonably practicable, the risk to safety from its overturning.

The mast of a vertical-masted FLT will generally prevent a FLT overturning more than 90 degrees. A variable reach truck FLT capable of rolling over 180 degrees or more, which is used where there is a risk of it rolling over, would need a Roll-Over Protective Structure (ROPS) which should be provided where appropriate.

Regulation 28: Self-propelled work equipment

Self-propelled work equipment which may, while in motion, involve risk to the safety of persons must have:

  • facilities for preventing its being started by an unauthorised person (such as key controls and authorised access)
  • safe systems to prevent rail mounted devices colliding
  • braking and stopping devices to enable it to slow down, stop and park safely
  • a secondary braking system where there is significant risk of the main braking failing
  • vision improving devices (such as mirrors) where the driver’s direct field of vision is inadequate to ensure safety
  • appropriate lighting to enable use at night or in dark places
  • appropriate fire-fighting equipment, where anything carried / towed, constitutes a fire hazard.

Regulation 29: Remote-controlled self-propelled work equipment

Where remote-controlled self-propelled work equipment involves a risk to safety while in motion it must be designed and constructed to ensure it stops automatically once it leaves its control range.

Where there is a risk of crushing or impact it must incorporate features to guard against such risk (for example, motion sensor trip devices).

Regulation 30: Drive shafts

Where the seizure of the drive shaft between mobile work equipment and its accessories, or anything towed, is likely to involve a risk to safety, every employer should ensure that the work equipment has a means of preventing such seizure. If seizure is unavoidable, they should take every possible measure to avoid an adverse effect on the safety of an employee.

Where mobile work equipment has a shaft for the transmission of energy between it and other mobile work equipment, and the shaft could become soiled or damaged by contact with the ground while uncoupled, the work equipment must have a system for safeguarding the shaft.

A ‘drive shaft’ is a device, which conveys the power from the mobile work equipment to any work equipment connected to it. In agriculture these are known as power take-off shafts.
