Agricultural tractors and Automatic Guided Vehicles (AGVs)

Agricultural tractors

A tractor can overturn anywhere - on silage clamps, near field drains and even on the flat. Most overturns happen on slopes because drivers misjudge slopes, ignore changing ground conditions and forget the effect of loads on stability

The main issues affecting stability are:

  • driving across and turning on slopes
  • rear-mounted equipment
  • raised front end loads
  • rearward overturns where an object being pulled resists movement
  • vertical jack-knifing of tractor / trailer combinations.

Tractors are also involved in other accidents, including:

  • crushing injuries when hitching or unhitching tractor mounted machines
  • entanglement around power take-off shafts and other mechanical hazards associated with attached machinery
  • contact with (or arcing from) overhead power lines
  • collisions with other vehicles, structures and pedestrians.

Automatic Guided Vehicles (AGVs)

Automatic Guided Vehicle (AGV) is a generic term that encompasses all transport systems capable of functioning without driver operation. The systems are used primarily in manufacturing and distribution settings and have been available since the 1950s.

Early systems used detectors on the AGV to follow a buried electrical circuit or a magnetic guide tape laid on the floor surface. These systems are becoming obsolete and most modern systems use varying degrees of computer programming to give instructions to the AGVs and use laser triangulation for navigation. A rotating laser, complete with a sender (source) and receiver (detector) is mounted on each AGV. It strobes the surroundings and receives feedback from reflectors mounted on walls, columns and equipment, which is used to calculate the vehicle’s exact position.

Modern AGV designs tend to be based on a standard vehicle platform which can be customised to meet any automated material handling requirements.

Hazards associated with the use of Automatic Guided Vehicles (AGVs) include:

  • collision between AGVs and people
  • trapping points between AGVs and stationary objects or fixed structures
  • dangerous occurrences by inadvertent movement of the AGV, for example, where AGVs interact with storage and retrieval machines and the load may be displaced or collapsed, or other devices may be impacted upon.

Safeguards include:

  • segregating AGV operating areas from pedestrian traffic
  • provision of trip devices in the vehicle’s direction of travel, such as front and rear bumpers, which will stop the movements of the AGV if depressed (optical devices may also be used)
  • provision of trip whiskers, probes, optical devices or bumpers along the sides which will stop the vehicle when activated
  • means to slow the AGV from full to crawl speed, should the optical sensors detect an obstacle
  • means to ensure accurate positioning of the AGV at transfer points
  • emergency stop buttons accessible from any side
  • automatic monitoring of the safety system including brakes and sensors so that appropriate action (such as an emergency stop) can be taken, should a system failure occur
  • audible and visible signals to indicate that the vehicle is moving or about to move, or that an obstacle has been detected.
