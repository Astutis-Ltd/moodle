Inspection and testing

Regulation 6 of PUWER requires every employer to ensure that, where the safety of work equipment depends on the installation conditions, it is inspected to ensure that it has been installed correctly and is safe to operate:

  • after installation and before being put into service for the first time
  • after assembly at a new site or in a new location.

Where work equipment is exposed to conditions causing deterioration which is liable to result in dangerous situations, it is to be inspected to ensure that health and safety conditions are maintained and that any deterioration can be detected and remedied in good time:

  • at suitable intervals
  • each time that exceptional circumstances which are liable to jeopardise the safety of the work equipment have occurred.

Where the general risk assessment under Regulation 3 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 has identified a significant risk to the operator or other workers from the installation or use of the work equipment, a suitable inspection should be carried out.

Inspection is only necessary where there is a significant risk resulting from:

  • incorrect installation or re-installation
  • deterioration
  • as a result of exceptional circumstances which could affect the safe operation of the work equipment.

Significant risk: One which could foreseeably result in a major injury or worse.

The guidance recommends that inspection regimes are required for the following equipment:

  • fairground rides
  • machinery where the danger zone is approached during operation, such as injection moulding machines, guillotines and die-casting machines
  • complex automated equipment
  • integrated production lines.

The purpose of an inspection is to identify whether the equipment can be operated, adjusted and maintained safely and that any deterioration (for example, defect, damage, wear) can be detected and remedied before it results in unacceptable risks.

The extent of the inspection required will depend on the potential risks arising from: the type of equipment, where it is used, and how it is used.

An inspection will vary from a simple visual external inspection to a detailed comprehensive inspection, which may include some functional tests, dismantling and / or testing.

Examples of inspections include:

  • quick checks before use, such as electric cable condition on hand-held power tools, functional testing of brakes, and lights on mobile machinery
  • weekly checks, such as presence of guarding, function of safety devices, tyre pressures, and the condition of windows, mirrors and CCTV on mobile plant
  • more extensive examinations, undertaken every few months or longer, such as general condition of a ladder, close examination of a safety harness and portable appliance testing.

Functional or other tests (NDT of safety-critical parts, for example) may be necessary to check that the safety-related parts, such as interlocks, protection devices, controls, etc. are working as they should be, and that the work equipment and relevant parts are structurally sound.

The need for any testing should be decided by the competent person who determines the nature of the inspection.

The persons who determine the nature of the inspections required and who carry out inspections must be competent to do so.

The person who actually carries out the inspection should have sufficient knowledge of the equipment to:

  • enable them to know what to look at (know the key components)
  • know what to look for (fault-finding)
  • know what to do (reporting faults, making a record, who to report to).
