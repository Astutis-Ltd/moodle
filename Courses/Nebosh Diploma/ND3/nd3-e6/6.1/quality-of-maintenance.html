Quality of maintenance

Under the Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998 (PUWER) the employer has an absolute duty to maintain work equipment in an efficient state, in efficient working order and in good repair. Here ‘efficient’ relates to how the condition of the equipment might affect health and safety and not productivity.

The quality of maintenance is a major contributory factor affecting safety and operational costs. It affects the operating life of plant and also the risk of accidents during or following maintenance.

The possibility of a fatal accident is present in many maintenance activities across all industries and continues to account for a large number of avoidable workplace deaths each year. In addition to the increased risk of accidents (during or following maintenance) equipment reliability and production can be reduced if maintenance work is not of a good standard.

Poor maintenance performance can have a direct effect on the health and safety of staff and contractors who carry out maintenance. Where maintenance is carried out in an area accessible to others, there is also a direct risk to the health and safety of non-maintenance staff.

Poor maintenance performance that leads to premature equipment failures can also have significant commercial consequences, even if no accident occurs, including:

  • lost production
  • repeated maintenance work
  • additional maintenance work to correct subsequent damage to plant and equipment
  • damaged customer perception of service quality.
