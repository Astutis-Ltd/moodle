Ductile failure and brittle fracture

Deformation (and ultimate failure) of a material depends on many variables such as stress state, rate of loading, ambient temperature and molecular structure.

Ductile and brittle are therefore relative terms, with no clear universal distinction between the two. Under the action of tensile stresses, most metallic materials are ductile in nature. However, a decrease in temperature, an increase in the rate of loading, and the presence of complex state of stress (for example, due to a notch) will increase the likelihood of a brittle failure.

Ductile failure

Ductile materials that are subjected to a tensile or shear stress will elastically or plastically strain to accommodate the load and absorb the energy. This is followed by ductile fracture, which occurs when the deformation processes can no longer sustain the applied load.

  • Single act of overstressing / above SWL. Ductile fractures are normally caused by simple overloads or by applying too high a stress to the material
  • Purity of material. The level of strain required to cause fracture is determined by the purity of the material, e.g. at room temperature, pure iron can undergo deformation up to 100% strain before breaking, while cast iron or high-carbon steels can barely sustain 3% of strain
  • Cold work hardening is the strengthening of a metal by plastic deformation. This strengthening occurs because of dislocation movements and dislocation generation within the crystal structure of the material. Cold work hardening reduces the ductility of a metal
  • Plasticity of material. Plastic materials distort easily with quite small stresses but do not easily fracture
  • High temperatures cause materials to become more ductile. High temperature is the most significant factor in causing otherwise brittle metals to become more ductile.
