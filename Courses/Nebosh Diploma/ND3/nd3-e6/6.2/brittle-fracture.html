Brittle fracture

Brittle fracture occurs when mechanical loads exceed a materialâ€™s ultimate tensile strength, causing it to fracture into two or more parts without undergoing any significant plastic deformation or strain failure.

Material characteristics and defects (such as notches, voids, inclusions, cracks) and residual stresses, are the typical initiation points for the formation of a crack, leading to brittle fracture.

Once the crack is initiated the material will undergo catastrophic failure fairly quickly under a sustained load. There is little energy absorbed (compared to ductile fracture) during the brittle fracture process. This failure mode commonly occurs in brittle materials, such as ceramics and hard metals.

Some materials, such as cast iron, glass and ceramics, are naturally brittle and prone to brittle failure.

Inherently brittle materials are all intolerant to processing particularly if it involves changes of phase (such as solid to liquid) as their molecular structure cannot tolerate dramatic differences in volume or the mechanical stresses arising from thermal gradients.

Case hardening is a simple method of hardening steel which involves heating the steel and plunging it into case hardening compound (high carbon content). The steel is then reheated until red and plunged into a water bath for rapid cooling. The case hardened material has a hard external surface and a softer, more flexible interior. The case hardened external surfaces are more prone to brittle fracture.

The presence of very low temperatures, impact loading and residual tensile stresses (within structural steelwork, for example) can increase the likelihood of brittle failure.

Brittle failures look very different to ductile failures:

  • The exposed surface will be clean and bright, due to the sudden nature of the failure
  • The cross section of the material will not have reduced
  • Sometimes there are characteristic chevron markings across the fractured surface.
