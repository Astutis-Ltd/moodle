Acoustic methods

Acoustic methods (also known as sonic testing, vibration testing or mechanical impedance testing) depend on exciting vibrations in a specimen by a local impact and then measuring some property of the vibrations such as resonant frequency or decay time. The most common acoustic method is the modern equivalent of the railway wheel tapper, using a controlled- energy impact instead of a hammer, and a transducer detector instead of ears. Electronic or digital data analysis and recording may also be used.

Resonant inspection analyses the natural whole-body resonant frequencies of a component, which are determined by the dimensions and stiffness of the component. Changes in either will affect the resonances and should therefore be detectable.

Resonant inspection allows an entire component to be inspected in one test which will identify defects, material properties, dimensions, shape and coatings.

Acoustic ranging directs a burst of acoustic energy towards an acoustically reflective target object. The time it takes for an echo to return to the ranging apparatus is measured to determine the distance between the ranging apparatus and the object.

Acoustic Emission Testing (AET)

Acoustic Emission Testing (AET) is a very versatile, non-invasive way to gather information about a material or structure. It has many industrial applications such as, assessing structural integrity, detecting flaws, testing for leaks, or monitoring weld quality. It is used extensively as a research tool.

The technique detects the transient elastic waves produced by a sudden redistribution of stress in a material arising when a structure is subjected to an external stimulus such as a change in pressure, load, or temperature. The stress waves propagate to the surface of the materials where they are recorded by sensors.

AET is unlike most other NDT techniques in three main ways:

  • The signal originates within the material. Instead of supplying energy to the object under examination, AET simply listens for the energy released by the object
  • AET deals with dynamic processes, or changes, in a material and therefore only active features (e.g. crack growth) are highlighted
  • AET can only qualitatively gauge how much damage is contained in a structure. To obtain quantitative results about size, depth, and overall acceptability of a part, other NDT methods (often ultrasonic testing) must be used.
