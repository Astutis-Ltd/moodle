Radiography

Radiography uses X-rays or gamma-rays to produce a usually life size image of an object on film.

X-rays and gamma-rays are very short wavelength electromagnetic radiation, which can pass through solid material, being partly absorbed during transmission.

The source of radiation is either an X-ray tube or a pellet of radioactive material emitting gamma-radiation (usually cobalt-60 or iridium-192).

Gamma-ray sources are much less expensive and much more portable than X-ray machinery, however exposure times are generally much longer than with X-rays.

The radioactive source is placed on one side of a specimen and a photographic film on the other side, an image is obtained on the film of the thickness variations in the specimen, whether these are surface or internal.

Radiographic techniques are highly specialised and pose a range of challenges:

  • The equipment must be used either inside a protective enclosure, or with appropriate barriers and warning signals, to ensure that there is no radiation hazard to personnel
  • The radiographic film has to be photographically processed (developed, washed, fixed and dried) after exposure
  • The images must be visually interpreted by a highly skilled technician.
