Advantages and disadvantages of different NDT techniques

Technique Main uses Advantages Disadvantages
Dye penetrant

Used to inspect large areas very efficiently.

Works on most nonporous materials.

Locates cracks, porosity, and other surface breaking defects that have enough volume to trap and hold the penetrant material.

Low cost.

Indications produced directly on surface of material.

Covers large surface area and complex geometry.

Only detects surface breaking defects.

Requires smooth non-porous surface.

Requires surface preparation.

Cleaning residue from inspected materials.

Chemical storage and handling.

Acoustic emissions testing

Inspection and monitoring of pipelines, pressure vessels, storage tanks, bridges, aircraft, and cherry pickers.

Testing of composite and ceramic components.

Process control applications such as monitoring welding processes.

Immediate indication of the strength or risk of failure of a component.

Can be used for complete volumetric inspection using multiple sensors.

Permanent sensors can monitor the ongoing condition of the item.

Disassembly and cleaning is not required in the assessment process.

Flaws may go totally undetected if the loading is not high enough to cause an acoustic event.

Signal discrimination and noise reduction may be necessary for effective application in noisy environments other NDT methods (often ultrasonic) must be used to obtain quantitative results about size and depth of flaws.

Ultrasonic

Locates surface and subsurface defects in many materials including metals, plastics, and wood.

Measures the thickness of materials.

Characterizes material properties.

Superior depthpenetration to other methods.

Only requires single sided access.

Provides distance information.

Requires minimum part preparation.

Surface must be accessible to probe and couplant.

Highly skilled operator required.

Surface finish and roughness can interfere with inspection.

Thin parts may be difficult to inspect.

Can miss linear defects oriented parallel to the beam.

Reference standards are often needed.
Eddy currents

Used to detect surface and near-surface flaws in conductive materials, such as metals.

Sorts materials based on electrical conductivity and magnetic permeability.

Measures the thickness of thin sheets of metal and nonconductive coatings such as paints.

Detects surface and near surface defects.

Test probe does not need to contact the part.

Minimum part preparation is required.

Only works on conductive materials.

Ferromagnetic materials require special treatment to address magnetic permeability.

Depth of penetration is limited.

Flaws that lie parallel to the inspection probe coil winding direction can be missed.

Requires highly skilled operator.

Surface finish and roughness may interfere.

Reference standards are needed for setup.
Magnetic particle inspection

Magnetic particle inspection can detect surface and near surface defects in ferromagnetic materials.

Relatively low equipment costs.

Rapid inspection of large surface areas of complex parts.

Can detect surface and subsurface flaws.

Surface preparation is less critical than for dye penetrant inspection.

Indications are produced directly on the surface of the part and form an image of the discontinuity.

Only works on ferromagnetic materials.

Proper alignment of magnetic field and defect is critical.

Large currents are needed for very large parts.

Requires relatively smooth surface.

Paint or other nonmagnetic coverings adversely affect sensitivity.

Demagnetization and post cleaning is usually required.
