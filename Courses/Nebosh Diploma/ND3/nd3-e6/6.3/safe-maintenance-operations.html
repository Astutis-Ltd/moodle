Safe maintenance operations

In addition to the requirement to maintain equipment in safe working order there is a duty (Regulation 22 of PUWER) to ensure that maintenance operations can be carried out (so far as is reasonably practicable) without risk to health and safety.

The need for safe maintenance should have been addressed by the manufacturer at the design stage. Ideally access for maintenance activities should be achievable without exposure to risk, for example, safe positioning of lubrication points that can be accessed safely even while the machine is in motion, or adjustment points positioned so that they can be used without opening guards.

Otherwise the installation should be designed so that the work can, so far as is reasonably practicable, be carried out with the equipment stopped or inactive.

If equipment has to run during a maintenance operation and this presents risks, appropriate controls should be introduced to reduce the risk, for example:

  • provision of temporary guards
  • limited movement controls
  • crawl speed operated by hold-to-run controls.

The following steps should generally be considered to ensure that the risks arising from maintenance activities are well managed:

  • Staff undertaking maintenance activities should be competent to undertake point of work risk assessments including dynamic assessments should the nature of the work (and risk) evolve in unexpected ways
  • Manufacturerâ€™s instructions should always be followed, unless there are good reasons otherwise
  • Work equipment may need to be constructed or adapted to prevent exposure to risk or to protect against the consequences e.g. lubrication and adjustment points can be repositioned to be accessible at ground level thus avoiding the need to work at height
  • Equipment should normally be shut down and any residual / stored energy safely released (e.g. pneumatic pressure dumped, parts with gravitational / rotational energy stopped or brought to a safe position) before maintenance work begins
  • For high-risk equipment procedures for isolation (i.e. disconnecting the equipment from the energy source) and locking off (i.e. means to prevent inadvertent reconnection) may be required
  • A permit-to-work may be used to ensure risks are properly understood and addressed and that any involved parties are clear on their role and responsibilities
  • Where significant hazards cannot be avoided or engineered out appropriate measures should be taken to protect people and minimise the risk, including:
  • Physical measures such as temporary guarding, slow speed hold-to-run control devices, safe means of access (e.g. hand holds and non-slip surfaces) and appropriate PPE etc.
  • management measures such as safe systems of work, supervision and monitoring of personnel competence (training, skill, awareness and knowledge of risk).
