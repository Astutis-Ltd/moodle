Isolations

As has been previously mentioned, maintenance work should, so far as is reasonably practicable, be carried out with the equipment stopped or inactive.

Careful consideration must be given to:

  • effectively isolating external energy sources and safely dissipating any residual energy
  • dissipating any residual energy stored, or material in the machine or equipment
  • ensuring that the machinery cannot be re-energised (intentionally or accidentally) while the maintenance works are underway.

Isolation (and energy dissipation) of external energy sources may require management of the following:

  • Mechanical power transmission: by isolating clutches, by removal of drive belts or chains, or shaft sections. Scotching may also be used
  • Electrical power: by isolating switches by removal of fuses, or by removal of plugs from sockets. Earthing or short-circuiting may also be needed
  • Hydraulic or pneumatic power: by isolating valves, by electrical isolation of hydraulic pumps, or by disconnection from pneumatic mains. Open venting to atmosphere may also be required
  • Services: isolation of water, steam, gas or fuel supplies. Draining or venting may also be needed
  • Process and material supplies: isolation of process lines and line blinding or blanking off. Draining or venting may also be needed.

The following sources of residual energy storage or material internal to the machine or equipment may also need to be dealt with, as follows.

  • Mechanical power: allow flywheels or high speed rotating parts, such as centrifuge bowls, to run down, and to minimize the potential energy of other parts
  • Electrical power: discharge of capacitors, disconnection of stand-by batteries, or earthing and short-circuiting of high voltage circuits
  • Hydraulic power: discharge of accumulators, or relaxation of pressurized pipe-work
  • Pneumatic power: discharge of air pressure throughout the system (except where used for hold-up. Note: where hydraulic or pneumatic hold-up devices have been provided for mechanical parts, those selected should be of high reliability
  • Services: residual steam, gas or fuel may need to be vented, purged or drained
  • Process and material supplies: emptying, venting, purging, draining and / or cleaning may be required before entry.

After external and internal isolation and dissipation of sources of possible hazards, a check should be made to ensure that no other hazard remains.

Where controls are remote from the plant or machinery, the operator may be unable to see persons, such as maintenance personnel, who may be within the danger zone(s). In these circumstances, designers and suppliers should have ensured that the relevant controls and facilities are lockable, or such that they can only be operated by the use of keys or tools.

Multi-pad lockable hasps or a multiple key exchange arrangement should be provided where more than one person is at risk. Each person should apply an individual padlock or key to each relevant control, or means of isolation and energy dissipation.
