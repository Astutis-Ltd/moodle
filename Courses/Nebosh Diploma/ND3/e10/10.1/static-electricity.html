Static electricity

Static electricity refers to the build-up of electric charge on the surface of objects. Static electricity is usually created when two different (dissimilar) materials are pulled apart or rubbed together, causing positive (+) charges to collect on one material and negative (−) charges on the other surface.

Considerable electrification occurs when separating two good insulators, or a good conductor and a good insulator, such as nylon carpets and shoe soles.

The unit of electrical charge is the coulomb.

A current of one ampere is a flow of one coulomb per second.

If these charges do not have a path to the ground, they are unable to move, i.e. are ‘static’. The generation of static charge is influenced by the following:

  • Speed of separation: the faster the surfaces separate, the faster the charge builds up
  • Surface area: the more surface area in contact, the greater the transfer of electrons and hence the larger the charge
  • The dielectric properties of the material: the better the insulating properties, the more charge it will accumulate
  • Good conductors such as metals tend to dissipate the charge immediately
  • Poor conductors / good insulators (paper, plastics and synthetic fabrics) will hold the charge for long periods.

In industry the following activities are likely to create static electricity:

  • liquid flows through a pipe or hose, or through an opening in a pipe or hose
  • spraying or coating
  • blending or mixing
  • filling tanks, drums, or buckets
  • passing dry powdered material through chutes or pneumatic conveyors
  • non-conductive conveyor belts or drive belts and moving appliances that are plugged into electrical outlets.

The main hazard of static electricity is the creation of spark that ignites an explosive or flammable atmosphere. The following four conditions would have to be met for this to be a concern:

  1. There must be a means for a static charge to develop
  2. Enough energy must build up to cause ignition
  3. There must be a discharge of this energy (a spark)
  4. The spark must occur in an ignitable vapour or dust mixture.

The most desirable method of control, which is the prevention of static generation, is rarely achievable in practice. Control strategies therefore focus on preventing a charge from building up to a hazardous level, by providing a means by which separated negative and positive charges may re-combine or flow harmlessly to earth, before sparking potentials are reached.

Control strategies include:

  • Equipotential bonding and earthing of metal components is generally the single most effective means of control. Equipotential bonding is the electrical interconnection of all exposed and extraneous conductors, which may become electrically charged, in such a way that dangerous voltages between any of the conductors, which may be simultaneously touched, are limited. Earthing refers to the connecting of one or more objects to the earth (zero potential) by a conducting strip. The integrity of bonding and earthing connections should be tested periodically, and particularly after equipment has been maintained or painted
  • Artificial humidification of the atmosphere is useful in certain instances as a backup to other control measures. A permanent relative humidity of > 65% produces a very thin film of moisture on exposed surfaces, which provides a conductive path for the dissipation of accumulated charge. (Note: humidification is ineffective with petroleum products as the necessary conducting film cannot build up)
  • Antistatic additives can be used to increase the conductivity of oils and related products
  • Ionisation of the air can increase the conductivity of the air, which can then provide a path to earth for static charges on equipment. This can be achieved in various ways including electrical static eliminators and radioactive sources
  • Conductive flooring and footwear should be provided wherever an easily ignitable atmosphere may exist. This is especially important in the case of gases and solvent vapours, since the static charge that can accumulate on the human body can be up to 25mJ, which is often sufficient to provide the ignition spark.
