Emergency response and first aid

In any accident involving electricity the first priority is to isolate the power supply. If this is not possible the contact between the victim and the conductor must be broken. This must never be attempted with bare hands. The rescuer should wear rubber gloves or use an insulator, such as a long handled wooden broom, and stand on an insulating material, such as rubber matting or a timber pallet.

Once the victim has been safely isolated from the supply help should be summoned (first aider and emergency medical response), and first aid should be administered. First aid would include:

  • Cardio-Pulmonary Resuscitation (CPR)
  • treatment of burns and other injuries
  • remaining with the casualty until medical help arrives.

The Distribution Network Operators (DNOs) are contractually obliged to provide good customer service and reduce the number of customer interruptions. This has led to an increase in live working.

As with all live work this needs to be justified to comply with Regulation 14 of the Electricity at Work Regulations 1989.

Live working is carried out on a variety of tasks including: tension insulator replacement, overhead conductor repairs, binder replacement, pole and cross arm changes and jumper connections.

Hot glove working involves staff working within safety distance zones and actually handling live conductors. This enables the work to be done without taking customers off electricity supply, significantly reducing power outage periods relative to traditional shutdowns.

Hot stick work involves the use of Fiberglass Reinforced Plastic (FRP) constructions, used (depending on the end attachment) as clamp sticks and switch sticks. Work is often characterised by the length of the stick. Short stick work involves the technician working by positioning himself on pole-climbers, part way up a pole or, more commonly, from an insulated bucket. Long stick techniques involve working from ground level.
