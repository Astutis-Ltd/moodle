Control measures

Suitability of equipment

As discussed in Element 5, Regulation 4 (1) of the Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998 (PUWER) requires every employer (and self-employed person) to ensure that work equipment provided for use at work is so constructed or adapted as to be suitable for the purpose for which it is used or provided.

The suitability of work equipment is considered from three aspects:

  1. its initial integrity
  2. the place where it will be used
  3. the purpose for which it will be used.

As an example, electrical equipment is not suitable for use in wet or flammable atmospheres, unless it is specifically designed for the purpose.

The equipment selected should be physically capable of doing the job, and designed and constructed so that mechanical and electrical stresses do not cause the equipment to become unsafe.

In a damp environment, battery or air powered equipment may be more suitable. Alternatively, reduced voltage equipment supplied by a transformer with 110V output, that is Centre Tapped to Earth (CTE), as typically used in the construction industry, might be more appropriate.

In environments that present an explosive atmosphere (for example, flammable aerosols, vapours, gases or dusts) suitably rated equipment should be chosen (Element 4).

In a conductive environment with restricted movement (such as inside a metal tank) additional precautions are necessary.

Protective systems

Precautions are required to prevent danger when a conductor (other than a circuit conductor) becomes charged as a result of the use of a system, or a system fault.

Depending on the circumstances this may involve introducing precautions to:

  • ensure that such conductors do not become charged
  • ensure that if a conductor does become charged, the values of voltage and current and the duration are such that danger will not arise.

A range of techniques may be used individually or in combinations, including:

  • earthing
  • fuses, Mini Circuit Breakers (MCB) and Residual Current Devices (RCD)
  • double insulation
  • use of safe voltages
  • isolated systems
  • earth-free zones.

These techniques are described in more detail over the next few pages.

Earthing

Earthing the exposed metal parts of an appliance (which should not normally carry a current) provides a low resistance path to earth for any fault current. An electric current will always try to find the path of least resistance to earth (zero potential), and the earth wire is designed to provide a more efficient route than a human being.

Many 230 / 400V power installations are designed so that an earth fault triggers the automatic interruption of the supply by fuses, or automatic circuit breakers. Such installations will require regular testing to ensure that the protective devices will work in the event of an earth fault.
