Protecting against electric shock in the event of a system fault

Various regulations are relevant in this regard, including:

  • Regulation 4: Systems, work activities and protective equipment
  • Regulation 8: Earthing or other suitable precautions
  • Regulation 9: Integrity of referenced conductors
  • Regulation 11: Means for protecting from excess of current
  • Regulation 13: Precautions for work on equipment made dead.

There are three main approaches which can be taken to reduce the risk if a fault occurs in a system, and there are often several techniques that can be applied to achieve the general aim, as shown in the table below. Combinations of measures and techniques may be applied to reduce the risk of shock.

Measure Technique
The system is designed and constructed such that uninsulated conductors cannot become live.
  • Double insulation.
If the conductors do become ‘live’, the potential difference involved, and the size and duration of the resulting electric current are such that no danger will arise.
  • Earthing
  • Equipotential bonding
  • Use of safe voltages
  • Current limitation
  • Connection to a common voltage reference point on the system.
If the conductors do become charged their environment is such that danger will not arise.
  • Separated or isolated systems
  • Earth-free, non-conducting environments.

Means for protecting from excess of current

Efficient means, suitably located, shall be provided for protecting from excess of current every part of a system as may be necessary to prevent danger.

Faults and overloads can occur on electrical systems. It is sensible therefore to protect systems and parts of systems against the effects of short circuits and overloads, if these would result in currents which would otherwise result in danger. This protection is most likely to be in the form of fuses or circuit breakers.

Means for cutting off the supply and for isolation

Where necessary to prevent danger, suitable means (including, where appropriate, methods of identifying circuits) must be provided for:

  • cutting off the supply of electrical energy to any electrical equipment
  • the isolation of any electrical equipment.

Isolation means the disconnection and separation of the electrical equipment from every source of electrical energy, in such a way that this disconnection and separation is secure.

The requirement does not apply to electrical equipment which is itself a source of electrical energy, but in such a case, as is necessary, precautions shall be taken to prevent, so far as is reasonably practicable, danger.

Working space, access and lighting

Adequate working space, adequate means of access, and adequate lighting must be provided at all electrical equipment on which (or near which) work is being done, in circumstances which may give rise to danger, for the purposes of preventing injury.

The working space dimensions should be adequate to allow people to move away from exposed live conductors without hazard, and to pass one another with ease and without hazard.

Natural light is preferable to artificial light, but where artificial light is necessary it is preferable that this be from a permanent and properly designed installation. Whatever level of lighting is used, it must be adequate to enable injury to be prevented.
