Properties of solids, liquids and gases

Most substances can exist in all three states of matter, i.e. as a solid, a liquid, or a gas. Whether a substance is solid, liquid or gas depends on its temperature and the pressure placed on it.

At room temperature (21°C) and atmospheric pressure water is a liquid. If the temperature is lowered to 0°C it begins to freeze and at -10°C is solid ice. If the temperature increases to 100°C the water boils and becomes steam. Chemically it is still water but it is in its gaseous state.

As has been explained, combustion always occurs in the vapour phase, liquids are vapourised, and solids are decomposed into vapour prior to combustion.

Solids

The ease of ignition of solid combustibles is largely determined by the surface area available for the mixing of flammable vapour and air. A solid block of wood has much less surface area than the equivalent mass of shavings and is much less flammable. Fine sawdust has a much greater surface area available than the shavings and is highly flammable (i.e. explosive when raised as a dust cloud).

The more finely divided the material, the more easily each particle is raised to its ignition temperature at which it gives off sufficient vapour to burn.

With bulk solid fuels, such as paper or timber, fire growth is relatively slow as the flame point has to heat up adjacent material to its ignition temperature before it will burn.

Liquids

Volatile liquids in open containers will constantly give off vapours. The flammability of liquids is determined by its flash point (the lowest temperature at which vapours are given off in sufficient quantities to flash momentarily when a flame is applied). Flammability categories under the EU Regulation on the Classification, Labelling and Packaging of Substances and Mixtures (CLP) are shown in the table below.

Highly Flammable Liquids (HFLs) have a flash point within the range of normal ambient temperatures and give off sufficient vapours to ignite at all times. HFLs must be correctly stored in closed containers with adequate ventilation to remove and dilute vapours.

Flashpoint – the lowest temperature at which a substance vaporises sufficiently to form an ignitable mixture in air. Flashes when an ignition source is introduced but stops burning when the ignition source is removed.

Liquids with higher flashpoints, which would not normally be regarded as a fire hazard, may present a significant risk when atomised into a spray or mist due to the increased surface area, as with solid dusts. Examples include diesel and mineral oils.

Gases and vapours

The ignition of gases and vapours is likely to be rapid and possibly explosive. However mixtures of flammable gas or vapour with air will only burn in mixtures that are between the lower and upper limits of flammability.

Vapour density (i.e. whether vapours evolved are lighter or heavier than air) of a gas or vapour is useful in predicting fire behaviour. Most flammable liquid vapours (such as petrol and LPGs) are heavier than air and will flow at ground level, collecting in drains, pits and basements, whereas some gases (such as hydrogen) are much lighter than air and will accumulate in the highest parts of a room or enclosure.
