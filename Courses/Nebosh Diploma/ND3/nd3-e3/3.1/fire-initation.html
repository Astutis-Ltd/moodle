Fire initiation

Fire comes from a chemical reaction between oxygen (usually from the air) and a fuel (for example, wood or petrol).

The combustion process is a gaseous, exothermic oxidation reaction (i.e. it occurs in the gaseous phase, gives out energy as heat and light, and chemically combines the fuel with oxygen to create products of combustion).

An example of a complete combustion reaction is as follows:

CH 4

+

One molecule of methane

2O 2

>

Two molecules of oxygen

CO 2

+

One molecule of carbon dioxide

2H 2O

+

Two molecules of water

Energy

890 J as heat and light

In reality complete combustion is almost impossible to achieve. A range of by-products such as carbon monoxide and pure carbon (soot) are formed as the reactions come to equilibrium. When combustion occurs in atmospheric air (78% nitrogen) various oxides of nitrogen (NOx) will also be formed.

For combustion reaction to occur, the fuel must be heated to its ignition temperature.

Once the combustion process is underway it is self-perpetuating. The heat of the flame itself keeps the fuel at the ignition temperature, so it continues to burn as long as there is fuel and oxygen around it.

For a fire to start, the three components of fuel, oxygen, and heat (ignition source) must be present. To put out a fire one of the three components must be removed. This is often presented as a ‘fire triangle’.
