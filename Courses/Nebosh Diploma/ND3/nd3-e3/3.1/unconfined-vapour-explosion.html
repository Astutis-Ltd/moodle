Unconfined Vapour Cloud Explosion (UVCE)

Unconfined Vapour Cloud Explosions (UVCEs) are considered to be the most destructive explosions in the chemical process industries. Prugh’s survey of 29 UVCEs in the USA in the 1970s and 80s identified the property damage costs as between $5 million and $100 million per event, and the total loss of life at 140 (average 13 per year).

UVCEs occur in the following sequence:

  • A large quantity of flammable vapour is suddenly released, usually as a consequence of a vessel rupture. (This is likely to be a minimum of 5 tonnes for a flammable mix to occur in the open air)
  • The vapour disperses around the site and mixes with air
  • The vapour cloud is ignited.

Quantitative studies have shown that:

  • The risk of ignition increases as the vapour cloud size increases
  • A fire is a more likely outcome than an explosion
  • The explosion efficiency is typically small (2% of combustion energy converted into blast wave)
  • Turbulent mixing of fuel and air, and ignition distant from the release increases the impact of the explosion.

There are two known mechanisms for generating an explosion in a relatively unconfined vapour cloud:

  • Deflagration,where the flame accelerates to high speed, which requires a mechanism for generating the flame acceleration (turbulence)
  • Detonation which, if sustained, can be much more damaging. Detonation may arise from the coalescence of a strong shock wave and a fast-moving chemically reacting front, which together can propagate faster than the speed of sound and produce over-pressures at the wave front in excess of 10 bar.

Example

Buncefield, 2005

It is estimated that a pancake shaped vapour cloud covering an area of around 120 000 m2 with an average height of around 2 m formed (volume of 240 000 m3), and evidence suggested that the ignition source was in the emergency pump house.

High levels of overpressure (>200 kPa) were seen in all areas within the cloud, irrespective of the nature of the terrain and amount of congestion. The maximum overpressure was probably much higher.

However, the overpressure diminished rapidly with distance away from the edge of the cloud and evidence suggests overpressures in the region of 5-10 kPa within 150 m. Another distinctive feature was the direction of net impulse (directional damage) which, within the cloud, acted in the direction opposite to the direction of the explosion propagation, whereas outside the cloud, acted in the direction of the explosion propagation.

The deflagration and detonation explosion mechanisms were assessed for their consistency with the observed explosion characteristics.

Deflagration was found to be inconsistent with the significant near-field damage.

Detailed modelling of the area surrounding the emergency pump house supported the proposition that the trees and undergrowth along Three Cherry Trees Lane caused flame acceleration to a velocity of several hundred m/s, a speed at which a transition to detonation was considered possible.

Detonating vapour clouds are known to generate high overpressures, and modelling confirmed the direction of the net impulse both within and outside the cloud, and the rapid decline in overpressure with distance from the edge of the cloud as consistent with a detonation. (Detonating VCEs are also referred to as percussive VCEs).

Example

Flixborough, 1974

Nypro UK Ltd operated a Nylon 66 plant at Flixborough, using conventional low temperature, liquid, catalytic oxidation of cyclohexane to cyclohexanone, which was further processed to caprolactam, the Nylon 66 monomer.

The conversion process was relatively inefficient resulting in a continuous circulation of some 200 tons of C6 through 6 cascade reactors of approximately 25 tons in a stepped sequential arrangement.

On 1 June 1974 the plant loop was fully charged with C6 being warmed up to process temperature (160°C) and pressure (120 psi).

The failure of the dog-leg pipe lead to two offset, opposed 28” jets of some 200 tons of 2 phase, flashing superheated cyclohexane. Modelling suggested that a highly turbulent vapour cloud with some 10-20 tons of hydrocarbons in the flammable range was released over the site, and the nearby hydrogen reformer was identified as a credible ignition source.

The explosion killed 28 people and injured 36 others on site, devastated the site and caused widespread property damage to the surrounding area.
