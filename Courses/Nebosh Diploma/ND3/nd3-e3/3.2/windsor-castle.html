Windsor Castle fire, 1992

The fire began in The Queen’s Private Chapel at 11:33 on Friday 20 November 1992, when a spotlight ignited a curtain.

The fire spread rapidly, due to lack of fire stopping in cavities and roof voids, damaging more than 100 rooms.

The restoration costs were more than £36.5 million and much effort was put into returning damaged rooms to their original condition.

Structural examinations of fire damaged oak roof timbers revealed that beneath the charring, many of the oak trusses had sufficient residual sectional properties and strength to make restoration feasible.

As oak is a relatively dense timber it chars at a slow rate. The gradual development of a surface layer of charcoal protects the central core of structurally sound timber.

The rate of combustion of timber structural elements in fire depends upon:

  • the density of the wood species or composite concerned
  • the moisture content
  • the ratio of exposed surface areas to volume.

Sharp corners, coarse surfaces and fissures (cracks or splits) enlarge the surface-to-volume ratio, and exacerbate the effects of fire.
