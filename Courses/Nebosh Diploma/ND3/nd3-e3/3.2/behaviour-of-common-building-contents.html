Behaviour of common building contents

Furniture and fittings can have a major effect on fire spread by:

  • increasing the ‘fire loading’ of a building, thus making fire damage much more severe
  • spreading toxic smoke and blocking escape routes.

In some buildings statutory controls will be in place through licensing conditions for public entertainment, etc.

The following advice is sensible for all workplaces.

Floor coverings

Some floor coverings may produce large volumes of heat and smoke when involved in fire, although flames may only spread slowly across the surface. If new floor coverings are installed, they should comply with BS 5287:1988 – Specification for assessment and labelling of textile floor coverings tested to BS 4790.

Hanging curtains, blinds, drapes, etc.

All curtains or drapes should be non-combustible, or inherently flame retardant, or flame retardant treated to meet the performance requirements of BS 5867-2:2008 – Specification for fabrics for curtains and drapes – Flammability requirements.

Decorations

Fabric, foliage or decorations should be constructed from materials which have passed the BS 5867-2 Type B test, or which have been treated to render them fire retardant to the same standard. Plastic foliage or decorations of any type should be avoided.

Upholstered furniture

Upholstered furniture should comply with the performance requirements for medium hazard in BS 7176:2007+A1:2011 – Specification for resistance to ignition of upholstered furniture for non-domestic seating by testing composites and should be resistant to the ignition source as determined by the following standards:

  • BS 5852:2006 Methods of test for assessment of the ignitability of upholstered seating by smouldering and flaming ignition sources
  • BS EN 1021-1:2006 – Furniture. Assessment of the ignitability of upholstered furniture. Ignition source smouldering cigarette
  • BS EN 1021-2:2006 – Furniture. Assessment of the ignitability of upholstered furniture. Ignition source match flame equivalent.

Upholstered furniture should comply with the performance requirements for medium hazard in British Standard 7176:1995 and should be resistant to the ignition source: smouldering cigarette of BS EN 1021-1:1994, ignition source: match flame equivalent of BS EN 1021-2:1994, and ignition source 5 in section 4 of BS 5852:1990.

Filling materials for use in furnishings should be restricted to combustion modified foam or other materials having at least the same standard of fire performance.

Furniture should be maintained free from tears, rips, etc. which would result in the filling being exposed.

In fire engineering literature the quantity of combustibles within an enclosure is termed the ‘fire load’.

Fire load density refers to the quantity of fuel per unit area. It is normally expressed in terms of MJ/m2 or kg/m2 of wood equivalent. The fire load should consist of all combustible building contents and the relevant combustible parts of the construction, including linings and finishings.

Fire load densities may be determined from a fire load classification of occupancies and / or specific fire load surveys and calculations.
