Units of measurement

Candela

The candela is the SI unit of luminous intensity. It is equal to one lumen per steradian.

Historically one candela was the amount of visible light emitted by one standard candle in a given direction.

The operational definition of a candela, as defined by the 16th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in 1979, is:

“the luminous intensity, in a given direction, of a source that emits monochromatic radiation of frequency 540×1012 hertz and that has a radiant intensity in that direction of 1⁄683 watt per steradian.”

Lumen (Im)

The Lumen is the SI unit of luminous flux, used in describing a quantity of light emitted by a source or received by a surface.

The lumen was created as the measure of total luminous power (i.e. visible light power) as defined by a set of ideal human eyes.

One lumen is defined as one candela-steradian (cd sr), or the amount of light power needed to send 1 candela of light in all directions.

Lux (lx)

The Lux (lx) is the SI unit of illuminance or luminous flux per unit area. It is a measure of the intensity, as perceived by the human eye, of light that hits or passes through a surface.

One lux is equal to one lumen per square metre.

It is analogous to the radiometric unit watts per square metre, but with the power at each wavelength weighted according to the luminosity function (a standardized model of human visual brightness perception).

Standards for workplace lighting

The Guidance to Regulation 8 of the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations 1992 cross refers to specific HSE guidance - HSG38 “Lighting at work “ and a range of publications by the Chartered Institution of Building Services Engineers (CIBSE).

Since then the Society of Light and Lighting (SLL), a specialist division of CIBSE has reviewed and updated the guidance and published a series of 21 SLL Lighting Guides, and other technical content.
