Characteristics of an HRO

Lekka’s (2011) literature review for the Health and Safety Laboratory (HSL) distilled Weick and Sutcliffe’s characteristics of an HRO together with resilience engineering principles and definitions of high risk systems into a different set of five categories as presented in the figure and discussed below. This model is specified in the learning content for the NEBOSH Diploma syllabus.

Containment of unexpected events

HRO's typically:

  • have back-up systems in place to respond in the event of failures and redundancy arrangements to cross-check important decisions
  • allow people with expertise, irrespective of rank, to make safety-critical decisions in emergencies
  • have a clear hierarchical structure and an understanding of who is responsible for what during routine operations
  • are able to oscillate between hierarchical structures in routine operations and flat structures, deferring to expertise, in emergencies
  • invest in training and technical competence
  • use well-defined procedures to cover all possible unexpected events.

Effective problem anticipation

HRO's typically:

  • engage with front line staff in order to obtain ‘the bigger picture’ of operations (i.e. are sensitive to operations)
  • are attentive to even seemingly minor or trivial signals that may indicate potential problem areas within the organisation
  • use near misses and incidents as indicators of a system’s health (preoccupation with failure)
  • systematically collect and analyse all warning signals and avoid making assumptions regarding the causes of failures
  • Use accident investigations to identify the potential systemic factors contributing to incidents (reluctance to simplify).

Just culture

HRO's typically:

  • have open reporting systems for near misses and accidents without fear of punishment
  • follow-up of accident investigation outcomes by implementing corrective actions
  • empower staff to abandon work on safety grounds
  • foster a sense of personal accountability for safety.

Learning orientation

HRO's typically:

  • provide continuous technical training
  • systematically analyse incidents to identify their root causes and accident types within the organisation
  • communicate openly the outcomes of accident investigation
  • update procedures in line with the organisational knowledge base.

Mindful leadership

HRO's typically:

  • proactively commission audits to identify problems in the system (often in response to incidents that occur in other similar industries)
  • facilitate the ‘bottom-up’ communication of bad news
  • engage with front line staff through site visits
  • invest resources in safety management
  • balance profits with safety.
