What can other organisations learn from HRO’s?


Clearly much of what has been discussed here can be seen as good health and safety management and as features of organisations with a positive health and safety culture.

However, as discussed, most of the research into HRO’s has been qualitative, typically employing a case study-based approach. This approach has provided rich descriptions and understandings of HRO processes in very specific contexts but has not provided a clear theoretical framework that would help explain why and how HROs are able to operate and sustain high levels of operational efficiency and reliability where others would fail.

The lack of an explanatory framework to show the underlying mechanisms that enable HROs to successfully maintain high levels of safety performance limits the ability to apply those mechanisms to other organisations.

HRO theory and research has also been criticised due to the small range of ‘exotic’ organisations that have been studied and because of incompatibilities with research carried out in other ‘unpredictable’ organisational contexts, for example healthcare research has shown that effective anticipation and prevention of errors in real time is not always possible.

There is a lack of empirical research to show the extent to which HRO processes are transferable to more general organisational contexts and it is therefore possible that HRO processes that are effective in some contexts may be ineffective or even detrimental in others.

Another, more practical, consideration is whether or not a cost benefit analysis would justify HRO levels of investment in engineering, management systems and human resources in lower risk, mainstream organisations.

There have been several attempts to apply HRO principles and reliability enhancing practices in healthcare settings.

Roberts et al. (2005) introduced HRO processes in a children’s medical centre which included: having several clinical staff check on a patient’s health (redundancy); provision of training for clinical staff to increase their awareness of how things could go wrong (culture of continuous learning); and enabling the best qualified staff to make decisions in in emergencies and patient care planning (deference to expertise).

In contrast Tamuz and Harrison (2006) argued that some HRO principles were ineffective or counter-productive in certain contexts, for example the double-checking of hazardous medication, a form of redundancy that is widely used in nursing. Excessive reliance on double-checking was seen to undermine safety critical tasks by breeding a culture of complacency or a sense of diffusion of responsibility.

Frankel, Leonard, and Denham (2006) discussed the importance of open communication in helping healthcare organisations become more reliable arguing that the dominant ethos / mentality in hospital operating theatres is the expectation that staff are ‘experts’ and infallible which does not foster an environment where more junior staff can openly raise concerns.

A final consideration is that reliability is not always synonymous with safety. In the original research much of the discussion of the reliability of the nuclear power station and electricity distribution network related to the reliability of power supply. Subsequent research challenged this noting the potential conflict between reliability of supply and safety, and that the one was not necessarily a good indicator of the other.
