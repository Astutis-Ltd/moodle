What is an HRO?


One of the original researchers, Karlene Roberts (1990) suggested that to identify a HRO it was necessary to ask the question:

How often could this organisation have failed with dramatic consequences? If the answer to this question is many thousands of times the organisation is highly reliable….(and)….performance reliability matches productivity as a dominant goal.


Another, La Porte (1996) suggested that HRO’s are ‘hazardous systems’ that produce ‘nearly accident-free performance’ or ‘function in a nearly error free fashion’

The problem with these definitions is in identifying the tipping points that determine whether an organisation has high, ordinary or low reliability.

The statistics offered in support of the view that the three organisations demonstrated a higher level of reliability than organisations engaged in other activities has been challenged successfully at many levels.

As a consequence of these difficulties in defining HRO’s Rochlin (1993) noted that:

There is therefore, no a priori way to evaluate… the mathematical or statistical performance of the organisation… relative to any theoretical optimal condition


Rochlin then turned his attention to defining HRO’s through the presence of key features which he determined to be:

‣
Technologically complex systems – later
referred to as ‘interactive complexity’
Interaction among system components is unpredictable and / or invisible.
‣
Highly hazardous
 Potential for catastrophic consequences.
‣
Tight coupling
 A high degree of interdependence among a system’s components including people, equipment and procedures.

This technological approach seemed to preclude many high hazard workplaces such as railways and the oil and gas sector from being considered as HRO’s as the technology and systems were not deemed to be sufficiently complex.

Weick and Sutcliffe (2001) determined that a more useful way of defining organisations was by focussing on the way the organisation goes about its business, the way it ‘thinks and acts’ thus reconceptualising HRO’s as ‘mindful organisations’. This may be seen to be useful as it moves the discussion away from outcome (How safe does an organisation have to be, to be an HRO?) to process (What does an organisation need to be doing to achieve its desired goal?).

Weick and Sutcliffe boiled down the findings from the HRO literature into the following five key characteristics, which are held to produce a collective state of ‘mindfulness’.

1)
Preoccupation with failures rather than successes
‣
Recognise that continued success breeds complacency

‣
Hunt for lapses and errors

‣
Well-developed incident reporting systems

‣
Alert to early warning signals

2)
Reluctance to simplify interpretations
‣
Simplification by discarding information regarded as inherently dangerous

‣
Simplify less and see more

‣
Have redundancy or slack in the workforce to ensure all relevant information is collected and analysed
3)
Sensitivity to operations
‣
Frontline operators strive to maintain situational awareness

‣
Frontline operators are aware of the ‘big picture’

‣
Silo thinking is designed out
4)
Commitment to resilience
‣
Not disabled by errors or crises

‣
Able to react effectively and deal with them

‣
Committed to learning from them

‣
Have back-up systems to catch and correct errors
5)
Deference to expertise
‣
Operational decision making migrates to those with expertise not with hierarchical authority

Weick’s research clearly positions a HRO not only as a mindful organisation, but also as a learning organisation, clearly overlapping with safety culture research in the process. Weick also suggested that being an HRO is not an ‘all or nothing’ concept but that the more of these characteristics an organisation embodies the closer it gets to the ideal.
