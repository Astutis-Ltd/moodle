Introduction

The term “high reliability organisation” (HRO) evolved from research countering Perrow’s work on Normal Accident Theory after the 1979 Three Mile Island nuclear plant incident which implied that accidents happen and failures are inevitable in complex high-hazard organisations.

In 1984 the Berkeley ‘High Reliability Organisations’ project began when Todd La Porte from the Political Science Department, Karlene Roberts from the Business School, and Gene Rochlin from the Energy and Resources Group at the University of California, Berkeley Campus, came together to explore their common interest in studying organisations that were effectively managing intrinsically hazardous, technically complex systems.

Each had come to the to the same perspective from their different academic disciplines that the time and effort spent on researching organisational failure was not being matched by research into organisations that were operating safely and reliably in similarly challenging circumstances.

Using existing contacts, the group was able to pull together leaders, managers and supervisors from air traffic control, a nuclear power station and a US navy nuclear aircraft carrier for a number of joint workshops to investigate commonalities of approach.

The vast majority of literature on HRO’s is derived from this original research.
