Get started: prepare


The preparation stage requires the employer to understand:

‣
Why it is important to talk to employees about health and safety.

‣
What the law requires and how it applies to their workplace.

‣
How to gain commitment from the business and the workforce.



Why it is important to talk to employees about health and safety

Accident rates are lower where employees genuinely feel they have a say in health and safety matters.

Employee involvement in health and safety management relates to a more positive health and safety climate – 77% of employees felt encouraged to raise concerns in a good health and safety climate compared to 20% who felt encouraged to do so in a poor health and safety climate.

In poor health and safety climates, accident rates are highest among workplaces where employees do not feel they can have a say.

Stronger employee involvement means better control of common workplace risks such as slips and trips – very effective in 76% of cases where employees felt they were always consulted but only very effective in 40% of cases if they thought they were rarely, or never, consulted.

Employers can learn about the risks through consultation – the risk of stress and slips and trips occur practically everywhere, but awareness of them is higher where there is employee involvement (62%) compared to where there is no involvement (28%).

Research has also shown that workplaces with health and safety committees where some members are selected by unions have significantly lower rates of work-related injury than those found in workplaces with no co-operative health and safety management.

Workplaces where employees are involved in taking decisions about health and safety are safer and healthier as employees influence health and safety through their own actions.

A workforce that feels valued and involved in decision-making plays a big part in a high-performing workplace. Empowering the workforce, giving them the right skills, and getting them involved in making decisions shows them that their health, safety and wellbeing is taken seriously. Workers are not only able to raise concerns but offer solutions too. There can be an impact far beyond health and safety management if the workforce is not engaged on health and safety issues which affect them.

Building upon this legal foundation to create a genuine partnership between employers and employees creates a culture of collaboration where concerns, ideas and solutions are shared and acted upon, and where the whole workforce is engaged in the management of health and safety.

HSE research has shown that stronger employee involvement means:

‣
better control of common workplace risks

‣
lower accident rates

‣
a more positive health and safety climate where employees feel encouraged to raise concerns.


If a recognised trade union appoints ‘safety representatives’ the employer shoul consult with them on health and safety matters affecting the employees they represent.

In a small business where trade unions are not present consulting directly with individuals is often effective in giving everyone a chance to have a say in health and safety matters.


Gaining commitment from the business and the workforce

Commitment from all sides is vital for employee involvement to be effective and successful in making improvements to health and safety. This means the employer, employees, and trade unions where they are recognised, need to gain buy-in to the process.


Commitment from the business

Leadership and commitment from management is important in making involvement successful. Beyond the legal duty to consult there are many positive reasons for management to commit to consultation with the workforce.

‣
Show how co-operating with employees in other areas of the business has led to improvements;

‣
Demonstrate evidence of the benefits to the business (discussed in the ‘why it is important’ section earlier)

‣
Look at current health and safety issues which could be addressed through consultation. For example, working together to address an increase in a particular type of injury;

‣
Explain that engaging the workforce is an investment, and it may take time to develop trust, but it has been shown to reduce workplace injuries and accidents; and

‣
Provide reassurance that it will not diminish effective management because consultation does not mean that agreement has to be reached on every issue, but that areas of disagreement are discussed and the views of others are considered and respected.



Commitment from the workforce

Employees are more likely to engage and believe in consultation when senior managers show personal and long-term commitment and listen to the views of employees because they want to hear what the workforce has to say.

Employees are more likely to communicate with their employer if:

‣
they are committed to the business goals, including the health and safety goals;

‣
they think it is in their interests to participate;

‣
they trust management and find them approachable; and

‣
management actions match their words.
