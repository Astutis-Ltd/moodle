The principles and application of root cause analysis tools

5-Whys

The 5 Whys was developed in the 1930’s by Sakichi Toyoda, a Japanese inventor and industrialist, and the founder of Toyota Industries. The technique became an integral part of the Lean philosophy in the 1970’s and is still a vital part of the Toyota production system.

Toyota has a “go and see” philosophy, making decisions based on what is actually happening on the shop floor (work as done), rather than on what a senior manager thinks might be happening (work as intended).

A 5-why investigation should include people with practical experience as they can provide valuable information regarding any problem that appears in their area of expertise.

5 Whys is used for troubleshooting, quality improvement, and problem solving, and is equally effective in incident investigation. It is most effective when used to resolve reasonably straightforward problems.

The method is remarkably simple: when a problem occurs, you drill down to its root cause by asking “Why?” five times. When the root cause is identified and a countermeasure becomes apparent, it is implemented to prevent the issue from recurring.

The term ‘counter-measure’ is used to mean an action or set of actions that addresses the root cause and seeks to prevent the problem from arising again. A ‘solution’ may be more superficial, merely addressing the symptoms of the problem.

The ‘5’ is just a ‘rule of thumb’ It does not matter if it takes 3 why’s or 10, to get to the root cause, the important thing is to get to the root of the problem and to specify suitable countermeasures.

Fishbone diagram

Professor Kaoru Ishikawa, a pioneer of quality management, developed his ‘cause and effect analysis’ technique in the 1960s.

The technique involves brainstorming around a structured mind-map template that looks like the skeleton of a fish – A ‘fishbone’ or ‘Ishikawa’ diagram, as shown below.

Although the technique was originally developed as a quality control tool it can be comfortably used as a health and safety root cause analysis tool.

This version has six main ribs to structure the investigation around six key variables of: materials, equipment, environment, people (MEEP), processes and management. Other variables can be used.

The bow-tie model

The exact origin of the ‘bow tie’ model is unclear, but it was adopted by Royal Dutch Shell as a company standard in the early 1990s and thereafter gained widespread support throughout the oil and gas sector and other high hazard industries.

The model uses the layering of barriers (defences in depth) to control hazards as seen in James Reasons models.

The approach can be used proactively to evaluate the adequacy of risk control or reactively to understand the failures that caused an incident or compromised its mitigation.

The pictorial representation of a sites major hazards and corresponding controls is believed to help workforce understanding of hazard management and their role in it.

The centre of the bow tie represents the accident (realisation of hazard or top event) and a cause-consequence model is then developed around it. Hazard identification techniques are used to identify all the threats that can lead to the top event. Existing barriers (protective measures) intended to prevent the top event are then represented and consideration given to the need for additional barriers. This is shown on the left of the bow tie and is in essence a fault tree.

Focus then moves to the right of the bow tie where consideration is given to the escalation of consequences from the top event. For each consequence barriers (recovery measures) exist, or can be developed to detect the accident, protect people and other assets from it, or mitigate its consequences. This is an event tree.

Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)

Fault trees have been used since the early 1960s. They use deductive reasoning to work backwards in time to examine preceding events that may have contributed to an incident or system failure.

Causal factors are deductively identified, organised in a logical manner and represented pictorially in a tree diagram which depicts combinations of causal factors (equipment failures, human errors etc.) and their logical relationship to the top event.

There are two approaches to FTA:

  • The qualitative or traditional FTA is used to identify potential causes and pathways to the top event failure. This approach is often used in nuclear industry applications and many other instances where the potential causes or faults are sought out, without interest in their likelihood of occurrence; and
  • The quantitative FTA which models an entire product, process or system allocating probabilities (determined by analysis or test) to the vast majority of the basic events, so that the probability of the top event occurring can be calculated.

FTA’s may be used in a variety of ways, notably:

  • at the design stage of a system to identify potential causes of failure and select between different design options;
  • at the operating phase to identify how major failures can occur and the relative importance of different pathways to the top event; and
  • to analyse a failure which has occurred to identify root causes and display diagrammatically how different events came together to cause the failure.

A fault tree is developed as follows:

  • the top event to be analysed is defined. This may be a failure or maybe a broader outcome of that failure;
  • the possible immediate causes or failure modes leading to the top event are identified;
  • each of these causes/fault modes is analysed to identify how their failure could be caused;
  • the analysis continues until further analysis becomes unproductive. In a hardware system this may be the component failure level. Events and causal factors at the lowest system level are termed base events; and
  • For a quantitative analysis probabilities are assigned to base events to enable the probability of the top event to be calculated. For quantification to be valid it must be able to be shown that, for each gate, all inputs are both necessary and sufficient to produce the output event.

Except for simple fault trees, a software package is needed to properly handle the calculations when repeated events are present at several places in the fault tree. Software tools help ensure consistency, correctness and verifiability.

In a completed fault tree the top event is linked to the initiating events through a series of intermediate levels where the necessary conditions for the propagation towards the top event are combined at ‘and’ or ‘or’ gates. In a large tree there will be many levels, which grow in complexity and detail as the analysis proceeds away from the top event.

The main symbols used in a fault tree are illustrated and explained below:

And gate – fault occurs if all input events true
Or gate – fault occurs if any input event true
Base event – further analysis not useful
Undeveloped events – not analysed further at this time
Event – Event which is further analysed (may be the top event or an intermediate event)
Transfer gate – Event analysed at point A on a different page

Probabilistic (or Quantitative) fault trees

Probabilities

Probability is about the chance of something happening. To understand how numbers are used to describe probabilities consider the probability scale.

The scale illustrates the chances of a particular event happening. Unlikely events are closer to 0 and likely events are closer to 1.

  • A probability of 0 means the event is impossible and will never happen;
  • A probability of 1 means that the event is certain to happen; and
  • A probability of ½ (or 0.5 or 50%) means that the event has an even chance of happening.

The probability of something which is uncertain to happen actually happening is presented as the proportion of one. This may be a fraction, a decimal or a percentage.

With a coin toss the probability of the coin landing on head or tails is 1. The probability of it landing on heads is ½ or 0.5 or 50%. The probability of it landing on tails is also ½ or 0.5 or 50%. As these are the only possible outcomes (the coin is not going to land on its edge!) the total adds up to 1.

With a dice roll the probability of the dice landing on a six (or any specific number) is 1/6 or approx. 0.167 or approx. 16.7%. The probability of it not landing on six is 5/6 or 0.833 or 83.3%. (As with the coin toss – the probabilities add up to one as these are the only possible outcomes).

There are the same 36 possible outcomes as shown in the first table. Each dice has a one in six (or 6 in 36) chance of rolling a six, but as can be seen the double six in the bottom right corner is an overlap. The probability of throwing a single six is therefore 6/36 + 6/36 – 1/36 which equals 11/36.

Note also how the probability of not throwing a six (5/6 x 5/6 = 25/36) balances the total range of probabilities to one (1) (25/36 + 11/36 = 1).

In a probabilistic fault tree the overlap can be ignored (as the probabilities of causal events will tend to be small fractions e.g. 1/100 or 1/1000 and therefore the overlap will be an incredibly small number) and the output probability can be approximated by adding the individual probabilities.

Event Tree Analysis (ETA)

An event tree is a graphical technique for representing the possible outcomes of an incident that results from a selected initiating event. The analysis considers the functioning/not functioning of the various systems designed to mitigate the consequences of the initiating event be they human or engineering.

ETA’s can be used qualitatively to determine the range of outcomes and quantitatively to determine their probabilities or frequencies. Event trees are often used with qualitative assessment of the effects in a qualitative risk assessment.

An event tree starts by selecting an initiating event. This may be an incident such as a dust explosion or a causal event such as a system failure.

The functions or systems which are in place to mitigate potential adverse outcomes are then listed in sequence.

The factors that determine the outcomes are then considered in turn. These may be:

  • safety functions built into the system;
  • alternative external conditions which may affect the outcome; or
  • different operator responses.

Each is expressed as one or two alternatives which can be represented as “yes / success” or “no / failure”. Conditional probabilities of success or failure can then be allocated to each choice. (NB conditional probabilities are not obtained from tests under normal conditions but are estimates of the probability of functioning under the adverse conditions arising from failure to contain the initiating event).

The event tree is constructed, working from left to right, starting with the initiating event and then branching as each factor is considered in turn. (See the diagram below)

Each path through the tree represents the probability that all of the events on that path will occur. The frequency of the outcome is represented by multiplying each of the individual conditional probabilities and the frequency of the initiating event.

It is common to group the end points into a crude classification to give a better overview of the total results (e.g. on-site injuries or deaths; off-site injuries or deaths etc.).

The main usage of ETA is to explore the possible history of an event showing all the potential escalating and mitigating circumstances. It gives a simple description of the possible outcomes and provides the means to estimate their frequency/probability.

ETA can be used for modelling, calculating and ranking (from a risk point of view) different accident scenarios following an initiating event.

It can be used at any stage in the life cycle of a product or process.

Qualitative analysis can help to brainstorm potential scenarios and sequences of events following an initiating event to consider how outcomes are affected by various treatments, barriers or controls intended to mitigate unwanted outcomes.

Quantitative analysis is useful in considering the acceptability of controls. It is most often used to model failures where there are multiple safeguards.

Worked example

The diagram below shows a simple probabilistic event tree illustrating consequential mitigating and aggravating events after an initiating event of an explosion on site.

Following the explosion a fire may or may not start. (Event 1)

If there is no fire there are no further negative consequences. If the fire takes hold a water sprinkler system is expected to extinguish it. (Event 2)

If the sprinkler system works the fire will be controlled, if not the fire will escalate.

There is also an automatic fire alarm which is intended to give early warning to facilitate safe evacuation. (Event 3)

The consequences are dependent upon the combinations of successes and failures of the mitigatory measures provided.

If the likelihood of the event is known and data on the reliability of the mitigatory measures is available, the tree can be quantified. Remember, where the likelihood of something happening is known the chance of it not happening is determined by balancing to one (1). Similarly when the reliability of a component is known (chance of success) the failure rate is again determined by balancing to one.

The probability of any particular outcome can be calculated by multiplying the given probabilities.

In this instance, the probability of the fire being controlled, and the alarm being raised is:

0.01 x 0.8 x 0.95 x 0.01 = 0.00075
