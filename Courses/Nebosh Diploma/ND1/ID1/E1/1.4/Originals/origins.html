Origins and meaning of ‘self-regulation’


‘Self-regulation’ refers to the principle that an organisation is able to set its own internal standards, rather than having standards imposed upon it from another, usually governmental, body. As already discussed, many jurisdictions have legislation in place that sets out the minimum standards of health and safety to be achieved. It might be argued that in an ideal world this would be unnecessary, since organisations would recognise the intrinsic value of managing health and safety without the need to be told by government to do so – in other words, that a self-regulatory approach is more effective than regulation by or from central government.

To some extent, the ‘goal-setting’ system of health and safety legislation outlined in this chapter allows a degree of self-regulation, insofar as organisations have the flexibility to decide exactly what measures are appropriate for control of risk in their particular circumstances. However, this is not pure self-regulation, since the organisation will still have to conform to minimum standards that are set by government.

The following discussion uses the UK as its foundation, but similar arguments apply equally in other jurisdictions.

In the UK, the Robens Report (which led to the creation of the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974) advocated a goal-based approach to health and safety regulation and also expressed the need for a ‘more effectively self-regulating system’.

It was hoped at the time that this would grow out of an increased atmosphere of communication and co-operation between employer and employees, capitalising on the role of the Trade Unions. In reality, a hybrid approach developed in the UK with the HSE taking the lead in the publication of guidance and advice leaving much of industry in a position of following their lead rather than taking the initiative.

The approach has generally been successful with rates of workplace fatalities, injuries and illnesses decreasing.

The debate over the merits of self-regulation (and the broader issue of deregulation) over state regulation is part of a broader political and philosophical argument about the required levels of state involvement in, and regulation of, business activities.

In 1992 John Major’s government responded to ongoing calls from business and the Conservative Party to lift the burden on business by launching a ‘deregulation initiative aimed at removing unnecessary red tape from business.’

Lord Sainsbury was appointed to head up a central Deregulation Task Force overseeing eight Business Task Forces as part of the Cabinet Office. The government position at the time was that:

A certain amount of regulation is essential, not least to ensure public safety and promote quality standards, but these benefits have to be weighed against the cost of enforcing regulation and complying with it.


The New Labour administration set up the Better Regulation Task Force in 1997 ‘to advise the Government on action to ensure that regulation and its enforcement are proportionate, accountable, consistent, transparent and targeted’ (The Task Force became the Better Regulation Commission on 1 January 2006).

In October 1999, the task force published its interim report defining self-regulation as:

...the means by which members of a profession, trade or commercial activity are bound by a mutually agreed set of rules which govern their relationship with the citizen, client or customer.


It was argued that self-regulation carries numerous advantages including:

‣
being more flexible

‣
more readily complied with

‣
more relevant to the needs of a particular industry

‣
ensures confidence in an entire industry

‣
more cost-effective

‣
can lead to a better relationship between industry and clients.


The Lofstedt Review (2011) is the latest UK Government-commissioned review of health and safety regulation. Its purpose was to:

Consider the opportunities for reducing the burden of health and safety legislation on UK businesses whilst maintaining the progress made in improving health and safety outcomes.


It is also important to note the role of ISO management standards such as ISO 45001 as a framework for transnational private (self-)regulation and that ISO standards have evolved to the common annexe SL framework which is much more goal-setting and self-regulatory in approach.
