Benefits of schemes that promote co-operation
on health and safety between companies


Not all organisations have access to directly-employed competent health and safety advice. This is especially true of small companies and ‘micro-businesses’ that may not have the resources to seek and pay for professional health and safety advice. In such cases, larger organisations may be in a position where they can share knowledge and expertise with the smaller organisations to their mutual benefit.

An example of this would be a large organisation that uses small contracting companies. When the small company tenders for a contract, it may be asked to provide information on its safety arrangements. This will be evaluated by the safety experts in the large company and the answers taken into account when awarding the contract. If the small company is considered to have unsatisfactory arrangements for the management of safety, the larger company could simply refuse to consider them any further. However, schemes such as the HSE’s Good neighbour scheme operate to encourage those organisations with good safety resources to share knowledge and best practice with those that do not.

In the above situation, the larger company could help the smaller one to develop its safety systems, perhaps by creating an action plan or even offering some free advice from its safety practitioner. Such an approach could potentially strengthen the working relationship between the two organisations, with the smaller company becoming safer and the larger one benefitting from employing a contractor with a known good standard.

Supplier auditing is another common method of ensuring that desired standards are met and of promoting co-operation on health and safety between organisations. This approach involves sending an auditor to a supplier’s premises to check on such things as quality of manufacturing processes, as well as safety management and environmental issues. Agreed standards will need to be achieved and maintained if the contract is to continue, which will have an obvious influence on the way in which the supplier manages itself.

Both of the above examples could be said to be part of an ‘Input-Process-Output’ model, where a company seeks to ensure that the inputs to its production processes, for example, are a known standard and will not ‘contaminate’ its own arrangements. Such schemes facilitate the sharing of best practice and help to improve standards of health and safety management industry-wide.
