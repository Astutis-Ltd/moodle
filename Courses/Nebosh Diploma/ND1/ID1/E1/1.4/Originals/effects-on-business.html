Effects on business of adverse stakeholder
reaction to health and safety concerns


It should go without saying that, whatever the accuracy of a report, a health and safety concern can have a detrimental effect on stakeholder perception, which can lead to reduced investment and to less take-up of the company’s product.

At the time of writing, there is a Europe-wide scandal over the presence of horsemeat in processed foods. Even though horsemeat itself is not dangerous, supermarket chains that stock processed foods have seen a marked decrease in sales. It remains to be seen what the full extent of the scandal is, but it is already apparent that the supermarket chains may be losing money because of it. The consequence of this has also been that local butchers’ shops are seeing a boom in sales.

Following a health and safety concern in a given organisation, other organisations may be reluctant to do business with it, for reasons outlined in the previous sub-section. This can lead to reduced revenues and a corresponding reduction in profits. In turn, there might be less money available to invest in health and safety initiatives, equipment and personnel, meaning that standards inevitably start to deteriorate. This ultimately leads to higher rates of accidents, incidents and ill-health, which raises further concerns about the organisation’s overall approach to safety management. In some cases, this can become a vicious circle from which it may be hard to recover.

The Perrier mineral water contamination incident of 1990 was a case in point.

In 1990, tests of this famous brand of sparkling mineral water showed the presence of benzene (a known carcinogen) in a small sample of bottles.

Perrier water was in part marketed on the basis of its purity, so the revelation that samples contained a potentially carcinogenic contaminant had a significant impact on the company’s share price as stocks were withdrawn and consumer confidence was shaken.

A company spokesman reassured the public that there was no significant health risk. This was echoed by a spokesman for the US Food and Drug Administration, who said his agency’s Hazard Evaluation Board had collected samples of Perrier and found no immediate risk to the public from the benzene in the water. He said:

At these levels there is no immediate hazard [...] The hazard would be that over many years, if you consumed about 16 fluid ounces a day, your lifetime risk of cancer might increase by one in a million, which we consider a negligible risk. You don’t have to be concerned if you just had a bottle of Perrier.


Despite these reassurances, 70 million bottles of Perrier water were withdrawn from shelves in the USA, followed by further recalls as contamination was found in Europe.

The water was withdrawn for several months until the source of the contamination could be identified and the purity of the product restored. By the time the product was reintroduced, many consumers had moved away from the brand to others that they felt were just as good.

In 1992, Perrier was bought out by the Nestlé Corporation and by 1995 its market share in North America had fallen to half the pre-incident level.
