The use of non-conformity to an accredited
health and safety standard as a form of
enforcement


From your International General Certificate or equivalent studies you are expected to be familiar with the ILO-OSH and ISO45001 standards for health and safety management systems. Both standards provide an organisation with a framework for managing health and safety risks while simultaneously offering external stakeholders a degree of confidence in an organisationâ€™s ability to manage those risks.

Certification to either of these systems demonstrates a good standard of safety management and can provide the holder with a competitive advantage. In many cases, certification is used as a baseline for the selection of business partners, so its loss clearly has the potential for a detrimental impact on an organisation.

Performance measurement plays a significant role in both of these models. A range of techniques, both active and reactive, can be used to establish the level of compliance against the standard. Where performance measurement is conducted internally, identification of non- conformities will facilitate prompt rectification, thus maintaining high levels of performance and leading to continuous improvement. In this way, the use of non-conformities helps in the overall self-regulation of safety management.

Monitoring may also be performed by external bodies as a way of enforcing the maintenance of the standard. Supplier audits, mentioned earlier in this chapter, are a good example of such an enforcement approach. Whilst there will not be any criminal proceedings, the threat of losing a contract following a supplier audit, or of suffering damage to reputation, may be sufficient to ensure that an organisation maintains a high standard of performance.

Where the audit is completed by an awarding body, the threat of losing certification altogether becomes even more serious, since it is not just one contract that may be affected.

Therefore, it may be said that the use of accredited standards are an important ingredient of a self-regulatory model and that non-conformances can be used by both the organisation and external stakeholders to monitor performance and facilitate continuous improvement.
