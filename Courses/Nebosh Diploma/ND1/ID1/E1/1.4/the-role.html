The role and function of corporate governance in a system of self-regulation

The principle of corporate governance is closely related to that of self-regulation insofar as the organisation sets itself targets for compliance with certain standards of its choosing.

Corporate governance principles can be applied to most organisations, but it is the larger ones who potentially have the most use for them and who stand to reap the greatest benefit.

Throughout the 1990s a series of reports into the management of large corporations were developed into a non-statutory code of practice that, in the UK is now administered by the Financial Reporting Council. The code is further supported and reinforced by the rules that companies who are listed on the London Stock Exchange must follow. This code of practice therefore provides a framework for the management of all risks within a business, the aim being to safeguard the company’s assets and the shareholders’ investments.

A widely-used term in corporate governance is ‘internal control’. A system of internal control requires the Board to take an active role in the management of a range of risks faced by the business.

The Board are charged with identifying the risks to the business and with considering how well the business is capable of dealing with those risks. Where doubts over the organisation’s ability to deal with certain risks are raised, the Board is charged with taking the lead in managing them.

The idea of Board-level involvement in the identification and management of business risks sits well alongside the principle that health and safety should be led by the most senior people in the organisation. Direct involvement of the Board in this way has the following benefits:

  • Health and safety issues are considered at the same level as other business risks, thus raising their profile
  • The organisation can send a clear message that it is committed to the management of health and safety
  • Stakeholders and regulators see that safety and health issues are being taken seriously
  • Senior decision makers lend authority to decisions concerning the management of health and safety issues
  • Adequate resources can be made available for health and safety
  • Health and safety issues are more likely to be considered when making general business decisions.

The process starts with the senior management team recognising that health and safety is an important issue and establishing their commitment to its management. Health and safety management systems, such as ISO 45001, which is suited to larger organisations, embody this principle.

The management team, having recognised that safety is important and having made a commitment to high standards can then be involved in setting the strategic direction for health and safety management. Since the top-level decision makers have been involved, this should mean that any safety and health management initiatives are aligned with wider business risk management issues, thus further helping to embed principles of effective safety management throughout the organisation and reducing the impression that health and safety is a secondary function to be considered only because legislation says so.

Having set standards, it will be up to the Board to ensure that adequate resources are provided and that appropriately competent persons are appointed to manage safety issues on a day-to-day basis.

Finally, the Board should periodically review progress against the targets it has set. Once again, this is fully in line with the requirements of standard safety management systems such as ISO 45001. The results of the review will allow the Board to set new targets based on current achievements, the aim being to improve continuously.

Achievement of targets and the establishment of high standards of safety and health management can be capitalised upon by reporting such successes in the company annual report, thus helping to gain competitive advantage.
