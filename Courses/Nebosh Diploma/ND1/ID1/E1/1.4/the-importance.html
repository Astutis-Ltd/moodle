The importance of the media and their role in changing attitudes to health and safety

In the modern world, more than at any time in history, there is direct access to an enormous range of information in a variety of sources. The media play a central role in producing and disseminating information around the globe, helping to inform and shape opinions worldwide.

‘The media’ is a term that is normally associated with news reporting. In the modern age, news is reported by use of conventional ‘print’ media in the form of newspapers, periodicals and magazines, and as broadcast media, the obvious examples being television and radio. The last 30 years has also seen the rapid rise of the Internet, which offers unparalleled access to information in a way not dreamt of a generation ago.

In terms of print and broadcast media, newspapers and public service broadcasters such as the BBC will publicise stories that they consider to be in the public interest. Therefore, serious and catastrophic events, usually involving loss of life and large-scale destruction of property are regularly reported. Examples include the Chernobyl and Fukushima nuclear plant disasters, offshore disasters such as Piper Alpha and the Deepwater Horizon explosion, train crashes such as Paddington or Clapham, environmental disasters such as the Exxon Valdez, and a host of other equally serious events, many of which obtain worldwide publicity.

Incidents do not always gain worldwide expose, however. Some, though serious, are not picked up by global news networks, but are reported locally in newspapers and on local radio and television stations. This local reporting can have a significant impact on a business. If an organisation has won an award for excellence in safety management, then such publicity is likely to have a positive effect. If there has been a serious incident, the opposite will be true.

The Internet has been harnessed by health and safety organisations such as the UK’s Health and Safety Executive, which offers an extensive range of guidance and information. Until very recently, information had to be purchased and sent via the post as a hard copy, which was expensive and time-consuming. Today, the same information is free to download at the press of a button. It is therefore right to say that access to health and safety information has never been greater, which should enable organisations of all sizes to obtain the information that they need in order to address safety concerns.

The HSE and OSHA websites also carry information on those organisations that have been convicted of health and safety offences. This can help other organisations to evaluate their health and safety performance. The threat of being ‘named and shamed’ in such a public manner may also give organisations food for thought when it comes to deciding whether or not to develop and implement sound systems of safety management.

One possible problem with media coverage, especially the print media, is that coverage of health and safety issues may be negative. In the UK, for example, a number of daily newspapers regularly feature stories of poor health and safety decision making, which are apt to cause resentment to the whole idea of safely managing risks and which may cause people to ignore genuine and necessary safety arrangements. Such stories embed themselves in the memories of the readership who could be forgiven for thinking that health and safety is unnecessarily bureaucratic.

Unfortunately, successes in health and safety management seldom get the same level of popular press attention, and so it is left to organisations and professional health and safety bodies, such as IOSH, to advertise successes in the media.
