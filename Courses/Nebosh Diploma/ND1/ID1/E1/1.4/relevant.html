Relevant influential parties

Employer bodies

These are bodies that work to promote the interests of employers generally, a good example of which is the Confederation of British Industry (CBI). According to the CBI website their mission is:

...to promote the conditions in which businesses of all sizes and sectors in the UK can compete and prosper for the benefit of all. To achieve this, we campaign in the UK, the EU and internationally for a competitive policy landscape.

Since health and safety issues affect all organisations to some degree, the CBI and organisations like it (such as chambers of commerce) in other countries around the world will be involved in active lobbying of Government to help to ensure that health and safety policy and legislation does not adversely affect the growth and development of those organisations that it serves.

Trade associations

Whereas organisations such as the CBI and the Federation of Small Businesses act for employers generally, trade associations are associated more closely with specific areas of economic activity. Trade associations work to promote developments and improvements in quality, health and safety and environmental management, as well as in technical standards. They do this in a number of ways, including publishing guidelines, information and briefing notes on regulatory developments, as well as codes of practice.

Safety issues of relevance to the industry or trade group represented by a trade association are circulated to members on a regular basis, thus helping to keep the membership up to date with current issues and developments. This helps to regulate health and safety performance through the development of ‘industry best practice’, which forms a useful frame of reference for member companies and which may be referred to by the enforcing authorities when conducting incident investigations.

Trade unions

Trade unions represent the interests of workers, rather than those of employers. Of central importance to the role of a trade union is employment protection, which includes health and safety issues. A trade union will lobby employers to ensure that employment rights are upheld and satisfactory standards of workplace health, safety and welfare are maintained. They will also provide support to those of their members who may wish to challenge the policies or actions of their employers.

Trade unions were very powerful in the UK around the time that the Health and Safety at Work Act was introduced, meaning that they were able to influence policy makers to include, within the Act and in subsequent Regulations, specific rights for union-appointed safety representatives.

As well as appointing safety representatives, trade unions also run training courses in health and safety and provide a great deal of relevant information to their membership. The unions remain a powerful force for workers’ rights and continually lobby Government on behalf of the labour force.

Professional groups

These are organisations that are set up to support, govern and regulate the professions. There are many examples, such as the Institute of Chemical Engineers (IChemE), the Chartered Management Institute (UK), the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society (USA), Occupational Therapy Australia, American Society of Safety Professionals (ASSP) (formerly the American Society of Safety Engineers -ASSE) , the Board of Certified Safety Professionals (BCSP) and so on.

In the context of health and safety, the major professional associations are the American Society of Safety Professionals (ASSP) and the UK-based Institution of Occupational Safety and Health (IOSH), which, with more than 47 000 members worldwide, is the biggest health and safety membership organisation in the world. IOSH is the world’s only Chartered body for health and safety professionals and is the first European safety body to be awarded NGO (non- governmental organisation) status by the ILO.

IOSH regulates its members through a code of conduct, with which all Chartered members and Fellows must adhere. They also regulate and administer the system for professional development, ensuring that current and future health and safety practitioners maintain the highest level of knowledge and competence in their chosen field. IOSH runs regular safety campaigns and lobbies Government on behalf of the profession to ensure that health and safety issues remain on the agenda.

Pressure groups

These are non-governmental groups of individuals who hold similar values, and who campaign for action to promote change on specific issues. Such groups often represent alternative viewpoints that may not be well represented in the mainstream. The formation of a pressure group therefore helps to raise awareness of issues, and can result in influence being brought to bear on political parties and governmental policy makers.

For example, a group in the UK known as ‘Families Against Corporate Killers’ (FACK) describes itself as:

‘a national campaigning network which will campaign to stop workers and others being killed in preventable incidents and will direct bereaved families to sources of legal help and emotional support’.

The general public

This is the largest group of all. Public opinion can be very influential in government decision making, but is likely to be heavily influenced by the media (see below). By themselves, individual members of the public cannot exert direct influence, but if they band together in pressure groups, or lobby their MP in sufficient numbers, then they can become a catalyst for change.

In the context of health and safety at work, public perception can have some influence. In the UK, members of the public regularly read reports of ridiculous decisions made in the name of health and safety (but not by trained safety practitioners), which create an impression that the health and safety profession as a whole ‘gets in the way’ or is unnecessary. This perception can carry over into the workplace, working against otherwise worthwhile safety arrangements. Ultimately, if such negative perceptions are held widely enough, they may even influence government to act to reduce what is felt to be an over-abundance of rules and ‘red-tape’, leading to a reduction in controls and an increase in risk.
