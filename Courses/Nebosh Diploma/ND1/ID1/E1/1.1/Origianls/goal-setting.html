‘Goal-setting’ and ‘prescriptive’ legal models


There are two alternative styles of health and safety law, known as ‘goal-setting’ and ‘prescriptive’.

‘Prescriptive’ approaches seek to set out exactly what a duty holder must do to comply with the law. Much of the health and safety legislation in the USA is considered to be prescriptive in nature. The clear and unequivocal nature of such requirements offers a higher degree of certainty than the alternative ‘goal-setting’ approach.

A weakness of the prescriptive approach lies in the fact that law may be written to encompass all manner of different types of working environment and sizes of organisation, making it virtually impossible to produce a set of rules that can be applied equally in all cases. This may mean that small businesses in particular are at a disadvantage, as they have to meet the same standards as their larger, better resourced cousins.

Goal-setting approaches, such as those used in the UK since the 1970’s and Europe generally since the late 1980’s, are risk management approaches based on the premise that those who create workplace risks are best placed to determine how to manage them.

In the UK, the law on health and safety at work has developed from the very early 19th century. Highly prescriptive laws developed along industry lines, with different laws for factories, construction, offices and shops, agriculture, offshore and so on. Over time the system of health and safety law in the UK became unwieldy and fragmented, so in the early 1970s a commission headed by Lord Robens was established to see what could be done to address the problem. The Robens Commission recognised a need for change and recommended that the old system of prescriptive, industry-centric law be replaced with a single ‘goal-setting’ Act of Parliament that set out general principles in such a way that individual duty holders were given the flexibility to tailor their safety management solution to meet the needs of their particular working environment. This recommendation led, in 1974, to the enactment of the Health and Safety at Work Act, which is recognised as being one of the most successful pieces of health and safety legislation in the world.

The approach adopted by the UK’s Health and Safety at Work Act, and other similar legislation from other jurisdictions, is a ‘goal-setting’ one. This allows organisations the flexibility to put in place measures that are relevant and appropriate to them without being constrained by rules that are more suited to an entirely different set of circumstances. Risk assessment becomes the guiding factor in a goal-setting regime. The disadvantage of the goal-setting approach is that it does not offer the objectivity and certainty of a prescriptive system, and frequently requires professional knowledge and judgement to interpret.

Goal-setting approaches can also lead to differences of opinion, which can result in confusion. For example, following a risk assessment, an organisation may have put in place control measures that it feels are appropriate to the task at hand, but an enforcement officer may take an opposing view and bring enforcement action against the company for a failure to meet the required standard. Ultimately, such matters are resolved by the courts, who will hear evidence from both sides plus, where appropriate, from experts in the field, before reaching a decision as to what measures were in fact appropriate in the circumstances.

In practice, although many systems are goal-setting in nature, there are still elements of prescription. An example of this appears in the Health and Safety at Work Act, which includes many goal-setting provisions, as well as some prescriptive ones.

An example of a goal-setting provision is to be found in section 2(1) of the 1974 Health and Safety at Work Act, which states:

It shall be the duty of every employer to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety and welfare at work of all his employees.


The employer’s goal here is to ensure [...] the health, safety and welfare at work of all his employees. The phrase that makes this a goal-setting provision, though, is ‘so far as is reasonably practicable’. This allows the employer to make a judgement as to the extent of measures that it will be appropriate to implement in their particular situation. Thus, this same piece of law applies equally to the smallest and to the largest companies – it sets out what must be done and leaves it up to the employers to decide how to do it.

A more prescriptive requirement is found in section 2(3) of the same Act, which says:

[...] it shall be the duty of every employer to prepare and as often as may be appropriate revise a written statement of his general policy with respect to the health and safety at work of his employees and the organisation and arrangements for the time being in force for carrying out that policy, and to bring the statement and any revision of it to the notice of all of his employees.


This sub-section prescribes not only the requirement to have a safety policy, but also sets out what such a document must contain. However, even this is not truly prescriptive, since it is up to the employer to decide such things as what goes into the statement, what arrangements to set out and how often to review the policy.

In contrast to the more detailed approach to illumination in the USA, the European Workplace Directive (89 / 654), Annex 1, merely states that:

Workplaces must as far as possible receive sufficient natural light and be equipped with artificial lighting adequate for the protection of workers’ safety and health.


In the UK Regulation 8 of the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations requires that:

Every workplace shall have suitable and sufficient lighting, and….the lighting …. shall, so far as is reasonably practicable, be by natural light.


It falls on the employer to determine what is ‘suitable and sufficient’ and ‘reasonably practicable’, although the UK’s Health and Safety Executive (HSE) provides advice through guidance notes.


Hierarchy of state and federal laws

In some legal systems, such as in the United States, the law is arranged hierarchically. In the US system, for example, there is an overarching federal law, which sets out law that applies equally to all 50 states. In addition to this there are ‘local’ or ‘state’ laws that are peculiar to individual states.

Taxes are a good example of this. Items purchased in the US will attract a federal tax, on top of which is levied a state tax that varies from state to state. This arrangement is also reflected in the fact that laws are enforced by different agencies at County, State and Federal level.

However, the US law on health and safety at work is federal law, which means that one law applies right across the nation, thus avoiding the apparent variability of standards and requirements seen in other parts of the world.

Other economic areas have slightly different approaches which do not add up to a federal system, but which may have similarities.

In the European Union (EU), Regulations and Directives are made, which apply to all member states. In essence, member states have no flexibility to interpret EU Regulations, which automatically bind member states and must be complied with. Directives, however, are only binding as to the result to be achieved, leaving individual member states free to interpret and enact the directive as they see fit.

Thus, if one travels across Europe, one will see apparent differences in standards. If we assume that the legal requirements are being met, then the explanation for this apparent difference lies with the differences in interpretation of the directive, which can and does create a certain amount of inconsistency across the EU. European Union member states retain their individual national identities and sovereignty, meaning that they are free to enact whatever laws they please, provided they do not conflict with EU law. Where conflict does arise, the member state in question will be called to account for its actions and may have to make changes to its laws to bring it into line with the wider EU requirements.

Another example of a difference is the attempt in Australia to harmonise Australian health and safety law through the enactment of the Model Work Health and Safety Act. Historically, Australian states and territories have had their own laws of health and safety but it has been recognised that harmonisation might offer advantages in terms of a holistic and consistent approach across the country. On 1 January 2012, harmonised laws were implemented in New South Wales, Queensland, the Australian Capital Territory, the Northern Territory and the Commonwealth.


Note: The progress of harmonisation of the Model Work Health and Safety Act across the jurisdictions of Australia are displayed on the Safe Work Australia website.

Western Australia, Tasmania and South Australia delayed the introduction of the model laws, while Victoria has said that it will not be introducing them at all. Thus, companies doing business in more than one state will still have to abide by different state laws, in much the same way as companies that operate across Europe have to.


The United Arab Emirates (UAE), although ‘United’ in name, have no single piece of legislation, federal or local, specifically dealing with health and safety. Across the UAE as a whole there are estimated to be around 170 different pieces of legislation covering health and safety, with clear differences in application in each emirate. A number of the UAE’s laws contain references to health and safety, but these are often very general and lack specific technical requirements or standards of compliance. For example, the UAE’s Labor Law includes generic guidelines on health and safety standards that can in theory be applied to any industry or workplace. However, the practical use of these provisions is limited. Therefore, the Labor Law is supplemented by Ministerial Decisions, an example of which is number 32 of 1982, which deals specifically with construction, although it still avoids technicalities or codified requirements.
