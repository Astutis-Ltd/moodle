Compensatory mechanisms


In the event that a person suffers loss (such as injury or ill-health), they may wish to claim damages, the aim of which is to compensate the claimant for the loss they sustained and to place him or her, so far as it is possible to do so, in the position they would have been in had the loss not been suffered.

If the injured party dies and leaves behind dependents, then some jurisdictions, such as the UK, will allow the surviving dependants to bring proceedings in the same way as would have occurred had the injured party survived. In the UK this is made possible by the Fatal Accidents Act 1976.

There are two main compensatory schemes, known as either the fault or no-fault liability systems.

In a fault liability system, both liability (responsibility for the loss) and quantum (the amount of compensation to be awarded) will be considered. The issue of liability will normally be considered first, followed by a consideration of the amount to be paid should the claimant establish their case. In complex cases, this frequently requires a separate trial, known as a ‘quantum trial’ or ‘quantum hearing’. In a no-fault liability system, liability will not be contested, leaving only the issue of quantum to be considered.


Fault liability

It is important to note that in fault liability systems there is no automatic right to compensation. There is also no fixed tariff for amounts to award for particular injuries, although guidelines provided by bodies like the UK’s Judicial Studies Board do help in the initial assessment of general damages, which will be discussed in more detail below.

An essential feature of a fault liability system is that, as its name suggests, some fault on the part of the defendant(s) must be proved before compensation will be paid. The claimant needs to persuade the court that ‘on the balance of probabilities’ the defendant is liable. As mentioned above, once liability has been determined, the exact amount of compensation to be paid can then be discussed.

In a fault liability system, claims are made in the law of tort (‘tort’ being an old Norman-French word meaning a ‘civil wrong’).

In tort claims, a claimant (such as an injured worker) would typically sue a defendant (such as their employer) in the tort of negligence, alleging that a breach of a duty of care that was owed by the employer to the injured worker led directly to the injury, and that the employer was therefore liable for any losses incurred.

To be successful, the claimant needs to show that:

1)

The defendant owed him a duty of care:

a)
The relationship must be proximate or a ‘neighbour’ relationship such as exists between employer and employee

b)
The loss must have been foreseeable

c)
It must be fair, just and reasonable for such a duty to be imposed.



2)

The duty of care was breached, i.e. the employer has not taken the care that a reasonable employer should have.

An employer’s duty of care to employees includes the provision of:

‣
a safe place of work including safe access to and egress from the place of work

‣
safe plant and equipment

‣
safe systems of work

‣
competent and safety-conscious co-workers (through appropriate information, instruction, training and supervision).



3)
The breach of duty led directly to the loss (i.e. there must be a clear link between the alleged breach of duty and the loss that has been sustained – in other words, the loss would not have occurred ‘but for’ the breach).


The fault liability system has distinct advantages, the first and arguably most important of which is based on the principle of ‘corrective justice’. This principle holds the responsible party accountable for the loss. This should motivate prospective duty holders to do what they reasonably can to prevent losses from arising in the first place.

The second major advantage of a fault-based system is that each case is considered separately on its merits, allowing damage awards to be tailored to meet the specific needs of the claimant. This allows for the possibility that a claimant will be found partially responsible for their loss (known as ‘contributory negligence’), which will lead to liability being apportioned according to the court’s view of the level of responsibility that should be borne by each side. Thus, if a court decides that the defendant is liable, but that the claimant contributed to his or her injuries by, say, 25% (a typical figure for not wearing a seatbelt in a road traffic collision), then the final damages award will be reduced by 25% to reflect that level of responsibility.

Quantum: an amount of money legally payable in damages.

The disadvantages of fault liability systems are that the process of litigation can take considerably longer, because the issue of primary liability will need to be resolved before considering quantum. This will lead to greater stress for the claimant, who will be faced with a protracted period of litigation followed by trial, the outcome of which will be far from certain and may result in a finding that the defendant had no liability, leaving the claimant without any damages, but possibly with a costly legal bill.

In jurisdictions where fault liability predominates, such as the UK, it is essential that employers have in place insurance (known as ‘Employers’ Liability Insurance’) to cover them in the event of a claim, although there are exceptions. There are two important reasons for this:

1)
The injured party is able to gain access to a fund of money that will pay out if they win their case, thus avoiding situations where an insolvent employer may not be able to meet a damages award, even though they are held liable

2)
As premiums are in part assessed on the basis of claims history and risk levels, the employer will be motivated to try and reduce the premium by maintaining good standards of health and safety, thus reducing the risks of injury or ill-health.


Claims from the public and other businesses are covered by Public Liability Insurance.

Finally, it is important to note that, in a fault liability system, the insurance will only pay out in cases where the defendant is found liable. Also, the insured will generally agree to pay the first $X of a claim in return for a lower annual insurance premium (a ‘policy excess’). This excess amount can be considerable and may be higher than the sum being claimed, meaning that the compensation payment comes from company funds, not insurance – a fact that may further motivate employers to take steps to reduce risks.


No-fault liability

A no-fault liability system is one where fault does not have to be proved, although the claimant will still need to show that the defendant was involved. For example, the injury occurred at work.

These schemes achieve justice through results, rather than by apportioning responsibility. Misconduct can go unpunished but the consequences of the misconduct are insured against to provide guaranteed funding for medical care and vocational rehabilitation.

A good example of a no-fault liability system can be found in New Zealand, where a Crown entity known as the Accident Compensation Corporation (ACC), created under the provisions of New Zealand’s Injury Prevention, Rehabilitation and Compensation Act 2001, provides comprehensive 24 hour no-fault personal injury cover to all New Zealand citizens, residents and visitors.

Contributions to the no-fault liability fund are made from several sources:

‣
the government (on behalf of non-earners)

‣
all taxpayers

‣
employers

‣
the self-employed

‣
motor vehicle owners.


Cover is managed under six accounts:

‣
Work account – for workplace injuries

‣
Residual claims account – workplace injuries that were sustained before 1 July 1999 or involving earners outside the workplace before 1 July 1992

‣
Motor vehicle account

‣
Earners’ account – personal injuries outside the workplace to those in paid work

‣
Non-earners’ account – personal injuries that occur outside the workplace to those not in paid work

‣
Treatment injury account – personal injuries arising from medical treatment (known in the UK as ‘clinical negligence‘).


The ACC adjudicates on claims. Mediation and conciliation routes are available to deal with disputes and there is a right of appeal to an independent Appeal Authority.

Not surprisingly, such a system costs a lot of money. In 2019 231 800 new work-related injury claims were made including 37 000 more serious injuries involving entitlement claims. The total expenditure for 2019 was over 8.25 Billion NZD for new and other active claims which includes paying for rehabilitation, compensation, administration, injury prevention programmes, levy collection, etc.

The scheme covers all work accidents and illnesses ‘arising out of and in the course of employment’. Employers in New Zealand are required by law to have cover, with an invoice being sent by the ACC after June of each year. The amount payable by each employer is based on the wage bill and the type of work, with up to a 10% discount being available for those employers who can demonstrate good safety practices.

ACC cover extends to the following:

‣
Treatment costs

‣
Transport to assessments, treatment and rehabilitation, and a petrol allowance and car modifications

‣
Medical aids and assistance

‣
Up to 80% of weekly loss of earnings, subject to a statutory maximum (the employer covers the first week).


No-fault / workers’ compensation schemes offer certain advantages over tort-based approaches, including:

‣
greater certainty of receiving compensation – the claimant has the right to receive limited benefits regardless of fault

‣
consistency of approach based on the injury sustained, not the facts of the case

‣
actions in tort against employers are largely eliminated

‣
speedier resolution of claims

‣
reduced level of stress for the claimant who will not have to become involved in protracted litigation

‣
more efficient accident investigation as blame avoidance is less of an issue

‣
Insurance funds are not diverted into legal costs and are therefore more readily available to the injured party.


The disadvantages include:

‣
Lack of accountability of employers, directors and senior managers

‣
Loss of the ability to make a full damages claim in tort (see below)

‣
damages are limited to fixed amounts, which may be lower than the total loss

‣
ordinarily provides no compensation for pain, suffering and other non-economic loss

‣
injured party does not get ‘justice’ through seeing the guilty party pay, especially where criminal proceedings are not taken

‣
lack of deterrent effect on the employer

‣
difficulty in defining the parameters of the scheme – dealing with psychological injuries poses particular problems

‣
typically lower value compensation awards (though some may argue that this is an advantage)

‣
the employer may be liable for an unavoidable accident where there would be no liability in tort.


As mentioned, in return for a no-fault system, people have had to give up their right to sue for personal injury, although they may still be allowed to sue for exemplary damages (see section on ‘Damages’ below). For example, in similar systems in the USA and Canada, ‘workers’ compensation’ schemes, as they are known, contain clauses barring the worker and his / her family from bringing an action against the employer, employer’s agent or co-worker in respect of personal injury, disablement or death arising out of employment. As long ago as 1913, Sir William Meredith, Chief Justice of Ontario, noted that:

...the workman is required as the price of the compensation he is to receive to surrender his right to damages under the common law.



Typical employers’ liability insurance claims (UK)

In the UK, since 1969, it has been compulsory for employers to carry a minimum level of employers’ liability insurance cover. At present, this must be a minimum of £5 million for each and every claim.

The number of claims for compensation against employers as a result of accidents fell steadily in the early 21st century and the total cost of compensation cases has remained, in real terms, static since 1989.

Research in 2005 showed that average damages for an Employers’ Liability Insurance (ELI) claim were £7 500. Britain paid out much less on civil compensation (as a percentage of GDP) than other major European countries and about a third of the USA figure. The average cost of employer’s liability insurance was 0.25% of total payroll costs, and was the lowest in Europe.

In 2020 there were just over 2 million reported workplace injuries and illnesses in the UK. 1.935 million were self-reported through the Labour Force Survey (LFS) and over 69 000 were reported by employers as required by the Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations (RIDDOR).

If an employee suffers accidental injury or illness due to work and successfully claims for compensation from their employer, the employer must notify the Compensation Recovery Unit (CRU) of the details of the claim.

According to the CRU in 2019 / 20 there were 89 461 successful employers’ liability claims settled in the UK. This means that roughly 4.5% of workplace-related employee injuries and illnesses (1 in 22) ends up with a successful claim against the employer.


Typical workers compensation claims (USA)

In the early 1900s the USA moved away from a negligence based compensation system for work injuries and a state-by-state pattern of legislative proposals designed to compensate injured workers began to emerge.

Workers compensation systems are administered by the individual states, generally by commissions or boards whose responsibility it is to ensure compliance with the laws, investigate and decide disputed cases, and collect data. In most states employers are required to keep records of accidents. Accidents must be reported to the workers compensation board and to the company’s insurer within a specified number of days.

Workers compensation covers an injured worker’s medical care and attempts to cover his or her economic loss. This includes loss of earnings and the extra expenses associated with the injury. Injured workers receive all medically necessary and appropriate treatment from the first day of injury or illness and rehabilitation when the disability is severe.

24 states including New York and California have state-run workers compensation funds. In the other states, employers can purchase workers compensation coverage from private insurance companies.

The only state in which workers compensation coverage is truly optional is Texas, where about one-third of the state’s employers are so-called nonsubscribers.

According to the National Council on Compensation Insurance’s (NCCI) Workers Compensation Statistical Plan database the average cost for all claims combined in 2017-2018 was $41 003. The NCCI data shows that injuries arising from motor vehicle collisions are the most costly at $78 466 per claim filed in 2017 / 18. Other causes with above-average costs were burns ($49 521), falls or slips ($47 516), and caught ($43 538).
