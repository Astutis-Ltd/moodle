Damages


Awards for compensation, known as ‘damages’, fall into two major categories: general and special. A third category, punitive, is controversial and used less frequently.


General damages

These damages represent the loss to the claimant that cannot be precisely quantified, that is, past and future non-financial loss and future financial loss.

General damages do not have to be specifically pleaded, though any material facts giving rise to a claim for general damages should be pleaded (for example: the injuries, loss of employment or handicap on the open labour market). General damages do not have to be specifically proved. They can be implied to a certain extent. However, evidence is required.

Heads of general damages are as follows:

‣
pain and suffering

‣
loss of amenity

‣
future loss of earnings

‣
loss of earning capacity

‣
loss of pension rights

‣
future expenses (including value of future unpaid help).



Special damages

Special damages represent the claimant’s actual financial loss between the date of injury and the date of trial. In other words, special damages represent those losses that are strictly measureable in money and susceptible to precise calculation. Every item of special damage must be specifically pleaded and proven. Examples of items that can be recovered as special damages include but are not limited to:

‣
Incidental damage to property, such as vehicle damage, a broken watch, damaged clothing, etc.

‣
Medical expenses such as prescription costs, physiotherapy, etc.

‣
Cost of travel to and from hospital to attend appointments or for treatment

‣
The cost of nursing care

‣
The cost of paid help such as housekeeper, gardener, dog walker, etc.

‣
Reasonable necessary expenditure on such things as a wheel chair, car conversion, alterations to the home, etc.

‣
Increases in ordinary expenditure, such as increased heating costs

‣
Loss of earnings to date of trial

‣
Loss of fringe benefits associated with employment, such as free or discounted goods or services, or use of a company car

‣
Other miscellaneous losses, such as the cost of a cancelled holiday.



Punitive damages

Punitive damages (known as ‘exemplary damages’ in the UK) are awarded in cases where the standard heads of compensatory damages do not appear to be sufficient. They are intended to make an example of the defendant.

In the UK, exemplary (punitive) damages are awarded only rarely and not at all for cases of negligence, breach of statutory duty or breach of contract. Therefore, this head of damages does not figure in claims for workplace injury in the UK.

Rules on the award of punitive damages are tight. In the UK, they can only be awarded in tort in three situations:

1)
Where there have been oppressive or unconstitutional actions by the servants of the government, such as civil servants, politicians and the police

2)
Where the defendant’s conduct has been calculated to make a profit that will exceed compensation payable to the claimant

3)
Where such damages are expressly authorised by statute, although currently there do not appear to be any major examples of this.


Insurers will not provide cover for exemplary damages, as to do so would be comparable in certain respects, to providing cover for fines in criminal law, which is not allowed as it is against the public interest.

In the USA, the issue of punitive damages is hotly debated. These damages are awarded by judges or juries and are frequently seen as excessive.

Case law

For example, in July 2000, a punitive damages award of $144.8Bn was awarded in a Florida class action against cigarette manufacturers.

In some US states, statutory caps have been placed on punitive damages awards following successful lobbying from insurance companies, whilst other states consider punitive damages to be unconstitutional, so do not allow them at all.

Case law

The case of Grimshaw v Ford Motor Company is a case in point. In May 1972, Lily Gray was travelling in a 1972 Ford Pinto motor car with a passenger, Richard Grimshaw, aged 13, when their car was hit by another traveling at around 30mph. The impact caused a fire, which killed Mrs. Gray and left Grimshaw with devastating injuries.

At trial, the jury awarded the Gray family $560 000, while the Grimshaws received $2.5 million in compensatory damages.

The jury also awarded $125 million in punitive damages. Following appeal this sum was reduced to $3.5 million.
