The role, function and limitations of legislation

It is clear that there are economic and social benefits to be gained from the effective management of health and safety. For some organisations, these benefits alone will provide sufficient motivation for them to take action to establish and give effect to arrangements that safeguard the health, safety and welfare at work of their employees and others who may be affected by their activities. However, not all organisations share such an enlightened view. Therefore, it has become necessary to establish formal rules of law on the management of safety that seek to ensure that organisations meet at least a minimum standard. In many jurisdictions around the world, these standards are to be found in legislation.

The term ‘legislation’ (also referred to as ‘statute law’) refers to law that is enacted by a country’s legislature or governing body. Legislation may take a number of forms, such as Acts of Parliament / Congress, Regulations (sometimes known as ‘Statutory instruments’), Orders etc. Collectively, legislation describes the minimum standards to be met by a duty holder.

Health and safety legislation operates in two ways. First it offers a frame of reference for duty holders, such that they know in general terms what is expected of them. Second, it offers the option of criminal punishment and / or civil remedies in the event that standards fall below an acceptable level. This threat of sanctions acts as another source of motivation for employers to address health and safety concerns that might otherwise be left.

Examples of well-known items of health and safety legislation include:

  • The UK’s Health and Safety at Work etc. Act, 1974 – a highly successful piece of legislation that lays down general principles that apply to all workplaces in the UK
  • The Occupational Safety and Health Act, 1970, – sets the federal framework in the USA for assuring healthy and safe working conditions, and authorises enforcement of standards developed under the Act
  • The Model Work Health and Safety (WHS) Act, 2011, – an Australian attempt to harmonise the different workplace health and safety laws that have historically developed independently across Australia’s states and territories.

What all of these legislative provisions have in common is that they lay down general principles to be applied and observed by named duty holders, who, in the context of health and safety are mainly employers, but also include workers, manufacturers and suppliers, landlords, etc.

However, the fact that legislation such as that identified above sets out general principles can be seen both as a strength and as a weakness. It is a strength insofar as the basic requirements should be clear and should give the duty holder a starting point when considering how to manage health and safety issues. It is a weakness in that general principles may not be enough, since they do not set out clear and unequivocal rules to follow to meet a specific situation. This means a degree of judgement is needed if the legal standard is to be met, which can lead to non-compliance.

There is also the fact that legislative requirements, while necessary, seek to impose duties rather than leaving it to the parties to decide of their own volition what action to take. This may mean that an organisation will put in place certain arrangements, not because it sees them as being intrinsically valuable to the business, but because it is legally obliged to do so. This sort of approach is seen regularly in statements of safety policy which use phrases like ‘The Company acknowledges that it is under a legal obligation to’ or ‘The Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 requires us to...’ In such cases, it might be said that the company is only aiming to establish legal compliance and has limited intention to go beyond that level, preferring instead to do the minimum required by law. However, the counter-argument is that most organisations will feel compelled to at least take some form of action to manage health and safety issues, if only to avoid criminal penalties or civil proceedings.
