Legal systems

Merryman (1985) defined a ‘legal system’ as:

... an operating set of legal institutions, procedures and rules. In this sense there are one federal and fifty state legal systems in the United States, separate legal systems in each of the other nations, and still other distinct legal systems in such organisations as the European Economic Community and the United Nations.

Thus, there are several major ‘legal systems’ currently in use in different countries or trading blocs. In some cases, such as with the common law, a system will have spread around the world, helping to provide some sense of uniformity and consistency, whereas other legal systems remain localised to particular geographical areas. Therefore, in an increasingly globalised economy, it is important to recognise that a company with a presence in more than one country is liable to meet different rules, which will be applied in different ways, some of which will be more stringent than others.

Significant legal systems include:

Civil codes

The world’s predominant legal system, which is very systematic and structured and gives precedence to written (codified) law. This system focuses on broad statements of general principles, often ignoring details. Civil systems are divided into two main streams, codified and uncodified.

  • Examples of codified systems can be found in the French Civil Code, throughout much of continental Europe, and in the Canadian province of Quebec. The US state of Louisiana also operates under a civil code, making it unique in the USA, the other 49 states of which use a common law system
  • Uncodified civil systems can be found in Scotland and South Africa. Scotland, being part of the UK, has a very different legal system to that of its immediate neighbours, all of whom use the common law.
Common law A system that originated in the UK and spread overseas with the development of the British Empire to countries such as the USA, Canada, Jamaica, Australia and New Zealand. The common law is based on the principle of judicial precedent (Stare decisis), the chief feature of which is that the law is not codified in one place but is developed on an incremental basis through a series of judicial decisions. This allows the common law to keep pace with societal and technical change, without the need to revise complex statutory provisions.
Religious law For example, Talmudic law from Israel and Sharia law, common in the Islamic world.
Customary law Such as exists in Andorra, Guernsey and Jersey.

The law itself is set out in different forms in different jurisdictions and legal systems, with some forms of law being easier to interpret than others.

Sources of law in common use include:

Constitutions All but three of the world’s countries have written constitutions in the sense that there is a single document to call a ‘Constitution’. Those that do not include Israel, New Zealand and the United Kingdom.
Treaties For example, the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU).
European Regulations and Directives For example, the EU’s ‘Framework Directive’ (Directive 89 / 391 / EEC).
Acts of Parliament or Acts of Congress For example, the UK’s Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974, or the US Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970.
Subordinate legislation For example, Regulations made under the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974, whose purpose is to make explicit what is implied in the Act.
Case law A defining feature of the world’s common law jurisdictions, which provides interpretation of statute (for example, the meaning of key words or phrases in law produced by Parliament) or stands alone as a body of law in its own right (for example, in the UK, the crime of murder is a common law offence).

Legal requirements may be augmented by more detailed guidance, which helps the duty holder to interpret and apply the law. Many examples of such guidance will be encountered throughout the remainder of the course.
