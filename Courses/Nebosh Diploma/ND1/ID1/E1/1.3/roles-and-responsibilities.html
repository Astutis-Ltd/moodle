Roles and responsibilities of national governments, enterprises and workers

The roles and responsibilities of national governments, enterprises (employers) and workers are set out in the ILO Occupational Safety and Health Convention 1981 (C155), which is elaborated upon in ILO Occupational Safety and Health Recommendation 1981 (R164).

National governments

ILO Occupational Safety and Health Convention 1981 (C155) requires national governments of those member states who have ratified the convention to:

  • Consult with employers’ and workers’ representative organisations to formulate, implement and periodically review a coherent national policy on occupational safety, health and the working environment, the aim of which shall be to prevent workplace accidents and injury to health by minimising, so far as is reasonably practicable, the causes of hazards inherent in the working environment (Article 4)
  • Indicate in the national policy referred to in Article 4 the functions and responsibilities of public authorities, employers, workers and others (Article 6)
  • Use laws or regulations or any other method consistent with national conditions to give effect to Article 4 (Article 8)
  • Enforce occupational safety and health laws through an appropriate system of inspection and provide for adequate penalties for violations of the laws and regulations (Article 9)
  • Provide guidance to employers and workers so as to help them to comply with legal obligations (Article 10)
  • Give effect to the national policy referred to in Article 4 by ensuring that the competent authorities carry out the following functions.
    1. Determine conditions governing the design, construction and layout of undertakings, the commencement of their operations, major alterations affecting them and changes in their purposes, the safety of technical equipment used at work, as well as the application of procedures defined by the competent authorities
    2. Determine work processes, substances and agents the exposure to which is to be prohibited, limited or made subject to authorisation or control by the competent authority or authorities, health hazards due to the simultaneous exposure to several substances or agents shall be taken into consideration
    3. Establish and apply procedures for the notification of occupational accidents and diseases, and produce annual statistics on the same
    4. Hold inquiries into serious situations
    5. Publish annually, information on measures taken in pursuance of the national policy referred to in Article 4 and on work-related occupational accidents, diseases and other injuries
    6. Introduce or extend systems to examine chemical, physical and biological agents in respect of the risk to the health of workers (Article 11).
  • Take measures to ensure that designers, manufacturers, importers, etc. of machinery, equipment or substances:
    1. Satisfy themselves that, so far as is reasonably practicable, the machinery, equipment or substance does not entail dangers for the safety and health of those using it correctly
    2. Make available information concerning the correct installation and use of machinery and equipment and the correct use of substances, and information on hazards of machinery and equipment and dangerous properties of chemical substances and physical and biological agents or products, as well as instructions on how known hazards are to be avoided
    3. Undertake studies and research or otherwise keep abreast of the scientific and technical knowledge necessary to comply with (a) and (b) above (Article 12).
  • Protect workers who remove themselves from a work situation which they have reasonable justification to believe presents an imminent and serious danger to his life or health (Article 13)
  • Promote the inclusion of questions of occupational safety, health and the working environment at all levels of education and training (Article 14)
  • Make arrangements to ensure the necessary co-ordination between various authorities associated with giving effect to the Convention and, where national conditions and practice permit, to establish a central body (such as the HSE in the UK, or OSHA in the USA) (Article 15).

Recommendation R164 elaborates on the above and requires the national competent authority (such as the HSE in the UK) to:

  1. Issue or approve regulations, codes of practice or other suitable provisions on occupational safety and health and the working environment, account being taken of the links existing between safety and health, on the one hand, and hours of work and rest breaks, on the other
  2. Review legislative enactments concerning occupational safety and health and the working environment in the light of experience and advances in science and technology
  3. Undertake or promote studies and research to identify hazards and find means of overcoming them
  4. Provide information and advice to employers and workers and promote or facilitate co-operation between them and their organisations, with a view to eliminating hazards or reducing them as far as practicable, provide a special training programme for migrant workers in their mother tongue where appropriate
  5. Provide specific measures to prevent catastrophes, and to co-ordinate and make coherent the actions to be taken at different levels, particularly in industrial zones where undertakings with high potential risks for workers and the surrounding population are situated
  6. Secure good liaison with the International Labour Occupational Safety and Health Hazard Alert System, set up within the framework of the ILO
  7. Provide appropriate measures for disabled workers.

Enterprises

ILO Occupational Safety and Health Convention 1981 (C155) places general requirements on employers to:

  • Ensure that, so far as is reasonably practicable, the workplaces, machinery, equipment, processes, and the chemical, physical and biological substances and agents under their control are safe and without risk to health and to provide, where necessary, adequate protective clothing and equipment to prevent, so far as is reasonably practicable, risk of accidents or of adverse effects on health (Article 16)
  • Co-operate with other undertakings sharing the same workplace (Article 17)
  • Establish measures to deal with emergencies and accidents, including adequate first-aid arrangements (Article 18).

With respect to arrangements for workers and / or their representatives, Article 19 of C155 requires that:

  1. Workers co-operate in the fulfilment by their employer of the obligations placed upon him
  2. Representatives of workers co-operate with the employer in the field of occupational safety and health
  3. Representatives of workers are given adequate information on measures taken by the employer to secure occupational safety and health and may consult their representative organisations about such information provided they do not disclose commercial secrets
  4. Workers and their representatives are given appropriate training in occupational safety and health
  5. Workers or their representatives and, as the case may be, their representative organisations, are enabled to enquire into, and are consulted by the employer on, all aspects of occupational safety and health associated with their work
  6. Following a report of imminent and serious danger to life or health of workers, until the employer has taken remedial action, if necessary, the employer cannot require workers to return to a work situation where there is continuing imminent and serious danger to life or health.

Article 20 makes it clear that co-operation between management and workers (or their representatives) is key in meeting the above obligations.

Article 21 states that occupational safety and health measures shall not involve any expenditure for the workers.

As with the requirements on national governments, R164 expands on the Convention requirements applicable to employers and sets out the following key provisions:

  1. To provide and maintain workplaces, machinery and equipment, and use work methods, which are as safe and without risk to health as is reasonably practicable
  2. To give necessary instructions and training, taking account of the functions and capacities of different categories of workers
  3. To provide adequate supervision of work, of work practices and of application and use of occupational safety and health measures
  4. To institute organisational arrangements regarding occupational safety and health and the working environment adapted to the size of the undertaking and the nature of its activities
  5. To provide, without any cost to the worker, adequate personal protective clothing and equipment which are reasonably necessary when hazards cannot be otherwise prevented or controlled
  6. To ensure that work organisation, particularly with respect to hours of work and rest breaks, does not adversely affect occupational safety and health
  7. To take all reasonably practicable measures with a view to eliminating excessive physical and mental fatigue
  8. To undertake studies and research or otherwise keep abreast of the scientific and technical knowledge necessary to comply with the foregoing clauses.

The Recommendation also states that, in order to facilitate co-operation between employer and employee, employers should, where appropriate and necessary, appoint workers’ safety delegates and committees. Workers’ safety delegates, representatives and / or committees should:

  1. Be given adequate information on safety and health matters, enabled to examine factors affecting safety and health, and encouraged to propose measures on the subject
  2. Be consulted when major new safety and health measures are envisaged and before they are carried out, and seek to obtain the support of the workers for such measures
  3. Be consulted in planning alterations of work processes, work content or organisation of work, which may have safety or health implications for the workers
  4. Be given protection from dismissal and other measures prejudicial to them while exercising their functions in the field of occupational safety and health as workers’ representatives or as members of safety and health committees
  5. Be able to contribute to the decision-making process at the level of the undertaking regarding matters of safety and health
  6. Have access to all parts of the workplace and be able to communicate with the workers on safety and health matters during working hours at the workplace
  7. Be free to contact labour inspectors
  8. Be able to contribute to negotiations in the undertaking on occupational safety and health matters
  9. Have reasonable time during paid working hours to exercise their safety and health functions and to receive training related to these functions
  10. Have recourse to specialists to advise on particular safety and health problems.

Paragraph 13 of the Recommendation suggests that employers should, where necessary, make provision for:

  1. The availability of an occupational health service and a safety service, either within the undertaking, jointly with other undertakings, or under arrangements with an outside body
  2. Recourse to specialists to advise on particular occupational safety or health problems or supervise the application of measures to meet them.

Finally, it is recommended that employers should be required to set out in writing their health and safety policy and arrangements and to bring them to the notice of every worker, in a language or medium the worker readily understands.

Workers

The key requirements on workers are set out in ILO Occupational Safety and Health Recommendation 1981 (R164), which requires them to:

  1. Take reasonable care for their own safety and that of other persons who may be affected by their acts or omissions at work
  2. Comply with instructions given for their own safety and health and those of others and with safety and health procedures
  3. Use safety devices and protective equipment correctly and do not render them inoperative
  4. Report forthwith to their immediate supervisor any situation which they have reason to believe could present a hazard and which they cannot themselves correct
  5. Report any accident or injury to health which arises in the course of or in connection with work.
