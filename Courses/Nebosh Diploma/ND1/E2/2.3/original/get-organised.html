Get organised: plan


Getting organised involves understanding:

‣
What affects how to involve the workforce;

‣
What the workforce should be consulted about;

‣
When consultation should occur, so that it happens in good time; and

‣
Arrangements for training health and safety representatives.



What affects how to involve the workforce?

The following factors about the business, the workplace and the workforce will impact on how best to engage with employees.


These factors determine whether it is best to consult individuals or representatives, the methods to use, the organisation of inspections and investigations, and co-ordination between committees, among other things.

As an example, a high-risk, unionised workplace with a large workforce spread over multiple sites may have union representatives from each site as a member of a site-based health and safety committee that meets regularly, and feeds into a corporate health and safety committee; whereas a non-unionised, smaller workplace, located on one small, low-risk site, is more likely to consult directly with employees on a day-to-day basis.


What should the workforce be consulted about?

The workforce must be consulted about anything in the workplace that could substantially affect their health and safety. The specifics will vary from workplace to workplace.

In general, employees must be consulted about:

‣
any change which may have a substantial effect on the workforce’s health and safety. Such as introducing new or different procedures, types of work, equipment, premises, and ways of working (e.g. new shift patterns);

‣
arrangements for getting competent people to help meet obligations under health and safety laws, for example the appointment of a health and safety manager

‣
information on workplace risks and precautions they should take. Employees and representatives should be involved in determining the best way for information to be shared and issues of language, literacy and learning disabilities should be considered as appropriate

‣
the planning of health and safety training; and

‣
the health and safety consequences of introducing new technology.


Employees, or their representatives should also be consulted as a part of any workplace risk assessments. This is a valuable way of involving the staff that do the work as they know the risks involved and scope for potentially dangerous short cuts and problems.

Employees are more likely to understand why procedures are put in place to control risks and follow them if they have been involved in the process of developing health and safety practices in their workplace.

Formal consultation is not required before every task specific risk assessment conducted in the workplace, but consultation should form part of the general risk assessment process.


Training arrangements for health and safety representatives

Health and safety representatives must be equipped with appropriate skills and knowledge to be able to perform their role.
