Behavioural attributes of an effective leader


Ed Schein noted that ‘the unique and essential function of leadership is the manipulation of culture’. The IOGP (International Association of Oil and Gas Producers) considered that ‘Safety Culture starts with leadership; leadership drives culture, which in turn drives behaviour.’

Inquiries in the aftermath of several major accidents have identified the lack of leadership as a critical causal factor.

The Hidden Report into the Clapham Junction train crash (1988) concluded that:

...a concern for safety which is sincerely held and repeatedly expressed but, nevertheless, is not carried through into action, is as much protection from danger as no concern at all...


Lord Cullen’s report into the Ladbroke Grove (Paddington) train crash (1999) noted that:

The commitment to safety of senior executives is not visible at the working and operational level and the safety policy lacks credibility in the eyes of the employees.

...conviction needs to come from the top, needs to be broadcast from the top, needs to be continually refreshed from the top and the top needs to be seen, visibly seen as active in its representation of its value...


The IOGP report on ‘Shaping safety culture through leadership’ cites the work of Krause in identifying seven key safety leadership characteristics and associated behaviours that can influence Safety Culture:

‣
Credibility – what leaders say is consistent with what they do

‣
Action orientation – leaders act to address unsafe conditions

‣
Vision – leaders paint a picture for safety excellence within the organisation

‣
Accountability – leaders ensure employees take accountability for safety-critical activities

‣
Communication – the way leaders communicate about safety creates and maintains the safety culture of the organisation

‣
Collaboration – leaders who encourage active employee participation in resolving safety issues promote employee ownership of those issues

‣
Feedback and recognition – recognition that is ‘soon, certain and positive’ encourages safe behaviour.


The HSE’s leadership and worker involvement toolkit considers the leaders primary purpose to be influencing others to reach a goal and advocates a transformational approach to positively impact on workers attitudes and behaviours and organisational performance by energising and motivating their workers to:

‣
view their work from different perspectives

‣
be aware of their organisation’s vision

‣
reach their full potential by challenging themselves

‣
work to benefit the team rather than just themselves.


An effective health and safety leader should:

‣
Challenge the status quo – strive for continual improvement and challenge workers to come up with better, safer ways of working

‣
Create a vision – consult the workforce to identify and set clear health and safety goals, involve them in planning and decision making, and make sure everyone knows what they need to do

‣
Inspire workers to be healthy and safe – provide enough resources for workers to do their jobs safely - time, competent personnel, supervision and equipment, share expertise and help workers overcome barriers, develop mutual trust, and reward workers who successfully work safely

‣
Be a good role model – set a good example to the workers by putting health and safety first. Promote safe work behaviour and practices – encourage workers to do it because they want to, not because they have to. Be fair – trust and respect workers when making health and safety decisions

‣
Show consideration – treat others as you would expect to be treated yourself, get to know each worker as an individual, respect their opinions and show concern for their safety and well-being. Develop a team spirit where health and safety comes first and everyone looks out for one another

‣
Communicate regularly – update workers on developments and performance in health and safety and encourage feedback, be approachable and receptive to workers’ ideas, and respond to concerns immediately and discuss the actions that will be taken.
