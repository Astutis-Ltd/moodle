Specified leadership theories

The NEBOSH Diploma Syllabus requires consideration of the following leadership theories and their likely impact upon health and safety performance:

  • transactional
  • transformational
  • authentic
  • resonant.

Transactional leadership

Transactional leadership considers leadership to be ‘a process of exchange’ that relies on good contractual relationships, i.e. offer and acceptance and the exchange of items of value.

Leaders transact with followers to get things done by creating expectation and setting objectives and providing recognition and reward for successful task completion (and punishments for non-completion).

Many would consider these transactional processes to be management rather than leadership. Transactional skills are necessary for getting things done but are done within the context of a management framework. True leadership skills are essential to effect cultural change and improve safety performance.

Transformational leadership

Transformational leadership is about transforming group goals and actions through inspiring and giving purpose and direction to group members.

Transformational leaders:

  • Provide the energy to stimulate and transform followers
  • Challenge followers’ basic thinking to encourage better mind-sets and better practise
  • Help individuals to raise aspirations, improve abilities and satisfy needs.

Research into the attributes of ‘exemplary’ and ‘superior’ transformational leaders has identified the following key behaviours and attitudes:

  • Challenging the process – innovate, experiment and strive for continual improvement
  • Inspiring a shared vision – share the vision of a positive future state to inspire buy-in and participation
  • Enabling others to act – foster collaboration, develop trust, empower others to make decisions, and encourage personal development
  • Modelling the way – Demonstrate clear consistent values, beliefs and behaviours – show others what is expected of them (lead by example)
  • Encouraging the heart – recognise accomplishments and celebrate achievements and share success.

Transformational skills are essential to engage employees and facilitate positive cultural change.

Authentic leadership

The roots of authentic leadership are based on ancient Greek philosophy ‘know thyself’. More than 2 400 years after Socrates emphasised the importance of self-awareness, leadership researchers are still investigating the importance of self-awareness, self-regulation and authenticity as critical aspects of leadership.

Bill George (2007) is often credited with popularising authentic leadership in management studies and general culture. George believes that the only valid test of a leader is his or her ability to bring people together to achieve sustainable results over time and that real leadership requires years of development and hard work.

George holds that authentic leaders usually demonstrate the following five traits:

  1. Pursuing their purpose with passion
  2. Practicing solid values
  3. Leading with their hearts as well as their heads
  4. Establishing connected relationships
  5. Demonstrating self-discipline.

To discover their purpose, authentic leaders must understand themselves and the passions that drive them. The values of authentic leaders are shaped by their personal beliefs and developed through introspection, consultation with others, and years of experience. An authentic leader lives their values even under extreme pressure, consequently trust is built and maintained.

George offers Warren Buffet as an example of an authentic leader and someone who has exercised consistently high levels of self-discipline, in a career of over 40 years, becoming the most successful investor in businesses in the USA.

Banks et al’s review of authentic leadership literature in 2016 offered Walumbwa’s (2008) definition as the most generally accepted definition of authentic leadership:

“a pattern of leader behaviour that draws upon and promotes both positive psychological capacities and a positive ethical climate, to foster greater self-awareness, an internalized moral perspective, balanced processing of information, and relational transparency on the part of leaders working with followers, fostering positive self-development.”

The following were offered as the major characteristics of authentic leadership:

  • Self-awareness (know thyself) - Understanding personal strengths and weaknesses and core values. An authentic leader knows what they stand for
  • Relational transparency (being genuine) - being genuine and honest in relationships with others. Making sure that people know where they stand, whether they like it or not
  • Internalised moral perspective (doing the right thing) – driven by ethics and fairness. Not out for personal gain at other people’s expense, dishonesty, or exploitation
  • Balanced processing (fair-mindedness) - listening to others and taking on board a range of views before choosing a direction and planning accordingly.

Walumbwa et al (2008) also developed an ‘authentic leadership validation scale’ to determine the extent to which an individual was demonstrating those characteristics, based on responses to the following eight items.

  1. Seeks feedback to improve interactions with others
  2. Accurately describes how others view his or her capabilities
  3. Says exactly what he or she means
  4. Is willing to admit mistakes when they are made
  5. Demonstrates beliefs that are consistent with actions
  6. Makes decisions based on his/her core beliefs
  7. Solicits views that challenge his or her deeply held positions
  8. Listens carefully to different points of view before coming to conclusions.

Walumbwa’s model suggests that authentic leaders:

  • Show others that they genuinely desire to understand their own leadership to serve others more effectively
  • Act in accordance with deep personal values and convictions to build credibility, respect and trust
  • Encourage diverse viewpoints and build networks of collaborative relationships with followers
  • Lead in a manner that followers perceive and describe as authentic.

Resonant leadership

Resonance, in this context, is the property of being ‘in tune’. The concept of resonant leadership was introduced in the book ‘Primal Leadership’ by Goleman, Boyatzis and McKee (2001) and tied in with Goleman’s earlier work on unleashing emotional intelligence.

Boyatzis and McKee developed the concept with two sequel books ‘Resonant Leadership’ in 2005 and ‘Becoming a Resonant Leader’ in 2008.

Resonant leaders work at developing their emotional intelligence competencies of self-awareness, self-management, social awareness and relationship management. Boyatzis and McKee noted that even good leaders find it very difficult to sustain their effectiveness, and resonance, over time.

The best leaders give of themselves constantly and when they sacrifice too much for too long, they can become trapped in the “sacrifice syndrome.” Leadership is stressful as power and influence creates distance between leaders and other people, potentially leaving leaders lonely and isolated from supportive relationships.

The stress associate with leadership is termed ‘power stress’ and is difficult to deal with over time as leaders eventually become exhausted and burn up or burn out.

Exhausted leaders suffer physically and emotionally, and their cognitive functioning is impaired. Resonance is then replaced by dissonance and because emotions are contagious, dissonance spreads quickly to those around the leader and eventually permeates the organisation.

Rather than constantly sacrificing themselves to workplace demands, leaders can use specific techniques to combat stress, avoid burnout, and renew themselves physically, mentally, and emotionally.

Three paths of mindfulness, hope and compassion are advocated for creating resonance and for restoring and renewing the leader. Mindfulness hope and compassion are held to spark physiological changes that help to reverse the negative effects of power stress. By mindfully attending to oneself and others, encouraging an optimistic vision of the future, and caring for others, leaders can ignite or reignite resonance in themselves and those around them.

  • Mindfulness starts with self-awareness, being authentic and consistent. Mindfulness is also useful in picking up cues to attend carefully to oneself, to others or to the working environment
  • Hope, like other positive emotions, has a positive impact on brain chemistry leading to a more positive perception of events happening around us. This leads to a contagious calm optimism, which can help people to cope better with physical and mental challenges
  • Compassion. Effective leaders are in tune with people, which involves caring about them, which invokes the curiosity, respect and real empathy that constitute compassion.

Goleman identified four different styles of resonant leadership:

  1. Visionary – see the organisation’s overall picture, and share it with their employees, encouraging them to work together to achieve the team’s goals. If employees appreciate the big picture’s aim, they will be actively sticking around a job to achieve it
  2. Coaching – focuses on the personal development of employees. Leaders show a genuine interest in individuals, building connections, growing trust, and leading to higher levels of motivation and better results
  3. Affiliative – about building relationships and working together. Affiliative leaders boost employee morale by giving value to the employees, and their feelings by being empathetic. This helps build collaborative relationships boosting efficiency and productivity
  4. Democratic – uses the team’s knowledge to provide feedback on, or cooperate in, the decision-making process. Empathy is essential in understanding the thoughts and feelings of a diverse group. This approach is helpful in building buy-in or consensus.
