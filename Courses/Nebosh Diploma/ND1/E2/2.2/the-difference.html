The difference between leaders and managers

The distinction between managers and leaders has been much debated over many decades. Nowadays it is generally accepted that the concepts although distinct are not mutually exclusive.

The role of the manager is to:

  • carry out traditional defined management functions such as planning, budgeting and recruiting
  • fulfil the requirements of a prescribed role such as figurehead, negotiator etc.

Leadership, on the other hand, is about more than fulfilling a role within a system. It is about:

  • challenging the status quo
  • providing a vision, direction and strategy
  • communicating to motivate and inspire others to do their bit.

Therefore it is possible for people not in management roles to be leaders (distributed leadership), and it is possible for people in senior management roles to be:

  • good leaders / good managers
  • good leaders / bad managers
  • bad leaders / good managers
  • bad leaders / bad managers.

Warren Bennis summarised the distinction succinctly as: ‘managers are people who do things right and leaders are people who do the right things’.

Historical perspectives on leadership

In the early twentieth century much of the research into leadership would be generally classified as trait theory trying to identify specific physical and/or psychological characteristics (traits, attributes or competencies) that distinguish leaders from followers. This included research into ‘great men’ and charismatic leaders.

From the 1940s onwards the focus shifted from traits or characteristics to behaviours. Behavioural theory approaches to leadership sought to identify the types of behaviours that are most and least effective in the context of organisational performance. Lewin, Lippitt and White identified three basic leadership styles: authoritarian, democratic and laissez faire, based on what leaders do and how they behave, and concluded that democratic leadership approaches were generally best.

Later research in the 1980s by French et al and by Smith and Peterson showed that while this was generally the case, for productivity a present autocratic or authoritarian leader had the best results.

Style: Autocratic

  • Key features: Gave orders, task focussed, aloof
  • Liking for leader : Low
  • Atmosphere/morale : Aggressive, dependent, self-orientated
  • Productivity: High when leader present - Low when leader absent

Style: Democratic

  • Key features: Called for suggestions, discussed plans, behave as team members
  • Liking for leader : High
  • Atmosphere/morale : Friendly, group centred, task oriented. Good morale workers were interested, cooperative and cheerful.
  • Productivity: Relatively high – unaffected by presence of leader

Style: Laissez-faire

  • Key features: Left the group to its own devices. Minimal participation
  • Liking for leader : Low
  • Atmosphere/morale : Friendly, group centred, play oriented. Low morale, lack of interest.
  • Productivity: Low – worse when leader present

By the 1960s contingency theories of leadership came to the fore, recognising that the effectiveness of particular leadership behaviours or styles is contingent upon the demands of the situation such as historical, economic or political context.

The most recent wave of research (1980s onwards) has largely focussed on transformational leadership (including charismatic leadership and ‘new’ leadership) which is about a leader’s ability to energise and motivate others to perform beyond their original expectations.
