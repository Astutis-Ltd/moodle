Perception

Sensory information must be organised into meaningful units before it can be interpreted.

Research into visual perception (which has been studied in much greater detail than perception of other sensory data) has shown that information is primarily organised through pattern recognition. In the 1930s the Gestalt psychologists identified the ‘laws’ or principles of perceptual organisation.

Principle of similarity Objects that seem to belong together will be grouped together.
Principle of proximity Objects close to each other will be seen as forming a group even if they are dissimilar.
Principle of closure Closed figures are preferred to fragmented and disjointed lines. Gaps are filled in to reveal the complete object.
Principle of continuity Lines are seen as following the smoothest path.
Principle of pragnanz Pragnanz means good figure – also known as the rule of simplicity. Reality is reduced to the simplest form possible.
Principle of good Gestalt Symmetrical and well-rounded figures are preferred to less structured forms.
Figure ground (Multi-stable perception) One aspect of an event is perceived as the figure or fore- ground and the other as the ground or back-ground.

Many researchers have suggested that pattern recognition is the basis of perception. Two major theories have been proposed:

Template matching theory suggests that ‘templates’ stored in the memory for any given pattern or shape. When a new object is seen it is matched with an existing template as an indication of what the pattern represents. Template matching uses a ‘top down’ approach to explain object perception by matching it with stored information. The major problem with this approach is that patterns cannot be identified unless they have already been stored in memory.

Distinctive feature analysis has formed the basis of much research into pattern perception. The approach suggests that the whole form of the pattern is less significant than crucial features which always occur in the target character regardless of other variables. This is a ‘bottom up’ approach in that it is stimulus driven and not memory driven. Challenged to identify a fruit that is green in colour and round in shape most people would recognise the distinctive features of an apple!

The phenomenon of seeing patterns that do not actually exist, such as faces in clouds, or the man in the moon, is called apophenia.

Perceptual set

The state of mental readiness for action is known as ‘set’ and it enables an individual to anticipate what is coming and act accordingly.

Perception can be ‘set’ by various factors including: attitudes and prejudices, emotional state, and motivation, though expectation is the most significant.

James Bruner’s research in the 1950s showed how strongly expectation could influence perception in a simple study showing people either numbers or letters, in a series, one at a time. An ambiguous figure that could be seen as a 13 or a B was then shown. All who had been shown a series of numbers saw it as 13, whereas those who had viewed letters saw a B.

Most people would expect a dial to work clockwise, a switch to flick down for ‘on’, or the cold tap to be on the right.

Stereotypes

A stereotype is an expectation about the characteristics, attributes, or behaviours of members of certain groups.

Social identity theory suggests that individuals need to map and define their identity in the context of group membership, and uphold the values of their group relative to others. Thus stereotypes are formed.

Stereotypes tend to be more focused than categories such as: age, sex, race, religion, politics and occupation, and are based upon comparisons of average or typical characteristics in one group against another.

Risk perception

HSE research has identified eight distinct factors that will influence an individual’s perception of risk:

Psychological time and risk

Warnings about the link between smoking and lung cancer have been ineffective in stopping people smoking, because the time lag between smoking and the onset of lung cancer can be up to 40 years.

If workers in a chemical plant were instructed to evacuate the workplace because of a leak of a toxic substance that posed the same level of risk but with imminent effects they would not hesitate.

Numerical representations of risk

Many people experience difficulty in understanding and interpreting statistical probabilities, thus there is the need to introduce additional qualitative characteristics to enable the conceptualisation of risk.

Risky situation or risky individual?

Individual differences and situational factors can interact and prompt risk taking behaviours.

Researchers that emphasise the significance of the individual variables have identified links between maturity, personality types and risk taking behaviour.

One theory suggests that individuals are equipped with ‘risk thermostats’, and that safety interventions do not affect the setting of the thermostat. Regardless of workplace controls the individual will behave in a way that maintains the level of risk with which he was originally content.

Perceived control

When people feel in control of a risk they don’t feel stressed by it. This has been shown to be a key factor in the acceptability of risk.

Familiarity

Research has shown that there is truth in the old adage ‘familiarity breeds contempt’. People tend to underestimate familiar risks and overestimate unfamiliar risks.

Framing effects

The way risk based data is presented (or framed) has been shown to introduce significant biases. Changing the description of a risk from positive to negative (i.e. talk about costs rather than benefits, or losses rather than gains) will have a measurable effect on observed behaviour.

Perceptions of vulnerability

People with the perception of low vulnerability or invulnerability (to the consequences of smoking, drink driving, sexual activity etc.) are not likely to modify their behaviour.

Some people have an ‘unrealistic optimism’, and assume that the harmful consequences will happen to someone else. This makes them more willing to take risks in all areas of their lives, including the workplace. Young men, in particular, may be prone to feeling invulnerable.

Perception of hazardous substances

Workplace studies have shown workers to perceive the risk associated with water based pesticides to be lower than solvent based, because water is natural and inert, and must therefore be lower risk than solvent. This is irrational as it does not consider the risk of the pesticide itself.
