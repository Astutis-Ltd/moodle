Attention


Everyone knows what attention is. It is the taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one of what seems several stimulus objects, or trains of thought.

William James, (1890)

Without filtering mechanisms human beings would be overwhelmed with sensory information. The attention mechanisms enable valuable or interesting data to be processed whilst ignoring the rest.

Attention has been extensively researched. There are three areas of research particularly relevant to health and safety:

‣
sustained attention (vigilance)

‣
selective attention

‣
divided attention.



Sustained attention

Experiments into sustained attention, in the 1950s, explored how long an individual could perform a repetitive task while maintaining a level of focused attention. The accuracy of the participants work was seen to decrease over time, an effect termed ‘performance decrement’. The degree of performance decrement was reduced by the following intervention.

Task
‣
Central positioning of control displays

‣
Use of brighter and longer signals.

Individual
‣
Feedback on performance (positive or negative)

‣
Moderate doses of amphetamines

‣
High introversion (personality profile scores).

Working environment
‣
Moderate disturbance (for example, telephones)

‣
Presence of others especially managers.



Selective attention

Donald Broadbent worked with air traffic controllers during the second world war and researched how they were able to focus their attention (selectively attend). Broadbent’s research involved overloading them with stimuli so that they had too much information to process at the same time.

Broadbent’s main conclusion was that people can pay attention to only one channel at a time (single channel processing) and that the filter, which selects the channel, does it on the basis of the physical characteristics of the information coming in and did not consider the meaning of the message.

Later research (Cherry, 1953) identified the ‘cocktail party effect’ where particularly relevant information (such as a person’s name) was picked up by the research participants’ unoccupied ear, whilst actively listening to information with the other ear.


Divided attention

Allport’s module resource theory proposed that a number of limited-capacity processing modules exist. The theory explains how it is easier to divide attention between dissimilar tasks (using different processing modules) than it is to divide attention between similar tasks. Similar tasks compete for resources from the same processing module resulting in interference and consequently a loss of attention.
