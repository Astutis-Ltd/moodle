Sensation


Information from sensory receptors (via the below table) is translated into electrical, neural impulses that can be transmitted along sensory neurons to the brain. 

The retina (eye) The brightness of light entering the eye is interpreted by rod cells. Colour is discerned by cone cells.
The cochlea (ear) Detects amplitude (loudness) and frequency (pitch) from sound waves transmitted through the ear.
The olfactory epithelium (nose) Responds to chemicals carried in the air, interpreted as smell. It is not known how many types of olfactory receptors there are.
Taste buds (mouth and tongue) Sweet, sour, bitter or salty tastes are detected from chemicals dissolved in saliva.
Tactile receptors (the skin) Pain receptors, mechanoreceptors (force) and thermo-receptors (temperature) are present in various concentrations throughout the skin.
Proprioception (the body and inner ear) Respond to information from within the body about the position of muscles and limbs. Includes balance receptors and kinaesthetic receptors which respond to information about movement.

The quality of the input data into the process may be compromised by genetic defects, illness, damage, ageing, or environmental factors:

‣
Colour-blindness (genetic)

‣
A cold affecting the sense of smell (illness)

‣
Vibration White Finger reducing the sensitivity of the tactile receptors in the fingertips (damage)

‣
Presbyacusis (hearing loss due to ageing)

‣
Extremely low temperatures affecting touch or background noise impeding hearing (environmental).


Medical screening and health surveillance can be useful in identifying potentially vulnerable individuals before (too much) harm is caused.
