Human components of work systems

Sensors (human senses)

The human body receives information through sensory receptors for sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch and proprioception. Vision is the dominant sense, followed by hearing, though sense of touch and kinaesthetic feedback are also important in the design of controls.

Processes

The brain manages both low level processes to control basic sensori-motor responses, and higher level cognitive processing which allow for planning, problem solving and decision making.

Some models compare the brain to a computer system and refer to ‘online’ and ‘offline’ processing.

Online processing relates to decision-making whilst the control process is operational and are typically skill based actions involving fine-tuning of the physical control of a process.

Offline processing tends to occur between operations and involves decision making and problem solving to refine processes going forward. Decisions of this type tend to be knowledge based.

Effectors

The primary effectors are the means by which information is entered into the machine, i.e. the hands, the feet and the voice. The feet can effect simple controls such as the accelerator, brake and clutch in a car when driving.

The hands have the most scope for effective control and offer a range of options for manipulating objects, from precision grips, for example, holding a pen between fingertip and thumb, to a power grip where a lever is grasped at the base of the fingers and palm.

Machine components

Controls

Effective human interaction with machines is dependent upon the design and construction of suitable controls which can be engaged by the effectors.

Controls can also provide direct feedback, for example, when turning a car steering wheel the level of resistance provides information about road conditions and/or tyre pressure.

Control design is discussed in more detail later.

The controlled process

The controlled process is what the machine does, for example, a pillar drill making holes in sheet metal or a nuclear fission reactor producing electricity in a power station.

Display

Operating traditional machinery utilises information received directly from the control process and from feedback. Information is also provided indirectly through dials and gauges.

Control design

Anthropometric data should be used to determine appropriate dimensions for controls and the controls should be designed and positioned to operate in low stress postures with minimal pressure on body parts. The following variables should also be considered:

Force

If high levels of force are required the design should allow for it to be applied easily, for example, through the use of a power grip design rather than a precision grip.

Feedback

Accurate controls are engineered to ensure there is a high level of correspondence between the movement of the control and the response of the controlled process, for example, the movement of a steering wheel and the corresponding movement of the vehicle on the road.

Layout

High priority controls should be located so that they are easily seen and easily accessed. (The optimum reach envelope). All controls should be within the zone of maximum reach.

Functional groupings

Controls with related functions should be grouped together. Care should be taken to ensure that operation of one control does not cause interference with adjacent controls.

Sequential controls should be laid out as per the operating sequence. Interlocks can be used to ensure that controls can only be operated in the correct sequence.

Expectation

Cultural influences will need to be considered, for example, in Europe flicking a switch downwards turns the system on. In the United States of America the same action would switch the system off.

Controls on similar machines will be expected to perform in a similar way.

Compatibility

The direction of movement of a control should be compatible with the movement of the corresponding display.

Distinctiveness

Combinations of shape, colour, texture and size are used to provide effective means of distinguishing between controls.

Shape Geometric shapes can be used to distinguish between types of control, giving both visual and tactile cues. Research suggests operatives can cope with up to 12 different options on a control panel.
Colour Colour choices should be limited to red, orange, yellow, green and blue. The following factors should be considered:
‣
The control should stand out against the background in terms of colour and contrast

‣
Expectations, for example, red is danger / green is safe

‣
Reduced colour discrimination / colour blindness

‣
Strong colours strongly attract attention – and should be used appropriately.

Note: Colour codes are only effective in a well-lit environment.
Texture Tactile characteristics such as rough or smooth, hard or soft may be useful to differentiate controls where lighting is poor.
Size The use of three size groupings – small, medium and large (with a minimum of 15 mm diameter and 10 mm thickness differences) corresponding to different control activities has been shown to be effective.

Positions

The control should be in a position relative to the display where both can be viewed simultaneously by the operator without compromising comfort.

Control layouts should be spatially compatible (in a similar layout) to that of the display. Think about the relationship between the controls and the burners on a cooker hob. This will illustrate the point.

Labelling

Labels should be on or close to the control they relate to.

Labels written traditionally from left to right are easiest to interpret, and the quickest to read and react to. Plain English and/or readily understood symbols of an adequate size are essential.

Problems can arise from:

‣
language or literacy issues

‣
damage or obscuration from wear and tear or environmental factors.

Operational methods

Switches, buttons and dials can be used to distinguish between different control functions.

Display design

The primary objective of any display is to ensure that the required information can be received and understood with the necessary levels of efficiency and effectiveness.

Specific factors to consider include:

‣
Dials: Most populations will ‘expect’ a dial to be read clockwise. A moving pointer on a dial is suitable for global estimation (i.e. where are we now compared to maximum or dangerous levels?) and the perception of rapid change. Dials are not effective for providing warnings or handling complex information

‣
Digital displays: Can be difficult to read when data is changing rapidly, but provide a useful service where accuracy is required and where numerical data is presented constantly or with minimum changes.

Ergonomic design of crane cabs

HSE have identified two main ergonomic concerns with the design of crane cabs used offshore:

  • Poor working posture – much of the work is done sitting, often leaning forwards, head position will vary considerably to follow loads
  • Difficulty to use – controls can require continuous operator application for considerable periods of time. Space is often limited and control location is compromised as a consequence.

Design improvements have included:

  • Routing steering columns through the dash rather than the floor to optimize floor space and leg room
  • Providing adjustable controls to fit the operator and mounting gauges on the steering column for easier visibility
  • Mounting microphones in a position that allows comfortable communication.

Ergonomic design of aircraft cockpits

The term ‘glass cockpit’ is used to describe a modern cockpit with electronic / digital displays.

Many aviation accidents (for example, Kegworth) have been caused by unrecognised or improperly interpreted instrument readouts. Ergonomically designed displays are intended to prevent such errors.

Information depicted on each instrument is grouped according to necessity and function and instruments and controls are located in optimal positions for comfortable view and easy hand access.

Advanced software and hardware technologies are used to reduce pilot workload and enhance intuitive decision-making.

Mental fit, task complexity and performance

As has been discussed ergonomic is as much about mental fit as it is about physical fit. Socio-technical systems should be designed to make best use of human skills and to compensate for human weaknesses.

Standal (2013) identified seven key objective task complexity factors that can impact upon human performance as shown in table 2.23.

Note also the additional impacts that the organisation (through the imposition of time pressure); and the individual (through personal characteristics such as knowledge, training and personal attributes) can have on subjective task complexity.

Task analysis – Predicting and preventing error

Task analysis is a “catch-all” term for a range of similar techniques that involve taking a task, activity or process and systematically breaking it down into its component steps or actions.

Industrial engineers use the approach to improve task efficiency; and ergonomists to identify and address potential for human failures.

In general health and safety practice a job safety analysis (aka job step analysis or job hazard analysis) is often used to identify hazards and otential human errors at each action step in a process with a view to introducing corresponding preventive and / or protective measures.

When using such reductionist (breaking down) techniques it is important to not lose sight of the whole. A holistic view can often lead to a top level solution that makes more sense overall than a series of lower level solutions.

The basic stages of a job safety analysis can be remembered as SREDIM.

Select Select an appropriate task to be analysed. JSA is not suitable for jobs defined too broadly e.g. “overhauling an engine”; or too narrowly, e.g. “positioning car jack.”
Record Record each step in the process. Observing somebody actually doing the task helps to ensure the process is accurately captured.
Examine

Examine each step to identify hazards. Tools such as MEEP (Materials / Equipment / Environment / People) can be used to help identify all potential hazards – unsafe acts and conditions.

A team approach with input from operational and supervisory staff is usually recommended.
Develop Develop a safe system of work using hierarchical approaches to specify appropriate control strategies.
Implement Implement the safe system of work, ensuring appropriate consultation and worker involvement.
Monitor Monitor the ongoing effectiveness of the system and revise as necessary.
