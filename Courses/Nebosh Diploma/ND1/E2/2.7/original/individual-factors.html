Individual factors


Physical stressors and human reliability

Physical stressors such as heat, cold, noise, vibration and light may all impact upon human reliability.


Heat

High internal temperatures (38.5°C) have been shown to increase the speed of performance due to an acceleration of the body’s internal clock. Risk taking behaviours and error rates also increase with high internal temperatures. The relationship is dose responsive – diminution of performance increases the longer workers internal temperature remains high.

Relative humidity also has an impact. 35 to 40% is the optimal range, while levels over 75% have been shown to lead to diminished performance.


Cold

Cold causes peripheral vasoconstriction, shivering, impaired neural conduction and poor muscular control, all of which may affect reaction times. Research has shown that work at lower temperatures (6°C) was slower and more cautious than work at 18-24°C.


Noise

Noise levels can:

‣
affect the performance of auditory tasks by masking important audible cues

‣
interfere with mental processes (too loud to hear yourself think!)

‣
lead to a reduction in satisfaction with the working environment.


Vibration

Vibration can affect task performance by causing body parts to resonate, degrading vision and manual control. At 5 hertz (Hz) the shoulder girdle resonates, between 20 and 30 Hz the head resonates between the shoulders and at higher frequencies the eyes may also resonate.


Light

Poor workplace lighting can cause discomfort or disabling glare, visual fatigue and eye strain. A lack of natural lighting usually leads to increased levels of dissatisfaction with the working environment.

Sunlight also has biochemical and behavioural effects on humans and is an important cue for the ‘body clock’ or circadian rhythms. Lack of exposure to daylight, due to night shift work, can give rise to ‘shift lag’, (displacement of the body clock).


Under-stimulation, fatigue and stress

Under-stimulation

Having too little to do or having a job that does not offer stimulation, variety or challenge can also be sources of stress, causing ‘rust-out’ (as opposed to the ‘burn out’ caused by over-stimulation).

‘Rust-out’, due to boredom, and lack of stimulation in the workplace can be prevented by changing the ‘micro’ work environment through job-enrichment e.g. increasing workers’ skills, providing more autonomy in the job and more opportunities for decision making.

By improving the amount of skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy and feedback, it is possible to improve both motivation and job performance and reduce levels of stress.

Fatigue

Fatigue may be considered to be a temporary inability or a decrease in ability or a strong disinclination to respond to a situation, because of inadequate recuperation from previous mental, emotional or physical over- activity.

The symptoms of fatigue may include muscle pain, difficulty in concentrating, or sleepiness and the resulting decline in mental and / or physical performance.

The HSE considers that a fatigued person will be less alert, less able to process information, will have slower reaction times and less interest in working compared to a person who is not fatigued. These issues together with memory lapses, absent-mindedness, decreased awareness, lack of attention, underestimation of risk, reduced coordination etc. can lead to errors and accidents, ill-health and injury.

Most occupational fatigue symptoms result from disturbances of sleep and the circadian rhythm / biological clock. Reports of fatigue are higher among shift-workers than among day-workers and highest among night workers.

The main causes of fatigue are:

‣
Loss of sleep (known as sleep deficit or sleep debt) which may be:

‣
‘Acute’, e.g. having 5 hours sleep instead of the usual 8 on a given night

‣
‘Cumulative’ as a consequence of having 7 hours sleep instead of the usual 8 over several nights

‣
Poor quality sleep – lots of interruptions

‣
Having to work at a ‘low point’ in the day e.g. early hours of the morning, mid to late afternoon and after a meal

‣
Long working hours, particularly if these are as long as 14 to 16 hours

‣
Poorly-designed shift work

‣
Inadequate breaks during the working day.


Workload is also a consideration in that workers are more easily fatigued if their work is machine-paced, complex or monotonous.

Stress

Stress is the adverse reaction people have to excessive pressures or other types of demand placed on them. There is a clear distinction between pressure, which can create a ‘buzz’ and be a motivating factor, and stress, which can occur when this pressure becomes excessive.

Stress can result in a range of effects that can affect an individual’s physical well-being, increase the likelihood of human error in the workplace and impact negatively upon the organisation.

Attitude

An attitude represents an individual’s degree of like or dislike (positive or negative view) of an object, where the object may be a person, place, thing, or event.

An attitude involves thoughts, feelings and predispositions to act towards an object. (i.e. involves the head, the heart and the hands!).

Attitudes can be modified as a consequence of feedback on behaviour and are also influenced by the prevailing attitude of a peer group.

The early understanding of the relationship between attitude and behaviour was often stated as an ABC model, i.e. the attitude prompted behaviour, which led to a consequence. A favourable consequence reinforces the attitude and subsequent behaviour and an unfavourable consequence weakened the attitude. However research failed to prove a direct causal relationship between attitudes and behaviours.

Richard La Piere’s (1934) article “Attitudes versus Actions” explored racist attitudes and behaviours in California. He visited 251 hotels and restaurants with a Chinese companion and was refused entry only once. A later questionnaire asking the same establishments if they would accept Chinese guests received 128 responses with 92% responding ‘No’.Clearly other contextual factors meant that the behaviour wasdemonstrably different to the predominant racist attitude.

The causal link between attitude and behaviour is much weaker than the reverse link between behaviour and attitude. When a person’s behaviour changes and becomes incompatible with the corresponding attitude a state of discomfort, known as “cognitive dissonance” occurs. To resolve the discomfort the attitude changes to be consistent with the newly-adopted behaviour.

Skills

The knowledge, skills and abilities framework (KSA) is a common tool used by human resources professionals to match a workers qualities to a given role. In this context skills are the capabilities or proficiencies developed through training or hands-on experience, as opposed to abilities which are innate. Whereas knowledge is theoretical, skill relates to the practical application of that knowledge.

Note the definition of skill compared to competence – which is the ability to conform to a required standard or competency which has a broader definition.

Clearly it is important that workers are demonstrably capable of undertaking the tasks required of them. The more critical the task the greater the requirement for skilled, competent workers.


Habits

Woods (2017) noted that modern social and personality psychology has paid scant attention to habits until relatively recently This is due to outdated historical research that placed habits in conflict with goals. In modern theory, habits are considered to be implicit context–response associations in memory that guides human responses in conjunction with goals.

Early researchers proposed a variety of reinforcement-based models of habit. Thorndike’s laws (1911) highlighted learning through rewards and response repetition and Skinner’s (1938) work on operant conditioning also emphasized the importance of responding to rewards.

Modern research suggests that habits develop as people repeat an action for a reward. Through instrumental learning, implicit cognitive associations form between the recurring features of the action and the rewarded response. As habits strengthen, people naturally become less sensitive to rewards. Habits are not goal dependent. Habits are an important part of the brain’s framework, and account for around 40% of human behaviour. They may be performed quickly, efficiently, and rigidly, and with limited thought, allowing individuals to perform routine behaviours without needing to make small decisions every step of the way.

Allowing daily actions to be driven by routine offers certain benefits as it conserves mental reserves for concerns that require active consideration, but it also comes with drawbacks. Notably it can cause people to perform actions on autopilot when they should be paying attention.

Getting into good routine safety behaviours, such as pre-commencement checks or donning PPE at shift start-up can quickly become habit forming. Studies on habit formation indicate that a minimum of 21 days is required to form a new habit, whereas the median timeframe is 66 days.


Personality

Personality is made up of a person’s unique characteristic pattern of thoughts, feelings and behaviours. Personality arises from within the individual and remains fairly consistent throughout life.

Personality has certain fundamental characteristics, including:

Consistency: People act in the same ways or similar ways in a variety of situations.

Psychological and physiological: Personality is a psychological construct, but is influenced by biological processes and needs.

Impact upon behaviours: Personality does not just influence behaviour; it causes people to act in certain ways.

Multiple expressions: Personality is expressed in thoughts, feelings, close relationships and other social interactions.

There are many models of the aspects of human personality. Costa and McCrae’s five factor model (also known as the Big 5 or OCEAN) identified five main aspects of personality as shown below.
