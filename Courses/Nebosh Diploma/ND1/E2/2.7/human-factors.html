Human factors

Introduction

The definition of human factors used in HSG48 is:

“Human factors refer to environmental, organisational and job factors, and human and individual characteristics which influence behaviour at work in a way which can affect health and safety.”

A simple way to view human factors is to think about three aspects: the individual, the job, and the organisation and how they impact on people’s health and safety-related behaviour.

The individual and the job come together to create a risk interface. The job takes place within the context of the organisational culture – this is often the greatest influence on workplace behaviour.

Ergonomics

The literal interpretation of ergonomics is ‘law of work’ (from the Greek – Ergon = work and Nomos = natural law). The word was created by Professor Wojciech Jastrzebowski in Poland in 1857.

World War II saw the birth of the science now referred to as ergonomics. As the design of aeroplanes and tanks etc. became increasingly sophisticated, it became increasingly important to consider the design of the operator / machinery interface to reduce the likelihood of human error and disastrous consequences.

Professor Hywell Murrell proposed the name ergonomics for the burgeoning new science at a meeting of the British Admiralty in 1949. The Ergonomic Society was then formed in the UK in 1952 and The Human Factors Society was formed in the US in 1957.

The International Ergonomics Association (IEA 2000) defined ergonomics (or human factors) as:

The scientific discipline concerned with the understanding of the interactions among humans and other elements of a system, and the profession that applies theoretical principles, data and methods to design in order to optimize human well-being and overall system performance.

The IEA has also defined three areas of specialisation within ergonomics:

  • Physical Ergonomics – concerned with human anatomical, anthropometric, physiological and biomechanical characteristics as they relate to physical activity (for example, working postures, materials handling, repetitive movements, MSD / WRULDs). Physical ergonomics and anthropometry and physiology in particular are further discussed below
  • Cognitive Ergonomics – concerned with mental processes, such as perception, memory, reasoning, and motor response, as they affect interactions among humans and other elements of a system (for example, mental workload, decision-making, skilled performance, human-computer interaction, human reliability, work stress and training)
  • Organisational Ergonomics – concerned with the optimisation of sociotechnical systems, including their organisational structures, policies, and processes (for example, communication, crew resource management, work design, teamwork, organisational culture etc.).

Physical Ergonomics

Definitions of the contributing sciences are shown below.

Human Anatomy The science of the structure of the human body and the relation of its parts.
Human Physiology

The study of the functions of the human body – in particular the physiological capacity of the human: strength, work capacity, and the result of exceeding these capacities, fatigue, includes:

  • Work physiology – expenditure of energy
  • Environmental physiology – effects on humans of the physical environment. (Discussed below).
Biomechanics Is the science concerned with the internal and external forces acting on the human body and the effects produced by these forces, i.e. the application of forces by gravity and muscles.
Anthropometry The scientific study of the measurements and proportions of the human body.

Anthropometry

Anthropometry translates literally as ‘the measurement of man’ (Greek – anthropos = man and metron = measure). Anthropometric data is used to specify the physical dimensions of work space and work equipment and ensures that the task is designed to fit the man.

The natural variations of human populations have significant implications for the design of equipment etc. If the scale is from the smallest adult female to the largest adult male in a population, the male will be 30 to 40% taller, 100% heavier, and 500% stronger.

In non-safety critical situations equipment design accommodates 90% of the population, based upon a normal distribution.

Physiology

Health and nutrition, previous injuries, lifestyles and natural abilities all play a part in contributing to a person’s physiological capacity to undertake a specific task. Strength, capacity, endurance and fatigue all vary with physiological differences.

Strength

Strength is affected by a number of factors:

  • Gender: On average men are 33% to 50% stronger than women
  • Age: Muscle strength peaks are reached in men at about 20 years old and in women a few years earlier. Maximal cardiac efficiency and muscle strength decrease significantly with age. A 65 year old man has about 70% of the mean value of aerobic power for a 25-year-old
  • Training: Muscle strength can be enhanced through training. In an occupational setting this must be highly specific to the movements required by the task to be effective.

Work capacity

An individual’s capacity to undertake physical work can be measured directly by examining maximal oxygen uptake (ability to take in O2 in the blood via the lungs), or indirectly by measuring heart rate. Heart rate is a reliable proxy measure of workload and is easily measured in the workplace (O2 uptake measurements require specialist laboratory equipment).

A moderate work rate would see young, healthy workers utilising approximately 25-30% of their maximal aerobic power (oxygen uptake) (heart rate of 110 beats per minute) over an eight hour work shift.

Exceeding these limits can lead to fatigue and loss of coordination, with consequential errors and injuries. Environmental factors such as temperature, humidity and air velocity need to be considered carefully as they are likely to negatively impact upon the performance of individuals doing strenuous work.

Endurance

Efficiency of muscular contraction is necessary to enhance endurance and work capacity. Work tasks can be designed to facilitate this by:

  • eliminating unnecessary movements
  • using muscles according to their correct function
  • making use of body weight and momentum and of gravity
  • maintaining balance
  • varying movements, position and posture
  • employing postures which allow maximum torque
  • using accessory supports for counterthrust or stability
  • providing opportunity for training and practice (to improve co-ordination and reduce unnecessary contraction).

Physical fatigue

If particular movements are carried out continuously it is reasonable to expect all the muscles to tire. Stabilising (static) muscle work is more fatiguing than active muscle work (contractions that cause movement).
