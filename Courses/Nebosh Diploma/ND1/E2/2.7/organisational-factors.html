Organisational factors

The effect of organisational factors on culture

The HSE’s human factors model shows how leadership, culture, provision of resources etc impact upon behaviours at work. Leadership (as discussed in section 2.2) and culture are opposite sides of the same coin. It is, or should be, the organisation’s leaders that establish and drive core values and set the style and tone of expected behaviour. Simply speaking leadership should drive culture, which drives behaviour, which drives performance.

The standards of an organisation are typically the lowest standards that the senior leaders are willing to accept.

The lack of leaders who have bought into the importance of effective health and safety management within the organisation, will mean that health and safety does not become established as a core value and the organisation does not develop or maintain appositive safety culture.

Senior leadership commitment demonstrated by ‘walking the walk’, leading by example, making sure adequate resources are provided etc is essential as is driving those values and the commitment t them throughout all levels of the organisation.

Organisational factors that affect human reliability

Working patterns

Flexible working patterns offer potential benefits to employers and employees. Employers might experience increased productivity and employment, whereas workers may experience greater control and flexibility, and in some cases, reduced stress. However, the increased adoption of flexible work and employment patterns may also pose potential challenges for health and safety including:

  • Part-time and temporary workers may not receive equal training opportunities or health and safety protection as full-time, permanent employees;
  • Less secure forms of employment may generate feelings of job insecurity and stress, as a result of isolation or conflicting demands between work and home life;
  • Temporary workers are likely to be at increased risk of injury, as the risk of workplace injury is increased during the first 4 months within a new job; and
  • The injury rate per hour worked is higher for part time workers than those working full-time.

Homeworking

The growth area of work over the past decade or so has been in teleworking which uses the home as a base, but is distinct from homeworking in that much of the work is conducted from alternative locations such as the car and hotels. The potential health and safety issues relating to these forms of working are still being researched. The covid-19 related lockdowns of 2020 and 2021 has led to a huge increase in remote working which provides a significant population for ongoing research.

Hours of work

HSE research has shown the relative injury incident rates for employees working longer and shorter hours than the weekly average of 39.5 hours. Generally speaking:

  • the risk of injury decreases with the number of hours worked;
  • those working less than 16 hours a week have double the rate of injury to those working 30-50 hours a week; and
  • the increased risk is independent of other occupational factors.

Shift work

There is no specific definition of shift work in law, but it usually means:

  • a work activity scheduled outside standard daytime hours, where there may be a handover of duty from one individual or work group to another; or
  • a pattern of work where one employee replaces another on the same job within a 24 hour period.

The HSE estimates that around 14% of the working population (3.6 million people) are doing shift work ‘most of the time,’

Early research into the effects of shift work revealed that around 10% of employees enjoyed it; 70% accepted it and 20% were unable to cope with the demands and “dropped out”. The 20% that drop out are likely to be those with the lowest tolerance and the research results should

HSE commissioned research into shift work and ill health has shown that:

  • Mortality rates for shift workers are no different to the population at large but are higher than for day workers;
  • Rates of cardiovascular disease are higher than in the population at large. This may be attributable to smoking, raised blood pressure and increased body mass;
  • Shift workers have a higher risk of gastric and duodenal ulcers, possibly due to irregular meal times, poor diet, smoking and alcohol consumption;
  • Shift workers have a greater tendency towards anxiety, depression and general malaise; and
  • Electroencephalography has shown that shift workers have less sleep and poorer quality sleep, the resulting increase in fatigue may result in unsafe practises.

The research suggested that all employees aged 40 and above should have regular health checks; and that the following classes of employee should not be allowed to shift work:

  • Workers on medication with critical dose schedules;
  • Workers with cardiovascular problems;
  • Workers with gastro intestinal disorders; and
  • Workers with epilepsy.

There is a general consensus that productivity, safety and health are all diminished as a consequence of shift work. The effects have been found:

  • to be higher on the night shift;
  • to rise with increasing shift length over eight hours;
  • to increase over successive shifts, especially if they are night shifts; and
  • to increase when there are not enough breaks night shift.

The main causal factors identified in research are:

  • Circadian Rhythms - humans are active and perform best during the day and need to sleep at night when performance is generally poorer. The ‘body clock’ sets the daily cycle of biological activities (circadian rhythms) such as chemical and hormone release that influence body activity. Biological activity is reduced at night which creates a need for sleep. The body clock can be adjusted over a period of weeks or months but in the interim shift workers experience ‘shift lag’ which is similar to jet lag.;
  • Sleep deprivation and fatigue – Research has shown that sleep deprived and/or fatigued workers performance diminishes and numbers of errors increases. This is particularly true for tasks requiring:
    • Vigilance and monitoring;
    • Decision making;
    • Awareness;
    • Fast reaction time;
    • Tracking ability; or
    • Memory.

It should be noted that operating conditions on night shifts may be very different, e.g. lower staffing levels, less management presence, and more maintenance activities.

Payment systems

A literature review into ‘piece rates’ (I.e. payment based on units produced or delivered, to increase productivity) and their effects on health and safety (Johansson et al, 2010). 27 of the 31relevant studied articles found negative effects of piece rates on different aspects of health and safety such as increased accident rates and injuries, and musculo-skeletal health problems.

The effect of weakness in health and safety management systems on human failure

From a traditional (command and control) management perspective clarity of policy and workplace standards is imperative. Workers must know what is expected of them and managers must know what standards to hold workers to account against. Lack of clarity and precision introduces potential for variability which is seen as a weakness providing potential for human failure. As discussed in section 2.5 the safety II view is that variability is as inevitable as the gaps between ‘work as prescribed’ and ‘work as done’ and preparing workers to be resilient and adaptable is the key to ongoing system success.

The planning for implementation stage of the health and safety management system (HSMS) development requires effective risk assessment through the co-ordinated efforts of all members of the organisation. The required task analyses of work activities should consider the potential for human failures as per HSG48 and use ergonomic design principles to manage that potential to acceptable levels.

In the ‘doing’ stage organising for health and safety requires appropriate arrangements are in place to address the 4 C’s of control, cooperation, communication and competence. Weaknesses in any of these areas is likely to increase the potential for human failure.

The back end of the system requires monitoring arrangements to be implemented to ensure that work is being undertaken safely, as expected. Proactive monitoring with appropriate timely interventions can have a significant impact on increasing positive behaviours in the workplace and thus reducing the likelihood of human failure.

The influence of formal and informal groups within the organisation

A social group is created when two or more people who interact with one another, share similar characteristics and have a sense of unity. People form groups for a variety of reasons, including security, social needs, goal achievement, shared interests and self-esteem.

In the workplace groups may be created as part of an organisations architecture or may develop informally amongst colleagues.

It is important to understand a little of ‘group dynamics’ to understand the positive and negative impacts that formal and informal groups can exert in an organisation.

Whether created as part of the organisation, or not, the key characteristics of a group will be:

  • Interaction
 Members interact with each other.
  • Interdependence
 Members rely upon each other.
  • Influence
 Members influence each other.
  • Sense of belonging
 Members feel a sense of belonging to the group.
  • Common Goals
 Members work together to achieve common goals.
  • Need satisfaction
 Members look to fulfil personal needs through group membership.
  • Roles and Norms
 Group interactions are structured through roles and norms.

The term ‘group dynamics’ was coined by Tuckman (1965) who suggested that group behaviour tended to follow a pattern of development as shown in the below table.

Group norms, conformity and compliance

Group norms are unspoken rules that develop over time, establishing acceptable and unacceptable practice and defining expected standards of behaviour.

Group norms establish:

  • the core values of the group;
  • the framework for acceptable social behaviour; and
  • establish the groups distinct identity.

Once a group norm has been established there is a tendency for group members to conform to the norm, as a result of social or “peer pressure”. Peer group pressure arises where a group has established its values and normed its behaviours and will not tolerate group members expressing incompatible opinions or behaving differently.

Formal and informal discussions with groups of workers such as safety committees, safety circles, departmental meetings etc may be affected if there is a reluctance to express different opinions.

Compliance relates to responses to direct requests from the group leader. Group members will tend to comply with such requests without modifying their personal attitudes and beliefs.

Group decision making

Research into group decision making has identified several interesting phenomenon:

Group think: A cohesive group can be unduly influenced by pressures to conform and comply. Situations may arise when the group become overly focused on agreement and unanimous decision at the expense of a realistic solution to the problem at hand.

Group polarisation: James Stoner (1961) identified that group decisions were often riskier than individual decisions, a phenomenon termed “risky shift”. Subsequent research has shown that group decisions may also shift towards an overly cautious / conservative decision. The polarisation of views has been attributed to the normative and informational influences of the group.

Social loafing: Most of the time people work harder in a group than they do as individuals, however some individuals may expect the other group members to manage without them and ease off and take a back seat. This is known as social loafing.

Group memory: A group has demonstrably better memory recall than the individual member with the best memory.

Communication mechanisms and their impact on human failure

Spoken and written communication can be critical in maintaining safety. This can include general communications in the form of safety information; communications between team members or between different teams during operations or maintenance work; and emergency communications.

All personnel require access to key information to help them safely negotiate the hazards in the workplace.

Communications are particularly important in a wide range of safety critical tasks and activities such as lifting operations, emergency response, entry to confined spaces, as well as coordination of activities between different parties and organisations.

The key principles to be adopted for safety critical communications can be summarised as:

  • Use of risk assessments to identify who needs to communicate, and what their communication needs are;
  • Selection of appropriate media (e.g. face-to-face, procedure, radio) and methods (e.g. written, verbal);
  • Consideration of the timings of key communications e.g. draw attention to hazards before people are required to carry out tasks;
  • Use of appropriate language (consider literacy, first language and jargon);
  • Highlighting safety critical steps in procedures and draw attention to them in training;
  • For important messages use of two or more methods/media of communication e.g. written back up to a verbal communication
  • Recognising that safety signage is not a substitute for communicating, though it may be part of it.

Failure of communication at shift handover has contributed to the cause of many industrial accidents including the Sellafield Beach Incident (1983) (not on syllabus) where highly radioactive waste liquor was discharged to sea, due to a failure of communication between shifts; and Piper Alpha 1988.

Effective communication is essential when a task and its associated responsibilities are handed over to another person or work team. This can occur at shift changeover, between shift and day workers, or between different functions of an organisation within a shift e.g. operations and maintenance.

The goal of the handover is the accurate reliable communication of task-relevant information across shift changes or between teams to ensure continuity of safe and effective working.

Effective handover consists of three elements:

  • A period of preparation by out-going personnel;
  • Handover where out-going and in-coming personnel communicate to exchange task-relevant information; and
  • Cross-checking of information by in-coming personnel as they assume responsibility for the task.

To ensure effective handovers it is essential that high risk handover situations are identified and that clear, effective handover procedures are in place. Al staff should understand the safety critical nature of shift handover and the importance of taking their part in the process seriously. Consideration should be given to the communication skills of those involved and additional training and support should be provided as required. When planning maintenance works all efforts should be made to ensure the work can be completed in a single shift, thus avoiding the need for a handover.

Shift handover should be:

  • Conducted face-to-face;
  • Two-way, with both participants taking joint responsibility;
  • Done using both verbal and written communication;
  • Based on an analysis of the information needs of incoming staff;
  • Given as much time and resource as necessary.

Improvements should also be made by designing support equipment, such as logs and computer displays, with consideration of the operator’s needs; and involving the end-users when implementing any changes to existing communication methods at shift handover.
