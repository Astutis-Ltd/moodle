Improving individual human reliability

As discussed earlier the coming together of the individual and the job creates a risk interface where it is possible for hazards to injure the individual or for individual error to adversely affect the task or equipment and create further hazards.

Ergonomics is all about ensuring the physical and psychological fit between the individual and the task and equipment. Understanding the ways that humans fail and the variables that influence the likelihood of failure facilitate the effective design of tasks and equipment to fit the individual and reduce the likelihood of failure.

The variables which make it more or less likely that human(s) will fail are termed Performance Influencing Factors (PIFs). Optimising the characteristics of the job, the individual and the organisation that influence human performance will reduce the likelihood of all types of human failure.

The personal performance influencing factors (PIFs) are:

  • physical capability and condition
  • fatigue (acute from temporary situation, or chronic)
  • stress / morale
  • work overload / under load
  • competence to deal with circumstances
  • motivation vs. other priorities.

Most of these factors have been discussed elsewhere in this element and unit. This section discusses:

  • The role of health surveillance and occupational health support in ensuring or physically and psychologically fit to work
  • The role of health and safety training in evaluating competence, identifying training needs and evaluating the impact of training interventions on human reliability.

Health surveillance and occupational health support

Health surveillance

Regulation 6 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations requires every employer to provide his employees with appropriate health surveillance having regard to the risks to their health and safety which are identified by the assessment.

Health surveillance is about putting in place systematic, regular and appropriate procedures to detect early signs of work-related ill health among employees exposed to certain health risks and acting on the results to prevent any progression of work- related ill health.

Health surveillance begins with a baseline assessment of health when an employee takes up a new job followed by the use of appropriate techniques (from self-checks to medical surveillance and biological monitoring) to detect harmful health effects at an early stage so that workers can be protected and kept fir for work.

Baseline health assessments can help ensure that an individual is generally in good physical and mental health and has the required abilities to undertake a given role.

The HSE’s fatigue index can be used to evaluate the effects of rotating shift patterns. The index looks at the following five key factors associated with the development of fatigue to provide an overall index for the pattern of work:

  • time of day
  • breaks
  • shift duration
  • cumulative fatigue
  • rest periods.

The Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) can be used to determine the likelihood of dozing in a range of everyday situations.

Under the Working Time Regulations employers are required to ensure that workers are fit for night work and must offer a free health assessment to anyone who is about to start working nights and to all night workers on a regular basis. The HSE advises that if a worker suffers from health problems that are caused or made worse by night work he / she should, wherever possible be transferred to day work.

Health surveillance arrangements can be implemented to detect early signs of psychological harm form stress and strain in the workplace.

Occupational health support

Occupational health services aim to:

  • promote and maintain the highest degree of physical, mental and social well-being of workers in all occupations
  • prevent ill- health caused by working conditions
  • protect workers from occupational risks to their health
  • create and maintain an occupational environment adapted to each workers physiological and psychological capabilities
  • adapt work to each worker and each worker to the job.

Occupational health services can support stress management in the workplace in a number of ways including through risk assessments, counselling services, and return to work programmes.

Health and safety training

Training is a planned and systematic effort to modify or develop knowledge, skills and attitude through learning experiences to achieve effective performance in an activity or range of activities.

As shown earlier, training is one of many interrelated influences on an individual’s behaviour. It can be seen that the individual cannot be considered in isolation – the influence of the job as designed, and the organisational culture also impact upon behaviour.

Safety training may look to influence an individual’s attitude by improving knowledge of the hazards and the consequences of risky behaviour. However the behaviour will only change if the person desires that change, and if the work climate is supportive of the change.

  • The practical parameters of the project are determined, including:
    • timeline
    • delivery options
    • organisational contraints.

2. Design/develop

The process begins with a detailed specification of the learning outcomes and designing an appropriate means of assessing the learners’ fulfilment of the objectives. The training materials content is then developed and designed. The session is planned to appeal to the learning preferences of a target group and a suitable range of learning opportunities are designed using a range of different media.

3. Deliver

The training is delivered to plan. Learners are tested against the objectives and given opportunity to feedback on the event.

4. Implement

After completion of the training the learners return to the workplace to implement the learning under an appropriate level of supervision.

5. Evaluate

Workplace practice should be monitored over short, medium and longer terms to determine the level of practical success of the training programme.

Has the desired behaviour change been affected? Has the level of skilled work improved?

The Advisory Committee on Safety in the Nuclear Industry (ACSNI) published a report in 1990 considering the contribution made by training to safety. They concluded that for training in safety critical activities:

  • Health and safety training should be an on-going process, regularly reviewed and updated in keeping with technical and organisational developments. Safety should be an aspect of all organisational training and should focus on raising safety awareness, and understanding hazards and controls
  • Overconfidence has been found to be a precursor of major accident hazards as often as ignorance. Training therefore should not focus purely on knowledge transfer and development of technical skills but needs to be provided in the context of improving safety culture with a degree of emphasis on forethought and sensible risk minimisation
  • An independent assessment of competence is recommended before authorisations are issued by site management. Regular on-going assessments of competence are also recommended to guard against any gradual deterioration of performance over time.
