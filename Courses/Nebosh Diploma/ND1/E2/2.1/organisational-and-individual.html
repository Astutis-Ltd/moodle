Organisational and individual goals

The organisation’s goals will be established by the strategic apex (for example, the board of directors) of the organisation. These goals can only be achieved if the groups and individuals within the organisation share them and work collectively towards achieving them.

The integration of personal goals with the overall objectives of the organisation is the role of the management, and it is achieved through the co-operation of employees and / or the management control of employee efforts.

Additional factors include effective communication channels, and systems for ensuring the competence of key personnel.

Co-operation

Co-operation is the process of working together to achieve shared goals. To be effective the process should deliver positive outcomes for all participants.

With specific regard to health and safety management, encouraging employee participation in risk control brings a pool of knowledge and experience to the process to the benefit of the organisation as a whole, and the people working in it. Health and safety effectively becomes ‘everybody’s business’ and the safety culture (Element 2.4) improves.

Successful organisations often go further than strictly required by law and actively encourage and support consultation in different ways. (Consultation arrangements are discussed further in Element 2.3).

Control

Management control is about motivating employees to perform effectively in pursuit of the organisation’s goals.

Management control systems are essentially continual improvement cycles constructed around the essential elements of planning, doing, checking and acting (PDCA).

Control mechanisms utilise a combination of behavioural controls (controlling employees behaviour), and output controls (controlling performance through measurement of outputs / results).

Control of people

Performance management is the systematic process for improving organisational performance by developing the performance of individuals and teams through understanding and managing performance within an agreed framework of planned goals, standards and competence requirements.

Performance standards are the basis of planning and measuring employees’ performance and are essential if policies are to be translated from good intentions into a series of coordinated actions.

Good performance standards link responsibilities to specific outputs and can be used to specify how individuals will be held accountable for their health and safety responsibilities.

They should specify:

  • Who is responsible?
  • What are they responsible for?
  • When should the work be done?
  • What is the expected output?

Control of processes

Effective risk management controls are required at each stage of the business process:

  • Inputs – aim to minimise hazards and risks ent
  • Processes – focus on containing risks arising during business operation
  • Outputs – prevent the export of risks off-site, or in the products and services generated by the business.

An effective health and safety management system is needed to manage the layers of controls and to provide a framework for ensuring the adequacy of the level of control at any given time.

Competence

Competence is the ability to perform adequately in a given role. The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (Regulation 7) state that:

A person shall be regarded as competent . . . where he has sufficient training and experience or knowledge and other qualities to enable him properly to assist in undertaking the measures referred to...

The competence of key personnel is vital to the success of the organisation and must be secured for operational staff, all tiers of management, and specialist support.

Communication

Effective communication is about more than the availability of communication channels or media, it is about getting the right message to the right people at the right time. In the context of safety management, effective procedures are required for managing internal and external communications.

Internal communication channels are required between the various levels and functions of the organisation, and different businesses or sites in a large organisation (top down / bottom up  / cross functional / centre to periphery and back).

External communication systems should be in place to address receiving and responding to relevant communications from external parties (for example, stakeholders or enforcement agencies).
