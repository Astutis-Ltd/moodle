Management of conflict


A ‘conflict situation’ is one in which the concerns of two people appear to be incompatible. In such a situation an individual’s behaviour can be evaluated on two basic dimensions:

‣
Assertiveness – the extent to which the person will attempt to satisfy his own concerns

‣
Co-operativeness – the extent to which the person attempts to satisfy the other person’s concerns.



These two basic dimensions of behaviour define five different modes for responding to conflict situations:

Competing (assertive and uncooperative)
‣
Pursues own concerns at the other person’s expense

‣
A power-oriented mode in which power is exercised to win the argument.


Accommodating (unassertive and cooperative)
‣
Neglects personal concerns to satisfy the concerns of the other person

‣
Yields to the others point of view.


Avoiding (unassertive and uncooperative)
‣
Pursues neither his own concerns nor those of the other individual

‣
Sidestepping the issue or withdrawing from a threatening situation.


Collaborating (assertive and cooperative)
‣
Attempts to work with others to find some solution that fully satisfies all concerns.


Compromising (middle ground for both assertiveness and cooperativeness)
‣
Objective is to find some expedient, mutually acceptable solution that partially satisfies both parties.
