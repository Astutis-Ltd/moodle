Organisational structure


Organisational structure has re-emerged as a management hot topic in the twenty-first century, as a result of the globalisation of business and major increases in the volume of information flow. Ways of work have changed significantly in the last twenty years because of technological advances but organisational structures have not.

The ‘structure’ includes not only the organisational chart and allocation of responsibilities, but also the linking mechanisms between the roles and coordinating structures.

There is no ‘one best way’ to design an organisation’s structure, in any given context multiple good forms may exist.

As soon as a small firm hires employees, some kind of organisational structure inevitably develops. The organisational structure takes shape as consequence of deliberate choices and ad-hoc developments. Organisations tend to ‘grow’ in an organic, evolutionary manner maturing over time.

The life-cycle of an organisation is shown below:

1 Entrepreneurial stage The success of the organisation is associated with its flexibility, growth, resource acquisition and the development of external support.
2 Collectivity stage Typified by informal communication and structure, co-operation, commitment and personalised leadership.
3 Formalisation and control The organisation stabilises, rules and procedures, increased efficiency and goal attainment become increasingly important.
4 Structure elaboration The external environment is monitored to identify opportunities for renewal and/or expansion.
Decentralisation of structure occurs.

Organisational size is the most complex variable influencing structure. Increasing size leads to potential for problems in the execution of work and management of staff, necessitating more formal relationships, more rules and standardised procedures. In larger organisations an increased bureaucracy leads to better performance.

The way that job tasks are formally divided, grouped and co-ordinated is typically determined by six key elements.

Work specialisation The degree to which tasks within the organisation are split into separate jobs.
Departmentalisation The basis by which jobs are grouped together to ensure effective co-ordination.
Chain of command The lines of authority from the top to the bottom of the organisation that determine to whom individuals and groups report.
Span of control Determines the number of tiers and managers an organisation has by establishing how many employees managers can efficiently and effectively control.
Centralisation / decentralisation The degree to which formal authority and decision making is focused within / or diffused throughout the organisation.
Formalisation The degree to which jobs can be standardised. Requires employees to work within a rigid framework of rules, minimising discretion.

As mentioned above another means of distinguishing organisations is by the number of management tiers. Large complex bureaucratic (mechanistic) organisations may have many layers and be hierarchical in nature. Smaller, simpler (organic) organisations may have only one or two layers and would be considered flat. Although this description is largely a function of size some large organisations do strive to delayer through approaches such as distributed leadership.

Flatter organisations tend to have better personal communication and tend to be more dynamic and adaptable but will not have as much specialist knowledge and expertise, and relationships and lines of command may become confused as the organisation grows.

Conversely hierarchical organisations have defined systems for organisational communication and can develop employees into specialist areas and management roles. Decision making can become process driven rather than goal driven and can be slow. Interdepartmental rivalries and ‘empire building’ can present challenges as efforts may not be focused on the good of the organisation as a whole. Communication between departments can also be less effective in this context.

Whatever structure an organisation has capturing it diagrammatically in an ‘organogram’ can aid an individual’s understanding of their place in the organisation, lines of communication and the chain of command.

Mintzberg considered most organisational structures to comprise five parts:


Strategic apex Directors focused on ensuring the organisation fulfils its primary objectives.
Operating core Workforce responsible for manufacture of product or delivery of service.
Middle managers Connection between strategic apex and operating core.
Techno-structure Designs, plans and changes workflow.
Support staff Provide support services outside the operating workflow, for example, IT services.
