Internal and external influences


PESTLE analysis

A PESTLE analysis is a technique that is often used by marketing and HR professionals to audit the factors that can influence an organisation’s operating environment with the aim of developing appropriate responses to changes.

Not all of the factors can be directly affected by the organisation but an understanding of the operating environments is essential if opportunities are to be maximised and threats minimised (SWOT).

The six elements of a PESTLE analysis form a framework for reviewing an organisations strategy, position, direction, marketing proposition, business idea etc.

Political The political operating environment will directly influence health and safety enforcement and management through the government of the day’s position on regulation/deregulation. Other political policies in areas such as tax, migration, employment law, benefits etc. will also impact.
Economic As discussed in Element A1 a range of economic factors can influence an organisation’s health and safety performance including: levels of economic growth or decline, unemployment levels, wage rates, working hours and the cost of living etc.
Sociological Demographic changes such as: cultural norms and expectations, levels of health consciousness, population growth rates and age distributions, attitudes to work and career, concerns on health, safety or environmental issues etc.
Technological Technology is leaping forward every two years. Keeping abreast of technological development is essential in ensuring that all ‘practicable’ or ‘reasonably practicable’ safeguards are in place.
Legal Legal changes in health and safety legislation can require urgent changes in the management of specific health and safety concerns. Developments in other areas, such as anti-discrimination, can also have a significant influence.
Environmental Including local environmental and ecological variables as well as broader considerations such as Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR).


Specified internal and external influences

The NEBOSH Diploma syllabus requires consideration of specific internal and external influences as shown below.



Internal influences

Finance

Organisations are motivated in much the same way as individuals. When times are hard the primary motivation is the need to survive and higher needs will tend to be disregarded. In such times organisations may well look to save money from training and maintenance budgets and other areas that may be perceived as less critical in the short term.

Production and organisational goals

The primary objective of an organisation is survival, and thereafter to turn a healthy profit. The position that ‘health and safety is our No 1 priority’ is not a universal truth. It might be hoped that when people’s lives are at risk it will be seen to be so but, in general, organisational efforts will be focussed on the organisations ‘raison d’etre’ – be it manufacturing a product or delivering a service.

Time pressure to meet production demands has been shown to increase accident rates, and productivity bonuses have also been shown to compromise safety.

Finding the appropriate balance between health and safety and production is critical in developing a positive safety culture.

Trade unions

HSE research has shown that good working relationships between management and staff lead to:

‣
better control of common workplace risks

‣
lower accident rates

‣
a more positive health and safety climate where employees feel encouraged to raise concerns.


Historically the Trade Unions have been prime movers in building such relationships through arrangements for formal consultation (Element 2.3). An effective safety committee with a co-operative relationship between management and the trade unions is a good indicator of a positive health and safety culture.


External influences

Legislation

Health and safety legislation establishes a minimum standard of acceptable behaviour in the workplace with the aim of protecting employees from less scrupulous employers.

The Health and Safety at Work Act and supporting regulations provide a framework for managing health and safety to that minimal acceptable standard.

Non-compliance with legislation is liable to result in enforcement action which could result in the imposition of fines or prison sentences. This may be seen to have a deterrent effect.

Parliament/HSE

The government of the day will have a philosophical perspective on the appropriate level of regulation / deregulation of health and safety. The government will also set the strategic direction of the Health and Safety Executive (HSE).

Enforcement agencies

Enforcement of health and safety legislation is split between the HSE, Local Authorities and the Office of Rail Regulation.

In addition to law enforcement the enforcement agencies are committed to supporting government strategies and increasingly look to work in partnership with business.

Courts and tribunals

Courts and tribunals interpret legislation and set legal precedents which influence health and safety practice. Sentencing practice may also influence the level of priority that organisations give to the management of health and safety.

The role of courts and tribunals was discussed in Element 1.

Contracts

There is an implied term in the ‘contract of employment’ that the employer will exercise reasonable care for the employee’s physical and psychological wellbeing. A failure to address genuine concerns could amount to a fundamental breach of contract. With a ‘contract for employment’ (client / contractor relationship) there will be terms requiring each party to take reasonable care of the health and safety of the other party.

Client/contractor relationships

Supply chain pressures have a major impact upon the management of health and safety. High quality organisations will require partners to perform to similarly high standards and will not use organisations that do not work to those standards. The management of contractors in supply chains is discussed more fully in Element 9.

Good contractors can also exert pressure by informing the client of concerns and shortcomings to positively influence behaviour.

Trade unions

As well as influencing organisations on a local level Trade Unions function as pressure groups, lobbying government regarding the health, safety and welfare of their members. They also fund research and provide training at various levels.

Trade Unions have also had a major impact through their involvement in civil legal class actions and key individual cases in support of their members.

Insurance companies

More than 850 000 people are injured or made ill as a result of their job each year and around 60 000 of these successfully obtain compensation.

In 1999 the Association of British Insurers (ABI) reported that the cost of claims was 54% higher than the monies received from insurance premiums. The cost of employers’ liability insurance has continued to rise to redress the balance.

Many insurers are now taking an active interest in risk management, especially regarding the control of long term health hazards. Insurance companies can influence organisational behaviour through the provision of training and consultancy support services and also by setting conditions for employers’ liability insurance.

Public opinion

The public provides the organisations workforce and customer base. A major accident may have a negative impact on public opinion and perception – either directly or via the media.

Socially responsible organisations can positively influence public perception through marketing themselves based on a strong ethical standpoint.
