Safety culture


Investigations into major disasters such as Chernobyl, Piper Alpha, and Clapham Junction, highlighted that sophisticated engineering and technical safeguards can fail because people fail to do what they are supposed to do, and that these failures are not only attributable to individual errors, but to malpractices that corrupt the social systems that makes organisations function (i.e. are attributable to poor safety culture).


Kings Cross

A cultural change in management is required throughout the organisation.

Judge Fennel, 1988.

Piper Alpha

It is essential to create a corporate atmosphere or culture in which safety is understood to be, and is accepted as, the number one priority.

Lord Cullen, 1990.



The Confederation of British Industry (CBI) describes the culture of an organisation as:

…the mix of shared values, attitudes and patterns of behaviour that give the organisation its particular character, or ‘the way we do things round here.’

They suggest that the safety culture of an organisation could be described as:

the ideas and beliefs that all members of the organisation share about risk, accidents and ill-health.

The HSE has expressed concerns about companies that talk about ‘safety culture’ in a very narrow way, usually when referring to the inclination of employees to comply with rules or act safely. Broader issues of organisational culture and style of management can be even more significant, for example, where management has a natural, unconscious bias for production over safety, or a tendency to focus on the short term, or can be highly reactive.

The HSE has a definition for safety culture.

The safety culture of an organisation is the produce of individual and group values, attitudes, perceptions, competencies, and patterns of behaviour that determine the commitment to, and the style and proficiency of, an organisation’s health and safety management.

Organisations with a positive safety culture are characterised by communications founded on mutual trust, by shared perceptions of the importance of safety and by confidence in the efficacy of preventive measures.


An IOSH review of various definitions of safety culture noted that most definitions included or referred to:

‣
norms and policies related to safety

‣
common values, beliefs, attitudes and behaviours regarding safety

‣
the joint values, attitudes, competences and behaviours of individuals and groups that establish organisational commitment to, and style and proficiency of, a safety programme.


Every group of people develops a ‘culture’. In an organisation with a good safety culture everyone puts health and safety high on the list and adopts the same positive attitudes to health and safety. This influences the ways in which individuals in the group handle new events and decisions.

A positive safety culture will need to be:

‣
An informed culture: Workers understand and respect operational hazards and are alert to the ways that safeguards can be breached

‣
A learning culture: A willingness to learn from incidents and mistakes to improve systems and performance

‣
A reporting culture: A willingness to report incidents (accidents and near misses)

‣
A just culture (fair blame): The antithesis of a ‘blame culture’ where the investigation looks at the guilty party rather than the act and the reasons behind it. Blame cultures inhibit reporting and learning but a ‘no blame’ culture is also ineffective. Blame should be attributed when it is deserved (for example, gross negligence / misconduct / extreme violations). People should be held accountable for their actions. Accountability is about trust and respect. Blaming is about judgement and discrediting the blamed.


Additional pre-requisites for (or features of) an effective culture includes:

‣
Management commitment: Management commitment should be demonstrated by the proportion of resources (time, money, people) and support allocated to health and safety management and by the relative status of health and safety against other business priorities such as production, cost etc. The active involvement of senior management in the health and safety system is very important

‣
Visible management: Managers should lead by example when it comes to health and safety. Good managers appear regularly on the ‘shop floor’, talk about health and safety and visibly demonstrate their commitment by their actions – wearing PPE, following safety rules, where appropriate putting health and safety concerns over commercial considerations. Above and beyond visible demonstration the commitment of senior managers should be ‘felt’ by the workers and believed to be sincere. See also the notes on leadership in Section 9.1 and the importance of credibility, taking action and leading by example

‣
Good communications: Open two way communications are essential. Questions about health and safety should be part of everyday work conversations. Management should listen actively to what they are being told by employees, and be seen to take what they hear seriously

‣
Active employee participation: Ownership of health and safety has to be built at all levels and the knowledge that employees have of their own work is a valuable resource. Employees should be actively involved in safety workshops, risk assessments, plant design etc.

‣
Effective health and safety training: Training has an important role to play in improving employees understanding of not just site specific hazards and controls, but also the principles of sensible risk management.


Note: The antithesis of the above is also true, i.e. the absence of any of these components will not only impede the development of a positive culture but may also contribute to the development of a negative culture.


Sub-cultures

A number of studies have found the presence of subcultures within an organisation. Sub-cultures are likely to develop where groups of employees experience different working conditions or are exposed to different management styles. In the general management literature, the lack of cultural cohesion can be seen as an impediment to effective organisational performance. However this is not the case with health and safety. Work groups are likely to view risk differently depending on the type of work they do (for example, shop-floor production workers compared to office based colleagues). An appreciation of sub-cultures can lead to useful contextual insight into the different risk and hazards experienced by workgroups.


Safety culture and safety performance

Safety culture is consistently and independently associated with safety performance, i.e. an organisation with a positive safety culture shows good safety performance (and an organisation with a negative safety culture shows poor safety performance) irrespective of other variables such as organisation size or risk profile.

Employee perceptions of and attitudes towards safety have also been shown to be consistently and independently associated with individual health and wellbeing.

The evidence that these associations are not limited to particular sectors of industry suggests that they are robust and generally applicable. The findings also suggest that measuring safety perceptions at an individual level makes a significant contribution to understanding the profile of factors associated with employee health and safety.
