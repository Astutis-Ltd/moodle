Changing the culture


As discussed earlier the unique and essential function of leadership is to manipulate culture. In an organisation with a poor safety culture and poor safety performance, transformational leadership is essential to challenge currently unacceptable mind-sets and behaviours and to sell the vision of a better future state.

Thus the process for effecting cultural change is no different to that of any other transformational change management programme.

Kurt Lewin, a physicist and social scientist first presented his model of organisational change in 1947. Using the analogy of changing the shape of a block of ice Lewin described a journey with a beginning, middle and end. Lewin’s ‘Unfreeze, Change, Freeze (or Refreeze)’ model is still relevant and used today, although it has been criticised for being too simplistic.


Stage 1: Unfreezing

This first stage is about getting ready to change and involves preparing people for change and ideally creating a situation in which they want the change.

Strong leadership is essential in developing the vision of the future state, convincing employees of the need for change, and inspiring and motivating them to work together to achieve the new objectives. Employee engagement at all levels is also essential for success and consultation, provision of information and training can all help in this regard.

Unfreezing and getting motivated for the change is all about weighing up the ‘pros’ and ‘cons’ of the proposed change and ensuring that the ‘pro’s’ outnumber the ‘con’s’ before taking any action.


Stage 2: Change or transition

Change is a transitional process. People need time to learn about and understand the changes. Support through training, coaching and effective communication is an essential part of the process.

Attempting to impose a change without consultation or to try and effect a change too quickly can be counter- productive leading to worker resistance and antipathy.

A clearly mapped process with achievable ‘stepping stones’ along the way can help maintain enthusiasm by presenting opportunities to share successes long the way.

Trial and error mistakes will inevitably be made as part of the process and should be treated as opportunities to refine the transitional processes whilst keeping an eye on the objectives. Feedback should be provided on successes, failures and lessons learned as a matter of course.


Stage 3: Freezing (or refreezing)

Freezing or refreezing is about establishing stability once the changes have been made, and as the changes are accepted and become the new norm. It takes time for people to form new relationships and become comfortable with the new routines. The benefits of the change should be regularly reinforced going forwards to ensure it is accepted and maintained.

The idea of freezing is not compatible with modern thinking about change being a continuous and flexible process and in the current age of ‘accelerated change’ time for bedding in is an unlikely luxury.

Modern models of change, such as the ADKAR model (Hiatt, 2003) in the below figure, are more explicit about the need for on-going reinforcement.


Awareness of the need for change
Planned communications to ensure employees fully understand why change is necessary.

Desire to participate in and support the change
Employees are able to make informed personal decisions to support, and participate in the change. Building desire can be partly achieved through incentives (needs or rewards).

Knowledge on how to change
Two types of knowledge need to be addressed: knowledge on how to change (what to do during the transition) and knowledge on how to perform after the change. Knowledge can be provided through training and education, coaching, forums and mentoring.

Ability to implement required skills and behaviours
Ability is the difference between theory and practice. Practical performance needs to be supported through practice, coaching and feedback.

Reinforcement to sustain the change
Appropriate behaviours are reinforced through positive feedback, rewards, recognition, performance measurement and corrective action.

Challenges to effective culture change

The absence of any of the ‘building blocks’ discussed above may compromise the success of the cultural change initiative.

Resistance to change may be driven by any number of practical or cultural variables, for example:

‣
Fear of the unknown – lack of clarity on the need for and benefits of change

‣
Concerns regarding personal competence and the ability to deliver going forward

‣
Lack of trust and no belief that the reasons for change are positive and beneficial

‣
Familiarity and comfort in the way things are / were

‣
The sense that changes are being forced or imposed on the workforce and that their views don’t matter and haven’t been considered

‣
Culture shock as a result of acquisition and the need to ‘fit in’ with a larger organisation with very different values or when a ‘new broom’ corporate leader is appointed from outside the organisation comes into conflict with the organisations established identity

‣
Poor timing, e.g. if there are other active priorities and competition for resources, including management time and effort

‣
Initiative fatigue where a dynamic and evolutionary organisation is regularly introducing new management initiatives to the extent that managers and workers are demotivated by the latest fad

‣
When changes are all encompassing and too much is being changed too quickly without any opportunity to learn from the journey and be satisfied that the changes are beneficial.
