Proactive safety management (safety II and safety differently)

Introduction

Although notions of ‘Safety II’ and ‘Safety Differently’ are often grouped together Hollnagel considers that the terms refer to two completely different ideas.

Dekker explains that ‘Safety differently’ is about relying on people’s expertise, insights and the dignity of work as actually done to improve safety and efficiency, and about halting or pushing back on the ever-expanding bureaucratisation and compliance of work. Hollnagel considers this to be a ‘Safety I’ approach as its focus is still on the elimination or reduction of harm, even if it is done in a more constructive and effective way.

Hollnagel considers that ‘Safety II’ is not about safety at all, but about how to develop and maintain the potentials of an organisation to function under varying conditions so that the number of intended and acceptable outcomes is as high as possible.

Hollnagel’s view is that as technical and socio-technical systems have continued to evolve with ever more powerful information technology, systems and work environments have gradually become more intractable (difficult to deal with), and the bi-modal approach of ‘Safety I’ no longer fits.

Focus on the presence of positives

The ‘Safety II’ approach changes the definition of safety from ‘avoiding that something goes wrong’ to ‘ensuring that everything goes right’ – or more precisely to the ability to succeed under varying conditions, so that the number of intended and acceptable outcomes (in other words, successful everyday activities) is as high as possible.

The basis for safety and safety management therefore becomes an understanding of everyday activities and why things go right.

Safety II assumes that systems work because workers are able to adjust what they do in response to the conditions of work. Workers need to be able to identify and overcome design flaws and functional glitches by adapting their performance accordingly. If they can detect when something is about to go wrong, they can intervene and keep things on track. This ‘performance variability’ is seen as a positive where the variability represents the adjustments that form the basis of ongoing safety and productivity.

Devolve, declutter, decentralise

Dekker (2012) coined the term ‘safety differently’ when approached by a group of organisations to help them improve after their safety performance had plateaued, despite ever more bureaucracy and compliance requirements.

‘Safety differently’ is said to be about relying on people’s expertise, insights and the dignity of work as actually done to improve safety and efficiency, whilst pushing back on the ever-expanding bureaucratisation and compliance of work.

Safety improvements (i.e. reductions in fatalities and serious injury rates) have flatlined despite a vast expansion of compliance with its associated paperwork and bureaucratic accountability which generates significant costs and non-productive time.

Dekker’s work with three Australian organisations (Origin Energy, Queensland Health and Woolworths) in devolving, decluttering, and decentralising their safety bureaucracy delivered interesting findings.

Origin Energy made significant cost-savings by reducing the size of their Safety Management System by 90% by making safety a field-focused, operational issue, cutting centralised safety staff and reducing the bureaucratic accountability requirements imposed on engineers in the field.

Queensland Health stopped assembling state-wide caregivers centrally to tell them how to do a particular procedure. They distributed experts throughout the state, using local process simulations as tools for discovery and sensemaking, and asking people what they needed and wanted.

Woolworths Supermarkets ran a randomized controlled trial, taking everything related to safety out of a group of stores and telling the store manager ‘don’t hurt anyone.’ Injuries went down, innovations went up and a deep sense of ownership blossomed. One store in that group won the annual safety prize.

The organisations each discovered that much of their compliance and bureaucracy was self-inflicted, going far beyond legal compliance. The majority of the permits, checklists, rules, guidance and procedures used were driven internally or by contracting arrangements to other organisations. There were ‘too many cooks in the rule-making kitchen’ with no calls for evidence of the value of the rule. It was easy to add rules but almost impossible to take them out.

The focus of their efforts shifted from documentation to trusting and empowering their people. They resisted the urge to centralise, standardise and control everything their people do, and instead focussed on harnessing autonomy, mastery and purpose as drivers for people’s motivation to do the right thing.

People are the solution

Since the ‘second age’ of safety management in the 1980s people have been seen as the weak link in socio-technical systems and the most likely point of failure as evidenced by decades of research into human factors, human failure and behavioural safety etc. Historical issues with blame culture and the need to develop more just cultures reinforce the notion that people are the problem.

Both Hollnagel (2013) and Dekker (2012) have made capital out of comparing their respective approaches against traditional safety / safety I. In both comparisons the general point is made, although in fundamentally different ways that in traditional safety people are the problem whereas in safety II and safety differently, they are the solution.

Dekker proposes that people are not the problem to control but the solution, advocating that employers evolve from attempting to control workers by forcing compliance with rules but instead understand how the workers create success on a daily basis, and harness their skills, competencies and common-sense to build a safer workplace.

Hollnagel’s view is that with the increasing complexity of socio-technical systems it is imperative that that people are developed to be resilient, to deal well with the efficiency-thoroughness trade-off (ETTO principle – not on syllabus) and to be able to react and adapt to system changes to keep the system working safely, as intended.
