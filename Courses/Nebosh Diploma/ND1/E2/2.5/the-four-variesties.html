The four varieties of human work

The idea that how people think that work is done and how it is actually done are two different things dates back to the onset of ergonomics. In the 1940’s research into the impact of cockpit design on aircraft accidents considered ‘imagination vs operation’. In the 1950’s the French ergonomists Ombredanne and Faverge realised that the analysis of work had to consider both work as prescribed in procedures etc (le travail prescrit), and the observation of work actually done (le travail réalisé).

Nowadays four basic varieties of work are typically considered: ‘work as imagined’, ‘work as prescribed’, ‘work as disclosed’ and ‘work as done’ as illustrated in the below figure. The varieties of human work overlap in parts with areas of commonality, and areas of difference. Hence the importance of considering all four.

Work as imagined

When thinking about how we or others work we use our imagination. ‘‘work as imagined’’ is the work that we imagine others do and the work that we imagine we or others might do, now or in the future.

The imagination of human work takes place at different levels within organisations. Senior management and middle management, along with non-operational specialists and support staff, will all have an imagination of the work of operational staff; and operational staff will have an imagination of the work of all of the above.

These imaginations, or mental models, will be wrong in various ways, e.g they may be over-simplified, incomplete or incorrect.

‘Work as imagined’ is influenced by the other three varieties of work, which each have their limitations:

  • past experience of ‘work as done’(Useful but can quickly become outdated);
  • knowledge and understanding of ‘work as prescribed’ (Often over or under-specified and unrealistic); and
  • exposure to ‘work as disclosed’ (Often partial and biased).

‘Work as imagined’ also applies to what we think we would do in a scenario, which may well be different to what we would really do. As Hollnagel (2016) stated “‘work as done’ differs from what we imagine we would do in the same situation, but thinking about it from afar and assuming more or less complete knowledge.”

Where decisions about the work of others are made by those whose imagined view of work is incorrect, ‘work as imagined’ can become very problematic, leading to badly designed work and work environments, and unintended consequences, including adaptations to ‘work as done’ to overcome constraints and to work around other unintended consequences.

Work as prescribed

‘Work as prescribed’ is the formalisation or specification of ‘work as imagined’, or ‘work as done’ or ‘work as disclosed’, or some combination of the three. It takes on a number of forms in organisations, including task-oriented such as procedures and checklists or job-oriented (e.g. rules and job descriptions).

Work is often prescribed by more senior members of an organisation who are often distanced from operational activity and have little contact with the front line, policy makers or external organisations, such as regulators. This distance can account for significant differences between an organisation’s policy and practise. ‘work as prescribed’ is often assumed to be the safe and correct way to work and is often the ultimate arbiter of whether performance is satisfactory.

‘Work as prescribed’ will be subject to risk assessment and control as well as other measures to control and standardise ‘work as done’. These controls may involve soft constraints such as rules, which are possible to overcome and hard constraints such as forcing functions, which are difficult or impossible to overcome.

Except for extremely simple tasks it is practically impossible to prescribe all aspects of human work, even when the work is well-understood. There are many ways in which work can be done so even well prescribed work can and will probably be done in other ways.

The pre-conditions for, and conditions of, work cannot always be forseen or guaranteed. Assumed system conditions for staffing levels, competency, equipment, procedures, time etc are often somewhat more optimal than those found in practice.

It is often impossible to articulate in a linear written form the precise way that work is done so that the instructions are useable and easy to follow.

Work as disclosed

‘Work as disclosed’ is the way that work is talked or written about. This is typically based on a partial version of one or more of the other varieties of human work but in addition the message is tailored for its purpose or objective and to be palatable, as expected and understood by the audience. It is often based on what we want and are prepared to say in the context of what is expected and imagined consequences.

‘Work as disclosed’ differs from ‘work as prescribed’ when prescribed work does not provide a good basis for explaining how things work. (As mentioned earlier, it may be under or over-specified, or just not how things are really done). In such circumstances a supervisor might explain to a newcomer ‘how things work around here’ (work as done), in a summary form, or in a way that is very different to how work is officially prescribed.

‘Work as disclosed’ also differs from ‘work as imagined’ because what we think or believe may be different to what we are prepared to say. This can be significant when communicating with outsiders such as regulators or auditors, or senior managers.

People will sometimes modify or limit what they say about ‘work as done’ based on consequences. In an environment where people are punished for trade-offs, workarounds, and compromises, which the staff believe to be necessary to meet demand, then the overlap between ‘work as disclosed’ and ‘work as done’ may be deliberately reduced.

The cultural anthropologist Dr Margaret Mead was often credited with saying “What people say, what people do, and what they say they do are entirely different things.”

In an organisation with a fair and trusting culture there will be a big overlap between ‘work as disclosed’ and ‘work as done’.

Work as done

‘Work as done’ is actual activity – what people do. It takes place in an environment that is often not as imagined, with a broad range of influencing variables which can all have unintended consequences, including:

  • multiple, shifting goals,
  • variable and often unpredictable demand,
  • degraded resources (staffing, competency, equipment, procedures, and time), and
  • systems of constraints, punishments, and incentives.

‘Work as done’ is mostly impossible to prescribe precisely and is achieved by adjustments, variations, trade-offs and compromises that are necessary to meet demand.

Gaps between ‘work as prescribed’ and ‘work as done’ may be known and accepted and even encouraged – at least implicitly – at supervisory and local management levels, so long as demand is met.

Considering the risks of disclosing all aspects of ‘work as done’, workers may keep aspects of it secret, or protect their working environment so as not to expose it. Aside from the risk of sanctions, the reason for this is a suspicion that if a decision maker sees a snapshot of ‘work as done’, then they may generalise from that snapshot and look to change systems, work-patterns, or resources in pursuit of increased efficiency.

‘Work as done’ can be examined via observation, but this is challenging as if workers now what should be done, they are likely to perform under observation. The work may also be difficult to understand or unsafe to observe.

‘Work as done’ is the most important and yet most neglected variety of human work. It is the variety that outsiders (those who do not do the work) pay least attention to. Much attention is paid to the other varieties of work, and this would not be a problem if it were not for the fact that these other varieties are so often mistaken for or used as a proxy for the real thing: ‘work as done’.

Hollnagel (2016) noted that “We lack models based on what people actually do, on the recurrent patterns of behaviour.”

Conclusion

The early ergonomists were right. The analysis of work cannot be limited to work as prescribed in procedures etc (le travail prescrit), nor to the observation of work actually done (le travail réalisé). Similarly, it cannot be limited to work as we imagine it, nor work as people talk about it. Consideration must be given to all four varieties of human work to provide a holistic understanding of the reality of work.
