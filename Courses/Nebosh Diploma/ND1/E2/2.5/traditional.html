Traditional safety (safety I / modernist safety)

As can be seen from the introduction approaches to health and safety management have evolved significantly since the 1970’s and continue to do so. All the proponents of the more recent ‘proactive’ safety management models acknowledge that there is much of the traditional approaches to be admired and continued with but that additional approaches are also required.

As has been discussed elsewhere, traditional safety management has significant proactive elements, notably risk assessment and management and the use of proactive monitoring data to provide leading indicators of performance.

Criticisms of traditional safety should be considered holistically, otherwise there is a danger of exaggerating the weaknesses and ignoring the positives.

The traditional view of safety is framed by reference to its opposite, the absence of safety and is typically defined by the absence of accidents and incidents, or as the ‘freedom from unacceptable risk’, or ‘a state where, as few things as possible go wrong’, or where the number of adverse outcomes is kept ‘as low as is reasonably practicable’.

As a result, the historical focus of safety research and safety management has largely focussed on unsafe system operation rather than on safe operation.

As can be seen this is based on a bimodal / binary view of work activity in which work is either successfully completed or fails. Where work goes wrong because of a technical, organisational or human failure the focus is inevitable on understanding the root causes to disable the cause-effect links and prevent similar future occurrences.

The two main concerns with this approach are that:

  • Work systems are now much more complex than in the days of the steam engine and are much less likely to totally fail
  • People are seen as a weak link in the system and a cause of accidents.

As frontline staff are the last ones to ‘touch the system’ before it breaks it is they who are most likely to be held responsible or blamed when things go wrong.

Professor James Reason (1997) and others developed the concept of ‘just culture’ to address the issue of unfairly blaming workers for workplace accidents (blame culture). A ‘just culture’ is regarded as an essential part of the foundations of a safety culture. Reason defined a just culture as “an atmosphere of trust in which people are encouraged (even rewarded) for providing essential safety related information, but in which they are also clear about where the line must be drawn between acceptable and unacceptable behaviour.”

The concept was subsequently embedded in EU legislation, notably Article 2 of Regulation 691/2010 on air navigation services which defines it as “a culture in which frontline operators and others are not punished for actions, omissions or decisions taken by them which are commensurate with their experience and training, but where gross negligence, wilful violations and destructive acts are not tolerated.”

The counter view from Safety II is that variability in modern socio-technical work systems is inevitable and that the systems continue to work effectively when workers can adapt to variations and adjust their ways of working to match changing work conditions. Workers therefore are a strength in their system and their adaptability typically keeps things working well.

The Safety II view of traditional safety as ‘reactive’ is because it is based on identifying things that have gone wrong (through incident investigation) or things that could go wrong (through risk assessment) with the focus on addressing root causes to prevent similar future occurrences. In contrast Safety II is deemed proactive because it focuses on ensuring that as much as possible goes right.
