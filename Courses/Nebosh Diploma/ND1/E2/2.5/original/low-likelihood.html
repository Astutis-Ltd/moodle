Low likelihood / high consequence events


Proponents of proactive approaches criticisms of traditional safety includes the view that low likelihood, high consequence events are unlikely to be considered in traditional safety as event have not happened.

This would seem to be a rather narrow view. The third edition of HSG65 (HSE, 2013) clearly states that health and safety risks from things that happen very infrequently but with catastrophic effects (high-hazard, low-frequency events, such as an oil refinery explosion) would be high-priority in a risk profile.

Effective risk evaluation of such events has long been acknowledged as challenging. The UK Governments Blackett Review of High Impact Low Probability Risks published it’s findings in 2011 The most notable over-arching factor in these recommendations is the repeated need for the inclusion of external experts and readiness to consider unlikely risks. Additionally, the report makes clear that behavioural matters and the role of social science in risk management needs to be enhanced.

Dekker’s view (2012) is that traditional safety approaches drive a preoccupation with high-frequency / low-consequence things such as not wearing safety glasses or having coffee in a cup without a lid. Dekker believes that focusing on these relatively irrelevant things and being able to show the paperwork on it creates an artificial sense of safety and safety culture whilst losing sight of what is important and safety critical and accidentally ‘blowing stuff up’.


Resilience

Thomas Tredgold, an early 19th century English engineer, first used the word ‘resilience’ to describe a property of timber, and to explain why some types of wood were able to accommodate sudden and severe loads without breaking.

In the early 1970s the term was used as a synonym for stress resistance in psychological studies of children. It soon became more broadly used in psychology, and in 2007 was defined by Tisseron as: “The capacity to withstand traumatic situations and the ability to use a trauma as the start of something new”.

From a resilience engineering perspective the focus is on resilient system performance. Hollnagel et al (2010) defined resilience engineering as “The intrinsic ability of a system to adjust its functioning prior to, during, or following changes and disturbances, so that it can sustain required operations under both expected and unexpected conditions.”

Note that changes may be positive as well as negative and it is important that systems can respond effectively to opportunities as well as threats, stresses and negative disturbances.

Hollnagel (2012) noted that as work systems are larger and more complex than ever before with many more details to consider, accurately describing systems in detail is an impossible task. Hollnagel suggests it is time to abandon the idea that ‘work as imagined’ will correspond to ‘work as done’; and to recognise that for work to succeed it is necessary that performance is variable or flexible rather than rigid.

As mentioned previously it is essential that people are developed to be resilient within well-designed systems and are able to react and adapt to system changes to keep the system working safely, as intended.


Performance variation

According to the logic of Safety-I, the goal of ‘safety’, can be achieved by constraining all kinds of performance variability. Examples of frequently used constraints are selection, training, authorisations, restrictions, barriers, procedures, standardisation, rules, and regulations.

Hollnagel’s Safety II perspective is that all human activity (individually and / or collectively) is variable in the sense that it is adjusted to the conditions. As discussed earlier, in the context of increasingly complex socio-technical systems, recognising the variability of work (mismatches between work as described and work as done) and preparing workers to be able to effectively respond to variation is essential. Variability is a strength, or even a necessity, rather than a liability.

Dekker is also of the view that workers must use their experience and judgement to manage the realities of work, such as design glitches and poor organisation. Tightly controlling behaviour and preventing workers from using their discretion and knowledge to deal with issues is considered to create an environment in which small problems can escalate into something more catastrophic.

Hollnagel identified three main motivations for people to adjust or vary their performance:

1)
To create or maintain good working conditions,

2)
To compensate for something that is missing, and

3)
To avoid future problems.



Limitations of traditional and proactive safety management

Traditional

Safety II advocates fundamental criticism of traditional safety is that the approach is philosophically flawed in focusing on the absence of risk rather than the presence of safety. Disciples of ‘safety differently’ believe modern approaches should be more people focused and seek to praise the positive rather than criticise the negative.

One of Hollnagel’s primary criticisms of traditional safety is that it is ‘reactive’ though the terms is not used narrowly in the traditional way to refer to after the event but to learning from what goes wrong, or could go wrong rather than learning from what goes right.

A key principle of safety differently is that safety should be an ethical responsibility, not a bureaucratic activity. Dekker’s view of traditional safety is that it has lost focus on what is important and become overly bureaucratic and bogged down with excessive paperwork focused on demonstrating system and legal compliance. Dekker is also critical of the top-down authoritarian stance taken by many organisations where rules are imposed on workers and systems implemented to force compliance.


Proactive

Criticisms of Safety II are more likely to be in response to its perceived criticism of Safety I rather than any fundamental disagreement with the idea that resilience engineering approaches may have a part to play in safety management. The major criticism of safety differently is the lack of empirical evidence and peer reviewed research to demonstrate its impact (Cooper, 2020).

Hollnagels resilience engineering approach requires people to be able to anticipate system changes as well as being able to react and adapt as necessary to keep the system working safely, as intended. This is seen as proactive and criticised as it suggests that workers need to be able to predict the future. The same assertion could, of course, also be levelled at traditional risk assessment approaches.

Another stated limitation is that organisations may be averse to trying a new approach. This is a typical example of organisational inertia or resistance to change. People tend to be resistant to change because it is stressful, and organisations are no different and often strive to maintain the status quo. The challenges of managing organisational change are addressed in Element 7.


The effects of proactive safety management on health and safety culture

Dekker’s view (2012) of safety culture is of “a very empowering idea, shifting our focus onto the context surrounding people’s work” that has unfortunately been accompanied by burgeoning safety bureaucracy with organisations deploying vast systems for loss prevention, safety management, auditing etc in pursuit of it.

Dekker states that traditional approaches to safety culture are plateauing, with typically lower returns for each new initiative. Dekker believes that driving effective safety cultures forward requires organisations to:

‣
Shift focus from counting negatives to understanding what makes an organization normally successful;

‣
Challenge common wisdom and industry standards; and

‣
See people as a solution to be harnessed rather than a problem to control.


Since James Reason (1997) modelled the concept of a ‘just culture’ it has been generally accepted as a foundation stone for an effective, positive safety culture.

Dekker (2012) considers Reason’s model to be retributive as it “asks which rule has been broken, who did it, how bad was the infraction, so what does the person deserve?” and advocates an evolution to a restorative just culture model which would ask: “Who has been hurt or potentially hurt? What are their needs? Whose obligation is it to meet those needs?”

Hollnagel considers notions of safety culture and just culture to be artefacts of a ‘safety I view’ still focused on the ‘causality credo’ and why things go wrong. He does however advocate a fair management culture in the context of the safety II or resilience engineering perspective. Hollnagels’s ‘fair culture’ recognises that work is based on performance adjustments whether things go right or wrong and seeks to resolve the potential, and actual, conflict between what is explicit (or prescribed) and what is implicit (or tacit).

Given that the legal systems that employing organisations operate within often require the establishment of blame to obtain compensation it is unclear how these evolutions could occur in practise.
