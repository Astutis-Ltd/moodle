Selecting a specialist contractor


Specialist contractors are used by many organisations to support existing in-house resources, for example in construction, manufacturing, and the service sector. Typically, such specialists provide health and safety technical support in the form of specific hazard surveys, risk assessments and control options, monitoring and testing activities, and health surveillance. Dependent on the type of issue to be addressed, confirmation of compliance or otherwise with health and safety regulations such as hazardous or dangerous substances, noise and vibration, etc., is an essential output from such specialists.

Guidance from the British Occupational Hygiene Society (BOHS, 2019) for selecting specialist contractors such as occupational hygienists, for detailed or technical work, or work involving the use of specialist equipment, suggests that employers should consider the following general questions:

‣
What is the challenge that I am seeking to address?

‣
If I engage specialist help, what do I need it to do for me?

‣
Will the specialist help be good enough to solve my problem?


Answers to those questions will better inform the process and lead on to more specific competence-related questions such as:

‣
Do they have evidence of relevant competence, such as formal qualifications and training?

‣
Do they have practical experience related to the industry, or processes?

‣
Can they explain why they are competent to advise on this problem?

‣
Do they have the resources to complete the work within the required timescale?

‣
Will they need to sub-contract the work or any part of the work?

‣
Can they provide references for completed work that is like the project being undertaken?

‣
Are they a member of a relevant professional body, for example being included in the list of BOHS Directory of Occupational Hygiene Consultants?


Other factors to consider are:

‣
Consultant competence and supervision – are there identified competences for the work and also are there effective processes to train, assess and monitor staff?

‣
Quotations – is everyone involved clear on the scope of work?

‣
Report writing and quality control – who will do this?

‣
Co-operation and co-ordination – has relevant information for planning the work been shared? Are the relevant processes and staff available? Has suitable safe access / egress been provided? Who will make checks during the work?


Effectiveness of solutions and follow-up support:

‣
Is the report practical and sensible? Is it factually incorrect or not easily understood? Does it refer to published guidance for report writing?

‣
Is it clear as to what the law requires, what the consultant’s professional judgement is, and also what is it that ‘must be done legally’ and what ‘could be done’ as best practise?

‣
Is there a process for dealing with complaints?


Whilst the information above is provided by the BOHS to assist employers in selecting external occupational hygienists, it can be applied to other specialist contractor activities. Specific industry professional bodies, such as those dealing with fire safety, process safety, ergonomics, building and civil engineering provide further details as to the competency requirements for membership, including continued professional development (CPD).
