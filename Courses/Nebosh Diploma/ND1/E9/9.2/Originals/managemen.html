Management of contractors within the
supply chain


Cooperation and coordination of activities within a supply chain is a legal and practical necessity. Good communication is key and having appropriate systems in place to ensure that all actors understand the key hazards and control measures required before commencing work is a good starting point. As discussed, much of this can be addressed through effective site induction.

To paraphrase Molte the Elder, “No plan survives contact with the enemy”. It is important to recognise that plans may need to be modified during operational activity. It is also essential that these changes are recognised and assessed and that controls are changed as necessary. This must be done in a timely manner to ensure that all parties are properly informed and able to play their part effectively.

Where there are multiple contractors engaged it is important that there is consistency of approach to risk assessment, safe systems and permits to work and incident reporting etc. It is generally accepted (Benevides et al, 2012) that for the best outcomes performance should be jointly managed and monitored by the client and contractors involved in the project.

In addition to regular meetings throughout the project both the client and the contractor should review the work after completion to see if performance could be improved in future.

The client should review both the job and the contractor. Consideration should be given to:

‣
the effectiveness of the clients planning and control of the project;

‣
the contractor’s performance; and

‣
how smoothly the job went.


Lessons learnt should be recorded and used to influence future decisions.
