The focal company’s role in promoting and
applying good OSH practice throughout the
supply chain


Focal companies of supply chains might be held responsible for the environmental and social performance of their suppliers, especially if they are a customer facing brand.

The main triggers for a focal company to take actions for promoting and supporting improvement in OSH in the supply chain are:

‣
sustainability and corporate social responsibility agendas;

‣
legal demands;

‣
customers’ demands;

‣
response to stakeholders;

‣
environmental and social pressure groups and reputational loss; and

‣
competitive advantages, profitability and business efficiency.


These reputational risk issues have consequently resulted in many focal companies taking proactive action to promote more socially responsible performance amongst their suppliers.

Clearly, there are potential health and safety implications which might ensue as a result of supply chain governance by a focal company. For example, outsourcing to specialist organisations that have a better understanding (and therefore control) of specific health and safety risks would seem to be an obviously positive impact. However, if the cost pressure on suppliers causes a reduction in the health and safety resources necessary for safe working the impact is clearly negative. (Walters and James, 2009).

It is important that the pressure exerted by a focal company is proportionate to the risk. It is known that focal companies operating in traditional high-risk sectors, such as the petrochemical industry, pay evidently more attention towards OSH matters in their contracting chain.

The HSE (2019) has long been concerned about ‘blue tape’ (“obligations imposed not by government regulation, but by businesses or business intermediary organisations”) leading to ineffective or disproportionate risk control, excessive administrative burdens and sometimes fear and confusion amongst smaller businesses in the supply chain.
