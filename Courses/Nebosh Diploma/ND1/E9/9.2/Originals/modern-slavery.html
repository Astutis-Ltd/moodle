Modern slavery in supply chains


Introduction

Modern slavery is the severe exploitation of other people for personal or commercial gain. The term encompasses slavery, servitude, forced and compulsory labour and human trafficking. Although these activities are unlawful modern slavery is a problem in every country in the world.

There are believed to be over 40 million people enslaved worldwide, generating an estimated US$150 billion in illegal profits per year. Types of slavery include child trafficking, forced labour / debt bondage, sexual exploitation, criminal exploitation and domestic servitude.

Modern slavery occurs in every industry including:

‣
Manufacturing of:
• electronics and high technology
• steel and automobiles
• garments and textiles

‣
Agriculture and fisheries

‣
Mining and minerals

‣
Shipping and transportation.


In 2017, potential victims were reported from 116 different countries of origin. The top six most common countries of origin for potential victims were Albania, Vietnam, Nigeria, Romania, the UK and India. The number of British people identified as modern slavery victims surged by 72 per cent between 2017-18 and 2018-19.


Duty to manage modern slavery in supply chains

The G20 is the international forum that brings together the world’s major economies. Its members account for more than 80% of world GDP, 75% of global trade and 60% of the population of the planet.

At the 2017 G20 Summit in Germany, member countries signed a declaration to eradicate all forms of modern slavery. The declaration specified the responsibility of businesses to ensure their supply chains are free from forced labour, child labour and other forms of modern slavery.

The UK is regarded as a world-leader for its response to modern slavery, introducing a Modern Slavery Strategy in 2014 and the first legislation to address modern slavery in the Modern Slavery Act 2015. The USA and Australia introduced their own Modern Slavery Acts in 2018.

The UK Act requires ‘commercial organisations’ who carry on a business or part of a business in the UK supplying goods or services with a minimum annual turnover of £36 million to publish a slavery and human trafficking statement each financial year on their website (if they have one).

Statutory guidance advises that the following should be included in the statement:

‣
Details of its structure, business, and supply chains

‣
Information on its policies in relation to modern slavery

‣
A summary of the due diligence processes adopted regarding slavery and human trafficking within in its business and supply chains

‣
Identification of the parts of its business and supply chains where there is a risk of slavery and human trafficking, focussing particularly on jurisdictions within which modern slavery is prevalent

‣
Identification of the steps taken to assess and manage such risk include details of its effectiveness in eliminating or otherwise reducing slavery and human trafficking within its business and / or supply chains

‣
Details of the training provided and available to its staff on slavery and human trafficking.


The UK’s Chartered Institute for Purchasing and Supply (CIPS) uses its global standards, networks, and expertise to ensure that procurement and supply chain management professionals have the capabilities and responsibility to deliver sustainability goals for their organisations.

The CIPS view is that procurement professionals have an important role to play in sourcing in a manner that enables and rewards suppliers for good employment practices, rather than purchasing in a manner which drives the use of modern-day slavery practices.

CIPS (2013) has developed a framework for organisations to demonstrate that they are taking the necessary due diligence to keep their supply chains slavery free as part of a wider risk management strategy addressing all ethical and sustainable procurement practices.
