Gross disproportion factor


A control measure is not reasonably practicable if its costs are grossly disproportionate to the benefits.

When the costs of a control measure are greater than the benefits there is disproportion. Whether or not the degree of disproportion is ‘gross’ is a judgement that must be made on a case by case basis.

There is no authoritative guidance from the Courts as to what factors should be taken into account in determining whether or not the costs are grossly disproportionate. The HSE tends to use a ‘rule of thumb’ that a factor of up to 3 (i.e. costs 3 times larger than benefits) would apply for risks to workers, for low risks to members of the public a factor of 2, and for high risks a factor of 10).

It is generally accepted that the GDF should be higher when the risks are close to the maximum tolerable level and lower when the risks are close to the broadly acceptable level. (Tolerability of risk in Element 4).

The consideration can be expressed as the following equation:

If   Costs  
Benefits 		  > 1 x GDF then   costs are grossly disproportionate to the benefits and the measure can be considered not worth doing for the risk reduction achieved.
