Introduction to operational budgets

A commercial budget is the financial expression of an agreed business plan for a given future period. It translates the organisation’s long-term objectives into short term operating plans.

The plan should clearly express the level of activity (revenue generation) and expenditure necessary to implement the plan and deliver on the principal objectives of the business.

To survive, let alone thrive, the organisation needs to generate a net profit by earning more than it spends. It also needs to manage cash flow to ensure that spending money is available when required for paying salaries, rent, utilities bills and other business critical expenses.

The budget is a modelled forecast of what is expected to happen based on as much accurate management information as possible including:

  • short and long-term business objectives
  • external variables such as trading climate and proposed legislative changes
  • opinions from all key areas of the business from product development to sales and marketing
  • availability of human resources and capital
  • current accounting information and previous budgets and accounts.

Operational or trading budgets are normally prepared in advance of and for a trading year, and then broken down into shorter operational periods, e.g. months or quarters, so that progress against the budget can be evaluated. Outline budgets may be prepared for three or five years ahead but forecasting this far in advance is much less reliable.

Research and Development (R&D) budgets and capital expenditure (Capex) budgets may cover much longer time periods. Ten year plans are not unusual in these contexts.

The Financial Director / Chief Finance Officer (FD / CFO) will prepare a draft budget for the organisation as a whole, starting with a sales forecast which indicates the level of expected activity and revenue generation. This will then be broken down into budgets for individual departments or cost centres which will be negotiated with the responsible managers and agreed as being realistic.

The budget should model expected cash-flow, profit and loss, and a balance sheet with total assets and liabilities.

A budget should not be a ‘financial strait-jacket’ but it should provide a ‘navigation chart’ to keep the business on course to achieve its objectives and to define individual responsibilities and accountabilities.

Budgetary control requires a frequent (e.g. monthly) comparison between the budget and the actual results and an analysis of the differences between the two. The comparison serves two purposes:

  • To investigate the reasons for the differences so that corrective action can be taken to increase activity, reduce expenditure, and increase profit
  • To determine whether or not the budget is realistic and if not whether it should be changed.
