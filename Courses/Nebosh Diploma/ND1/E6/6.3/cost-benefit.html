Cost benefit analysis for risk control decisions

Cost benefit analysis (CBA)

Cost benefit analysis (CBA) is a defined methodology for valuing costs and benefits that enables broad comparisons to be made between different investment options. All costs and benefits are quantified in cash terms and the benefit-cost-ratio (BCR) of each option can be calculated and compared thus enabling informed decisions to be made on the basis of a consistent, transparent process.

CBA may be undertaken in support of general commercial decisions or specifically in the health and safety context to help to determine whether or not a particular risk control measure is necessary to ensure safety so far as is reasonably practicable (SFAIRP), or to reduce a level of risk as low as is reasonably practicable (ALARP).

Cases such as the Ford Motor Company decision to go to market with the Ford Pinto in 1970 show the danger of making a decision purely on the basis of CBA. Having failed low speed impact tests the Pinto was known to have a problem with its petrol tank design that could have been addressed for $11 per vehicle but would have significantly delayed the launch date. Ford decided to launch without making the improvement knowing that collisions would result in fuel leaks, fires and explosions. Between 1971 and 1978, the Pinto was responsible for a number of fire-related deaths. Ford put the figure at 23 but other estimates suggest the figure was closer to 500.

In 1973, Ford’s Environmental and Safety Engineering division submitted a cost-benefit analysis entitled Fatalities Associated with Crash Induced Fuel Leakage and Fires for submission to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) in support of Ford’s objection to proposed stronger fuel system regulation. The analysis compared the cost of repairs to the societal costs for injuries and deaths related to fires in cases of vehicle roll overs for all cars sold in the US by all manufacturers. Ford estimated the cost of fuel system modifications to reduce fire risks in roll over events to be $11 per car across 12.5 million cars and light trucks (all manufacturers), for a total of $137 million. The design changes were estimated to save 180 burn deaths and 180 serious injuries per year, a cost to society of $49.5 million.

As the costs of improvement were more than 2.75 times greater than the societal benefits, it was used to argue against making the fuel system modifications.

CBA and health and safety risk controls

In the context of health and safety risk controls the point of the exercise is to inform a decision on whether or not a control measure is proprtionate to the risk (constitutes sensible risk management) or not (as per the Court of Appeal decision in Edwards v NCB 1949). Commercial considerations such as ability to pay are not directly relevant.

'Reasonably practicable’ is a narrower term than ‘physically possible’ … a computation must be made by the owner in which the quantum of risk is placed on one scale and the sacrifice involved in the measures necessary for averting the risk (whether in money, time or trouble) is placed on the other, and that, if it be shown that there is a gross disproportion between them - the risk being insignificant in relation to the sacrifice - the defendants discharge the onus on them.

The HSE has produced guidance on the correct use of CBA as part of as low as reasonably practicable (ALARP) decisions in accordance with its aim to create:

A culture in HSE that values the principles of risk assessment and management and working practice that embeds proportionate and effective risk governance.

Appropriate costs

The HSE considers the following as appropriate if directly incurred by the duty holder:

  • The necessary and sufficient costs for the purpose of implementing the risk reduction measure (no gold plating)
  • The costs of installation, operation, training and any additional maintenance
  • The business losses incurred from any shutdown of the plant undertaken solely for the purpose of putting the measure into place
  • Lost production during the period of plant shutdown (sacrifice implies non-recoverable cost).
    • For deferred production only the ‘interest’ on the lost production should be accounted for plus an allowance for operational costs during the implementation time and potential increase in operational costs at the end of life. (scheduling the work to coincide with planned downtimes (e.g. for maintenance) should be considered as a means of controlling these costs)
    • Ongoing production losses as a result of the measure.

Note: Savings as a result of the measure (e.g. reduced operational costs, avoidance of damage and reinstatement costs if relevant) should be offset against the above costs. These are not considered safety benefits but are counted as ‘cost savings’, i.e. they reduce the overall cost of implementing a measure.

Benefits

The HSE’s main concern is to ensure that all benefits of implementing a health and safety improvement measure are included and that the benefits associated with the measure are not underestimated in any way. The benefits should include:

  • All reduction in risk to members of the public, to workers and to the wider community, i.e. preventing:
    • fatalities
    • injuries (major to minor)
    • ill health
    • environmental damage if relevant (e.g. CoMAH).
  • Avoidance of deployment of emergency services and avoidance of countermeasures such as evacuation and post-accident decontamination if appropriate
  • The benefits of incidental risk control should be considered (i.e. a measure is implemented to control a specific problem but also delivers improvements in other areas).

Note: Re-instatement costs may need to be treated as a benefit rather than offsetting them against costs. This would be the case if the plant being re-instated were a safety-related plant (e.g. one that treats hazardous waste). This can represent a bias in favour of safety as the gross disproportion factor is applied to all benefits prior to them being compared to the costs.
