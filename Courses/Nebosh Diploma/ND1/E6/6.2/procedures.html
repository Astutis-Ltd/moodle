Procedures for resolving conflict and introducing change

A ‘conflict situation’ is one in which the concerns of two people appear to be incompatible. This may occur where individual objectives have not been aligned holistically with the organisation’s objectives putting colleagues in a position where they are competing for common resources.

Examples might include:

  • a production manager not wanting production down time for maintenance activities bringing conflict with the maintenance manager, or
  • the learning and development manager and health and safety manager requiring operational staff to attend a training course and the production manager feeling unable to spare the staff from production duties.

Influencing skills through consultation, persuasion and negotiation are important but in addition to the logical aspects of the dialogue, the personality types of the stakeholders will also be a factor and may predispose the parties to behave to type.

It is important to be professional, unemotional and respectful of the other parties position at all times. If the issue cannot be resolved through the use of authority or legitimate position power (i.e. in the management system the claim of one party clearly takes priority over the other) or persuasion, there may be a need to defer to a higher power for mediation and resolution.

In these situations it is important that the lessons are learned at an organisational level and systems are adapted or clarified to remove the opportunity for similar future conflict.
