Reviewing health and safety performance

Reviewing is the process of making judgements about the adequacy of performance and taking decisions about the nature and timing of the actions necessary to remedy deficiencies.

Reviewing should be a continuous process undertaken at different levels within the organisation. It includes responses by first-line supervisors and other line managers to remedy failures to implement workplace precautions which they observe in the course of routine activities, and to remedy sub-standard performance identified by active and reactive monitoring in their areas of responsibility.

Management reviews should be carried out by top management, on a regular basis (quarterly, semi-annually, or annually) and can be carried out by meetings or other communication means.

Reviews of the performance of parts of the health and safety management system can be held at more frequent intervals, if appropriate.

Top management reviews should focus on the overall performance of the health and safety management system with regard to its:

  • Suitability – appropriate for the organisations size, hazard burden and risk profile
  • Adequacy – fully addressing the organisation’s health and safety policy and objectives
  • Effectiveness – accomplishing the desired results.

In planning for a management review, consideration should be given to the topics to be reviewed, the appropriate participants and their responsibilities within the review, and specific evidence to be considered at the review.

Inputs into the review process

HSG65 considers that the main sources of information will be the results of monitoring activities and audits of the management system. ISO 45001 (and ISO 45002, guidelines for its implementation) recommends consideration of the following inputs:

  • status of actions from previous management reviews
  • the needs and expectations of interested parties
  • the extent to which the OH&S policy and the OH&S objectives have been met
  • information on the OH&S performance, including trends in:
    • incidents, nonconformities, corrective actions and continual improvement
    • monitoring and measurement results
    • results of evaluation of compliance with legal requirements and other requirements
    • audit results
    • consultation and participation of workers
    • risks and opportunities
  • adequacy of resources for maintaining an effective OH&S management system
  • relevant communication(s) with interested parties
  • opportunities for continual improvement.

Any of the input information could necessitate a redesign or modification to any part of the Health and Safety Management System (HSMS) or a change in direction or objectives.

As discussed in the earlier section on proactive safety management (Safety II) it is also important to take the opportunity to learn from what is going well in the organisation, as well as the failures.

Outputs from the review process

The outputs of the management review are effectively the conclusions drawn regarding the ongoing suitability, adequacy and effectiveness of the OH&S management system in achieving its intended outcomes; and any necessary decisions regarding:

  • continual improvement opportunities
  • any need for changes to the OH&S management system
  • resources needed
  • actions, if needed
  • opportunities to improve integration of the OH&S management system with other business processes
  • any implications for the strategic direction of the organisation.

The outputs should be communicated to workers or their representatives and a suitable record should be kept of the review process.

Progress against the review output remedial actions should be closely monitored. The relative priority of individual responses should be determined by the degree of risk involved and the availability of resources.

Depending on the decisions and actions agreed at a review, the nature and types of communication of the results of the review, and to whom they will be communicated, should also be considered.

Feeding information on success and failure back into the system is an essential element in motivating employees to maintain and improve performance. Successful organisations emphasise positive reinforcement and concentrate on encouraging progress on those indicators which demonstrate improvements in risk control.
