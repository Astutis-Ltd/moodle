Health and safety monitoring

Introduction

If you cannot measure, your knowledge is meagre and unsatisfactory

Lord Kelvin

The traditional approaches to measuring health and safety performance focussed on the measurement of negative outcomes or failures, i.e. injury and ill-health statistics. This is very different to measurement of other aspects of an organisations performance which focus on measures of success such as profits, return on investment and market share.

Health and safety differs from many areas measured by managers because success results in the absence of an outcome (injuries or ill-health) rather than a presence.

A low injury or ill-health rate, even over a period of years, is no guarantee that risks are being controlled and does not indicate low injuries or ill-health going forward.

Historical records can be particularly deceptive indicators of safety performance in organisations where major hazards are present, but there is a low probability of accidents.

There is no single reliable measure of health and safety performance. What is required is a ‘basket’ of measures or a ‘balanced scorecard’, providing information on a range of health and safety activities.

Problems with an over reliance on injury/ill-health statistics as an indicator of health and safety performance include:

  • under-reporting – particularly when injury and ill-health rates relate to reward systems
  • a low injury rate might be attributable to luck or fewer people exposed, rather than good health and safety management
  • injury rates often do not reflect the potential severity of an event, merely the consequence
  • people can stay off work for reasons which do not reflect the severity of the event
  • evidence shows there is not necessarily a relationship between ‘occupational’ injury statistics such as ‘slips, trip and falls’ and control of major accident hazards, such as loss of containment of flammable or toxic material
  • low injury rates can lead to complacency
  • low injury rate result in few data points being available
  • a failure (injury or ill-health) must have occurred in order to generate a data point.

Recognition of the drawbacks associated with the use of reactive injury and ill-health data has led to a search for more proactive or ‘up stream’ measures of performance. Often though, this has resulted in the measurement of things which can be easily counted, such as numbers of training courses or numbers of inspections.

What is necessary is a systematic approach to derive meaningful proactive measures that indicate the effectiveness, or otherwise of the risk control systems.

The objectives of active monitoring

  • progress against specific plans and objectives
  • effectiveness of the health and safety management system
  • degree of compliance with performance standards.

This provides a firm basis for decisions about improvements in risk control.

Active monitoring systems should be proportional to the hazard profile. Activity should concentrate on areas where it is likely to produce the greatest benefit and lead to the greatest control of risk.

Key risk control systems and related workplace precautions should therefore be monitored in more detail or more often (or both) than low-risk systems or management arrangements.

The objectives of reactive monitoring

Reactive monitoring systems are triggered by an event and should be designed and developed to ensure effective identification and reporting of:

  • injuries and cases of ill-health (including monitoring of sickness absence records)
  • other losses, such as damage to property
  • incidents, including near misses with the potential to cause injury, ill-health or loss
  • complaints by the workforce regarding health, safety and welfare issues
  • enforcement actions, such as prosecutions, enforcement notices or informal letters
  • civil claims for compensation
  • the costs arising out of all of the above.

Each event provides an opportunity for an organisation to check performance, learn from mistakes, and improve the health and safety management system and risk controls.

Collecting information on serious injuries and ill-health should not present major problems for most organisations, but learning about minor injuries, other losses, incidents and hazards can prove more challenging. There is value in collecting information on all actual and potential losses to learn how to prevent more serious events.

Monitoring, measurement and types of measures

Monitoring involves collecting information, such as measurements or observations, over time, which when collated provides management information on performance trends and fluctuations.

Quantitative, objective measures will be more reliable than qualitative, subjective measures.

Measurements can be either active or reactive, quantitative or qualitative, objective or subjective.

    Active or reactive

  • Active (or pro‑active) measures provide feedback on performance before an accident or incident
  • Reactive measures involve identifying and reporting on incidents to check the controls in place are adequate, to identify weaknesses or gaps in control systems and to learn from mistakes.

    Quantitative or qualitative

  • Quantitative measurements are numerical, for example; the accident injury incidence rate (AIIR) or the percentage of operators observed wearing ear defenders
  • Qualitative measures are descriptive and relate to the measurers perception of the quality of an object, for example; are the standards of housekeeping good, bad or indifferent or how competent is a colleague?

    Objective or subjective

  • Objective performance measures involve an impartial measurement
  • Subjective performance measures are influenced by the observer’s personal judgment.

Objectivity and subjectivity occur along a spectrum from the highly objective to the highly subjective. In a swimming race, competitors are accurately timed – a highly objective measure, whereas in a diving competition the divers are graded in a highly subjective way by judges interpreting competition rules.

Health and safety performance measurement is undertaken to provide management information to show how well, or not, an organisation is managing the risks arising from its undertaking.

Management information is essential to enable an organisations senior management to understand the organisations performance, solve problems, analyse trends and forecast the future.
