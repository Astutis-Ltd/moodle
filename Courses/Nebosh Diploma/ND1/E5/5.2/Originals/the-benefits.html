The benefits of measuring what goes right
(proactive safety management)


As discussed in element 2 traditional (Safety I) approaches to safety management have been criticised for focussing on things that go wrong, rather than on things that go right. ‘Safety II’ proposes a shift of emphasis, focussing on the things that go right, looking for measures of the presence of safety, rather than the absence of danger. It is important to note that Hollnagel has always advocated adding ‘Safety II’ thinking to traditional approaches to safety rather than replacing the old with the new.

With ‘Safety II’ it is important to look at what goes right as well as what goes wrong and to learn from successes as well as failures. Note the commonality of approach with resilience engineering and high reliability organisations as discussed in element 4.

Whereas ‘Safety I’ assumes that things go well because workers follow the rules and work as imagined, ‘Safety II’ assumes that things go well because workers can adapt and make sensible adjustments to cope with current and future situational demands. Therefore, ‘work as done’ may be significantly different from ‘work as imagined’. The variability that gets the job done safely on one day is the same variability that causes an accident on the next. Safety II proposes that rather than attempting to create safety management procedures which everyone must comply efforts are put into building resilient systems that can flex to accommodate unpredictable variables.


Safety 1 tends to focus on in depth analysis of things that go wrong at the extreme left of the curve. Safety II advocates looking at the breadth of information available under the central parts of the curve to understand what people are doing on a day-to-day basis to keep things going right.

Measuring what goes right involves understanding ‘work as done’, observing work and interviewing workers to understand the challenges workers face and the types of ‘performance adjustments’ they make to adapt to day-to-day variations. In addition to field observations and interviews there are a number of approaches used in social sciences and action research that could be useful here, such as cooperative or collaborative inquiry, appreciative inquiry and exnovation as defined in table 5.5

Co-operative / collaborative inquiry

A way in which people who have similar concerns and interests intentionally develop together their own experience and action to make sense of their lives and acquire new ways of looking at things and, above all, to know how to act to change the things they may want to change and how to do things better.

The inquiry follows a research cycle through the four different types of knowledge: propositional, practical, experiential and presentational.

Appreciative inquiry A model that seeks to engage stakeholders in self-determined change. Appreciative inquiry usually follows the 4D cycle of discovery, dream, design and destiny.
Exnovation Stabilisation of the last innovation. In a situation where a process has been tested and finally confirmed to be the best from all tested before, the exnovation occurs.

Workers typically make performance adjustments for one of three reasons:

1)
to maintain or create good working conditions

2)
to compensate for something that is missing

3)
to avoid future problems.


All three adjustments are used constantly at work, either individually or in combinations.

Looking for opportunities to learn how to improve in this way can result in ergonomic improvements to system design and worker development through training etc helping to deliver more reliable and resilient work processes.
