Leading and lagging indicators




Consider the following examples:

When driving on a long journey, running out of fuel could be an event of concern. The fuel gauge and low fuel warning provide leading indicators and will give early warnings that the vehicle is going to run out of fuel which will allow preventive action to be taken. Should the vehicle stop suddenly because it has run out of fuel that provides a lagging indicator.

Blood pressure level provides a leading indicator of an individual’s risk of experiencing a heart attack, stroke or liver failure and again provides an early warning and an opportunity for lifestyle changes or early medical intervention. Data that can be gathered on numbers or rates of heart attacks etc that may be associated with high blood pressure provide lagging indicators.

‣
Leading indicators can be actively monitored, focussing on the ongoing effectiveness of controls. They can be considered as measures of process or inputs essential to deliver the desired safety outcome

‣
Lagging indicators can be reactively monitored and provide measures of failure showing when a desired safety outcome has failed or has not been achieved.


There are clear benefits in having combinations of leading and lagging indicators to provide a complete picture of performance in a particular area.

Key Performance Indicators (KPI) for reviewing overall health and safety performance could include:

‣
Lagging indicators of accident, ill-health and incident rates (accompanied by analysis of both the immediate and underlying causes, trends and common features)

‣
Leading indicators such as:
• the degree of compliance with health and safety system requirements
• performance against specific objectives and plans
• level of unsafe behaviours and unsafe conditions in the workplace.


These indicators are consistent with the development of a positive health and safety culture. They emphasise achievement and success rather than merely measuring failure by looking only at accident data.

The obvious problem with lagging indicators is that the learning opportunity comes after a failure and associated losses. The challenge with leading indicators is finding the right things to measure. To be effective a leading indicator has to be causally linked to, and therefore an effective predictor of, the event and outcome of concern.

As an example, the amount of safety training undertaken isn’t necessarily a good leading indicator of safety performance, whereas a reduction in unsafe acts and conditions (which might be achieved as a result of a training intervention) could be (basic domino theory).
