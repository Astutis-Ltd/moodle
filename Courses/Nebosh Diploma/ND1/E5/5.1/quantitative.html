Quantitative analysis of accident and ill-health data

The impact that statistics can have on an organisation and organisational reputation

Effectively managing reputational risk requires recognition that reputation is a matter of perception of the various stakeholders of the organisation’s performance across a range of variables such as quality, governance, social responsibility, financial performance etc.

It can therefore be seen that an organisations reputation is distinct from its performance and may be better or worse. Understanding this potential disparity and managing the closure of any reputation-reality gaps is essential to maintain the organisations reputation.

When the organisation’s reputation is better than the underlying reality a substantial risk exists in that if a poor underlying reality is revealed the reputation will crash rapidly to match the reality. As an example, consider the positioning of BP as an environmentally responsible organisation in the early 2000’s as impacted by the Texas City refinery explosion in 2005 and the Prudhoe Bay, Alaska oil pipeline leak in 2006. The resulting media interest led to a 50% drop in share value with BP dropping from the second largest global oil company, by value, to the fourth.

“Character is like a tree and reputation like its shadow. The shadow is what we think of it. The tree is the real thing.”

Abraham Lincoln (1809 - 1865)

The availability of performance data statistics across a balanced scorecard of variables will be helpful in recognising and addressing any gaps between an organisation’s reputation and reality.

Good data across a range of trust factors such as integrity, social responsibility etc demonstrably impacts positively on staff recruitment and retention, customer engagement and shareholder investment.

Methods of calculating loss rates

The continuous measurement of accident and incident numbers over time allows trends to be plotted illustrating any fluctuations in performance levels over time.

However such fluctuations in performance can be influenced by a range of business variables other than the direct impact of health and safety management. For example: increases in levels of production, changes in staff levels or working patterns, or an increase in staff turnover.

To combat these additional variables accident rates are used. A rate does not consider the accident numbers in isolation but in the context of a specific denominator such as number of employees or number of hours worked. Providing that consistent definitions are used, rates also allow useful comparisons to be made of performance in different businesses etc.

Accident Incidence Rate (AIR)

This is probably the most widely used formula, and is the number of defined accidents, (e.g. RIDDOR reportable accident incidence rate) in a period per thousand employees.

Number of definied accidents

Average number employed

x 1 000

The HSE publishes accident incidence rates in its annual report (available from the HSE statistics webpages), where incidence is calculated as a rate of reportable injuries per 100 000 employees.

When comparing and benchmarking incidence rates across departments or sites, care should be taken to ensure a comparison of like with like, for example:

  • same accident type (definitions may vary between workplaces)
  • the working day is the same (e.g. longer shift lengths / overtime etc. result in more hours spent working and a likely increase in accident numbers)
  • full time staff are not compared with part time staff.

Accident Frequency Rate (AFR)

The AFR is the number of defined accidents in a period per 100 000 person hours worked. This can be a more useful measure than the AIR in organisations with many part time workers or long shifts and overtime working.

x 1 000 000

Accident Severity Rate (ASR)

The ASR is the number of working days lost in a period per 1 000 person hours worked. It indicates the severity of the accidents in terms of time lost due to injury in addition to the number of accidents. The results can be skewed by low morale (people taking off longer than their injury warrants), sick pay policies (people continuing to work in spite of injuries because they need their pay) and ‘light duties’ work (minimises days lost).

Total number of days lost in a period

Total person hours worked in period

x 1 000

Ill-health prevalence rate

The ill-health prevalence rate is calculated as

Number of people with a disease or condition at a specific time

Number of people in the population at risk at a specific time

x 1 000

If the data has been collected at a single point in time it is referred to as a point prevalence rate. It is often more convenient to collect data over a specified period of time, in which case it is a period prevalence rate.

In interpreting a prevalence rate the following factors should be considered:

  • Severity of illness – if the mortality rate is high the prevalence rate is depressed
  • Duration of illness – short duration illnesses will have a lower prevalence than long duration illnesses
  • Number of new cases – if many people develop a disease its prevalence is higher than if few people do so.

The incidence rate is used to describe the number of new cases of an illness occurring in a specific time period.
