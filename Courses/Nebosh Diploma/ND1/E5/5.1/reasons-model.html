Reason’s model of accident causation (Swiss Cheese Model)

Reason observed that organisational accidents (unlike individual accidents) typically have multiple causes involving many people operating at different management levels.

Reason’s basic theory is that for an organisational accident to occur, the protective barriers and safeguards (defences) designed to prevent losses must be breached.

Reason considered that an organisations processes could be divided into production processes and protection processes.

  • Production processes such as the manufacture of goods, extraction of raw materials, delivery of service etc. expose people and assets to danger
  • Protection processes, which are designed to protect people and assets from the danger arising from production processes.

Reason coined the term ‘defences in depth’ to explain the concept of successive layers of protection guarding against the possible failure of the one in front.

‘Defences in depth’ could look like this:

  • creation of hazard awareness and understanding
  • clear guidance on safe operation
  • alarms and warning of imminent danger
  • restoring systems to a safe state in off-normal situations
  • positioning barriers between hazards and potential losses
  • eliminating or controlling hazards that escape the barrier
  • providing means of escape and rescue should hazard containment fail.

In an ideal world the layers of defences in depth prevent the hazard from adversely affecting people and assets but in the real world each layer of defence will have gaps or weaknesses. Reason explained these gaps in his ‘Swiss cheese’ model.

The Swiss cheese model should be thought of as a moving picture with each defence coming into and out of play depending on local conditions. The ‘holes’ in each defence also move, shrink or grow in response to operator actions and local needs.

The ‘holes’ may be created by active failures and/or latent conditions.

  • Active failures are unsafe acts that have a direct and immediate effect on system safety. Typically they are errors and violations at the sharp end of the system
  • Latent conditions include: poor design, gaps in supervision, undetected manufacturing defects, training gaps and maintenance failures. Latent conditions may be present for many years before they combine with active failures and local circumstances to penetrate layers of defences. Latent conditions typically arise from strategic, top level decisions.

For an organisational accident to occur a rare conjunction of a set of holes in successive defences would be necessary for the hazard to reach and damage people or other assets.

Reason considered that protection processes fail because of a combination of human, technical and organisational factors: organisational factors lead to the creation of error-provoking local working conditions resulting in an individual (or team) unsafe act. If the defences subsequently fail the outcome is a loss.

The below figure shows Reason’s model to explain the sequence of accident causation and the corresponding, reversed, sequence of accident investigation. In an accident investigation the sequence works back from the loss, firstly considering how the defences failed and what active failures and latent conditions were involved. Subsequently for each unsafe act the investigation seeks to determine what local conditions shaped or provoked it, and ultimately what upstream organisational factors contributed to the development of each local condition.
