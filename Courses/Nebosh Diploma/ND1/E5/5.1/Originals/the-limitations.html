The limitations of accident and incident data


Introduction

It is difficult, if not impossible, to gain information from every member of a defined population. Typically, sample data is obtained from a (hopefully) representative sample of the population and inferences are made regarding the population as a whole.

The larger the sample the more likely it is to be representative of the whole. If several large samples were randomly taken from the same population you might expect slight variations, however they would be expected to be similar to each other and to the population as a whole.

Statistical models are used to assess how closely the sample data represents the population at large.

The mean is the simplest statistical model. Adding a range of values together and dividing by the number of values gives the arithmetical mean. This is a hypothetical value in that it need not match any of the actual values and may well be a fraction where this would be impossible (e.g. 2.4 children).

How representative a model is can be determined by comparing the differences (deviances) between actual observed measures and the model (the mean).


The total error in the model could be calculated by adding up all the deviances however this would balance out at zero, with the positive and negative deviances balancing each other out.

To address this, the sum of squared errors is calculated to give an overall positive number. This then poses a different problem in that the number gets ever larger with the population size.

Dividing the sum of squared errors by n – 1 (the number of observations minus one) calculates the variance which is the average error between the mean and the observations made.

The problem with the variance as a measure is that it is in units squared. By taking the square root of the variance the measure of average error is being made in the same units as the original measure. This is known as the standard deviation (SD or σ ‑ sigma).

The standard deviation is a useful measure of the fit of the model, i.e. how well the mean fits the data. A small standard deviation indicates that observed data points are close to the mean. A large standard deviation means that the observed data points are distant from the mean (i.e. that the mean is not an accurate representation of the data).

With a normally distributed population the standard deviation is also useful in understanding proportions under the curve. The point of inflection (see below) is the point at which the curve changes from convex to concave and begins to tail off. The distance from the mean to the point of inflection is equal to one SD. The range of mean plus or minus one standard deviation (M ± 1SD or 2SD or 2σ) encompasses approximately 68% of the sample population. 95% of the population is in the range M ± 2SD (or 4SD or 4σ). 99.7% of the population is within M ± 3SD (or 6SD or 6σ).


Statistical variability

A variable is something that changes (or varies). The variation may be between individuals (example: height, IQ, eye colour), by geographical location (example: site A or site B), or over time (example: by quarter or by year).

Variables may be:

Categorical – one that names distinct entities. Categorical variables may be:

‣
binary – when there are only 2 options. For example: Yes/No or Ill/Well

‣
nominal – when there are more than two options. For example: Over 3 day injury / Specified major injury/Dangerous occurrence

‣
ordinal – when categories are placed in order of occurrence.


Continuous – a continuous variable gives a score on a scale. Continuous variables include:

‣
interval variables – where the intervals on a scale represent equal differences in the property being measured

‣
ratio variables – have a meaningful zero and a difference on the scale means the same wherever it is along the scale.


When testing hypothesis (example hypotheses: exposure to chemical A causes illness B) the effect variable is said to be dependent upon the cause variable. These are also known as predictor variable and outcome variable.


Validity and reliability

Variables should be measurable consistently over time and across locations. For directly measurable variables, suitably calibrated equipment (examples: sound level meter, weighing scales or thermometer) help to ensure the consistency of measurement. In other cases variables are measured indirectly using tools such as self-assessment questionnaires.

The validity of a measure indicates whether an instrument actually measures what it is intended to measure.
The reliability of a measure relates to the consistency of measures across different situations.


Chance and bias

The results of a test sample may vary from the population at large due to chance or random fluctuation. This is most likely with a small sample which is less likely to be representative of the parent population than a large sample. For example, if a coin is tossed three times and comes up heads each time this is a matter of chance and is not representative of the expected result in a larger population. If a coin is tossed one thousand times, the heads: tails ratio should be close to 50:50.

Bias is a systematic (or non-random) variation between a sample population and the parent population. The major biases are:

‣
Measurement bias – Example: an incorrectly calibrated measurement device delivers consistently incorrect results

‣
Selection bias where a sample population is personally selected. Example: in market research work researchers are more likely to approach the people who appear most approachable – this group may be quite different from the parent population of all people on the street.
