Multi-causality theory


Accidents typically arise as a consequence of a combination of causes so rather than a linear row of dominoes it is better represented as a tree with multiple rows of dominoes coming together to cause the accident.

There are numerous tools and techniques available for structuring the lines of inquiry in an investigation. Their suitability will depend on the complexity of the accident.

In a simple example, the investigation works down from a top event which describes the accident and injury working through different rows of immediate, underlying and root causes.


In his 1971 book ‘Techniques of Safety Management’ Dan Peterson proposed an accident model that was an amalgamation of both the domino and multi-causality theories as shown above. Peterson suggested that there are many contributing factors, causes and sub-causes behind every accident, combining together, in random fashion, to cause accidents. Therefore there is a need to identify as many of these causes as possible during an investigation, rather than just one for each stage of the domino sequence.

Later in his career Peterson developed a ‘human factors’ model of accident causation to address his observation that accidents are caused by people and not ‘things’.
