Carroll's pyramid of CSR


Archie Carrol (1979) came up with a four-part definitional framework for CSR which was originally stated as follows: “Corporate social responsibility encompasses the economic, legal, ethical, and discretionary (philanthropic) expectations that society has of organisations at a given point in time.”

The four responsibilities create a foundation or infrastructure that frames the nature of a businesses’ responsibilities to the society of which it is a part.

In 1991 Carroll recast his definition in the form of a CSR pyramid. The purpose of the pyramid was to move beyond a definition and to illustrate the building block nature of the four-part framework.


The economic responsibility was placed as the base of the pyramid because it is a foundational requirement in business. Sustained profitability is essential to support society’s other expectations of the organisation. The infrastructure of CSR is built upon the premise of an economically sound and sustainable business.

Society is increasingly showing business that they are required to comply with their legal responsibilities. Law and regulations are the ground rules within which businesses must operate in a civil society.

Businesses are also expected to operate in accordance with their ethical responsibilities. This means that business has the expectation, and obligation, to do what is right, just, and fair and to avoid or minimise harm to all the stakeholders with whom it interacts.

Finally, businesses are expected to be a good ‘corporate citizens’, embracing their philanthropic responsibilities and giving back to the communities of which they are part through contribution of financial, physical, and human resources.

As companies seek to perform with respect to their various responsibilities, tensions and trade-offs inevitably arise. How companies decide to balance these responsibilities goes a long way towards defining their CSR orientation and reputation.

As an example, the economic responsibility to owners or shareholders requires a careful trade-off between short-term and long-term profitability. In the short-term, companies’ expenditures on legal, ethical and philanthropic obligations may appear to conflict with their responsibilities to their shareholders.

The pyramid is a sustainable stakeholder framework Each of the four components of responsibility addresses different stakeholders in different ways:

‣
Economic responsibilities most dramatically impact shareholders and employees should the business lose its financial viability

‣
Legal responsibilities are certainly important for business owners, although the threat of litigation against businesses arises most often from employees and consumer stakeholders

‣
Ethical responsibilities affect all stakeholder groups

‣
Philanthropic responsibilities most affect the community and non-profit organisations, but research has also shown that a company’s philanthropic involvement is significantly related to its employees’ morale and engagement.


The pyramid should be seen as sustainable in that these responsibilities represent long term obligations that overarch into future generations of stakeholders as well. The pyramid is intended to be seen as a dynamic, adaptable framework focussing on both the present and the future.

CSR is inseparable from stakeholder requirements and sustainability.
