Introduction

CSR has been defined as:

a commitment to improve community well-being through discretionary business practices and contributions of corporate resources

(Kotler and Lee, 2005); and

the commitment of business to contribute to sustainable economic development, working with employees, their families, the local community and society at large to improve their quality of life

(WBCSD, 1997).

Philanthropic capitalism is not a new concept, during the industrial revolution (19th century) some manufacturers saw the importance of balancing profits against the social and physical wellbeing of their workers.

After the Second World War the growth of multinational companies and the complexities of the global economy brought the question of the relationship between business and society back to the forefront.

The criteria used to define CSR today have been discussed in American and European academic literature since the 1950’s.

The globalisation of economies, brands and financial markets continues to evolve and many businesses have adapted by restructuring their activities and relocating many activities to regions with ‘cheap’ labour.

Greater ease of mobility, access to information, and the availability of instant communications have all contributed to the increasing concern about the social responsibility of organisations. Consumers, customers, shareholders and charitable donors all exert financial influence on organisations in relation to social responsibility.
