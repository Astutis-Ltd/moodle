The Kubler-Ross change curve

Dr. Elizabeth Kübler-Ross, a Swiss psychologist first explored the ‘five stages of death’ in her 1970 book “On Death and Dying”.

Also known as the ‘five stages of grieving’ the model was developed following extensive work with terminally ill patients and their families. It captured the most commonly observed stages of grief. The model does not describe a linear process. It is acknowledged that not all people go through all stages. Some people may experience only one or two and others may experience none at all.

Kübler-Ross also found that the model worked in dealing with issues other than death, notably coping with disability, relationship problems, debt and work issues.

Subsequently the Kübler-Ross model has been used to understand what workers experience psychologically during the transition period of organisational change where many workers effectively ‘mourn’ the loss of the status quo.

This insight can help managers understand the position employees are in so far as adapting to change is concerned and can therefore help managers determine appropriate guidance and methods of communication for those on the change path.

Although referred to as the ‘five stages’ the process is often presented as six or seven stages on a curve. The graph below shows the curve as currently presented by the EKR Foundation.

Shock and denial

The Stage of shock or denial is usually the first stage in the Kubler-Ross Model and is mostly short-lived. This is a phase during which one puts on a temporary defence mechanism and takes time to process certain disturbing news or reality.

Here, the manager’s role should be to help employees understand why the change is happening and how it can be helpful. Care must be taken to avoid overwhelming the employees with information.

Frustration (anger)

When the realisation hits, and the gravity of the situation is understood, the grieving person may become angry and may look for someone to blame.

At this stage it is important that managers realise that workers may vent their anger in excessive ways. This should be understood as a natural reaction and the organisations efforts should remain on clear communication and provision of support mechanisms. When anger passes thoughts turn to ways to postpone the inevitable and try to find out the best thing left in the situation.

At this stage employees realise that they must adapt to new situations and circumstances and try to find the best possible scenario for them to fit in and adapt to. At this point they may try to negotiate with management. The organisations efforts should now shift to ensuring that everyone gets the best training to enable the change to be incorporated successfully. This may take time and should not be rushed.

Depression / experimentation

Depression is the stage in which the person tends to feel sadness, fear, regret, guilt and other negative emotions. Some employees may now realise that there is no way out of the situation, and this may prove difficult to handle.

Other employees may move into the experimenting or learning phase. This may also be challenging for many employees and could result in low energy and morale. The focus here should be on ensuring that the training given looks to excite the employees about the change and helps them to get ready to move on and get stuck in.

Decision (acceptance) and integration

At this stage it is realised that fighting the change is not going to make the grief go away and the grieving person becomes resigned to the situation and accepts it completely.

At this stage people begin to embrace the change, accept the situation and start building new hopes and aspirations. Some workers will still be negative and be resigned to the change, others will react in a more positive way. Changes become integrated.
