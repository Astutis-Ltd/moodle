Key principles of managing organisational change

Kurt Lewin, a physicist and social scientist first presented his model of organisational change in 1947. Using the analogy of changing the shape of a block of ice Lewin described a journey with a beginning, middle and end. Lewin’s simple “Unfreeze, Change, Refreeze” model is still relevant and used today.

The first stage of ‘unfreezing’ is about getting ready to change and involves preparing people for change by removing barriers and ideally creating a situation in which they want the change.

The second ‘transitioning’ stage requires a clearly mapped process with achievable ‘stepping-stones’ to the future state and feedback provided on successes, failures and lessons learned each step of the way. Careful thought should be given to workers and managers needs for information, training, coaching and support throughout the change process. Trying to effect a change too quickly or without worker engagement can be counter-productive leading to worker resistance and antipathy.

The third ‘refreezing’ stage is about establishing stability once the changes have been made, are accepted and become normalised. It takes time for people to form new relationships and become comfortable with the new routines. Ongoing reinforcement of the benefits of the change is essential to ensure the change is accepted and maintained.

As discussed earlier organisational changes can introduce significant hazards to safety and health. A proportionate, thorough assessment of the direct and indirect effects of the change on risk controls, throughout the transitional period and consequent to the change is important in ensuring effective ongoing management of risk.

It is generally considered sensible to focus efforts on one significant change at a time and to proceed incrementally. Too many simultaneous changes, and rushed changes present opportunities for control to be lost.

The HSE has published guidance for employers on how to manage the impact of organisational change on their control of major accident hazards in accordance with a three-step process shown below.

Step 1: Getting organised

Policy

A strong and clear policy is required to:

  • set out principles, commitments and accountabilities in relation to impact on health, safety and the environment
  • commit to proportionate consideration of all organisational changes, large and small as even those not obviously connected to safety need to be considered.

Senior management accountability

Senior management commitment should be clearly demonstrated by appointing a senior manager of appropriate influence to champion the change – ensuring the provision of an appropriate level of resource and attention, proportionate to:

  • the complexity of the change
  • the scale of the hazards concerned
  • the degree to which the change may impact on the management of major hazards.

Change-management procedure

The change should be planned in a thorough, systematic, and realistic way ensuring that the following are made clear:

  • the processes or activities that are to be carried out
  • who is accountable and who is responsible for these activities
  • who else is involved, and how
  • what potential risk factors are to be considered
  • who reviews the change process, when and how.

All stages of the process should be adequately recorded to ensure transparency, auditability, and accountability.

Communication and co-operation

All concerned should be consulted from an early stage, and be kept informed and involved throughout the process. This will help to:

  • improve industrial relations and acceptance of the change
  • utilise the unique knowledge of employees and contractors.

Review and challenge

Senior management should be provided with adequate information to review progress regularly. Plans should be changed if risk assessment shows a potential significant risk.

Step 2: Risk assessment

Risk assessment is required to ensure that following the change, the organisation will have sufficient resources, competence and motivation to ensure safety without making unrealistic demands on people.

The risk assessment should address:

  • risks arising from the process of change (how the organisation gets there)
  • risks and opportunities resulting from the outcome of change (where the organisation wants to get to).

Assessment procedures

The main risks are identified through:

  • mapping of tasks, individuals and processes from the current position to the position after the change
  • scenario assessments when the reorganisation impacts staff who may have a role in handling or responding to crises such as upsets and emergencies.

The assessment should consider the following factors:

  • past experience and historical data
  • risks from using contractors
  • supervision and monitoring to ensure the quality and safety of work
  • contingency plans to maintain low risks (and not increase risks) should the change not go to plan
  • workload assessment to avoid the consequences of overloading (fatigue, shortcuts etc.)
  • the potential for human failures
  • the effects on emergency procedures
  • maintaining adequate welfare facilities
  • changes to the provision of appointed persons e.g. first-aiders, competent advisors etc.

Step 3: Implementing and monitoring

Implementation

The risk assessments should result in action plans, milestones and identify key performance indicators that can be used to monitor the impact of the change process on the management of major hazards.

Resources should be sufficient to ensure that exposure to risks is not significantly increased during the change and the plans should account for an increase in workload during the transition.

Monitoring and review

Risk assessments and plans for both the transition and progress should be regularly reviewed to determine progress against set objectives and key performance indicators.

Health and safety performance, during and after the change, should be subject to both proactive and reactive monitoring.

Where there is evidence of significant risk, decisions may need to be changed or reversed.

As the effects of change can be subtle or delayed it is important to plan reviews to check for the effects of change six to twelve months afterwards.

The review should ensure that lessons learnt from the change process are identified and taken on board in reviewing and updating the organisation’s change management procedures.
