Sickness and incapacity

HM Governments Departments of Work and Pensions (DWP) and Health (DoH) (2017) estimated that ill-health that prevents people from working costs the UK economy around £100 billion a year. To address this and develop a strong and sustainable economic plan it is imperative that the skills, talents, and contribution of every person of working age are harnessed as effectively as possible.

Good employment has also been shown to keep people mentally and physically healthy and reduce health inequalities; provide economic independence; and give choices and opportunities to fulfil broader ambitions in life. It should be noted however that being employed in some jobs may still have a worse impact on health than having no job at all, e.g. poor quality, low paid and insecure employment, such as temporary casual work and unregulated work, may be as, or more, detrimental to health than unemployment.

Sickness absence rates vary by gender, age, occupation, sector, region and the size of the workplace. Individuals who are out of work for long periods of time due to sickness experience a drop in incomes which can result in poverty and social exclusion and, the longer someone is not working the less likely they are to return to work - someone who has been off sick for 6 months or longer has an 80% chance of being off work for 5 years.

Employment and Support Allowance (ESA) was introduced as the benefit for people with a health condition or disability in 2008 and provides income replacement for those who are unable to work because of illness or disability. It is based on a functional assessment of capability for work. In 2020 1 976 000 claimed Employment and Support Allowance (NB because of the transition to Universal Credit it is not possible to get a direct comparison with levels of Incapacity Benefit pre-2008).

NICE (the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) published Guideline PH19 “Workplace health: long-term sickness absence and incapacity to work” in 2009 and replaced it NICE Guideline 146 (Same title) in 2019.

The guideline covers how to help people return to work after long-term sickness absence, reduce recurring sickness absence, and help prevent people moving from short-term to long-term sickness absence.

Societal expectations of equality

There are over 6.9 million disabled people of working age in the UK which is just under 1/5th of the working age population.

Despite recent progress, disabled people still face significant disadvantages. Only 50% of disabled people of working age have a job, compared to 80% in the population as a whole. Disabled people are half as likely as non-disabled people to have a degree and almost a quarter (23%), have no qualifications at all (Compared to 9% of the non-disabled population).

Societal expectations of equal opportunity for disabled people to and at work is aligned with a shift from the traditional medical model of disability (which viewed disabled people as broken and in need of ‘fixing’) to a social model which focusses on removing, or reducing, societal barriers that can prevent disabled people from fulfilling their aspirations and realising their potential.

The barriers are often classified as:

  • environmental (e.g. Ramp access);
  • attitudinal (the main challenge – prejudice and the expectation that disabled people are unable to work); and
  • organisational, where institutional prejudice is a concern.

As discussed in element 1, the Equality Act 2010 protects groups with “protected characteristics” from discrimination. Disability is a protected characteristic and is defined as “a physical or mental impairment which has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities”.

Employers are required to make ‘reasonable adjustments’ to help disabled workers overcome any disadvantage arising from an impairment. Reasonable adjustments may be required in regard of:

  • provisions, criteria or practices, including company policies such as flexible working hours;
  • physical features, such as the layout of and access to workplaces; and
  • provision of auxiliary aids such as arrangements to make information available in an accessible format such as Braille, large print or email.
