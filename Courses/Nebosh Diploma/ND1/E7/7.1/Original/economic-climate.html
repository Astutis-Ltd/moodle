Economic climate


HSE sponsored research into the effects of economic downturn on health and safety performance has been conducted during the most recent, and previous, recessions.

The key findings of the HSE and other research are that accident injury rates fall during periods of reduced economic activity.

Reasons for this include:

‣
an increase in average levels of experience (fewer new recruits / fewer young males) leads to a decrease in average risks of injury;

‣
reduced ‘work effort’ as a consequence of falling levels of demand leads to a reduction in average working hours, lower levels of fatigue and a corresponding drop in average risks of injury; and

‣
reduced incentives for workers to report minor injuries.


There is also some conflicting evidence that ‘corner cutting’ by employers can lead to increasing rates of injury, as a consequence of reduced investment and training, and/or the weaker bargaining position of workers due to higher unemployment.

The overall effect though is one of a reduction in accident injury rates.

In the 1989-1993 UK recession the rate of reported major injuries fell by > 10%, and the numbers of injuries fell even further, because of the drop in employment levels. The effects were most pronounced in the construction and manufacturing sectors.

There is much less evidence available regarding the effect of economic downturn on occupational ill-health, largely because of the latency period between exposure and illness in many cases.

The most recent recession affected different groups of workers to different extents. Unemployment rates for males and young workers increased significantly more than average levels.

Regional effects were also noticeable with higher increases in levels of unemployment prevalent in traditional industrial areas such as Wales, the west midlands, and the north-west.

Available evidence on the impact of economic recovery suggests that upward pressure on injury rates is to be expected due to increases in new hires and working hours.

The more rapid the recovery, the more marked it is likely to be especially in sectors where hiring is concentrated among workers with little previous experience.
