Migrant workers


The UK has an ageing population because of longer lifespans and reduced fertility rates. To be sustainable there will be a need for people to work longer and for an increased proportion of migrant workers.

According to the Office of National Statistics (ONS) in 2019 there were 29.33 million UK nationals, 2.31 million EU nationals, and an estimated 1.34 million non-EU nationals working in the UK. (i.e., around 10% of working age people are non-UK nationals).

Over 80% of migrant workers are under 35 and many are highly qualified with around 70% having studied in the UK before entering the workforce, often in highly skilled and well-paid sectors including banking, IT, education and medicine. TUC (Trades Union Council) research has demonstrated that these workers receive no or very little additional risk to their health and safety at work.

Other TUC research has shown that there are significant numbers of migrant workers employed in sectors that are low-paid and where zero-hours contracts are prevalent, such as food production, health and social care, hospitality, clothing manufacture and the gig economy. Workers in these sectors are likely to face an increased risk to their health and safety at work, particularly where English language skills are poor, residency status is at risk, or there are other vulnerabilities.

IOSH research in 2008 showed that many employers considered language barriers to be a problem but that 13/26 of the businesses surveyed had done nothing to improve health and safety communication to those with poor English language skills.

HSE sponsored research considered whether migrant workers were at increased health and safety risk relative to indigenous workers in similar positions. The research findings identified the following concerns:

‣
migrants are more likely to be working in sectors or occupations where there are existing health and safety concerns

‣
their status as new workers may place them at added risk, due to their relatively short periods of work in the UK and limited knowledge of the UK’s health and safety system

‣
motivation for coming to the UK premised on earning as much as possible in the shortest possible time, added to their risk factors, and

‣
limited means of communication between migrant workers and indigenous supervisors also may place these workers at greater risk.


HSE’s current advice to those who employ migrant workers is to focus on the following four areas to ensure their health and safety:

‣
Training: Make sure induction training to familiarise workers with workplace risks and precautions is clear and simple

‣
Communication: Must be clear and effective. Where necessary information should be provided in other languages, visual formats or simple English. Workers must understand what is required of them and need to know how, and with whom, they can raise concerns

‣
Competence: Occupational qualifications or skills needed for the job should be checked before migrant workers start work. Skill levels gained from overseas qualifications (e.g. for forklift driving) should be reassessed

‣
Attitude to health and safety: Migrant workers may have different expectations about health and safety responsibilities, e.g. workers from some cultures may assume accidents are their own fault, or just inevitable. Efforts should be made to ensure that migrant workers understand the importance of health and safety in the workplace, and how it is managed.
