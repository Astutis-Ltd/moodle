Government policy and initiatives


In addition to the introduction of legislation the government can impact upon health and safety standards and priorities by focusing the resources of the HSE and other departments on particular issues; and through structured media campaigns and propaganda.

Over the years the government, through the HSC/HSE, government departments and other agencies such as the NHS has introduced numerous policies and initiatives focused on the health and/or safety of workers.

The current HSE strategy “Helping Great Britain Work Well” was launched in February 2016 and sets the the strategic direction until 2020. There are six strategic themes:

Acting together:

‣
promoting broader ownership of health and safety in Great Britain

‣
the regulators will retain their key roles, providing targeted advice and guidance for key audience groups and taking enforcement action when and where necessary, however encouraging others to take ownership of health and safety is a critical component of the scheme.

Tackling ill health:

‣
highlighting and tackling the costs of work-related ill health

‣
a key element of the theme is earlier prevention, involving a greater focus on health issues at work, while continuing to ensure that maintaining standards around safety remains a priority

‣
this will require long-term and coordinated action across all sectors, bringing in additional partners such as the NHS and others.

Managing risk well:

‣
simplifying risk management and helping business to grow

‣
sensible and proportionate risk management is integral to delivering their business. This approach supports growth, enables innovation and protects an organisation’s most vital asset, its people.

Supporting small employers:

‣
giving SMEs simple advice so they know what they have to do

‣
managing workplace risks shouldn’t be complicated or costly for SME’s

‣
larger business and intermediaries have opportunities to work smarter, developing efficient supply chains and productive relationships by working with and supporting small businesses

‣
making support available which is quick and easy for small businesses to understand, so that they can fulfil their obligations.

Keeping pace with change:

‣
anticipating and tackling new health and safety challenges

‣
Great Britain leads the world in anticipating and tackling the new health and safety challenges that come with social, economic and technological change

‣
this ability to horizon scan combined with unrivalled expertise and research facilities means that Great Britain can play a leading role in anticipating new health and safety challenges.

Sharing our success:

‣
promoting the benefits of Great Britain’s world-class health and safety system

‣
by sharing best practice and the latest thinking and innovation around the world health and safety systems overseas can be influenced making it easier for British businesses to expand into new markets and territories

‣
there may also be commercial opportunities for selling British health and safety ‘products’ or consultancy services.


“Working for a healthier tomorrow” was Dame Carol Black’s review of the health of Britain’s working age population, the findings of which were presented to the Secretary of State for Health and the Secretary of State for Work and Pensions in March 2008.

The review identified the need for a fundamental change in the perceptions that:

‣
it is inappropriate to be at work unless 100% fit; and

‣
that being at work normally impedes recovery.


The review cited recent evidence showing that work can be good for health and can also help to reverse the harmful effects of long-term unemployment and prolonged sickness absence. (These issues are discussed in more detail later).

At the time around 7% of workers were on incapacity benefits and an additional 3% were off work sick at any one time. Each year, 600 000 people move onto incapacity benefits. The cost to the taxpayer in benefit costs, additional health costs and forgone taxes was estimated to be over £60 billion, with an overall cost in excess of £100 billion. (Greater than the annual budget for the NHS / equivalent to the entire GDP of Portugal).

The three principal objectives arising from the reports vision for health and work in Britain are:

‣
prevention of illness and promotion of health and well-being;

‣
early intervention for those who develop a health condition; and

‣
an improvement in the health of those out of work – so that everyone with the potential to work has the support they need to do so.


In addition an early intervention “Fit for Work” service is to be provided; based on a case-managed, multidisciplinary approach, to provide treatment, advice and guidance for people in the early stages of sickness absence. (See also the notes on ‘Sickness absence and incapacity’ later)
