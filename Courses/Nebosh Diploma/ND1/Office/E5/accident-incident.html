Accident/incident ratio studies

In 1931 Herbert William (Bill) Heinrich first presented an accident triangle to illustrate his theory of accident causation, showing that unsafe acts lead to accidents which largely result in minor injuries but will eventually result in major injuries or fatalities.

Heinrich’s original triangle (below) was developed while working for Travellers Insurance and followed a study of over 5 000 cases. Heinrich stated that if the same type of accident occurred 330 times, on 300 occasions there would be no injury, on 29 occasions there would be minor injuries, and on one occasion there would be a major injury.

In 1969 Frank Bird Jr., who was the Director of Engineering Services for the Insurance Company of America, undertook research into Heinrich’s findings. Bird was interested in determining what the actual reporting relationship of accidents was across a general population of workers.

Bird analysed 1 753 498 accidents reported by 297 cooperating companies, representing 21 different industrial groups, and employing 1 750 000 employees.

These and later triangles suggest that the outcome of an unsafe event in the workplace will typically be a ‘near miss’ but that a proportion will result in minor injuries and a smaller proportion still will result in major injuries or fatalities.

The theory is useful in promoting the reporting of incidents (near misses and undesired circumstances) as, if the outcome of an event is largely dependent on luck, the lessons should be learnt from the event and not the outcome.

If incidents are reported and investigated, and lessons are learnt and improvements made, the likelihood of a serious injury occurring should be greatly reduced.

However this theory is no longer universally accepted. There has been much conflicting research that suggests that accidents resulting in fatality or serious injury have different causal factors to those accidents that occur frequently and result in minor injury.

The data set from Heinrichs study has been challenged regarding the likelihood of 10/11 reported incidents in the 1920’s being near misses. The ratio of outcomes for different events is also questionable. The ratio may make sense for falls on the same level but is unlikely to be representative of the outcomes from falls from height, for example.

Bird’s universal data set showing a ratio of outcomes for accidents of all types is even more problematic.

It is difficult to extrapolate data from 1960’s manufacturing and industrial workplaces to the present day.

It assumes that all near misses are of equal value and equal potential gravity, which is not the case.

There are significant differences between minor and major accidents, notably regarding the activities undertaken, the potential levels of energy released and the characteristics and numbers of the safety barriers in place.

As Andrew Hale (2002) noted, it is inappropriate to interpret accident causation triangles as implying that chipping away at the base of the triangle to stop minor incidents such as slips, trips and falls will have any impact on preventing process safety or major accidents, and as Marsden (2021) stated:

People do not die because coffee drinkers are walking around with cups without lids. Similarly, minor accidents do not, in general, escalate into or (in high-hazard industries) predict major accidents.
