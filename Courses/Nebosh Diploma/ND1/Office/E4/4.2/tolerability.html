Tolerability of risk

The HSE first published the discussion document Reducing risks, protecting people (R2P2) in May 1999 to set out how the statutory bodies responsible for the administration of the Health and Safety at Work Act should approach decisions about the management of risk that are required of them under the Act.

Reducing risks, protecting people therefore influences the frameworks for proposing new legislation, Approved Codes of Practice (ACoPs) and guidance, and conducting research and providing information and advice.

The framework for decision making was based on the tolerability of risks (TOR) method which the HSE originally published in its 1988 guidance The tolerability of risks from nuclear power stations.

Advances in knowledge on how people view risks

The factors that affect people’s perception of risk are now generally held to depend upon individual perception of how the risk relates to the individual and their personal values.

The following factors have been identified as important regarding the perception of risks arising from man- made hazards:

  • How well the process (giving rise to the hazard) is understood
  • How equitably the danger is distributed
  • How well individuals can control their exposure
  • Whether risk is assumed voluntarily.

Other studies have suggested that making decisions purely on a quantified risk basis is too simplistic and that in reality views of risk are shaped by human minds and cultures and are influenced not only by the prospect of physical harm but also other factors such as ethical and social considerations, and the degree of trust in those creating the risk.

This might go some way towards explaining why high quality risk assessments, using all available data and best science and technology, conducted by leaders in the field often fail to reassure people.

Another model (the social amplification of risk model) has been used to explain why risks that are minor in quantitative terms, at times, produce massive reactions while major risks are often ignored. This model suggests that the impact of a particular risk begins with the initial victims and diffuses outward to society at large. In that process, public response to the risk can be amplified as a result of scientific challenges and over-dramatisation of the issue by the media using provocative terminology and images.

Collectively the research establishes that hazards give rise to concerns which can be categorised as individual or societal concerns.

Individual concerns address how individuals see the risk from a particular hazard affecting them and things they value personally. Individuals may be prepared to engage voluntarily in activities that involve high risks but as a rule are far less tolerant of risks imposed on them and over which they have little control, unless they consider the risks as negligible.

Individuals may also be willing to live with a risk if it secures them or society certain benefits, providing that the risks were kept low and clearly controlled.

Societal concerns relate to the risks which impact on society and which, if realised, could have adverse repercussions for the institutions responsible for protecting people, e.g. the Government of the day. Typical examples relate to nuclear power generation, railway travel, or the genetic modification of organisms. People are more averse to such risks and are therefore more likely to insist on stringent Government regulation.

Hazards giving rise to societal concerns share a number of common features:

  • could cause multiple fatalities
  • difficult to intuitively estimate the actual threat
  • exposure involves vulnerable groups, e.g. children
  • risks and benefits tend to be unevenly distributed – i.e. some people bear more of the risks than others
  • less risk may be borne now and more by future generations.

Conversely hazards with the following features do not, as a rule, give rise to societal concerns:

  • familiar
  • taken voluntarily for a benefit
  • individual in their impact.

Changes in the preferences, values and expectations of society

The preferences, values and expectations of society have never been static. Current shifts are linked in part to:

  • the rapid rise in information technology which makes it easier for people to access information on the risks that may affect them and society at large (without critical peer review this has led to public confusion and the amplification of some risks, and the attenuation of others)
  • the increased pace in exploiting advances in scientific and technological knowledge, which has led to an increased focus on technological risks
  • greater affluence in society has led to a lower acceptance of poor / dangerous working conditions which might previously have been accepted as a necessary consequence of industrial work.

These shifts in preferences and values result in:

  • a growing perception that industry and government should justify risks imposed on people
  • an increasing reliance by the public on regulators that they trust
  • calls for greater openness and involvement in the decision-making processes.

System for informing and reaching decisions

The approach the HSE adopts for reaching decisions on the degree and form of regulatory control of risk from occupational hazards reflects the principles of better regulation, i.e. targeting, consistency, proportionality, transparency and accountability, and follows the following, fluid six stage process:

Stage 1 Deciding whether the issue is primarily one for the HSE.
Stage 2 Defining and characterising the issue.
Stage 3 Examining the options available for addressing the issue, and their merits.
Stage 4 Adopting a particular course of action for addressing the issue efficiently and in good time, informed by the findings of the second and third points above and in the expectation that as far as possible it will be supported by stakeholders.
Stage 5 Implementing the decisions.
Stage 6 Evaluating the effectiveness of actions taken and revisiting the decisions and their implementation if necessary.

Criteria for reaching decisions

Though all six stages are important, getting Stage 4 right (adopting an appropriate course of action) is crucial. Getting it right depends to a large extent on the criteria adopted for deciding whether a risk is unacceptable, tolerable or broadly acceptable.

Research analysing the criteria used by regulators in the health, safety and environmental field has shown that, in general, the criteria can be classified according to three ‘pure’ criteria (equity / utility / technology).

Equity-based criteria start with the premise that all individuals have unconditional rights to a certain level of protection. This leads to standards, applicable to all, held to be usually acceptable in normal life. If the risk estimate derived from the risk assessment is above the limit and further control measures cannot be introduced to reduce the risk, the risk is held to be unacceptable whatever the benefits

Utility-based criteria apply to the comparison between the incremental benefits of the measures to prevent the risk of injury or detriment, and the cost of the measures (i.e. a cost benefit analysis comparing in monetary terms the relevant benefits (e.g. statistical lives saved, life-years extended) against the net cost of introducing it.

Technology-based criteria reflect the idea that a satisfactory level of risk prevention is attained when ‘state of the art’ control measures (technological, managerial, and organisational) are employed to control risks whatever the circumstances.

Each approach, in isolation, has its limitations, for example:

  • equity-based criteria may make decisions on worst case scenarios which systematically overestimate the risks and bear little resemblance to reality
  • utility-based criteria tend to ignore the ethical and other considerations beyond balancing costs and benefits
  • technology-based criteria often ignore the balance between costs and benefits.

The HSE’s Tolerability Of risk Framework (TOR) accommodates all three criteria giving it obvious strengths, such as:

  • capitalises on the advantages of each of the ‘pure criteria’ whilst avoiding the disadvantages
  • is very similar to the tests people apply in everyday life.

The above figure illustrates the TOR framework. The triangle represents increasing level of ‘risk’ for a particular hazardous activity (measured by individual risk and societal concerns) moving from the broadly acceptable region at the bottom of the triangle towards the unacceptable region at the top.

The lighter zone at the bottom represents a broadly acceptable region. Risks falling into this region are generally regarded as insignificant and adequately controlled. Regulators would not usually require further action to reduce risks unless reasonably practicable measures were available. The levels of risk in this region are comparable to those that people regard as insignificant or trivial in their daily lives, i.e. the risks from activities that are inherently not very hazardous or from hazardous activities that can be readily controlled to produce very low residual risks.

The darker zone at the top represents an unacceptable region. For practical purposes, a particular risk falling into that region is regarded as unacceptable whatever the level of benefits associated with the activity. Any activity or practice giving rise to risks falling in that region would, as a matter of principle, be ruled out unless the activity or practice can be modified to reduce the degree of risk or there are exceptional reasons for the activity or practice to be retained.

The zone between the unacceptable and broadly acceptable regions is the tolerable region. Risks in this region are typically those that people are prepared to tolerate in order to secure benefits, providing that:

  • the nature and level of the risks are properly assessed and the results used properly to determine control measures
  • the risk assessment is based on the best available scientific evidence ( or where evidence is lacking – the best available scientific advice)
  • the residual risks are not unduly high and kept as low as reasonably practicable (the ALARP principle – see later)
  • the risks are periodically reviewed to ensure that they still meet the ALARP criteria (e.g. by ascertaining whether further or new control measures need to be introduced to take into account changes over time, such as new knowledge about the risk or the availability of new techniques for reducing or eliminating risks).

Benefits for which people generally tolerate risks typically include employment, lower cost of production, personal convenience or the maintenance of general social infrastructure such as the production of electricity or the maintenance of food or water supplies.

As the model is conceptual it must be stressed that the factors and processes that ultimately determine the tolerability of a risk is unacceptable, tolerable or broadly acceptable are contextual and dynamic in nature, i.e. they will be influenced by the particular circumstances, time and environment in which the risk creating activity occurs.

Tolerability limits

The TOR framework can, in principle be applied to all hazards. When determining reasonably practicable measures for any particular hazard, whether the chosen options will effectively control the risk depends in part on where the boundaries are set between the unacceptable, tolerable or broadly acceptable regions.

Boundary between the ‘broadly acceptable’ and ‘tolerable’ regions for risk entailing fatalities

The HSE believes that an individual risk of death of one in a million per annum for both workers and the public corresponds to a very low level of risk and should be used as a guideline for the boundary between the broadly acceptable and tolerable regions. When compared against background levels of risk (typically a risk of death of one in a hundred per year averaged over a lifetime) a residual risk of one in a million per year is extremely small.

Boundary between the ‘tolerable’ and ‘unacceptable’ regions for risk entailing fatalities

The HSE does not have a widely acceptable criterion for this boundary, however in the guidance on ‘tolerability of risks in nuclear power stations’, the HSE suggested that an individual risk of death of one in a thousand per annum (p.a.) should on its own represent the dividing line between what could be just tolerable for any substantial category of workers for any large part of a working life, and what is unacceptable for any but fairly exceptional groups. For members of the public who have a risk imposed on them ‘in the wider interest of society’ this limit is judged to be an order of magnitude lower – at 1 in 10 000 p.a.

ALARP and CBA

In most situations, deciding whether the risks are ALARP (as low as reasonably practicable) involves a comparison of proposed control measures and relevant good practice, which is defined as: those standards for controlling risk that HSE has judged and recognised as satisfying the law, when applied to a particular relevant case, in an appropriate manner.

Where there is relevant, recognised good practice duty-holders are expected to follow it. Alternative measures can be taken if they are at least as effective in controlling the risk.

In some cases, such as with a new technology, where there is no relevant good practice, good practice should be followed as far as it can be, and then consideration given to whether there is any more that can be done to reduce the risk by going back to first principles to compare the risk with the sacrifice involved in further reducing it.

‘First principles’ comparisons can be done qualitatively, by applying common sense and / or exercising professional judgment or experience. For example if the costs are clearly very high and the reduction in risk is only marginal, then it is likely that the situation is already ALARP and further improvements are not required.

In some instances (often in high hazard industries or where there is a new technology with potentially serious consequences) a more detailed comparison has to be undertaken. This is where a more formal Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA) may provide additional insight to help come to a judgment.

In a CBA risk and sacrifice are converted to a common set of units (money) to enable direct comparison. Sacrifice is represented as a cost. Risk, in so far as it is being reduced, is considered a benefit.

Unlike in a standard commercial CBA where the rule is that the measure should be adopted only if benefits outweigh costs in ALARP judgments the measure must be adopted unless the sacrifice is grossly disproportionate to the risk.

There are many assumptions and uncertainties involved in CBA and the outcome of a CBA is only one of several considerations that go towards the judgment that a risk has been reduced ALARP. For example: in policy work and high hazards serious consideration should also be given to ‘societal concerns’.
