Risk treatment


Risk treatment is the process of selecting and implementing measures to modify the risk. It involves a cyclical process of:

‣
assessing a risk treatment

‣
deciding whether residual risk levels are tolerable

‣
if not tolerable, generating a new risk treatment

‣
assessing the effectiveness of that treatment.


Risk treatments that deal with negative consequences are sometimes referred to as ‘risk mitigation’, ‘risk elimination’, ‘risk prevention’ and ‘risk reduction’.

Risk treatment options are not necessarily mutually exclusive or appropriate in all circumstances. The options can include the following:

‣
avoiding the risk by deciding not to start or continue with the activity that gives rise to the risk

‣
taking or increasing the risk in order to pursue an opportunity

‣
removing the risk source

‣
changing the likelihood

‣
changing the consequences

‣
sharing the risk with another party or parties (including contracts and risk financing)

‣
retaining the risk by informed decision.


Selecting the most appropriate risk treatment option involves balancing the costs and efforts of implementation against the benefits derived, with regard to legal, regulatory, and other requirements, such as social responsibility and the protection of the natural environment. Decisions should also take into account risks which can warrant risk treatment that is not justifiable on economic grounds, for example: severe (high negative consequence) but rare (low likelihood) risks.

Risk treatment itself can introduce risks. A significant risk can be the failure or ineffectiveness of the risk treatment measures. Monitoring needs to be an integral part of the risk treatment plan to give assurance that the measures remain effective.

Risk treatment can also introduce secondary risks that need to be assessed, treated, monitored and reviewed.

These secondary risks should be incorporated into the same treatment plan as the original risk and not treated as a new risk. The link between the two risks should be identified and maintained.

A number of treatment options can be considered and applied either individually or in combination. The organisation can normally benefit from the adoption of a combination of treatment options.

Risk avoidance

Risk avoidance involves making a strategic decision not to become involved in, or action to withdraw from, a risk situation. Withdrawing from an activity can be an important option in project management if it becomes clear that the costs of achieving the project objectives are too high or that the objectives might not be realised, regardless of cost. It should be noted that avoiding risks also means that potential opportunities for gain are also lost.


Risk reduction

Risk reduction or optimisation (collectively risk modification) treatments are designed to optimise potential opportunities and minimise threats. Risk modification can involve changing:

‣
the likelihood of risk – increasing for a positive consequence / decreasing for a negative consequence

‣
the consequences should a risk occur – shifting towards the positive or less harmful

‣
both likelihood and consequences.


Risk transfer

In the ‘classic’ framework risks are considered to be transferred through insurance arrangements, contractual arrangements or partnerships and joint ventures. The notion of transferring a risk is slightly flawed in that if the insurance company or contractor go bankrupt or end up in court, the original risk is likely to still revert to the first party. In this context insurance is better described as a post-event compensatory mechanism.

The term ‘risk sharing’ is better used for partnerships and joint ventures where exposures and liabilities, and the potentials for gain are shared.

Risk retention pools would also fit into this category of risk sharing.


Risk retention

Risk retention means making a conscious decision based on evidence from the risk analysis and evaluation to take no further action in response to a particular risk, for the time being. This may be because a residual risk is acceptable and no further worthwhile actions are available. The risk would need to be monitored forward and reviewed as necessary should circumstances change.

All risks that are not avoided or transferred are retained by default. This is a viable strategy for small risks, where the cost of insuring against the risk would be greater over time than the total losses sustained.


Other treatments

Other researchers have identified the following additional treatment categories:

‣
Risk exploitation: actively seeking out risk in order to gain competitive advantage, since risk per se does not always carry a negative connotation, but can also lead to beneficial results.

‣
Risk ignorance: a ‘see-no-evil-hear-no-evil’ strategy, where many businesses willingly overlook the risks they are exposed to.
