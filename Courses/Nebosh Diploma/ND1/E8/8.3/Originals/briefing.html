Briefing management


If a criminal prosecution is seen as a possibility, the health and safety professional may be called upon to provide Management with information that will enable them to decide how to proceed. The safety professional may need to provide the following:

‣
details of the facts of the case

‣
details of relevant legal requirements

‣
an explanation of the ‘material breach’

‣
an overview of the options available to the enforcing authorities

‣
details of any proposed remedial action.


Facts of the case - the safety practitioner must ensure that they do not speculate or provide inaccurate or incomplete information. The facts must be presented in an objective manner, with everything of relevance being disclosed, including facts that may appear to be critical of the way in which health and safety is managed in the organisation. The guiding ethical principles here are honesty and integrity, so the safety professional should relate the facts fearlessly. A failure to relate all relevant facts could compromise the management team’s ability to make decisions and may mean that the legal defence team receives less information than they should, which could harm a potential defence.

Relevant legal requirements - the safety practitioner will need to identify what law is relevant to the situation and explain how the requirements should be applied in the context of the organisation’s undertakings. It is unlikely to be enough to simply list relevant law - management will need to understand how the requirements should have been met and what was actually done so that they can see whether they are likely to be found in breach or whether they may have a defence.

The material breach - this occurs where the HSE inspector believes there has been a contravention of health and safety law that is serious enough to require them to notify the person in material breach of that opinion in writing. Management should be briefed on what this is and the potential consequences of the breach in terms of the range of potential enforcement action that might be taken.

You should be familiar with the enforcement options available to enforcing authorities from your General Certificate studies.

‣
Fees for intervention

‣
Simple cautions

‣
Improvement or prohibition notices (Revisited in Element 1.3)

‣
Prosecution, summarily or on indictment. (Revisited in Element 1.3)


Remedial action - having set out the facts of the case and the relevant law, the safety practitioner may then be asked to brief management on appropriate remedial action that addresses the cause of the breach. Taking prompt, relevant and effective remedial action will of course help to remedy the circumstances that gave rise to the material breach. Doing so will also stand as a mitigating factor in the event the the organisation is convicted.
