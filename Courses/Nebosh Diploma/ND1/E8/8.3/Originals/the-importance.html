The importance of collating documentary
evidence for the defence case


Before deciding to prosecute, a prosecuting authority such as the HSE will need to be satisfied that doing so would be worthwhile and proportionate. Prosecutions are expensive to mount, so the HSE would not want to waste public money. In addition, the HSE would not want to gain a reputation for failing to secure convictions - such a reputation might undermine its effectiveness as an enforcement body. It is also necessary to check that a prosecution would be a proportionate response to the alleged offence, or whether a lesser degree of enforcement action such as the issue of a notice might be more appropriate.

In order to be satisfied that the prosecution is worthwhile, a two-stage test, known as the ‘Full code test’, can be applied.

In the first stage (the ‘Evidential stage’), the prosecuting authority (e.g. HSE, CPS) will need to satisfy itself that there is sufficient evidence to provide a realistic prospect of conviction. As stated in the Crown Prosecution Service’s ‘Code for Crown Prosecutors’:

"The finding that there is a realistic prospect of conviction is based on the prosecutor’s objective assessment of the evidence, including the impact of any defence and any other information that the suspect has put forward or on which they might rely. It means that an objective, impartial and reasonable jury or bench of magistrates or judge hearing a case alone, properly directed and acting in accordance with the law, is more likely than not to convict the defendant of the charge alleged. This is a different test from the one that the criminal courts themselves must apply. A court may only convict if it is sure that the defendant is guilty."


Deciding whether to proceed requires the prosecutor to consider the likely defence case and supporting evidence; it is at this stage that the prosecutor might conclude that the defence is too strong or that there is insufficient prosecution evidence to secure a conviction. If the case does not pass this ‘Evidential test’ then it will not proceed to trial.

If the ‘Evidential test’ is passed, the prosecutor will move to stage two and consider whether prosecuting would be in the public interest.

The second stage is known as the ‘Public interest stage’. For health and safety prosecutions, relevant ‘Public interest’ facts might include:

‣
the seriousness of the offence - more serious offences are more likely to be prosecuted

‣
the level of culpability of the accused - the greater the degree of culpability, or ‘blameworthiness’, the more likely a prosecution becomes

‣
the circumstances of any person exposed to risk - were they vulnerable etc

‣
the degree of harm caused

‣
impacts on the community

‣
whether prosecution is a proportionate response, or would another form of enforcement action be more appropriate.


None of the above points are conclusive, nor is this an exhaustive list of factors for consideration. The prosecutor needs to exercise judgement when coming to a final decision about whether or not to proceed with a prosecution.

It follows from the above that good quality, persuasive defence evidence may have a significant influence on whether the accused is prosecuted at all. There is, therefore, an obvious benefit to being able to quickly and efficiently locate such evidence and disclose it to the prosecutor before such a decision is made.

If the matter does come to court, having good defence evidence can cast sufficient ‘reasonable doubt’ on the prosecution case, such that the chance of being acquitted improves. If, however, the defendant has little or no supporting evidence, then a conviction becomes a distinct possibility given that the defendant will find it much harder to demonstrate that they did what was practicable or reasonable practicable as the case may be.
