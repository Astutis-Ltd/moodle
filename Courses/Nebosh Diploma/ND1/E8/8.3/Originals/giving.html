Giving advice as a safety professional


The safety professional will have been appointed as the nominated competent source of health and safety assistance, in compliance with Regulation 7 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999.

One aspect of competence is to know and understand the limits of one’s expertise. Safety practitioners should not attempt to advise on matters that are beyond their expertise; if they do, and the advice turns out to be wrong, the employer who acts on the advice may be in breach of the law. Not only could this result in disciplinary action against the safety practitioner but it could even result in the safety practitioner being investigated for a breach of s36 HSWA in that they caused the employer to be in breach. See, for example, R v Atterby (2009).

Richard Atterby was fined £1 000 and costs of £700, having pleaded guilty to an offence under Section 36(1) of the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 relating to his failure to make a suitable assessment to the risk to health of employees from a substance hazardous to their health.

The court heard that the failure of George Farrar (Quarries) Ltd to make a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risk to the health of their employees from exposure to respirable crystalline silica, arising from the processing of sandstone, was due to the poor quality of Mr Atterby’s advice.

The HSE Principal Inspector stated:

“The health risks to stonemasons arising from exposure to respirable crystalline silica can be devastating. The risk assessment done by Mr Atterby was superficial and totally inadequate and it led to a high risk of exposure to respirable silica during stonemasonry work Consultants should not attempt to give advice on matters unless they have adequate knowledge, training, skills and experience to make the right decisions about risks and precautions that are needed.”


It follows that where the Management team need to be advised on a matter that is outside of the safety practitioner’s area of expertise, a suitably qualified, competent person should be engaged to provide that advice instead.
