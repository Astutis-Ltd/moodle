Implications of the coroner’s conclusions


After any witnesses have given their evidence, the coroner will summarise the case. Legal representatives may address the coroner at this point, although they won’t make speeches like they would in a civil or criminal trial. In an inquest into a fatal accident at work, the coroner will then address the jury on how they should apply the law when making their decision, and may tell the jury which of the possible conclusions that are normally available in a coroner’s inquest are open to them.

In the case of a fatality at work, the following conclusions could be drawn:

‣
accidental death or misadventure

‣
alcohol/drug related

‣
industrial disease

‣
natural causes

‣
road traffic collision

‣
suicide

‣
an open verdict – not enough evidence to draw one of the other conclusions.


Although it is not the job of the coroner to apportion blame or to identify who may be guilty of an offence, if evidence suggests that someone may be criminally responsible for the death, then the coroner can pass that evidence to the police or Crown Prosecution Service.

A coroner’s decision or inquest conclusion can be challenged. Simple matters, such as a decision not to call a particular witness, might be challenged during the inquest. More significant challenges may require an application for judicial review to the High Court. This must be done within 3 months of the decision or of the inquest ending.
