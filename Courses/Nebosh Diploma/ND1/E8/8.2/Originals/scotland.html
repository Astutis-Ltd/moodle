Scotland


There are no Coroner’s Courts in Scotland. Accidental, unexpected, unexplained, sudden or suspicious deaths are investigated by the Procurator Fiscal.

Procurators Fiscal are qualified lawyers who are employed by the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) and who act on the instructions of the Lord Advocate. The Lord Advocate has responsibility in Scotland to investigate any death which requires further explanation. In other parts of the United Kingdom, this role would be undertaken by the coroner.

In the event of a death due to an industrial accident in Scotland, a Fatal Accident Inquiry (FAI) will be held. This will be presided over by a Sheriff and will take place in local Sheriff courts.

An FAI is a public court hearing that inquires into the circumstances of a death. The COPFS aims to hold FAIs as soon as practicable after a death.

During the FAI, the circumstances surrounding the death will be assessed in order to identify any issues of public concern or safety and to prevent future deaths or injuries. The Procurator Fiscal will call witnesses and lead the evidence. Just as in the Coroner’s Courts, other interested parties may also be represented and can question witnesses.

At the end of an FAI, a Sheriff will make a determination. The determination will set out:

‣
where and when the death occurred

‣
the cause of death

‣
any precautions by which the death might have been avoided

‣
any defect in systems that caused or contributed to the death

‣
any other facts which are relevant to the circumstances of the death.


As with the coroner’s inquest, an FAI cannot make any findings of fault or blame against individuals.
