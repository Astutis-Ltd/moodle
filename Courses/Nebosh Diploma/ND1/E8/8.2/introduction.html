Introduction

A coroner is a special judge (usually a specially trained lawyer or doctor) who is charged with investigating unnatural deaths, such as those which occur at work. The coroner’s investigation may include an inquest hearing.

The coroner’s role is to establish the identity of the deceased and to find out how, when and where they died. Coroners do not make findings of guilt nor do they apportion blame, although they may be critical of what happened.

In some cases the coroner may write a report aimed at helping to prevent further deaths in the future. This report is known as a ‘Report to prevent future deaths’ and will be sent to the organisations involved.

An inquest is a fact-finding inquiry held in a court. It is up to the coroner to decide on the scope of the investigation and what evidence he or she wishes to hear. The coroner may also wish to visit the scene of the incident.

In the case of a death that resulted from a “notifiable accident, poisoning or disease”, such as a fatal accident at work, the inquest will be held with a jury (s7, Coroners and Justice Act 2009). It is the jury’s job to decide the facts of the case and to say how the person died and the cause of death.

Present at the inquest may be certain ‘Interested persons’, a term which is defined in law (s47(2) Coroners and Justice Act 2009). Examples of interested persons include:

  • close family members and personal representatives of the deceased
  • a person who may by any act or omission have caused or contributed to the death of the deceased, or whose employee or agent may have done so
  • in a case where the death may have been caused by an injury received in the course of employment, or a disease prescribed under section 108 of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 (benefit in respect of prescribed industrial diseases, etc), a representative of a trade union of which the deceased was a member at the time of death
  • a person appointed by, or representative of, an enforcing authority.

Legal advice is not strictly required during an inquest but it may be prudent where enforcing authority representatives are in attendance and have their own legal representation.

Interested persons have certain rights, including:

  • the right to be told about the dates of inquest hearings
  • to receive documents from the coroner that may be used in the inquest (including witness statements and expert reports)
  • to receive evidence that other interested parties have disclosed to the coroner
  • to ask other witnesses relevant questions at the inquest.

Interested persons may be asked to give witness statements and to give evidence at the inquest. Evidence is given under oath. In the case of a fatal accident at work, any person from the workplace who can help the coroner to understand how the person died may be asked to give evidence, including:

  • senior managers
  • line managers or supervisors
  • colleagues who witnessed the incident
  • the health and safety professional.
