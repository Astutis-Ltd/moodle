Potential conflicts of interest


The role of the health and safety professional in protecting workers, employers and third parties, and the potential conflicts of interest that this brings was discussed in Element 6.1.

A ‘Conflict of interest’ occurs when the aims of two parties are incompatible with each other.

The IOSH Code of Conduct (at paragraph 1.4) addresses potential conflicts of interest. It requires safety professionals to “Avoid conflicts of interest wherever possible and if one arises promptly take appropriate steps to manage it”.

An example of a conflict of interest might be where the safety professional is asked to advise their employer on issues concerning a civil claim, and the claimant also asks for information or advice. The safety practitioner should avoid being in a position where they are giving, or appear to be giving, advice to both parties. In such a situation, the interests of the two parties are in conflict so it would be improper to assist both.

In any legal action, the safety professional’s overriding duty will be to assist the court by giving clear and truthful evidence.

The safety practitioner may have an interest in the outcome of the case. For example, they may wish to see their employer successfully defend the case, or perhaps they may have some personal sympathy with a claimant’s position and wish them to prevail. Such interests should not be allowed to interfere with giving honest and impartial evidence.

Some safety practitioners may find themselves giving ‘Expert witness’ evidence. According to the IOSH Code of Conduct (at paragraph 1.4):

"Where you are acting as an expert witness in judicial proceedings the overriding objective will be to assist the decision-maker to deal with the case justly. If you take on the role of giving expert witness evidence, you should disclose any previous or continuing relationship that you have with any of the parties in the case at the time you are instructed and in any report prepared for the case."


Giving evidence in legal proceedings where the party asking for your opinion is a friend or relative may give the impression of impropriety, even though the safety professional has disclosed their relationship and has satisfied themselves that they are giving their genuine opinion that coincidentally favours the relative or friend. Even if this is so, there is a danger that the safety professional’s objectivity may have been clouded by a sense of duty to their friend or relative. This may raise questions over the safety professional’s integrity and so such situations are best avoided.
