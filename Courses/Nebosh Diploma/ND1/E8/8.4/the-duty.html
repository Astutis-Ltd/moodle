The duty not to interfere with or alter evidence

The guiding principles of ethical conduct for safety professionals include honesty and integrity. There can be little doubt that interfering with, or altering, evidence in order to manipulate the outcome of legal proceedings is likely to be seen as dishonest and will raise profound questions over the integrity of the person involved.

It should be clear that no safety professional should ever interfere with, or alter, any evidence, especially where doing so is calculated to influence the outcome of legal proceedings. Instead, they should submit the evidence without alteration for the parties to litigation, and the court, to use and consider as they see fit.

The personal and professional consequences of interfering with, or altering, evidence can be severe.

Interfering with evidence is a common law criminal offence known as ‘Perverting the course of justice’, the ingredients of which were set out by Pollock, B in R v Vreones, where it was said that:

“The real offence here is the doing of some act which has a tendency and is intended to pervert the administration of public justice.”

Interfering with, or altering, evidence would be an example.

Persons convicted of perverting the course of justice can receive lengthy prison sentences.

Further, the Pre-Action Protocol in the Civil Procedure Rules, paragraph 7.1.4 states:

“[…] the defendant is under a duty to preserve the disclosure documents and other evidence (CCTV for example). If the documents are destroyed, this could be an abuse of the court process.”

An abuse of process can lead to the defendant’s statement of case (their defence) being struck out (CPR Rule 3.4(2)(b) ), meaning that the claimant will likely succeed, with the defendant being liable for costs.

Professional consequences can include a requirement to face disciplinary proceedings from the practitioner’s professional body (e.g. IOSH). The outcome can range from being given advice on conduct all the way up to expulsion from the institution.
