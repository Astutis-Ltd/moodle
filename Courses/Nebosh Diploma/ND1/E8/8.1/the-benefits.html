The benefits of maintaining an effective information management system

An effective defence to a claim for damages depends on being able to demonstrate, on the balance of probabilities, that the measures taken by the defendant to control foreseeable risks were reasonable in the circumstances. The defence will be helped by having documentary evidence to show the court; this evidence should be held within an information management system.

An information management system is used to collect and manage information and data, and make it accessible to anyone who needs it. As organisations grow and as time moves on, so the amount of information to be stored will increase. Without an effective information management system, information may become increasingly hard to find.

When defending civil claims (as well as criminal prosecutions) it is imperative that the organisation has an efficient system for collecting and storing all kinds of health and safety and employment-related information so that, when called upon, it can be accessed quickly and efficiently. A failure to find and make available the required information may have serious consequences including:

  • not being able to comply with relevant pre-action protocols
  • a higher legal costs bill in cases where litigation could have been avoided had evidence been available
  • the risk of losing the trial for want of supporting evidence.

The list of documents of relevance to health and safety at work that may need to be disclosed to the claimant’s lawyers is very long. Examples might include, but will not be limited to:

  • Accident book entries
  • First aider's report
  • Surgery record
  • Foreman/supervisor accident report
  • Safety representative's accident report
  • RIDDOR reports
  • Other communications between the defendants and HSE
  • Minutes of health and safety committee meeting(s) where the matter was discussed
  • Report to the Department of Work and Pensions (‘DWP’, formerly the ‘DSS’)
  • Documents listed above relative to any previous matter identified by the claimant and relied upon as proof of negligence
  • Earnings information where the defendant is the employer
  • Documents produced to comply with the the requirements of health and safety legislation e.g. risk assessments, test and maintenance records, health surveillance training records etc.

There may also be a need for information held by Human Resources such as back to work records, as well as purchasing records and receipts held by the accounts or purchasing department.

It is therefore important to have a way of quickly and easily accessing all of this information in the event that an injured person decides to bring a claim. The quicker this information can be found, the quicker the company can decide how to respond.
