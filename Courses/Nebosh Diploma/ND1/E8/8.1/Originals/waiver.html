Waiver of legal privilege


Waiver occurs where there is conduct that is inconsistent with maintaining confidentiality. The result of waiver is loss of confidentiality.

Privilege may be waived expressly or intentionally, whether in the course of litigation or otherwise, by a voluntary act of disclosure. The client may waive privilege, but so can the client’s lawyers, even if the client does not authorise them to do so.

Examples of the ways in which legal privilege might be waived include:

‣
intentionally placing the information into the public domain

‣
placing the material before a court

‣
accidental waiver by having a lengthy list of recipients for an email the contents of which (including attachments) are meant to remain confidential

‣
publicly quoting from Counsel's legal advice.


Cases of waiver of privilege are fact specific; it is for the court to decide whether or not privilege has been waived in any given situation. For instance, where a reference is made to privileged documents or advice, whether waiver has occurred will depend on the extent of the reference made. If the document or the fact that advice has been sought or given is simply mentioned in passing then this will not waive privilege. On the other hand, references to the substance of the document or to the advice itself may result in waiver.

If privilege has been waived, it cannot be reasserted i.e. the client cannot request for a communication to be privileged once the privilege has been waived.

The matter of litigation privilege was addressed in the case of R (on the application of) v Jukes [2018].

Jukes (J) had been a manager at a waste recycling company when an employee had been killed after coming into contact with parts of a baling machine, access to which should have been protected by a door fitted with an interlock. The interlock had been bypassed, so the deceased had been able to access the dangerous parts of the machine with it still running.

J was convicted under s7 HSWA, in that he had failed to take reasonable care for the health and safety of the employee who died. J appealed his conviction.

As a part of his defence, J had stated that he had not been responsible for health and safety and that someone else had the role of health and safety manager. He said that he was unaware that the interlock had been bypassed. However, the prosecution’s case was that J had in fact been the health and safety manager at the relevant time and that he had been aware of the status of the interlock. The prosecution relied on a signed statement that J had given to the company’s solicitors in which he said that he had been responsible for health and safety at the relevant time.

J argued that the statement which he gave to the company’s solicitors was privileged, so it should not have been disclosed to the prosecution.

The trial judge disagreed and the statement was duly made available to the prosecution. J was convicted.

At appeal, the Court of Appeal stated that a document would only attract litigation privilege if the following conditions were met:

‣
litigation was in progress or reasonably in contemplation at the time when the document was made

‣
the document was made or created with the sole or dominant purpose of conducting that litigation, and

‣
the litigation was adversarial, not investigatory or inquisitorial.


At the time that J provided his statement, matters were still at the investigatory stage and a criminal prosecution was not in reasonable contemplation. Sixteen months passed before he was interviewed by the police and the HSE.

In any event, it was decided that the privilege being claimed was not J’s to start with - it was the company’s or the Managing Director’s as it was they who were the solicitor’s clients, not J. Therefore, as he was not the client, J was unable to rely on the company or the Managing Director’s litigation privilege for his own benefit.

J’s appeal was denied.
