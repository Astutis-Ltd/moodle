Legal professional privilege


In some circumstances, communications relating to accidents etc may attract ‘Legal professional privilege’. The concept of ‘Legal privilege’ as it is known, is applicable to both civil and criminal law. It relates to confidential communications in whatever form (e.g. emails, text messages, reports, letters, memos, recordings etc) that the client wants to keep from opponents in litigation. However, not all documents will be ‘privileged’.

This is a complex area of law with many different circumstances where privilege may or may not apply. Privilege may also be waived, either intentionally or accidentally. It follows that advice should always be sought when considering whether legal privilege may be available.

Perhaps the best approach is always to assume that a communication may at some stage be disclosed to an opponent and to bear that in mind when committing ‘pen to paper’.

NB - Historically, the concept known in England and Wales as Legal Professional Privilege has been known in Scotland as ‘Confidentiality of communications’. In recent years, however, the Scottish legal system has increasingly aligned itself with the terminology used in England and Wales.


Legal privilege encourages frank disclosure of information between a client and their lawyer.

Who then is the client? The client can be:

‣
an individual,

‣
a partnership, or

‣
a company as represented through its directors.


‘The client’ will only include those individuals who are authorised, expressly or impliedly, to seek and obtain legal advice in relation to the particular matter under consideration, either from in-house or external lawyers.

Any person who does not fall into one of the above categories is unlikely to be classed as ‘The client’ even though they work for the organisation that has instructed the lawyer. Communications between such persons and instructed lawyers will probably not be privileged.

The client does not need to worry that privileged information will be disclosed to someone else, since the lawyer is under a duty not to disclose it without the client’s consent. This will be true whether the communication is requested by a court, an opponent in legal proceedings, law enforcement or a regulator such as the HSE.

The lawyer’s duty not to disclose privileged information will continue even after proceedings have concluded; privilege even survives the death of the client and may continue to be claimed by personal representatives and successors.

Key aspects of legal privilege include:

‣
privilege may only be claimed by the client

‣
privilege is absolute – it cannot be overridden by any other higher public interest (R v Derby Magistrates’ Court, ex parte B)

‣
privilege only applies in cases of adversarial litigation, such as in civil claims and criminal prosecutions; it does not apply in, say, a coroner’s inquest

‣
the communication(s) for which privilege is being claimed must be confidential

‣
the ‘dominant purpose’ test must be met (see below)

‣
privilege cannot be claimed retrospectively

‣
once waived, privilege cannot be reclaimed

‣
no adverse inferences may be drawn by the court if privilege is claimed.


There are two types of legal privilege - ‘Legal advice privilege’ and ‘Litigation privilege’.

Legal advice privilege applies to confidential communications specifically between a client and their lawyer; it does not extend to communications with third parties. The document must have been created for the dominant purpose of seeking and receiving legal advice in a relevant legal context.

For example, a client could claim legal advice privilege where it asks its lawyer for advice on the legal implications of a suspected breach of health and safety law, or where it asks for advice on its legal position when faced with a civil claim for damages.

Litigation privilege can be claimed for any confidential communication, and evidence of those communications, between a lawyer and his or her client and a third party, or between a client and a third party. Crucially, this only applies where the dominant purpose of the communication is for use in existing, pending or reasonably contemplated litigation.

For example, an employee was injured whilst using machinery at work. The internal investigation raised concerns over potential criminal liability, so the company asked its lawyer to advise. The lawyer appointed a technical specialist to visit the site and to produce a report on whether there would be grounds for a regulator to prosecute. In principle, the report would be protected by legal privilege because its dominant purpose is for use in existing, pending or reasonably contemplated litigation.

The client may find it hard to claim privilege for documents that may not have been written in ‘reasonable contemplation’ of legal proceedings. For example, an accident investigation report may have been written with the intention of finding the cause of an accident in order to prevent recurrence. At the time of writing there was no ‘reasonable contemplation’ of legal proceedings, so it would be hard to argue that the document was prepared for the ‘dominant purpose’ of litigation. In this case, privilege will likely not apply.

Simply marking a document as ‘Legally privileged’ does not automatically make it so. However, there is no harm in doing so and it may be helpful later on if the documents are effectively being written to obtain legal advice or to use in litigation.

In cases where there is existing, pending or reasonably contemplated litigation, it is helpful to speak to the company’s legal advisors at an early stage. Doing so will help the client to understand what is likely to attract privilege and what may not, and help them to set up the investigation in a manner that protects privilege as much as possible.
