The Limitation Act 1980

Claims are not always made immediately after an incident, while the event is still fresh in the memory. The Limitation Act 1980 allows time periods, known as ‘Limitation periods’, within which civil actions must be started. Examples of relevant limitation periods include:

  • 3 years for personal injury or ill health claims
  • 6 years for breach of contract claims
  • 6 years for professional negligence claims.

There are no equivalent limitation periods in the criminal law; most health and safety prosecutions are without time limit so can be brought at any time, making the need to collect and maintain evidence to defend criminal prosecutions all the more important.

In the case of personal injury and ill health claims, time starts to run from the ‘Date of knowledge’. This is usually the date when the injury becomes known or, in the case of a disease, the date of diagnosis. If the claimant is under the age of 18 then time does not start to run until their 18th birthday. Once the limitation clock starts to run, the claimant will have 3 years to issue proceedings i.e. to formally start their claim.

Claimants sometimes leave it very late to start their claim. The implication for the employer is that nearly 3 years may have passed before it is made aware that it is being sued. The employer then faces the task of having to locate and consider a potentially large number of documents to help it decide whether or not to admit the claim, or to resist it.

The need for an effective Information Management System becomes even more clear in the case of claims for illnesses that have long latency periods i.e. where exposure may have occurred many years in the past but the illness has only recently been diagnosed. In these cases the employer may need to recover documents from many years in the past. Without an effective way of managing this information its defence will be compromised from the outset.

Memories fade over time so the ability to store and locate relevant evidence is crucial. A memory of an event that happened several years ago is unlikely to be as clear as it was at the time of the event or shortly thereafter, so is not likely to carry much weight in court. However, if this memory is supported by contemporaneous documentary evidence, the defendant’s position will be much stronger and could even lead to the claimant dropping their claim completely.
