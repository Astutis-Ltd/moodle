The need to train managers regarding legal professional privilege

As can be seen from ‘Jukes’, above, privilege belongs to the client, i.e. the party who instructs the lawyer or who is authorised to seek and obtain legal advice in relation to the particular matter. Therefore, managers who do not fall into this category are unlikely to be seen as being ‘The client’ so will not have the benefit of privilege even if they think they do. This fact should be impressed on managers to ensure that they are aware that anything they disclose could find its way to the opposing legal team. This includes the findings of accident investigations in circumstances where the investigation was not completed with the intention of defending pending or future litigation; as we have already seen, such a document is disclosable to the opposing legal team. The manager who wrote the report would, in effect, be a witness and could be questioned on the content of the report should the matter come to trial.
