Qualitative, semi-quantitative or quantitative risk evaluation

Risk analyses vary widely in complexity, from simple, qualitative approaches to fully quantitative risk analysis (QRA).

Qualitative risk evaluation

In the simplest cases, reference can be made to specific legal limits, for example, people are liable to be exposed to noise levels above the lower exposure action value of 80 dB(A) LEpD, or not.

More complex qualitative judgements are often made within a framework set by legal standards and guidance, for example, the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (CoSHH) and its ACoP (L5) established a decision-making framework if hazardous substances are used.

The HSE suggests that the process of risk evaluation begins with consideration of how likely it is that each hazard could cause harm, as this determines whether or not more needs to be done to reduce the risk. Qualitative estimates of hazardous event likelihoods are usually derived based on a combination of analysis of historical data, and informal expert judgement or experience.

Risk matrices provide a relatively easy method for representing the likelihood and consequences of a hazardous event and have the following benefits:

  • easily understood
  • relatively quick to compile
  • graphically represent combinations of likelihood and consequences.

Qualitative estimates of event likelihoods typically involve categorising the event likelihood by subjectively interpreting descriptions (such as extremely unlikely, very unlikely, unlikely, improbable or probable) or equivalent numerical values.

Subjectivity in likelihood estimations is difficult to eliminate because of a lack of precision in defining the categories. Consequence estimation tends to be more straightforward, as it is easier to explicitly define consequence categories qualitatively.

Accurate, quantitative predictive data is essential for generating explicit definitions of likelihood and consequence categories.

Semi-quantitative evaluation

Risk matrices are often considered semi-quantitative when frequency ranges are used to define likelihood categories (see table below) and consequences are defined in terms of numbers affected for each outcome category, for example, a catastrophic consequence would involve more than five on-site fatalities or more than one off-site.

Semi-quantitative estimates of hazardous event likelihoods (for example, order of magnitude estimates) can be determined from a combination of analysis of historical data, simple modelling and informal expert judgement.

Providing they are properly constructed, risk matrices may be used to:

  • provide a ranking of event outcomes to enable effective prioritisation
  • identify intolerable risks
  • identify Safety Critical Events (SCEs)
  • provide an approximate risk analysis.

Quantitative Risk Analysis (QRA)

QRA techniques may be used as a basis for making decisions in more complex industries. QRA is specifically referred to in the Offshore Installations (Safety Case) Regulations 1992.

In a QRA both the frequencies of events and their consequences are quantified, using appropriate techniques, which usually involve a combination of the following approaches to fully quantify fault and event trees, and interpret using formal expert judgement:

  • using relevant historical data
  • derivation via analytical modelling techniques.

Historical data is not limited by the imagination of the analyst in deriving failure mechanisms and will not omit any significant routes to failure. The historical data should encompass all common relevant contributory aspects including:

  • reliability of equipment
  • human factors
  • operational methods
  • quality of construction
  • inspection, maintenance and operation, etc.

Potential shortcomings with historical data include:

  • unlikely to cover rare incidents
  • may include out-dated failure modes that may not be relevant to the specific case under study
  • the degree of under-reporting in the data needs to be accounted for (under-reporting tends to increase as the severity of the consequences decreases).

Modelling techniques are used to estimate incident frequencies when suitable historical data are not available or are inadequate. The most common and widely used techniques are Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) and Event Tree Analysis (ETA). Broadly speaking, numerical risk estimates are one or both of:

  • individual risk estimates
  • societal (or group) risk estimates.

QRA can be a useful tool to:

  • Identify those events which contribute most to the risk (safety critical events)
  • Estimate the benefits of a proposed risk reduction measure. In this case the results of a QRA would normally be coupled with a Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) in order to determine whether or not a proposed risk reduction measure would be reasonably practicable.
