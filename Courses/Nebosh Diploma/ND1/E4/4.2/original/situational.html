Situational awareness


The origin of the concept of situational awareness is often attributed to Oswald Boelke, a German pilot in the first World War, regarded as the ‘father of air-combat’. Boelke realised ‘the importance of gaining an awareness of the enemy before the enemy gained a similar awareness’, and devised methods for accomplishing this.

The idea gained prominence in the technical and academic literature in the late 1980’s. The initial push for research and development came from the aviation industry, where there is considerable pressure for pilots and air traffic controllers to develop better situational awareness.

Much of the research to date has been conducted in aviation, which has obvious safety implications. It is argued that the concepts could be extended to other safety critical domains such as power generation, petro-chemical, nuclear, command and control, medicine, etc. In essence, any task that requires people to keep track of events is a potential candidate for situational assessment research and application.

There are a number of modern theories of situational awareness, one of the major ones is Endsley’s Model of Situation Awareness in Dynamic Risk Assessment as shown in the below figure.




Endsley (1988) defined situational awareness as “the perception of the elements in the environment within a volume of time and space, the comprehension of their meaning and a projection of their status in the near future.”

The model was developed initially to understand aviation tasks such as piloting aircraft and air traffic control in the US air force where people are required to keep up to date with a dynamically changing environment.

The three levels of situational awareness represent ascending levels of situational awareness, not a hierarchical progression. A person who comprehends the current situation has better situational awareness than one who can perceive the data on a screen but does not know what it means, and a person who can project the likely future events and states of the system and environment has better situational awareness than one who cannot.

From this model, it can be seen that the main ways of improving situational awareness are through ergonomic system design and developing the competence of the workers engaging with the system.

More recently the Health and Safety Laboratory (HSL), in collaboration with the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) and principal contractors in the construction sector has developed the Leadership and Worker Involvement toolkit to help small and medium sized construction sites improve their health and safety performance through engaging with their workforce.

Situational awareness is one of the major tools in the toolkit. The toolkit defines it as “being aware of what is happening around you in terms of where you are, where you are supposed to be, and whether anyone or anything around you is a threat to your health and safety,” and considers that an individual’s knowledge, experience and education is key in helping a worker to understand what is going on around them in determining if it is safe to work.

The temporary loss or lack of situational awareness is a causal factor in many construction accidents. This can occur because there is so much ‘going on’ in the working environment, or a worker becomes so absorbed in their own thoughts, that they fail to spot those things that could pose a serious threat to their health and safety.

Situational awareness can be further reduced in times of high workload or when under pressure to get a job done to time. In these situations, it is essential to maintain a high level of situational awareness to stop accidents and near misses.

Situational awareness is only as accurate as the individual’s perception or reading of the situation, so what they think is happening may not accurately reflect reality. How a situation is read can be influenced by many things such as the type of information given, personal experience and distractions in the workplace.

It is important that workers know how many problems they face and how serious they are.

The basic approach requires workers to pause and make a quick mental assessment of the working environment, considering the following questions:

‣
Is there anything in the working environment that poses a threat to health and safety and if so, to what extent?

‣
Is the threat big enough that work should be stopped?

‣
Is there anything that can be done to safely reduce that threat in order that work can carry on safely?

Selecting risk controls

Risk assessment should be viewed as a means to an end rather than an end in itself. The reason risks are assessed is to ensure that they are effectively controlled.

Risk assessment and risk control are parts of the larger process of risk management. An effective risk management system should assess the risks that arise in the workplace, put in place sensible health and safety measures to control them and then make sure that they work in practice.


Principles of prevention

Regulation 4 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, as amended requires the employer to implement any preventive and protective measures in accordance with the principles of prevention specified in Schedule 1 of the Regulations.

Schedule 1 specifies the following general principles of prevention:

‣
avoiding risks

‣
evaluating the risks which cannot be avoided

‣
combating the risks at source

‣
adapting the work to the individual, especially as regards the design of workplaces, the choice of work equipment and the choice of working and production methods, with a view, in particular, to alleviating monotonous work and work at a predetermined work- rate and to reducing their effect on health

‣
adapting to technical progress

‣
replacing the dangerous by the non-dangerous or the less dangerous

‣
developing a coherent overall prevention policy which covers technology, organisation of work, working conditions, social relationships and the influence of factors relating to the working environment

‣
giving collective protective measures priority over individual protective measures

‣
giving appropriate instructions to employees.


The Approved Code of Practice (ACoP) to the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations (withdrawn) acknowledged that that the principles are not an individually prescriptive requirement but general principles that should be applied wherever it is reasonable to do so. The ACoP further suggested that adopting good practice (ACoP / HSE Guidance / BS EN ISO standards / Industry guides or standards) will be enough to ensure risks are reduced sufficiently.

The ACoP also offered additional interpretation on the principles of prevention, as follows. (Note: the order and content varied from Schedule 1 – and no reference was made to replacing the dangerous by the non-dangerous or the less dangerous).

a) 
Avoid risks

If possible avoid a risk altogether, e.g. Do the work in a different way, taking care not to introduce new hazards.

b) 
Evaluate risks which cannot be avoided

Evaluate risks that cannot be avoided by carrying out a risk assessment.

c) 
Combat risks at source

Risks should be tackled at source, rather than taking measures to deal with the consequences. Prevention is better than protection is better than mitigation.

If steps are slippery, treating or replacing them is better than displaying a warning sign.

d) 
Adapt work to the individual

Affected individuals should be consulted when: designing workplaces, selecting work and personal protective equipment, and drawing up working and safety procedures.

Monotonous work should be avoided and where possible individuals should be given control over work they are responsible for.

e) 
Adapt to technical progress

Opportunities for improving working methods and making them safer as a result of technological and technical advances should be exploited.

f) 
Develop a coherent overall prevention policy

A coherent policy should be developed and implemented to progressively reduce those risks that cannot be prevented or avoided altogether. The way work is organised, the working conditions, the environment and any relevant social factors should be taken account of.

g) 
Give collective measures priority over individual protective measures

Give priority to measures which protect the whole workplace and everyone who works there, and so give the greatest benefit (i.e. give collective protective measures priority over individual measures).

h) 
Give appropriate instructions

Ensure that workers understand what they need to do to help keep themselves and their colleagues safe.

i) 
Develop a positive health and safety culture

The avoidance, prevention and reduction of risks at work must be accepted as part of the organisation’s approach and attitude to all its activities. It should be recognised at all levels of the organisation, from junior to senior management.


Note: Although the ‘principles of prevention’ are not written as a hierarchy they do embody several hierarchical principles, notably:

‣
The first option is to avoid risk (design out or eliminate)

‣
Risks should be tackled at source (prevent / protect / mitigate)

‣
Give collective protective measures priority over individual measures (safe place is better than safe person).

Hierarchies of control

There are many hierarchical approaches to risk management defined in legislation, guidance and standards or in general use.

Hierarchies should be applied with caution, the overarching goals of getting the job done, keeping people safe, and complying with the law should not be lost sight of.

Any individual control measure is likely to have weaknesses and a combination of controls will be more reliable than any individual control. The ‘best’ control option for any particular hazard should be considered carefully in context.

Choice of control measure

As has been seen there are numerous approaches to determining the ‘best’ risk control options. Ultimately the decision regarding the control measures will involve a degree of compromise. The following issues should be considered:

‣
The extent to which the risk control measure reduces the risk – is it controlled to a tolerable level or ‘as low as is reasonably practicable’ (ALARP)

‣
Whether or not there is recognised best practise specified in ACoPs, guidance or British standards etc.

‣
The cost of the control measures. Some risk control measures can be introduced relatively cheaply but thereafter are expensive to maintain, whereas others are more expensive upfront but are then cheaper to maintain. Generally the measures at the bottom of the hierarchy – SSW / PPE are cheaper in the short term – but will have additional costs over time for training, supervision etc. Note: Cost Benefit Analysis is discussed later

‣
Short term measures may need to be introduced pending sourcing investment for engineering control measures, in which case the transitional arrangements will need to be effectively managed

‣
The extent to which the on-going effectiveness of the control measure relies on safe behaviour. The measures towards the bottom of the hierarchy (often called safe person strategies) are more vulnerable to human failure. Also, passive control measures are generally safer than active control measures – i.e. those that rely on people to do things

‣
The reliability of the control measure over time

‣
The need for on-going cleaning, testing, servicing and maintenance etc. of control measures.



Required levels of control

Employers are required to do all that is ‘reasonably practicable’ to protect the health and safety of their employees and others at work.

Determining whether or not a control measure is ‘reasonably practicable’ requires a balancing of the costs of dealing with the risk (money, time, effort) against the risk of injury (likelihood × severity).


The greater the risk the greater the efforts expected to control the risk. If there is gross disproportion between the two, i.e. significant resources are required for minimal risk improvement then it may be argued that the control is not reasonably practicable.

The objective is to manage risks to a tolerable level* which is one that people are willing to live with because it delivers certain benefits, and because there is confidence that the risk is being properly controlled.

Three important things are known about probabilities (the likelihood / chance / odds aspect of risk):

‣
There is no such thing as ‘zero risk’. Whatever a person is doing there is always a risk of injury or death. There is a 1 in 600 chance that a forty year old man will not live to be forty one

‣
However unlikely it is that something could happen it could still happen. There is a 1 in 14 000 000 chance of winning the lottery jackpot in the UK, but most weeks someone wins it

‣
Each individual’s risk (or odds or chance) will vary from the average because of the many variables such as age, gender, location, etc.


Note: tolerability of risk is discussed in more detail, later.


Cost benefit analysis of risk controls

Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA) is a method of evaluating the costs and benefits of an investment project which takes account of both internal and external costs and benefits and attempts to measure them in monetary terms.

A CBA is typically undertaken in four steps:

Step 1 Identify all costs and benefits using the principle of opportunity cost. In health and safety these are the costs of lost opportunities, either through people having to stand idle or not being able to produce at their regular job by virtue of being redirected to deal with the consequences of an accident.
Step 2 Measure the costs and benefits using money as a unit of account.
Step 3 Sensitivity analysis – consider the likelihood of the cost or benefit actually occurring.
Step 4 Discounting – take account of the timing of the cost and benefit.
A £1 000 benefit now is worth more than £1 000 benefit in 10 years’ time.

Costs

All appropriate costs must be included such as the costs of installation, operation, training and any additional maintenance of control measures and the business losses that would follow from any business interruptions while putting the measures into place.

Sacrifice implies non-recoverable costs i.e. lost production rather than delayed production. Costs incurred by third parties should not be counted.

The costs considered should only be those necessary and sufficient for implementing the control measure (not the gold plated or deluxe version). Any savings as a result of the measure (for example: reduced operational costs, avoidance of damage and reinstatement costs if relevant) should be counted as ‘cost savings’ and offset against the above costs. They should not be considered as safety benefits.

All translations into monetary costs should be carefully justified.


Benefits

The benefits should include all reduction in risk to workers, members of the public, and to the wider community. Prevented fatalities, injuries, illnesses and environmental incidents can all be accounted for.

All benefits of a measure should be included. If a risk reduction measure is identified for one type of accident but reduces other risks as well e.g. health risks, all benefits should be counted.

HSE produces annual cost estimates of injuries and new cases of ill-health.

From these total cost estimates, unit costs are calculated by dividing the total costs to society (as calculated by the HSE Cost to Britain model) by the number of new incidence cases for each category of injury and ill health.

The unit costs to society per each incidence of workplace injury and work-related ill health are most commonly referred to as the ‘appraisal values’.

Organisational arrangements for implementing and maintaining an effective risk assessment programme

Before looking to implement any new work programmes or processes it is important to consider what needs to be done pre-implementation to prepare the organisation for the change.

This will typically involve careful scoping of the project and good consultation and communication with management and workers.

Understanding the requirements of the programme in providing both operational safety at the point of work and management information to enable effective strategic management of risks is important in the scoping stage.

Effective consultation and communication is essential in ensuring that all stakeholders understand the need for the programme and are ‘on-side’ and that any potential barriers to successful implementation are understood and addressed early.

Practical considerations of implementation can be reviewed by considering Rudyard Kipling’s ‘six honest serving men’ – ‘What and Why and When and How and Where and Who’.

As an example:

What activities need to be assessed and why? (Understanding the why will help with getting the stakeholders on side)

When do the risk assessments need to be done? Practical considerations include being present to observe work activity and the phasing of the implementation plan with SMART objectives to keep the project on track.

How will the risk assessment be undertaken? Procedures will need to be documented, proformas designed and standards set for risk evaluation, prioritisation, specifying appropriate control measures etc. Also, how will the risk assessments be recorded and communicated?

Where will the risk assessments be done? Practical considerations regarding access and the health and safety of the risk assessors need to be understood an addressed.

Who will do the risk assessments? Operational staff are essential to the process, ‘fresh pairs of eyes’ should also be considered, and expertise seconded as necessary. The competence of individual risk assessors and calibration of risk perception across a team of assessors is important. This will require training, assessment of competence and possibly authorisations to conduct risk assessments on behalf of the employer.


Post-implementation also requires consideration. Additional control measures are likely to be required and action plans developed with SMART objectives to help ensure that additional control requirements are properly addressed.

Once additional control measures are in place they will need to be monitored to determine their initial and ongoing effectiveness. The management control loop will then need to be closed with a formal review and the specification of any follow on actions.
