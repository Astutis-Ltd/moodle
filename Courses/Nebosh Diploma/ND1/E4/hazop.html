HAZOP

Introduction

The Hazard and Operability (HAZOP) Study methodology was devised by ICI in the late 1960s, as an evolution of method study, for use during the design stage of a project to identify and correct design faults which might lead to Hazard or Operability problems. HAZOP is now also used for modifications and for reviews of existing plant.

HAZOP is the primary tool for the identification of weaknesses in the process design and is used world-wide within process industries including chemical, petrochemical, oil and gas, pharmaceutical and general manufacturing.

The Institute of Chemical Engineers (IChemE) HAZOP guide was first published in 1977. It has been regularly updated since and provides definitive guidance on the HAZOP study methodology.

There is also a British Standard, BS EN 61882:2016 “Hazard and Operability Studies (HAZOP studies) - Application Guide.”

Purpose

The main purpose of a HAZOP study is to identify and evaluate process and operation hazards within a planned process or operation that have not been identified or designed out in earlier stages. Hazards may harm people and property on or off-site, or may be harmful to the environment or have major financial consequences.

HAZOP studies may also be used to identify significant operability or quality problems. The need to consider quality issues varies greatly with the details of the operation but in some industries it is a crucial area. Many operability problems also lead to hazards, giving a dual reason for identifying and controlling them.

HAZOP studies should also cover aspects of maintenance operations, including isolation, preparation and removal for maintenance since these often create hazards as well as an operability problem. Detailed ergonomic analysis may be necessary for manual operations or activities.

Overview

The study begins with the creation of a conceptual model of the system or operation, using all available, relevant material such as:

  • Existing detailed design drawings;
  • An outline of operating procedures;
  • Material data sheets; and
  • Reports from earlier studies.

The system is then broken down into stages (sections) for analysis.

Consideration of possible deviations from the design intention of the stage under review leads to the identification of hazards and potential operating problems.

Deviations from design intent are generated by grouping together a guideword (see table below) and a parameter (also known as a property or system property). NB it is this process that distinguishes a HAZOP study.

Where the HAZOP team can suggest a cause for a given deviation, the team’s experience is used to estimate the consequences taking existing safeguards into account.

Where the risk is considered non-trivial or where an aspect requires further investigation, a formal record is generated to allow the problem to be followed up outside the meeting. The team then moves on with the analysis.

The validity of the analysis depends upon:

  • the right team members;
  • the accuracy of the information used; and
  • the quality of the design.

For a HAZOP study to be effective the design must be firm and the Piping and Instrumentation Diagrams (P&ID’s) must be frozen. This requires management commitment and forward planning.

Generating a deviation

A meaningful deviation is generated by coupling a Guideword and a parameter. A deviation can be generated by taking a parameter and combining it with each guideword in turn to see if a meaningful deviation results or the alternative approach is to take a guideword and try each parameter in turn.

Standard Guidewords Meaning
No (not, none) None of the design intent is achieved
More (more of, higher) Quantitative increase in a parameter
Less (less of, lower) Quantitative decrease in a parameter
As well as (more than) An additional activity occurs
Part of Only some of the design intention is achieved
Reverse Logical opposite of the design intention occurs
Other than (other) Complete substitution. Another activity takes place

In contrast to the list of guidewords, it is not possible to give a list of parameters. These must be thought out by the team members for the particular process being examined.

Examples of parameters for process operations include:

Flow Pressure Temperature Mixing Stirring
Transfer Level Viscosity Reaction Composition
Addition Time Separation Phase Speed
Particle size Measure Control pH Sequence
Signal Start/stop Operate Maintain Services
