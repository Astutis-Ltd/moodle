The purpose of employer’s liability and public liability insurance

Employers liability

If a business has employees then it is likely to be required by law for the organisation to have employers’ liability insurance.

If an employee is injured or becomes ill as a result of the work they do for their employer, they can claim compensation from the employer. However, if it is found that the organisation is liable, employers’ liability insurance will pay any compensation for employees’ injuries or illness.

When an organisation takes out employers’ liability insurance, they will have an agreement with their insurer about the circumstances in which the insurer will pay compensation. For example, the policy will cover the specific activities that relate to the business. If however, the insurer believes that the employer has failed to meet their legal responsibilities for the health and safety of their employees and that this has led to the claim, the policy may enable the insurer to sue the employer to reclaim the cost of the compensation.

Only a few businesses are not required to have employers’ liability insurance. If they have no employees, or are a family business and all employees are closely related to the owner, they may not need it. The Employers’ Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969 lays down the requirements for the insurance and where it is required.

The following employers are exempt:

  • most public organisations including government departments and agencies, local authorities, police authorities and nationalised industries;
  • health service bodies, including National Health Service trusts, health authorities, primary care trusts and Scottish health boards;
  • some other organisations which are financed through public funds, such as passenger transport executives and magistrates’ courts committees;
  • family businesses, i.e. if all of your employees are closely related to the owner (as husband, wife, civil partner, etc). However, this exemption does not apply to family businesses which are incorporated as limited companies;
  • companies employing only their owner where that employee also owns 50% or more of the issued share capital in the company.

The cover provided by employer’s liability insurance must be for at least £5 million. However, organisations, after looking at their risks and liabilities, may choose to take out more than £5 million cover. In practice, most insurers offer cover of at least £10 million. Employer’s must display a copy of the certificate of insurance where employees can easily read it.

Organisations can be fined if they do not hold a current employers’ liability insurance policy which complies with the law.

Public liability

Public liability insurance is different from employer’s liability. It covers organisations for claims made against them by members of the public or other businesses, but not for claims by employees. While public liability insurance is generally voluntary, employers’ liability insurance is compulsory. Organisations can be fined if they do not hold a current employers’ liability insurance policy which complies with the law.
