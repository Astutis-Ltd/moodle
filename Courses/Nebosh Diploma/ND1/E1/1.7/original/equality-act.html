Equality Act 2010


The Equality Act 2010 harmonised and replaced previous legislation (such as the Race Relations Act 1976 and the Disability Discrimination Act 1995). The Act covers the same groups that were protected by previous equality legislation, i.e. age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation, marriage and civil partnership, and pregnancy and maternity, which are now called ‘protected characteristics’.


Types of discrimination

‣
Direct discrimination occurs when someone is treated less favourably than another person because of a protected characteristic they have or are thought to have (perception discrimination)

‣
Discrimination by association is direct discrimination against someone because they associate with another person who possesses a protected characteristic. Previously applied to race, religion or belief and sexual orientation is now extended to cover age, disability, gender reassignment and sex.

‣
Perception discrimination is direct discrimination against an individual because others think they possess a particular protected characteristic. It applies even if the person does not actually possess that characteristic. Previously applied to age, race, religion or belief and sexual orientation, perception discrimination is now extended to cover disability, gender reassignment and sex.

‣
Indirect discrimination can occur when a company condition, rule, policy or practice applies to everyone but particularly disadvantages people who share a protected characteristic. Indirect discrimination can be justified if it can be shown to be reasonable and is ‘a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim’. A legitimate aim is a lawful decision made in running a business or organisation, but if there is a discriminatory effect the sole aim of reducing costs is likely to be unlawful. Indirect discrimination can be applied to age, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation and marriage and civil partnership, disability and gender reassignment.


The Equality Act has introduced a new definition of disability and made it easier for a person to show that they are disabled and protected from disability discrimination.

A person is disabled if they have a physical or mental impairment which has a substantial and long term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. The employer has a duty to make reasonable adjustments to help employees overcome disadvantage resulting from an impairment.

The Equality Act includes a new protection from discrimination arising from disability stating that it is discrimination to treat a disabled person unfavourably because of something connected with their disability (for example, a tendency to make spelling mistakes arising from dyslexia). This type of discrimination is unlawful unless the employer can show that it is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim.

The Equality Act also includes a new provision which makes it unlawful, except in certain circumstances, for employers to ask about a candidate’s health before offering them work.


Sex discrimination

A person directly discriminates against a woman if, on the ground of her sex, they treat her less favourably than they would treat a man. Typically this occurs on the grounds of sex or marital status.

Indirect discrimination is also an offence. This occurs where an apparently neutral requirement or condition is applicable, but members of a certain sex are less able to meet the requirement.

The law also requires that men receive equal treatment, but the vast majority of claims are from women.

Case law

Page v Freight Hire (Tank Haulage) Ltd. (1981)
Mrs. Page was a 23 year old HGV driver. Following a recommendation from ICI that women of child-bearing age should not be employed in driving lorries loaded with the chemical dimethyl- formamide (DMF), Mrs. Page was taken off that kind of work. She brought a claim of unlawful sex discrimination.

It was held that if there was a real danger to women from certain processes or chemicals then the duty to ensure their safety took precedence over the duty not to discriminate. As the evidence from the manufacturers was that the chemical was dangerous to women of Mrs. Page’s age, the step of taking her off that work was a reasonable one in compliance with Section 2 of the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974. There was therefore no unlawful discrimination.



Race discrimination

Race discrimination occurs when a person is treated less favourably because of race, colour, nationality and ethnic or national origin. Race discrimination covers four areas:

‣
Direct discrimination: treating someone less favourably because of their actual or perceived race, or because of the race of someone they associate with

‣
Indirect discrimination: where a policy, practice or procedure disadvantages people of a particular race, for example, requiring job applicants to have GCSE Maths and English indirectly discriminates against people educated in countries with a different system. Indirect discrimination can be justified if it is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim

‣
Harassment: when unwanted conduct related to race has the purpose or effect of violating an individual’s dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating or offensive environment for that individual

‣
Victimisation: unfair treatment of an employee who has made or supported a complaint about racial discrimination.


Case law

Essa v Laing Ltd (2004)
Essa was a Welshman of black Somali origin who worked as a labourer on the construction of the Millennium Stadium in Cardiff. He was subjected to racial abuse and insults.

His complaints to his employers were not taken seriously. Eventually Essa walked off the job and complained of constructive dismissal and race discrimination. His complaints were upheld and he was awarded £5,000 compensation for injury to feelings and £500 for financial loss.

Essa was treated for depression and allegedly underwent a major personality change, becoming prejudiced against white people and violent and aggressive. As a consequence of his psychological treatment, Essa appealed to the Employment Appeals Tribunal and then the Court of Appeal in pursuit of a larger compensation award.

Ultimately the appeal was dismissed. The Court of Appeal decided that the appropriate test to be applied in assessing compensation for a victim of unlawful race discrimination was the establishment of a causal link between the act of discrimination and the alleged injury.


Exemption example: Construction (Head Protection) Regulations 1989: Sikhs are exempted from the requirement of wearing a safety helmet when they are wearing their turbans while working on construction sites. In consequence, an employer will not be liable in respect of any injury caused because he failed to comply with the statutory duty. A Sikh who is injured because of failure to wear a safety helmet will only be able to recover damages to the extent the injury or damage would have occurred had a safety helmet been worn.

Disability discrimination

It is unlawful to discriminate against any disabled person in connection with employment, the provision of goods, facilities and services or the disposal or management of premises.

The Equality Act is supplemented by various regulations and a Code of Practice and Guidance Notes. In particular, the guidance must be taken into account when a tribunal is reaching a decision on whether a person is suffering from a disability.

Whether a person is disabled or not, according to the legal definition, has been subject to much interpretation through guidance and by the courts and employment tribunals. A person with a disability can be described as someone with:

…a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on his ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities.



Physical or mental impairment

Physical impairments must be real and not psychosomatic but if there are physical symptoms, the existence or non-existence of a physical cause is irrelevant.

A mental impairment does not have to be a clinically well-recognised illness but has to be a substantial impairment having a long-term adverse effect. Vague expressions such as depression or anxiety are not helpful but may be indicative of, a mental impairment if there is evidence of the long-term adverse effect of the illness.

Addictions to alcohol, nicotine or other substances do not in themselves constitute an impairment but if there is a physical or mental impairment, the fact that it was caused by such an addiction is not relevant.

A medical advisor can express an opinion about diagnosis, prognosis etc. but it is for the employment tribunal to determine whether or not a person is disabled.


Substantial and long-term adverse effect

Any impairment must have lasted, or be expected to last, for a period of months.

If the impairment ceases to have a substantial effect on a person’s ability to carry out day-to-day activities but is likely to recur it will still be treated as having that effect.

Persons who suffer from cancer, HIV and multiple sclerosis will be deemed to be disabled from the moment of diagnosis, rather than from the time the illness takes a long-term effect.

Anyone certified or registered blind or partially sighted is deemed to be disabled for the purpose of the Equality Act.

Whether an impairment has a substantial effect on a person’s ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities should not be judged purely on their ability to carry our their job. If the effects fluctuate and are exacerbated by work, then the performance of normal day-to-day activities both while at work and while not at work should be taken into account.

To assess whether an impairment has a substantial adverse effect or not, the tribunal should concentrate on what a claimant cannot do or can only do with difficulty, and not on what he can do. It is the effect of the impairment that must be substantial, not the severity of the impairment.


Normal day-to-day activities

Normal day-to-day activities are those which affect a person with regard to mobility, manual dexterity, physical co-ordination, continence, ability to lift, carry or move everyday objects, speech, hearing, eyesight, memory or ability to concentrate, learn or understand, or perception of risks of physical danger.
