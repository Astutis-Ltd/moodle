Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA)


The Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA) was introduced in response to a spate of scandals and disasters in the late 1980s / early 1990s where workers were afraid to raise issues with management (examples: Clapham train crash or Piper Alpha) or had raised them and been ignored (example: Herald of Free Enterprise).

The PIDA encourages people to ‘blow the whistle’ about (report) malpractice in the workplace and is designed to ensure that organisations respond by acting on the message rather than against the messenger.

The range of malpractices covered includes:

‣
crimes

‣
civil offences (including negligence, breach of contract, etc.)

‣
miscarriage of justice

‣
danger to health and safety or the environment. It also includes the cover-up of any of these.


The Public Interest Disclosure Act applies whether or not the information is confidential and extends to malpractice occurring in the UK and any other country or territory. In addition to employees, it covers agency staff, contractors, home workers, trainees and every professional in the NHS.

To be protected, disclosures must be made ‘in good faith’, i.e. be an honest attempt to address a genuine concern. Disclosures may be classed as internal, regulatory or wider.

Internal disclosures made in good faith to a manager or the employer will be protected if the whistleblower has a reasonable suspicion that the malpractice has occurred, is occurring or is likely to occur.

Regulatory disclosures made in good faith to prescribed bodies such as the HSE, where the ‘whistleblower’ has a reasonable belief that the information and their allegation(s) are substantially true, are protected if the whistleblower meets the test for internal disclosure and believes the information to be true and of interest to the regulator.

Wider disclosures to the media, MPs, the police and non-prescribed regulators are protected if, in addition to the tests for regulatory disclosures, they are reasonable in all the circumstances, are not made for personal gain and fulfil at least one of the following preconditions:

‣
reasonably believed they would be victimised if they raised the matter internally or with a designated

‣
regulator

‣
reasonably believed a cover-up was likely and there was no regulator

‣
had already raised the matter internally or with a prescribed regulator

‣
the concern was of an exceptionally serious nature.


An employee who makes a wide, public disclosure is more likely to be protected if there was no internal procedure set up.

Where the whistleblower is victimised in breach of the Act they can bring a claim to an employment tribunal for compensation. Awards are uncapped and based on the losses suffered. Additionally, where an employee is sacked, they may apply for an interim order to keep their job.
