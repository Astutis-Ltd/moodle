Tort of negligence

Negligence may be explained as careless conduct injuring another. In legal terms it was defined by Lord Wright in Lochgelly Iron and Coal Co. v McMullan (1934).

"In strict legal analysis, negligence means more than heedless or careless conduct, whether in omission or commission: it properly connotes the complex concept of duty, breach and damage thereby suffered by the person to whom the duty was owing."

For the injured party (claimant) to succeed in a negligence claim, they must prove:

  1. That the defendant (usually the employer) owed them a duty of care.
  2. That this duty was breached.
  3. That the claimant suffered a legally recognisable loss as a result of the breach.

In Scotland the terms pursuer and defender are used in place of claimant and defendant.

The duty of care

Prior to 1932 there was no generalised duty of care in negligence. The tort was only applied in particular situations where the courts had decided that a duty should be owed, such as road accidents or dangerous goods.

Case law

In Donoghue v Stevenson (1932) Lord Atkin attempted to lay down a general principle which would cover all the circumstances where there could be liability for negligence. He said:

"You must take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions which you can reasonably foresee would be likely to injure your neighbour. Who, then, in law is my neighbour? The answer seems to be – persons who are so closely and directly affected by my act that I ought reasonably to have them in contemplation as being so affected when I am directing my mind to the acts or omissions which are called in question."

The requirements that must now be satisfied before a duty of care is held to exist were established by Lord Bridge in Caparo Industries v Dickman (1990). These requirements are:

  • Foreseeability of the damage, i.e. whether a ‘reasonable person’ would have foreseen damage in the circumstances
  • A sufficiently ‘proximate’ relationship between the parties, i.e. a neighbour relationship
  • It must be fair, just and reasonable to impose such a duty.

Reasonable care

The standard of care required was defined in Paris v Stepney Borough Council (1951) as the care which an ordinary prudent employer would take in all the circumstances.

What is reasonable will vary with context, and with the characteristics of the individual to whom the duty is owed. In general, a greater level of care must be taken of more vulnerable individuals, such as children or disabled persons.

Case law

In Paris v Stepney Borough Council (1951) the House of Lords held that where a workman had a disability which increased the risk of serious injury, if he was injured in an accident, the special risk of injury was a relevant consideration in determining the precautions the employer should take to fulfil his duty of care to the workman.

In Yachuk v. Oliver Blais Co. (1949) a nine year old boy lied to a petrol station attendant to obtain a container of petrol. The boy claimed his mother’s car had run out of fuel, but he actually wanted it to light a bonfire. The boy was injured in an explosion when lighting the petrol soaked timber.

The court held that the petrol station attendant was negligent in selling the petrol to the child, and that he owed a greater duty of care to a child than an adult when selling petrol.

In the case of Latimer v AEC Ltd. (1953), a factory floor was flooded after an unusually heavy rainfall. After the flood subsided the floor was left slippery, so was treated with sawdust. There was not enough sawdust to treat the whole floor, so it was used where the need was felt to be greatest. Subsequently Latimer slipped on an untreated section of floor and was injured.

AEC Ltd was held to have taken reasonable precautions and exercised reasonable care.

Foreseeability (also date of knowledge of risk)

If harm cannot be foreseen, the defendant cannot be held liable for it. In many occupational health cases the issue of whether harm was foreseeable or not becomes embroiled with the issue of the assumed date of knowledge of risk.

Case law

In Joseph v Ministry of Defence (1980) a man who developed symptoms of vibration induced white finger after working as a caulker/riveter for many years, failed in his claim for damages.

The Court of Appeal held that in 1973 when the complaint arose little was known of the condition.

Knowledge of the condition has increased greatly since then and vibration white finger, when acquired in specified occupations, has been a prescribed disease under the Social Security (Industrial Injuries) (Prescribed Diseases) Amendment Regulations 1985 since 1 April 1985.

Case law

In British Coal v Armstrong and Others (1996) the Court of Appeal held that British Coal should have recognised by 1973 that the work undertaken by the claimants gave rise to foreseeable risk of VWF, and that they should have taken effective precautions by 1976.

In Thompson and others v Smiths Ship Repairers (North Shields) (1984) it was held that after the Ministry of Labour published Noise and the Worker in 1963, the defendants should have had the knowledge of the foreseeable risk and provided ear protectors and/or other reasonable precautions to protect their workers from the risk of hearing loss.

Proximity

This is in essence the ‘neighbour test’, i.e. is it reasonable for the defendant to have the claimant in mind, as a person who may be directly affected by the negligent act?

Case law

In the case of Kapfunde v Abbey National and Daniel (1998), Dr Daniel was a GP retained by Abbey National to provide medical services, including pre-employment assessments.

Ms Kapfunde was refused a permanent position with Abbey National on the basis of Dr Daniel’s advice that, as a sickle cell anaemia sufferer, she was likely to have a higher than average level of absence.

Ms Kapfunde argued that Dr Daniels owed her a duty of care in relation to the information she had provided concerning her sickness record, and that he had over-assessed the risk of her being off work due to illness related to sickle cell.

The Court of Appeal held that Dr Daniel’s relationship was with Abbey National and he did not have a sufficiently proximate relationship with Ms Kapfunde to owe her a duty of care.

Working relationships that are sufficiently proximate to be deemed a neighbour relationship include:

  • employer to employees
  • client to contractor and contractor’s employees (and vice versa)
  • designers, manufacturers and suppliers to customers / users
  • designers, manufacturers and suppliers to customers / users
  • occupier to authorised visitors.

The common law duty of care owed by an employer to its employees was defined in the case of Wilson’s and Clyde Coal Co. v English (1938). In this case, the employer was compelled by law to employ a colliery agent who was in charge of safety in the mine. Nonetheless, when an accident occurred, the employer was held liable. The case confirmed that the employer’s duty of care to his employees was personal, and could not be delegated to a manager or safety advisor.

The case also determined that employers must provide:

  • a safe place of work and equipment
  • safe systems of work
  • reasonably competent co-workers.
