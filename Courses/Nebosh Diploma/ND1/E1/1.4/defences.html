Defences to claims of negligence

As discussed earlier, for the injured party (claimant) to succeed in a negligence claim, he must prove:

  1. that the defendant (usually the employer) owed him a duty of care
  2. that this duty was breached
  3. that the claimant suffered a legally recognisable loss as a result of the breach.

Therefore it can be seen that the defence would be successful if it can disprove any of the above.

No duty of care owed

An employer will not be liable for an injury if he does not owe a duty of care to the injured person.

Case law

In Hewett v Alf Brown’s Transport Ltd. (1992) the claimant was the wife of a lorry driver employed to transport lead oxide. When cleaning her husband’s work clothes she banged his overalls against a garden wall and banged dust off his boots. As a result of inhaling lead oxide dust she suffered from lead poisoning and sued her husband’s employers for personal injuries suffered.

The action was dismissed as the husbands occupational exposure did not put him at risk it was not foreseeable that the wife would be at risk.

It was accepted that if there was a foreseeable risk to the family of an employee from contaminated work clothing, etc. then the employers owed a duty of care to those family members.

No breach of the duty of care

The employer may deny that he has failed to take reasonable care and has done everything which a reasonable employer would have done in the circumstances.

An employer can only take reasonable care within the limits of the knowledge which he has, or ought reasonably to have.

Breach did not cause damage (causation)

Even if the defendant has been shown to have acted negligently, it must still be shown that the injury resulted from that negligence.

If the injury would have happened regardless of the defendants negligence, then the breach of the duty of care did not caused the damage.

The sole fault of the employee

If it can be shown that the injury was the sole fault of the employee, again the employer will not be liable.

Case law

In Jones v Lionite Specialities (Cardiff) Ltd. (1961) a foreman became addicted to a chemical vapour from a tank. One weekend he was found dead, having fallen into the tank.

The employers were not liable.

Volenti non fit injuria – ‘to a willing person, no injury is done’

In the 19th century, the courts held that workers accepted the risks which were inherent in their occupation and had to rely on their own skill and care to keep them safe.

Case law

In Smith v Baker and Sons (1891) the employer pleaded volenti against an employee injured by a falling rock whilst drilling rock in a cutting beneath a crane.

The court held that although the employee knew of the danger and continued to work, he had not voluntarily undertaken the risk of a stone falling from the crane and hitting him.

Awareness of a risk does not indicate acceptance of it.

Lord Herschell defined the employer’s duty of care to his employees as:

The duty of taking reasonable care to provide proper appliances and to maintain them in proper condition, and so to carry on his operations as not to expose those employed by him to unnecessary risk.

Fault of a third party

An employer will not be liable if the injury is caused by a new intervening act by a person or event over which or whom the employer has no control.

Contributory negligence

Contributory negligence is more a means of mitigating against the costs of paying damages, rather than a true defence.

The Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act 1945 provides that if a person is injured, partly because of his own fault, and partly due to the fault of another, damages should be reduced in proportion to the claimant’s share in the responsibility for the damage.

Employers often argue that even if they were negligent, the employee failed to take care for his own safety and that the damages awarded should be reduced accordingly.

There is no objective or scientific basis for determining the percentage reduction.

Case law

In Uddin v Associated Portland Cement Manufacturers Ltd. (1965) a machine minder lost an arm when he leaned across a revolving shaft, in an effort to retrieve a pigeon that had flown into the factory. He sued his employer for breach of statutory duty (not securely fencing a machine) under Section 14(1) of the Factories Act 1961.

It was held that the employer was liable, as the machinery should have been properly guarded, but the employee’s damages were reduced by 80% to account for his own contributory negligence.

Limitations Act 1980

The Limitation Act 1980 sets a time limit for starting proceedings for claiming compensation for personal injury.

This is primarily three years from the date of the negligence that caused the harm or, in the case of disease, three years from the date of diagnosis.

This primary limit may be extended in the following circumstances:

  • if the injured person is suffering from mental disability then the time limit does not start to run until mental capacity has returned
  • for minors (under 18 years of age on the date that they were injured) the three year period does not start running until the eighteenth birthday.

The courts also have discretionary powers to alter the time limits, but such discretion is rarely exercised.
