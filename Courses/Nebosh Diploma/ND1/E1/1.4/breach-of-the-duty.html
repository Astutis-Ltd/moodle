Breach of the duty of care

The basic rule is that the defendant must conform to the standard of care expected of a reasonable person.

In Blythe v Birmingham Waterworks (1856) it was held that:

Negligence is the omission to do something which a reasonable man guided upon those considerations which ordinarily regulate the conduct of human affairs, would do, or doing something which a reasonable and prudent man would not do.

The behaviour of an ordinary person, in ordinary circumstances, (not involving special skill or knowledge) was considered by the House of Lords in Glasgow Corporation v Muir (1943). Two important aspects of the test were highlighted:

  • the standard of foresight of the reasonable man is an impersonal, objective test, independent of the idiosyncrasies of the individual concerned. The actual defendant is judged by this abstract, impersonal standard, even though he may be more ignorant or stupid, or cleverer or more knowledgeable
  • it is down to the judge to determine what, in the circumstances of the particular case, the reasonable man would have had in contemplation. So there is room for diversity of views and outcomes are, to an extent, unpredictable.

Res ipsa loquitur – ‘the thing speaks for itself’

Normally the burden of establishing that the defendant has breached his duty of care rests with the claimant. However in some cases the factual chain of events established by the claimant is said to ‘speak for itself’, i.e. there is a presumption that the event could not have happened unless the defendant had been negligent.

The burden is then put on the defendant to explain the accident, and to show that there was no want of care on his part, i.e. the accident was caused by the fault of a third party.

Case law

Scott v London and St Katherine Docks Co. (1865) held that a bag of sugar that fell from a crane and hit the claimant could not have happened, unless the defendant was negligent.

Breach caused the loss / injury

The basic rule for determining whether or not a breach caused the loss may be stated positively or negatively:

  • if the damage would still have occurred, even if the defendant had not broken the duty of care, then the breach did not cause the damage
  • if the damage would not have occurred but for the defendant’s breach of duty, then the breach of duty is a cause of the damage.

The rule is also referred to as the ‘but for’ test. It is designed to exclude things that have no bearing on the damage.

It is for the claimant to show that the breach of duty was the cause of the damage, and not for the defendant to show that the breach of duty was not the cause of the damage.

Case law

In Corr v IBC (Vehicles) Ltd (2008) the House of Lords considered the remoteness of damage when Mr. Corr committed suicide as a consequence of severe physical and psychological injuries caused by an accident at work. It was held that the chain of causation from the accident to the physical and psychological injuries, to depression and ultimately suicide was uninterrupted.

In Fairchild v Glenhaven Funeral Services (2002) Mr Fairchild had worked for several contractors in his career, all of whom had negligently exposed him to asbestos fibres. Mr Fairchild then contracted pleural mesothelioma.

It was not possible to show which employer was responsible for exposing him to the single fibre which caused his illness. The ‘but for’ test was inadequate in the circumstances. The House of Lords approved a new test of ‘materially increasing risk’ to be used in such circumstances together with the notion of proportionate liability.
