The law of torts

The law of torts is concerned with the redress of injuries or other wrongs (not including breaches of contract) by means of a civil action by the victim, typically with the aim of obtaining financial compensation ‘damages’ for the losses incurred.

There are many torts including trespass, nuisance and defamation. Of particular relevance with regard to obtaining ‘damages’ for injuries arising out of work are:

  • the tort of negligence
  • the tort of breach of statutory duty.

Note: In Scottish law the term ‘delict’ is used instead of ‘tort’.

‘Double-barrelled’ action

As an injured employee (the claimant) is entitled to sue for damages under both negligence and Tort of Breach of Statutory Duty (TBSD), ‘double-barrelled’ actions have become commonplace. The claimant sues the employer (defendant) separately but simultaneously for both breaches. Note: only one award of damages will be made.

Case law

The first ‘double-barrelled’ action was in Kilgollan v. Cooke & Co. Ltd (1956).

Kilgollan was struck in the eye by a piece of wire ejected from a machine and sued his employer for negligence and for breaches of the fencing requirements for machinery under the Factories Act 1937.
