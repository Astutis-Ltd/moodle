Tort of breach of statutory duty

The courts have considered at length whether or not a person, injured as a consequence of the failure of another to perform a statutory duty, could bring a civil action based on that failure.

The courts have generally held that action can be taken for the tort of breach of statutory duty (TBSD) in relation to occupational health and safety legislation, although there are notable exceptions and it cannot be presumed that all breaches of statutory duty will be actionable in civil courts. This will depend upon the purposes and objects of the statute in question.

To bring a successful action under the Tort of Breach of Statutory Duty (TBSD) the claimant must show five things:

  1. that the statute permits a civil action for breach of statutory duty
  2. that he is within the class of person that the statute or regulations were designed to protect
  3. the breach is one against which the statute is designed to protect
  4. there was a failure to carry out that duty
  5. the damage or injury must actually be caused by the breach.

Statute bar

Section 47 of the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 states that:

Nothing in this Part shall be construed as conferring a right of action in any civil proceedings in respect of any failure to comply with any duty imposed by sections 2 to 7 or any contravention of section 8...

However failures to observe the requirements of health and safety regulations are actionable under TBSD, unless there is a specific exclusion of civil liability.

Regulation 22 of the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, as amended states that:

  • breach of a duty imposed by Regulation 16, 16A, 17 or 17A shall, so far as it causes damage, be actionable by the new or expectant mother
  • any term of an agreement which purports to exclude or restrict any liability for such a breach is void.

Regulation 45 of the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2007 (CDM) stated that:

Breach of a duty imposed by the preceding provisions of these Regulations, other than those imposed by Regulations 9(1)(b), 13(6) and (7), 16, 22(1)(c) and (l), 25(1), (2) and (4), 26 to 44 and Schedule 2, shall not confer a right of action in any civil proceedings insofar as that duty applies for the protection of a person who is not an employee of the person on whom the duty is placed.

Note: CDM 2015 does not confer any rights of civil action for any breaches of the Regulations.

Class of person

The claimant must show that he is of the class of person that the statute or regulation was designed to protect. Much health and safety legislation was introduced to protect employees, therefore visitors, contractors and members of the public etc. are often unable to bring an action.

Case law

In Ricketts v Torbay Council (2003) Mrs Ricketts tripped on an uneven surface in a council car park. She sued for damages for breach of Regulation 12 of the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations 1992 – floors and surfaces must be free from anything that may cause any person to slip, trip or fall.

The claim was dismissed by the Court of Appeal. ‘Any person’ was held to be any employee rather than any person legally entitled to be there.

In Reid v Galbraith’s Stores Ltd. (1970) it was held that the provisions of the Offices, Shops and Railways Premises Act 1963 were intended to protect persons at work, and did not apply to a customer who was visiting the shop.

In Donaldson v Hays Distribution Services Ltd. (2005) Mrs Donaldson was crushed between a reversing lorry and loading bay while looking for a goods collection point at the rear of a store.

Regulation 17 of the Workplace (HSW) Regulations was held not to afford Ms Donaldson any protection, as she was not an employee.

Statute was intended to protect against the breach

An action for Tort of Breach of Statutory Duty (TBSD) can only succeed if the breach is one against which the statute is designed to protect, and not some other unconnected risk.

Case law

In Corn v Weir Glass (Hanley) Ltd. (1960) a glazier was carrying a sheet of glass down a stairway 5 feet 6 inches wide, which had no guard rail, when he fell from a height of between two and three feet sustaining injuries.

The courts debated the meanings of guard rail and hand rail under Regulation 27 of the Building (Safety, Health and Welfare) Regulations 1948 and held there was no breach of statutory duty, as he would not have been able to use a hand rail and that the function of a hand rail was not to protect against falling as per a guard rail.

In Fytche v Wincanton Logistics plc (2004) the claimant was employed to drive a 32-tonne articulated bulk dairy tanker. In wintry conditions in December 1999 the tanker got stuck on an icy country road. Rather than follow company procedures to telephone and wait for help, Mr Fytche decided to dig the tanker out. After about three hours working in the snow he was able to drive off. One of Mr Fytches boots leaked through a tiny hole next to the little toe and he suffered frostbite as a result. He brought an action for damages, alleging a breach of Regulation 7 of the Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992.

His claim failed. The obligation imposed on an employer to maintain and repair personal protective equipment, under Regulation 7(1) of the Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992 related purely to the risk in relation to which the equipment was supplied.

The boots were provided to protect against the risk of injury to feet from a milk churn.

In Close v Steel Company of Wales (1962) a worker was injured by a piece of machinery that flew out of a machine. He brought an action for a breach of Section 14 of the Factories Act 1961 – the duty to fence dangerous parts of machinery.

The claim failed. Section 14 was intended to prevent the worker from coming into contact with the machine, not to stop parts of the machine from being thrown at the worker.

Failure to carry out the duty

If the statutory duty is an absolute duty there can be no defence for a breach, but if the duty is qualified it will be a question of fact in each case as to whether or not a defendant has complied with the relevant standard.

Case law

In Furness v Midland Bank plc (2000) the claimant (an employee) slipped and fell on water on a half-landing of a flight of internal stairs leading down to a basement area.

Only a few droplets of water were found after the accident and nobody could explain where it had come from, or how long it had been there. The stairs were used regularly and there was no history of leakage or spillages.

The claimant alleged a breach of the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations 1992, Regulation 12(3), which requires so far as is reasonably practicable, every floor in a workplace and the surface of every traffic route in a workplace shall be kept free from obstructions and from any article or substance which may cause a person to slip, trip or fall.

The claim was dismissed as the degree of risk arising from the spillage was very small and the only way to protect against it would have been totally disproportionate to the risks involved.

Damage or injury must be caused by the breach

If an injury is caused by the act of the injured employee, which would not have occurred if the employer had performed his statutory duties, then the employer will be liable for damages, unless the act of the employee was an unnatural and improbable consequence of the breach. This is known as the causation rule.

Case law

In Griffiths v Vauxhall Motors Ltd. (2003) it was held that if an accident occurs and no risk assessment has been carried out, it is reasonable to conclude that the breach was in some way responsible for the accident or injury.

In McWilliams v Sir William Arrol & Co Ltd. (1962), a steel erector fell seventy feet from a steel tower in the building of which he was assisting and was killed. The employers provided safety belts for steel erectors on site but as they were not being used, they were taken to another site. His widow sued for a breach of Section 26(2) of the Factories Act 1937 for failing to provide a safety belt.

The House of Lords held that the employer was not liable in damages because the breach of duty was not the cause of the damage suffered since (a) on the evidence the deceased would not have worn a safety belt if it had been provided, and (b) there was no duty on the employers to instruct or exhort the deceased to wear a safety belt.

The recent ‘pleural plaques’ cases (for example, Grieves v FT Everard and Sons (2005)) raised an interesting point regarding what constitutes actionable damage. The presence of pleural plaques (areas of scar tissues on the membrane surrounding the lung) did not, by itself, justify an award of compensation as pleural plaques are symptomless and have no adverse effect on the body. The fact of the potential for future damage also failed to justify an award.

Case law

In Corn v Weir’s Glass (Hanley) Ltd. (1960) Mr Corn was a glazier who fell off a stairwell while carrying a pane of glass.

The case has an interesting discussion regarding the difference between hand rails and guard rails and whether or not the presence of the hand rail would have prevented the accident.
