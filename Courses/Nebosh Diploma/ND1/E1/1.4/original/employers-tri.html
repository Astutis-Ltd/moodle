Employers tri-partite duty to employees


In Wilsons and Clyde Coal Company v English (1938) it was established that the employer’s duty to take reasonable care of their employees may be broken down into three general categories.

1)
Safe plant, appliances and premises

2)
Safe system of work

3)
Reasonably competent fellow employees.



Safe plant, appliances and premises

All tools, equipment, machinery and plant must be reasonably safe for work.

Failures to provide necessary, sufficient, or effective equipment have been held to be breaches of the duty.

Case law

In Bradford v Robinson Rentals Ltd. (1967) a driver suffered frostbite as a consequence of a 400 mile winter journey in a van with no heating and defective windows.

The employers were held liable for failing to provide suitable plant.

In Davie v New Merton Board Mills Ltd. (1958) an employer who purchased tools from a reputable supplier and has no knowledge of any defect in them was held to have performed his duty to take care and was not liable for negligence.



The Employers’ Liability (Defective Equipment) Act

Section 1(1) of the Employer’s Liability (Defective Equipment) Act 1969 reversed the decision of the House of Lords in Davie v New Merton Board Mills (1959) to make an employer liable if an employee suffers personal injury in the course of his employment in consequence of a defect in equipment provided by the employer.

Section 1(1) states that where...

‣
an employee suffers personal injury in the course of his employment in consequence of a defect in equipment provided by his employer for the purposes of the employer’s business, and

‣
the defect is attributable wholly or partly to the fault of a third party (whether identified or not).


the injury shall be deemed to be also attributable to negligence on the part of the employer (whether or not he is liable in respect of the injury apart from this subsection).

Equipment is defined as including: any plant and machinery, vehicle, aircraft and clothing.

Note: the Employers Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969 was introduced at the same time to ensure that the facility to compensate an injured employee was in place.

The employer (most likely the insurance company) will then pursue the manufacturer through the courts in a counter claim. If the claimant has contributed to the cause of the accident and injury, a claim of contributory negligence may also be made.


Safe system of work

The employer is responsible for the overall planning of work operations to ensure they can be carried out safely. This includes: the layout of the work, the systems laid down, training and supervision, the provision of warnings, and protective clothing and equipment.

Consideration must be given to an employee’s forgetfulness, carelessness, or inadvertence, but the employer is not expected to guard against outright stupidity or perversity.

Case law

In Barcock v Brighton Corporation (1949) the claimant was employed at an electric substation. A certain method of testing was in operation which was unsafe and, in consequence, the employee was injured.The employers were held liable for failing to provide suitable plant.

The employers were held liable.


The more dangerous the process, the greater is the need to ensure that it is safe. On the other hand, the employer cannot be expected to take over-elaborate precautions when dealing with simple and obvious dangers.

Whether the employers duty to provide is passive (provide only) or active (compel use) has been extensively considered by the courts. The position can be explained through four propositions:

‣
Obvious risk / not serious injury –
In Qualcast (Wolverhampton) Ltd. v Haynes (1959) it was held that if the risk is an obvious one, and the injury which may result from the failure to use the precautions is not likely to be serious, then the employer’s duty is a mere passive one of providing the necessary precautions, informing the employees of their presence, and leaving it to them to decide whether or not to use them

‣
Risk of serious injury –
The employer’s duty is a higher one of doing all he can to ensure that the precautions are used

‣
Insidious risk / seriousness not obvious –
The employer’s duty extends to provision of propaganda, education, constant reminders, etc. to get the employees to use the precautions

‣
Employer has done all he can do –
Where the employer has laid down a safe system, provided the necessary safety precautions and equipment, instructed on their use, given regular reminders etc. and can do no more he will be absolved from liability.



Reasonably competent fellow employees

If an employer engages an incompetent employee whose actions injure another employee, the employer will be liable for a failure to take reasonable care.

Case law

In Hudson v Ridge Manufacturing Co. Ltd. (1957) an employee who was well known for committing practical jokes went too far and injured a fellow employee. The employers were held liable.

In Smith v Crossley Brothers (1951) an apprentice at a training school was injured by a practical joke played by two other apprentices.

The employer was not held liable, as the injury occurred through an act of wilful misbehaviour that could not have reasonably been foreseen.

It was held in Butler v Fife Coal Co. (1912) that an employer may be liable where he appoints an inexperienced person to perform highly dangerous tasks, and another employee is injured as a result of the first employee’s lack of experience.
