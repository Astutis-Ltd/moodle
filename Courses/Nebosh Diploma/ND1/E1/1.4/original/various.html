Vicarious liability


The tort of vicarious liability arises when one person (who has not committed a wrongful act) is legally liable for the acts of another person, which cause injury or damage to a third person.

Vicarious liability arises because the actual wrongdoer (a co-worker, for example) is unlikely to have sufficient financial resources to meet any claim, and so the injured party will seek to make the first person (the employer, for example) vicariously liable.

The general principle of an employer’s vicarious liability is that the employer is liable for the acts of his employee which injure a third party if the employee, when doing the act, was acting in the course of his employment.

Vicarious liability has been shown to exist even where the employer has expressly prohibited an employee from doing the act in question (Rose v Plenty (1976)).

In Lister v Hesley Hall Ltd. (2002), a sex abuse case in a children’s home, a new test was formulated to address events where an employee commits an unauthorised or prohibited act.

The test asks: Were the acts so closely connected with the employment that it would be fair and just to hold the employer liable?

Consequently earlier cases defended on the basis that the employee was on a frolic of his own must be treated with caution if there is a degree of connection with the employment. Vicarious liability is now appearing in a number of different areas of employment law, including sex discrimination and race discrimination.

An employer is not generally liable for the wrongful acts of an independent contractor. However, an employer may be liable if he:

‣
authorises the act

‣
has some control over the hazardous activity the contractor is undertaking

‣
coordinates or controls the activities of a number of independent contractors

‣
fails to show that he has exercised reasonable care in the selection of a competent contractor.



Case law

In the case of Mersey Docks and Harbour Board v Coggins and Griffith (Liverpool) Ltd. (1946) a stevedore company, Coggins and Griffith, hired a crane with its driver from Mersey Docks and Harbour Board. The nature of the contract was such that the driver was the servant of Coggins and Griffith, and would be instructed by them but Mersey Docks and Harbour Board continued to pay his wages and had the power to dismiss him.

The House of Lords was required to determine which of the two parties could be held vicariously liable for the negligent act of the crane driver, which injured a third party.

The House of Lords held that Mersey Docks and Harbour Board remained vicariously liable for the crane driver’s negligence. He was using his own discretion in operating the crane and this was deemed to have been delegated to him by Mersey Docks and Harbour Board.

The power to control the method of performing the work had not been transferred to Coggins and Griffith, and Coggins and Griffith had no control over the way he operated the crane.

In Viasystems (Tyneside) Ltd v Thermal Transfer (Northern) Ltd and others (2005) the Court of Appeal held that dual vicarious liability can apply in an employment situation. Viasystems suffered flood damage when a fitters mate (Mr. Strang) installing air conditioning ducting negligently activated the fire protection sprinkler system. Viasystems had contracted Thermal Transfer to install the air conditioning and Thermal Transfer subcontracted to Darwell who subcontracted CAT to provide labourers, fitters and fitters mates. Mr Strang was receiving instructions from both a CAT fitter (Mr. Megson) and a Darwell supervisor (Mr Horsley). CAT and Darwell were found to be jointly vicariously liable.
