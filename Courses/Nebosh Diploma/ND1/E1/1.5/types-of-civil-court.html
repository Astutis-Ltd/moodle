Types of civil court

County Court

The County Court deals with a range of civil cases including businesses trying to recover money they are owed, landowners seeking orders that will prevent trespass and as is relevant here – individuals seeking compensation for injuries.

Most County Courts centres are assigned at least one circuit judge and one district judge. Circuit judges generally hear cases worth over £25 000 or involving greater importance or complexity. They also hear many of the cases worth over £10 000 but not over £25 000.

The vast majority of civil cases tried in court do not have a jury and the judge hears them on his own, deciding them by finding facts, applying the relevant law to them, and then giving a reasoned judgment.

Judges also play an active role in managing civil cases once they have started, helping to ensure they proceed as quickly and efficiently as possible by:

  • encouraging the parties to co-operate with each other in the conduct of the case
  • helping the parties to settle the case
  • encouraging the parties to use an alternative dispute resolution procedure if appropriate
  • controlling the progress of the case.

Once the judge has heard the evidence from all parties involved and any submissions (representations) they wish to put forward, he delivers his judgment. This may be immediately, or if the case is complicated, at a later date.

Generally, civil cases do not involve the imposition of any punishment. However, anyone who does not comply with the court’s judgment can be arrested and prosecuted for contempt of court.

    Circuit judges are appointed to one of seven regions of England and Wales, and sit in the Crown Court and County Court within their particular region.

    District judges are full-time judges who deal with the majority of cases in the County Court. They are assigned on appointment to a particular circuit and may sit at any of the County Courts centres or district registries of the High Court on that circuit.

Court of Appeal

The Court of Appeal is presided over by the Master of the Rolls who sits with two other Lord Justices of Appeal and receives appeals (on points of law only) from:

  • Queen’s Bench (civil matters)
  • Queen’s Bench Divisional Court (criminal matters)
  • Crown Court
  • County Court
  • Employment Appeal Tribunal (EAT).

Supreme Court

The Constitutional Reform Act 2005 (CRA) was introduced to totally separate the powers and functions of politicians and judges, and create a judicial system which is totally independent in practice as well as in theory.

The CRA abolished the appellate functions of the House of Lords and replaced it with the Supreme Court, which consists of 12 senior judges appointed by Her Majesty. The first members of the Supreme Court were those Lords of Appeal in Ordinary who previously sat in the House of Lords.

Legal rulings given by the former House of Lords remain effective.

Employment tribunals

Industrial tribunals were created in 1964 to provide:

…an easily accessible, speedy, informal and inexpensive procedure for the settlement of disputes between employers and workers.

Tribunal proceedings are generally less formal than courts, in that any person may represent a party before an employment tribunal, and costs are not normally awarded to the winner.

They were renamed as employment tribunals by the Employment Rights (Dispute Resolution) Act 1998.

Employment tribunals are arranged on a regional basis and each comprises:

  • an independent Chairman (legally qualified and appointed from a panel drawn up by the Lord Chancellor)
  • two lay members, one appointed from a panel representing employers, and the other representing the workers.

In the health and safety context employment tribunals have three important functions, namely to:

  • hear appeals against the decision of an inspector to impose a prohibition or improvement notice*
  • hear claims for unfair dismissal, sex, race, age and disability discrimination
  • hear other claims arising out of the Employment Rights Act 1996 (for example, refusal to give time off work to safety representatives, failure to pay safety representatives for time off work, protection in health and safety cases, and refusal to pay medical or maternity suspension pay).

Decisions of employment tribunals do not constitute legal precedents though they are valued for their reasoning and guidance.

*Note: an appeal against a decision of an employment tribunal on a point of law relating to cases concerning health and safety enforcement notices will go to the Queen’s Bench Divisional Court, but appeals on all other matters will go to the Employment Appeal Tribunal.

Employment Appeal Tribunal (EAT)

The main function of the Employment Appeal Tribunal (EAT) is to hear appeals on points of law from the decisions of employment tribunals. The EAT comprises a High Court judge and two lay members, one from a panel representing employers, and the other representing the workers.

Procedures are relatively informal. Legal representation is not insisted upon, costs are not normally awarded to the winner, and the procedure is designed to hear cases in a quick and straightforward manner.

Appeals from the Employment Appeal Tribunal are to the court of appeal.
