Joint tortfeasors

A tortfeasor is a person who has committed a tort. Where a claimant has suffered damage at the hands of more than one tortfeasor, liability may be shared and action taken against joint tortfeasors.

Joint tortfeasors are jointly responsible to the same claimant for causing him the same damage, and share liability on the same facts.

In such cases each tortfeasor could be sued for the full amount of the loss, although the claimant cannot recover damages more than once. Where the proportion of fault can be determined, the claim may be enforced against either tortfeasor, who can then claim a contribution from the other to the extent of the other’s liability under the Civil Liability (Contribution) Act 1978.

Case law

In Lister v Romford Ice and Cold Storage Co. Ltd. (1957) a lorry driver, employed by the defendants, negligently reversed a company vehicle into a fellow employee (his father). The injured employee was successful in his claim and was awarded damages. After paying the claim the defendant’s insurance company subsequently successfully sued the lorry driver in the name of the company to recover what they had paid out.

The courts held that an employee can be sued instead of, or in addition to, the employer where he has been negligent.

Where the same damage is caused by two or more tortfeasors who are not acting jointly (separate and independent tortfeasors) they would be sued separately.

Where multiple tortfeasors independently inflict different items of damage on the same claimant (independent or ‘several’ tortfeasors) liabilities are separate, and each tortfeasor is only responsible for his own breach and the resultant harm caused.
