Damages

Damages or compensation for injury or loss are intended to put the claimant in the position they would have been in had they not suffered the loss. The claimant should not profit as a result of his loss.

In determining the monetary value of the compensation claim two elements, termed special and general damages, are considered:

Special damages financially compensate the claimant for losses suffered due to the defendant’s actions. They are ‘out of pocket’ expenses and directly quantifiable financial losses such as:

  • loss of earnings
  • the costs of replacing damaged property
  • medical expenses
  • expenses for assistance and facilities needed as a result of the injury.

Special damages cover losses incurred before the date of the trial and are calculated by the claimant’s legal team.

In Scottish law the term ‘patrimonial damages’ is used in place of ‘special damages’ to cover financial losses.

General damages flow naturally from the defendant’s wrongful action. There is a clear link between the defendant’s behaviour and the plaintiff’s injury. General damages are unliquidated which generally means that it is for the court to assign a monetary value to the injury suffered (the claimant and defendant may agree a figure subject to determination of liability).

General damages can include:

  • physical pain and suffering
  • physical disfigurement
  • physical impairment
  • loss of amenity or facility as a consequence of the injury
  • mental anguish
  • loss of companionship (paid to family members in wrongful death cases)
  • lowered qualify of life.

In Scottish law the term ‘solatium’ is used instead of ‘general damages’ to address damages awarded for pain and suffering etc. they may also be referred to as non-patrimonial damages.
