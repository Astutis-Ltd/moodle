Occupational circumstances that can impact mental ill-health

The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted numerous concerns about people’s mental health and wellbeing at work. The fear and uncertainty about the risk of infection and the impact of lockdown restrictions on day-to-day activities leading to job insecurity and isolation has made many anxious or depressed.

Fatigue is a feeling of constant tiredness or weakness that is typically caused by a combination of lifestyle, social, psychological and general wellbeing issues. Excessive fatigue slows reaction times, impairs memory and negatively affects mood, often leading to irritability and anger. Shift-work and unsustainable workplace stress are both known to cause fatigue and mental ill-health such as depression. The relationship between fatigue and depression is such that it is often difficult to determine which causes the other.

Chronic pain and mental ill-health also commonly co-exist. Research has shown that people suffering with chronic pain can become depressed and also that long-term depression can give rise to persistent pain.

Other considerations include violence, aggression, bullying and harassment which are discussed in Section 2.3 of this element and lone working which is discussed in Section 2.4.

Work-related stress

Work-related stress is defined as a harmful reaction that people have to undue pressures and demands placed on them at work. By its very nature, stress is difficult to measure.

HSE’s preferred data source for calculating rates and estimates for work-related stress, depression or anxiety are self-reports from the Labour Force Survey (LFS).

Historically the HSE also collected data on work-related stress through The Health and Occupation Research network for general practitioners (THOR-GP). These data, although historic, provide a general practitioners perspective and still useful data on work-related causes of stress. The two data sources may reflect different perceptions of work-related attribution to individual cases.

In the UK in 2020 / 21 there were an estimated 822 000 workers affected by work-related stress, depression or anxiety, a prevalence of 2 480 per 100 000 workers.

Labour Force Survey (LFS)

The LFS is a survey of households living at private addresses in the UK is to provide information on the UK labour market which can then be used to develop, manage, evaluate and report on labour market policies. The survey is managed by the Office for National Statistics in Great Britain.

The LFS is intended to be representative of the whole population of the UK, and the sample design currently consists of around 37 000 responding households in every quarter. The quarterly survey has a panel design whereby households stay in the sample for five consecutive quarters (or waves), with a fifth of the sample replaced each quarter. There is an 80% overlap in the samples for each successive survey.

In recent years prior to the coronavirus pandemic, the rate of self-reported work-related stress, depression or anxiety had shown signs of increasing and by 2020 / 21 accounted for 50% of all work-related ill health. The figures in 2021 plateaued and were not statistically different to the previous year (see image below). Evidence suggests this is not related to COVID-19.

The LFS analysis shows that in 2020 / 21 stress, depression or anxiety was most prevalent in education and human health / social work activities.

Higher than average rate of stress, depression or anxiety were found in professional occupations, notably amongst:

  • Health professionals
  • Teaching and other educational professionals.

The main causal factors cited by respondents were tight deadlines, too much responsibility and a lack of managerial support.

Although now dated, the THOR(GP) analysis from 2012 to 2015 has the benefit of GP insight following consultation. The five key causal factors of work-related stress and mental ill-health, as identified by GP’s were:

  1. Factors intrinsic to the job, such as scheduling, shift work and other organisational factors
  2. Interpersonal relationships including difficulties with superiors and bullying or harassment
  3. Changes at work including reduction of resource or staff and additional responsibilities
  4. Personal development considerations such as frustration at lack of career progress
  5. Traumatic events e.g. witnessing serious workplace accidents.
