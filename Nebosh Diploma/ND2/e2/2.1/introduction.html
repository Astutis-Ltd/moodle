Introduction

The World Health Organisation (WHO) has described mental health as:

“a state of wellbeing in which every individual realises his or her own potential, can cope with the normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully, and is able to make a contribution to her or his community”

Everyone has mental health, just as everyone has physical health. Both change throughout life, and like bodies, minds can become less healthy.

Mental health and mental illness are not the same thing. Mental health refers to emotional and psychological wellbeing. It is influenced by life experiences, and can impact the way an individual thinks, feels and behaves.

Mental illness encompasses a wide range of disorders often caused by genetics or brain chemistry. Each mental illness has different symptoms and can impact an individual's life differently. Mental illnesses are recognisable conditions that can be professionally diagnosed and treated.

Mental health problems are common with one person in four in the UK experiencing a mental health problem at some point in their life. Most mental health problems are mild with anxiety and depression being the most common.

Anxiety is generally described as an unpleasant feeling when worried, uneasy or distressed about something that may or may not be about to happen whereas depression is having feelings of extreme sadness, despair or inadequacy that last for a long time.

Often anxiety and / or depression are experienced as a reaction to a difficult life event such as moving house, bereavement, or problems at work.

Work related stress and Common Mental Health Problems (CMHP) such as anxiety and depression often go together. Work related stress may trigger an existing mental health problem or cause it to worsen.

Stress and CMHP can also exist independently. Many people experience the physiological changes such as high blood pressure that are associated with work related stress without ever experiencing anxiety and depression. Others may be anxious or depressed without ever experiencing work related (or other) stress.

In practice, it can be hard to distinguish when 'stress' turns into a 'mental health problem' and when existing mental health problems become exacerbated by stress at work.

Generally diagnosis and treatment through medication and / or talking therapies will be through a General Practitioner (GP). Occasionally sufferers may need to be referred for specialist help.

The majority of people with mental health problems are capable of continuing to work productively. Evidence shows that employment can be of great benefit, both to the employer and to the employee.

The mind is a 'suggestion engine' coming up with around 60 000 thoughts per day, or one thought every 1.5 seconds. Around 98% of these thoughts are repeated thoughts.

Repeated thoughts can change our perceptions, attitudes and beliefs which in turn influences our feelings and behaviours.

Mentally healthy people are able to properly filter through these thoughts and discard the bad suggestions by flexible thinking / cognitive shifting / having a change mindset that allows them to adapt to circumstances, change perspective and stay current and balanced.

Mentally unhealthy people tend to have inflexible minds / fixed mindsets and are prone to cognitive distortions or suppressive styles of thinking such as worry, rumination and problems.

Healthy people are able to navigate themselves around and through negative thoughts whereas unhealthy people tend to get 'bogged down'. Everyone has the power to choose which thoughts or suggestions to follow and there are therapies available to help people struggling with suppressive or negative thoughts. As Dalai Lama XIV said:

“If a problem is fixable, if a situation is such that you can do something about it, then there is no need to worry. If it's not fixable, then there is no help in worrying. There is no benefit in worrying whatsoever.”

The table below shows a range of common cognitive distortions and how they relate to anxiety and depression.

Anxiety (Future based) Depression (Past based)

All or nothing

  • Tendency to evaluate ourselves in terms of absolutes and nothing in between. This leads to worry about things not being perfect.

Mental filter

  • The tendency to focus on one negative aspect of a situation while ignoring all other positive evidence.

Overgeneralisation

  • Believing that because something bad happened once, it will happen over and over and over.

Disqualifying the positive

  • More destructive than mind-filtering, this involves taking a positive experience and turning it into a completely negative one.

Jumping to conclusions

  • Automatically jumping to negative conclusions without any basis for it, e.g. “I'll never be able to catch up”.

Magnification and minimisation

  • The tendency to magnify mistakes and weaknesses while minimising successes and strengths.

Personalisation

  • Feeling responsible and guilty when there is no reason for it.

Emotional reasoning

  • Using feelings / emotions rather than thoughts to make decisions.

Should statements

  • Dwelling on what we could have, should have, or would have done differently.

Labelling and mislabelling

  • Labelling and mislabelling of ourselves in a self-deprecating manner, such as “I'm useless” can lead to self-loathing.
