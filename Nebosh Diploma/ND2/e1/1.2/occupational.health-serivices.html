Ocupational health services

Depending on the scale of an organisation and the complexity of its activities there are a range of services that could be offered by an occupational health service e.g.:

  • Medical surveillance, health surveillance and biological monitoring (see Element 2)
  • Workplace health promotion providing advice on healthy diet, exercise, alcohol and substance abuse, and smoking cessation etc.
  • Health assessment services (fitness to work and pre-placement assessments as discussed in this element)
  • Management advice – contributing to health and safety policies, providing specialist input to risk assessments and health education and training programmes
  • Sickness management – contributing to policy, carrying out sickness and absence monitoring and keeping health records
  • Treatment services such as first-aid provision, physiotherapy and psychotherapy services
  • Immunisation services for protection against work-related illness, international travel etc
  • Confidential counselling services / Employment Assistance Programmes (EAP)
  • Rehabilitation services to help staff return to productive work
  • Employee wellbeing programmes (see Element 2).

Other possible functions, depending on the organisation and its activities, might include immunisation, drug and alcohol screening and involvement with environmental monitoring programmes.

The benefits of occupational health services

Poor management of workplace health can lead to work-related ill health and to high levels of sickness absence.

The associated costs and the impact on service delivery makes sickness absence an important business issue, and a key indicator of how well an organisation is managed.

Healthy, well-motivated workers are vital to the delivery of high-quality services. An effective occupational health service has a key role in achieving this.
