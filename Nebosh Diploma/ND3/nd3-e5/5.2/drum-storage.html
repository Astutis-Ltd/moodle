Drum storage

The principal objectives when establishing a storage compound for drums of highly flammable/explosive materials (such as nitrocellulose) are:

  1. to minimise the risk of a fire occurring in the storage area itself
  2. to protect the store from fires occurring elsewhere
  3. to mitigate the consequences of such a fire, particularly with regard to people.

Example: Nitrocellulose

About nitrocellulose

Industrial nitrocellulose (other than water wetted) is a considerable fire hazard. It is easily ignited and burns fiercely, emitting large quantities of heat and toxic fumes. It may explode if incorrectly packaged and stored in a confined space.

The burning rate depends on the type and quantity of desensitizing agent present:

  • Alcohol wetted grades initially burn at the rate of the alcohol, but as burning proceeds, the fire becomes fiercer
  • Wet grades (water wetted) are relatively insensitive to heat and not immediately affected by fire, but once partly or completely dried out produce bigger, hotter fires
  • Plasticised grades burn much faster and hotter than solvent wetted grades, and are more susceptible to ignition from frictional heating.

The desensitizing agent must not be allowed to evaporate because the nitrocellulose becomes increasingly more heat and friction sensitive, and potentially more dangerous to handle, as it dries out.

The type of packaging may also influence the fire behaviour:

  • Steel drums may vent their contents violently, causing short duration fireballs. Drums and lids may be projected considerable distances, particularly if venting occurs with the drum in a horizontal position
  • Nitrocellulose in fibreboard drums burns more evenly than when packed in steel drums, the dimensions of the fire and duration of burning relating fairly closely to the quantity stored and the stack configuration. Plasticised material may be violently ejected, even from fibreboard drums. The main effect of violent ejection is to increase the flame width of the fire around the stack.

All grades of nitrocellulose are incompatible with strongly alkaline or strongly acidic materials and oxidising agents. Contamination by any of these substances could result in decomposition and ignition of the nitrocellulose.
