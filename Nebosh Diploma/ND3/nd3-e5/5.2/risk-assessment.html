Risk assessment of dangerous substances

Introduction

Regulation 5 of DSEAR requires the employer to make a ‘suitable and sufficient’ assessment of the risks to their employees arising from a dangerous substance in the workplace.

No new work activity involving a dangerous substance can commence unless an assessment has been made, and the measures required by these Regulations have been implemented.

The risk assessment must be reviewed to keep it up to date, particularly if there are grounds to suspect that the risk assessment is no longer valid, or there has been a significant change in the workplace, work processes, or organisation of the work.

Hazards presented and assessment of risk

The recommended risk assessment process in the guidance to the Dangerous Substances and Explosive Atmospheres Regulations (DSEAR) follows the standard HSE ‘5 steps’ approach to risk assessment.

This section concentrates on the hazard identification and risk evaluation stages of the process.

Hazard identification (determination of the presence of dangerous substances) requires consideration of the following three variables:

  1. Substance or mixture which meets the criteria for classification as hazardous within any hazard class laid down in the CLP Regulation
  2. The physical and chemical properties of the substance or preparation, and the work processes involved, creates a potential for fire, explosion or similar energetic (energy releasing) event
  3. The work activity involves the creation or handling of potentially combustible or explosive dusts.

Risk is the chance, high or low, that someone will be harmed by the hazard. The definition of ‘risk’ in DSEAR builds on the definition of hazard and ensures that the risks addressed are those created by the physical and chemical properties of substances and preparations which can lead to fires, explosions and similar energetic events, and the harmful consequences of those events.

There are a number of factors that should be taken into account when undertaking a dangerous substances risk assessment. These factors are detailed over the next few pages.

The hazardous properties of the substance

Consideration should be given to the flammable or explosive properties of substances, which are:

  • brought into the workplace and handled, stored and used for processing
  • produced or given off as fumes, vapour, dust by a process or activity, or as a result of an incident or accident
  • used for, or arise from maintenance, cleaning, and repair work
  • produced as a by-product of any work or process, for example, waste, residues, scrap materials, etc.
  • naturally occurring in the workplace, such as methane in tunnelling and mining operations.

Supplier safety information

Consideration should be given to safety data sheets information on:

  • flashpoints or explosive or chemical properties
  • safe methods of using, storing and handling the substances.

The circumstances of the work

Careful consideration should be given to:

  • the work processes
  • the amounts of those substances present
  • arrangements for their handling, storage and transportation
  • risks presented by substances in combination.

Attention should also be paid to potential hazards arising from equipment used in processing or handling dangerous substances, including equipment that may be brought into an area where dangerous substances are present.

Work processes include:

  • technical means of supervision
  • connecting devices
  • control and protection systems
  • engineering controls and solutions
  • equipment
  • materials
  • machinery
  • plant
  • protective systems
  • warning and other communication systems.

The following should also be addressed:

  • The potential for dangerous substances to be released (either intentionally or unintentionally) from plant/equipment, or during handling
  • The potential for explosive atmospheres to form
  • The likelihood of unintentional ignition sources from equipment, etc.
  • The effectiveness of the plant/equipment to mitigate the effects of an incident.

Particular activities, which may present a high level of risk

Maintenance and repair activities such as dismantling equipment containing dangerous substances may expose workers to risks from dangerous substances and require specific safety procedures.

The likely presence of explosive atmospheres and the need for hazardous area classification

Hazardous area classification should be undertaken as an integral part of the risk assessment, to identify places where controls over ignition sources are needed and those places where they are not.

The results of the classification are used to control the equipment that may be used, or the work activities that may be carried out in hazardous areas so as to prevent ignition.
