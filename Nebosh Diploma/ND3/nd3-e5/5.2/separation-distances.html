Separation distances

Separation distances will depend on various factors, most important of which is the capacity of the tank. Guidance distinguishes between ‘small’ tanks used in small to medium chemical processes, and ‘large’ tanks associated with refinery and other large-scale storage facilities.

Separation distances are unlikely to give complete protection in the event of a tank fire or explosion, but should allow sufficient time for people to be evacuated, and for additional fire- fighting equipment and emergency procedures to be mobilised.

As an example, a large tank should be at least 16 metres away from the site boundary, any designated non-hazardous area, process area, or any fixed source of ignition.

Underground tanks and pipework

Underground tanks afford benefits over above ground tanks, in that the soil provides impact and fire protection, space is saved and separation distances are reduced. Disadvantages include the increased risk of corrosion, difficulty in detecting leaks, soil contamination, and the need for confined space access or excavation for all inspection and maintenance.

Vents

Tank pressure will vary during normal operation. Pressure may increase during filling, or if the ambient temperature rises. Pressure may drop during emptying or with temperature falls. The tank venting system should provide:

  • normal pressure relief
  • normal vacuum relief
  • emergency pressure relief.

Traditionally vents discharged into the atmosphere, but current environmental legislation often requires vapour emission controls. Now, for example, vapour recovery systems are required for petrol storage installations.

Fire protection

Additional fire protection may be necessary where the storage conditions are less than ideal, such as where it is difficult to achieve adequate separation distances. Measures might include:

  • fire resistant claddings or coatings
  • fire walls
  • water cooling systems
  • foam blankets or extinguishing systems.

Bonding and earthing

To minimise the accumulation of electrostatic charge and prevent incendive sparks, all metal parts of the storage installation should be bonded together and earthed.

Bunding

The probability of a major leak from a well-designed and maintained storage system is low, particularly if overspill protection has been fitted. However, as the consequences of a spillage of flammable liquid are potentially catastrophic, additional containment measures are essential.

Liquid tight bunding should be provided for all flammable liquids with a flashpoint of 55°C or below, and for materials which are stored at temperatures above their flashpoints.

The bund should have sufficient capacity to contain the largest predictable spillage. A bund capacity of 110% of the capacity of the largest storage vessel located within the bund will normally be sufficient.

Individual bunding is to be preferred to common bunding, particularly for large tanks.

Smaller capacity bunds may be acceptable, where liquid can be directed to a separate evaporation area or impounding basin.

Bunding should:

  • prevent the flammable liquid or vapour from reaching ignition sources
  • prevent the liquid entering the drainage or water systems where it may spread to uncontrolled ignition sources
  • allow the controlled recovery or treatment of the spilled material
  • minimise the surface area of the liquid and so reduce the size of any fire that may occur
  • prevent the spread of burning liquids which could present a hazard to other plant or personnel both on and off site
  • prevent contamination of land and water courses.

Intermediate Bulk Containers (IBCs)

Originally Intermediate Bulk Containers (IBCs) were intended for transport purposes only. Nowadays the use of IBCs for storing flammable liquids has significantly increased and they are commonly found in chemical warehouses.

The storage of flammable liquids in IBCs in warehouses poses particular problems, notably:

  • they are prone to early failure in a fire, allowing leaked contents to feed the fire
  • they degrade over time, and so are prone to leaks when used for long term storage
  • they are generally made of cheap, non-conductive plastic, so when non-conducting flammable contents splash around in transit the surface becomes electrostatically charged.
