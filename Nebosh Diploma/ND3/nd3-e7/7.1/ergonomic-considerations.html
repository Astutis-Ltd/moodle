Ergonomic considerations

When selecting work equipment, employers should take account of ergonomic risks.

Ergonomics is concerned with the interaction of humans and other elements of a system, and the design of such systems in order to optimise human well-being and overall system performance.

This involves designing the system to fit the human and to compensate for physiological or psychological weaknesses.

Anthropometric principles should be applied to ensure that operating positions, working heights, reach distances, etc. accommodate the intended operators.

Biomechanical and physiological analysis ensures that operators are not expected to exert undue force or stretch or reach beyond their normal strength or physical limitations. This is particularly important for highly repetitive work.

The general principles of the Essential Health and Safety Requirements (EHSRs) in Annexe 1 of the Machinery Directive (as presented in Schedule 2 of the Supply of Machinery (Safety) Regulations 2008 – SMSR) state that when machinery is being designed, there are a number of principles that should be taken into account, depending on the results of the risk assessment.

Under the intended conditions of use, the discomfort, fatigue and physical and psychological stress faced by the operator must be reduced to the minimum possible, taking into account ergonomic principles such as:

  • allowing for the variability of the operator’s physical dimensions, strength and stamina
  • providing enough space for movements of the parts of the operator’s body
  • avoiding a machine-determined work rate
  • avoiding monitoring that requires lengthy concentration
  • adapting the man / machinery interface to the foreseeable characteristics of the operators.
