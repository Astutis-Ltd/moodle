Assessment of risks

The Management of health and Safety at Work Regulations requires a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risks for all work activities for the purpose of deciding what measures are necessary for safety. For work in confined spaces this means identifying the hazards present, assessing the risks and determining what precautions to include in the safe system of work.

The key factors to include in the assessment of the risks associated with a confined space entry include:

  • Access arrangements
  • Likely atmospheres
  • The task to be undertaken
  • Materials, equipment and substances to be used
  • People at risk
  • The reliability of control measures, including PPE.

Access arrangements

Where confined spaces are designed and constructed the designer should ensure that the need to enter confined spaces both during normal work, or for cleaning and maintenance is eliminated where possible or otherwise minimised.

Where entry is required the design should incorporate easy access, taking account of requirements in the event of emergencies. Where possible access points should be sited at the bottom or low down in the structure. Where working at height cannot be avoided, the provision of safe means of access such as ladders, and safe working platforms must be carefully addressed.

The size of access point openings should be sufficiently large and free from obstruction to allow the passage of people wearing the necessary protective clothing and equipment, and to allow adequate access for rescue purposes.

Likely atmospheres

A lack of oxygen due to chemical reactions or displacement with other gases presents a risk of oxygen deprivation and ultimately asphyxiation.

Too much oxygen, e.g. from cylinder leaks or chemical reactions presents risks of oxidation reactions, fire or explosions.

Other chemical gases, fumes and vapours may present risk of chemical asphyxiation or have other toxic effects or present physico-chemical hazards of increased flammability or explosibility.

The task to be undertaken

Hazards that arise directly from the work to be undertaken in the confined space must be carefully risk assessed. The work itself may introduce the hazard into the confined space, e.g. use of cleaning chemicals, introduction of ignition sources or increased temperatures from hot-work on the internal or external surfaces of the confined space. Other hazards such as noise may be exacerbated within a confined space.

Alternatively, conditions may become hazardous when work is done in conjunction with residues, contamination etc.

The confined space may negatively impact on working posture. Totally enclosed spaces may also have poor lighting.

Materials, equipment and substances to be used

Depending on the nature of the confined space electrical equipment and other tools may need to be suitable for use in a potentially flammable atmosphere.

Materials need to be able to be safely handled into the confined space and to the point of work within it. Flammable and combustible materials should not be stored in confined spaces that have not been specifically created or allocated for that purpose.

Care must be taken to ensure specified substances will not give off toxic, flammable or explosible vapours on their own or in combination with any residual chemical that may be present in the confined space.

People at risk

As with any ‘suitable and sufficient’ risk assessment the impact of the hazard on vulnerable groups and individuals must be considered. To work effectively in confined spaces workers will need to be in good general health, able to move well in restricted spaces, able to use respiratory protective equipment, not have any psychological difficulties with confined spaces (claustrophobia) and possibly height and darkness.

The reliability of control measures, including PPE and RPE

Given the potential for loss of life both hardware and software control measures for specified and other significant risks require careful consideration. Equipment for safe access, lighting and other electrical equipment, pumps for controlling water levels or providing clean air, and equipment for isolating and locking-ff energy sources, chemical feeds etc, should all be properly specified, maintained in safe working order, and regularly inspected and tested. Back-up equipment may need to be available.

If there are any concerns regarding breathable oxygen levels or the presence of seriously toxic substances in the air breathing apparatus should be specified to guarantee a supply of breathable air.

A safe system of work needs to be in place (discussed later) and in high hazard confined spaces Permits to Work may need to be used.
