Exclusion of ignition sources

Inerting is an approach to preventing explosions / the formation of an explosive atmosphere through the reduction of oxygen levels. In a substantially closed system the oxygen content of the atmosphere within the plant can be controlled at a safe level (below the lower explosive limit). The maximum safe oxygen content will vary with the type of inert gas and the chemical reactivity of the material being processed. It will need to be determined experimentally.

Inerting is only likely to be effective in a fully-enclosed system with a minimum number of places where air can enter. The likelihood of air entering when process materials are added to or removed from the system needs to be considered. If it is likely, a purge cycle will be needed before the process restarts.

Many factors will influence the overall reliability of an inerting system, including:

  • the location and number of atmospheric sampling points
  • the type of sensor head
  • the frequency of calibration of the sensor
  • contaminants in the system that interfere with sensor readings
  • provision of safe means of control or shutdown, if the oxygen concentration exceeds a predetermined level
  • adequate supplies of inert gas for all foreseeable needs
  • the number of locations where air may enter the plant
  • the safety margin allowed when setting control levels for oxygen
  • the reliability of any electronic control system.

If the plant is held at a pressure slightly above atmospheric, air leaks into the system can be avoided, however the risk of asphyxiation from the inert gas, should it accumulate in the general atmosphere of the workroom, would need to be considered.

A sufficient reserve of inert gas is required to ensure that the plant can be shut down safely if a seal failure or similar unexpected leak occurs.
